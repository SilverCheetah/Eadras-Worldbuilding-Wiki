# Orcs

**Summary**: Orcs are powerful, honor-bound warriors descended from the [[velrathi|Orug]], living as both nomadic plains clans and integrated city communities with deep cultural exchange between both ways of life.

**Sources**: Orc.txt, Peoples of Eadras.txt, Compiled Lore - Velrathi and Their Descendant Races.txt, notes on various topics 1.txt, Extra large grok chat 5-9-2026.txt

**Last updated**: 2026-05-09

## Overview

Orcs are powerful, muscular warriors who live for battle and tribal honor. They are tough, relentless, and built for raw strength and survival â€” an honor-bound people who have split into two complementary cultures: nomadic plains clans and respected city communities.

They are descendants of the [[velrathi|Orug]] (natural devolution â€” smaller, less capable, but with a major cultural shift away from service into clan structure). The [[demon-war|Demon War]] caused a major cultural disruption that fundamentally changed Orc culture from its Orug origins.

The Orug bloodline diverged into **two modern descendant peoples**: **Orcs** and **Ogres**. Both carry diluted Orug blood; orcs preserved the cultural and martial inheritance and built the modern plains civilization described below, while ogres are bigger and less articulate, likely a tribe changed by wild magic during the long unraveling that followed the Demon War. (sources: Compiled Lore - Velrathi and Their Descendant Races.txt; [[peoples-of-eadras]])

## Drinks â€” Saddle Brew and Stonefire

The orcish split between nomadic plains life and settled clan-hold life shapes the drinks the orcs make. **Saddle Brew** is the low-alcohol, communal-rotating fermented drink the riders make in saddle-bags during long marches, brewed for one primary purpose: to make plains water safe to drink (*it prevents the squirts*). **Stonefire** is the high-proof distilled spirit produced only when the clans are settled in their holds or cities, used both for celebration and as a medical sterilizer for wounds and tools, and refined further into orcish [[aqua-vitae]]. See [[orcish-drinks]] for the full treatment, including the practice of pooling individual riders' batches into a common rotating supply, the tradition of stream-cooling the bags overnight, and the role of Stonefire as the centerpiece of the clan arrival ritual.

## Anatomy

Tall and heavily muscled, often six to seven feet tall. Rugged features and scars worn like badges. They favor rough leather, bone, and metal armor that shows off their strength.

## The Two Cultures

### Plains Orcs
Roam vast plains in nomadic clans, **following the herds across the plains** â€” a culture organized around mobility and craftsmanship using materials from the herds they follow. The closest real-world analogues are the historical **Mongols** and the **Plains Indians**: pastoral, mobile, herd-anchored societies whose social and material life is built atop the cycle of the herd. They maintain the old ways of the Orug â€” martial tradition, clan loyalty, and outdoor survival.

#### Clan-holds

Each clan maintains a **permanent clan-hold** â€” a fixed mountain or hill anchor at the edge of the clan's range, deliberately sited **off the plains so that the open grasslands remain reserved for grazing herds.** A clan-hold is **not a city.** It is a stone or earthwork compound: winter refuge, elder home, ceremonial site, archive of clan memory, smithy, and primary venue for clan-internal business. (source: notes on various topics 1.txt)

The clan-hold is occupied year-round by elders, smiths, the very young, the wounded, and rotating clan members on hold-duty. **Most orcs spend the majority of the year following the herds** â€” but every clan member knows their hold is there, holds the names of those buried beneath it, and returns to it across the year. The clan-hold is the place from which the clan **is** the clan; the herd-follow is what the clan **does**.

Plains Orcs do not have their own cities (the older source notes a possible singular exception, undetailed in canon).

### City Orcs
Have settled among civilized peoples while preserving their distinct identity. City orc neighborhoods feature heavily defensive stone walls. Inside, the homes are surprisingly warm and organized like a tent â€” furs, low tables, hanging fabrics. They wear modified city clothing with a martial cut. They **never go unarmed**, and many have armor plates sewn into inside pockets.

City orcs have been around long enough that they're not seen as threats â€” it's understood that you don't mess with an orc's honor.

## Cultural Exchange

Each group sends their young to live with the other for **one to several years** â€” typically a span between adolescence and full adulthood. City children go to the plains to learn the old ways; plains children go to cities to learn negotiation, trade, and to live among the other races. The exchange is a long-standing tradition and is the load-bearing reason the two halves of orc society remain culturally unified across long distances: every adult orc, plains or city, has lived the other half of their people's life. (sources: Compiled Lore - Velrathi and Their Descendant Races.txt; notes on various topics 1.txt)

## Honor, Mastery, and Competition

Orc culture is defined by **practicality, mastery, and personal honor.** Skill is valued above almost everything else. The orc cultural reflex is to **channel competitive drive into constant contests**: nearly every craft, trade, and martial discipline becomes a competition. A smith competes with smiths. A leatherworker competes with leatherworkers. A caravan guard competes with caravan guards. The competition is structured, public, and serious. It is also affectionate â€” the people one competes with are the people one ultimately knows and respects most. (source: notes on various topics 1.txt)

### Grandmasters

Those who have proven themselves supremely skilled in their discipline â€” who have outcompeted enough of their peers across enough years that the contests no longer challenge them â€” become **Grandmasters**. A Grandmaster **retires from active competition** and takes up the roles of **teacher, advisor, and judge.** Grandmasters set the standards by which younger orcs are evaluated, train the next generation, and adjudicate disputes within their craft. The transition from competitor to grandmaster is among the most respected passages in orc life and is the orc equivalent of an elder-status promotion.

Each clan tracks its grandmasters carefully; a clan with many grandmasters across many disciplines is a clan of high standing.

## Leadership and Succession

Orc leadership is **strictly merit-based.** A potential chieftain undergoes **years of testing and apprenticeship** under the current chieftain and the council of elders, demonstrating skill, judgment, restraint, and the capacity to hold the clan together under stress. The current chieftain typically names a **designated assistant** during their tenure â€” not as heir-by-blood, but as the person who has proven readiest to take the role. (source: notes on various topics 1.txt)

A wise chieftain **steps down** voluntarily when they are no longer capable, and the designated assistant takes over with the consent of the elders.

In extreme cases â€” a chieftain who has lost the capacity to lead but will not step down â€” **the elders and warriors together can remove the chieftain.** This is a recognized but rarely-invoked procedure; the cultural memory of bad chieftains who had to be removed is sharp, and chieftains generally read the room and step down rather than be removed.

Bloodlines are not entirely irrelevant â€” clans track who descended from previous chieftains and assistants â€” but blood is at most a tiebreaker among proven candidates. Skill and judgment carry the question.

## The Great Gathering

The high point of orc life is the **Great Gathering**, held **every three years**. **Nearly every member of every clan attends**, leaving only a small guard behind in each clan-hold and a minimal herd-watch on the plains. (source: notes on various topics 1.txt)

The Gathering is a massive event lasting weeks â€” among the largest organized assemblies on the continent at any time it is held. It serves all of the orc people's collective needs at once:

- **Trade** â€” clan-to-clan exchange of crafts, herd surplus, raw materials, and finished goods. The economic engine of plains-orc society.
- **Diplomacy** â€” treaties, dispute resolution, blood-debt settlements, and inter-clan agreements
- **Matchmaking** â€” courtship and marriage arrangements across clans, an explicit purpose of the Gathering and the chief means by which clans avoid genetic and political insularity
- **Skill demonstrations** â€” public exhibitions by master crafters, smiths, and warriors, often serving as informal qualifying contests for grandmaster status
- **Competition in every conceivable discipline** â€” the largest concentrated contest cycle in the orc calendar; many disciplines hold their decisive championships only at the Gathering, three years apart, and an orc's standing is significantly shaped by their Gathering record

City orcs travel from across the known world to attend; plains and city orcs reaffirm their unity at every Gathering. The location rotates by tradition. The Gathering is also the occasion when grandmasters meet across clans, when leadership transitions are formally witnessed, and when clan disputes that cannot be settled locally are brought before the assembled elders.

## Disposition â€” not a conquering people

Orcs are **fearsome warriors when their clans are threatened**, and they are widely respected â€” and feared â€” for their martial capacity. **They are not, however, a conquering people.** Their honor is rooted in:

- **Self-mastery** â€” the discipline to outwork, outpractice, and out-skill rivals through years of preparation
- **Protection of the clan** â€” the duty to defend kin, herds, and clan-hold against any threat
- **Proving superiority through skill** â€” winning the contest, the smith-off, the duel, the wager â€” *not* through territorial conquest or domination of other peoples

An orc clan defending its herds and lands will fight with a ferocity that has shocked invaders into retreat for thousands of years. The same clan, even if militarily dominant in a region, **does not conquer neighbors, demand tribute, or impose orc rule** on other peoples. Orc honor offers no reward for that kind of victory; the cultural reflex looks at conquest and sees a kind of cheating â€” the man who would rather take ground than win a contest is the man no orc respects. (source: notes on various topics 1.txt)

This is the cultural fact that has allowed orcs to integrate so deeply into mixed cities while remaining recognizably themselves. They are extraordinarily dangerous and uninterested in dominion. Both halves are necessary; either alone is misread.

## The Night of the Gray Wall â€” orcish mythic memory

The annual **Night of the Gray Wall** festival is the emotional center of the modern orcish religious calendar â€” the ritual reenactment of the [[battle-of-the-broken-conclaves|Battle of the Broken Conclaves]] in which the [[orugi|Orug]], [[aetherins|Aetherin]], and [[el-mer|El'mer]] held the Final Wall and were exterminated to the last individual after promised reinforcements failed to arrive. The festival's recitation form â€” *"They were betrayed. The reinforcements that were promised never cameâ€¦"* â€” is preserved at [[mythic-recitations#3-3-the-orcish-myth-the-night-of-the-gray-wall|Mythic Recitations Â§ 3.3]] alongside the canonical orcish mythic voice analysis at [[mythic-recitations#4-4-the-orcish-voice-emotionally-charged-ritually-reinforced-grief-rooted|Â§ 4.4]] â€” **emotionally charged, ritually reinforced, grief-rooted**, *fierce, mourning, and refusal-of-forgetting*. The orcs do not let pain dim with time; they hold it in ritual. Gray ash marks across the shoulders during the festival are a symbolic echo of Orug skin; the names of the fallen are recited collectively; bonfires and ritual combat displays make the festival visible to neighbouring communities.

The orcish identification of themselves as descended from *the diluted blood of the three* (orcs from [[orugi|Orug]], humans from [[aetherins|Aetherin]], "the smaller folk" from [[el-mer|El'mer]]) is consistent with the broader wiki canon on the engineered-race lineages and is what orcs in the modern era *actually believe and recite*.
