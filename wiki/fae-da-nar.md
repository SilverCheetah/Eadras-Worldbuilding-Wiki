# Fae-da'Nar

**Summary**: **Fae-da'Nar** is the hidden, multi-species **Woodkin** society of Eadras's Frontier wildlands, bound by [[four-laws-of-fae-da-nar|four simple Laws]] designed to allow many Woodkin races with incompatible instincts to coexist with humans without provoking exterminatory conflict. Its overriding purpose is **survival through coexistence with humans**; its judicial backbone is the [[druids|Druids]], whose neutral grounds are sacred peace territory. Its punishment structure is severe (first violation: warning; second violation: death). Its enforcement is decentralised and depends on Fey self-policing. Its existence is largely unknown to ordinary mortals, who experience it only as *the forest folk* and the [[frontier-hospitality|frontier hospitality]] customs that mirror its compact.

## What Fae-da'Nar is

Fae-da'Nar is **not a kingdom, not a council, not a single people**. It is a **survival compact** binding many Woodkin and Fey-adjacent races â€” fey, woodfolk, nature-spirits, fey-touched mortals, and various smaller hidden populations â€” into shared rules that allow them to occupy the wildlands without provoking organised human retaliation against the whole.

The compact addresses an existing problem: Woodkin races are too diverse to share a single legal code in the mortal sense (instincts, appetites, lifespans, body-plans, magical capacities all vary), and humans are too numerous and well-organised to ignore. A detailed statute code would fail immediately. The Fae-da'Nar response is *four simple laws*, comprehensible to every Woodkin species, addressing only the **behavioural thresholds** whose violation would endanger the hidden whole.

Its purpose is not justice in the abstract. Its purpose is **survival through coexistence with humans**.

## The Four Laws

Full treatment at [[four-laws-of-fae-da-nar]]. The Laws in compressed form:

1. **Never give humans reason to fear us.** *(The First Law)*
2. **Do not interfere in human society.** *(The Second Law)*
3. **Oppose predatory [[the-shunned|Shunned]] races when they threaten humans.** *(The Third Law)*
4. **Obey the [[druids|Druids]].** *(The Fourth Law â€” obey as judges, not as kings)*

Each Law's core function is **collective survival**, not individual virtue. The Laws operate as behavioural thresholds â€” *do this, do not do that* â€” rather than as moral philosophy. A Woodkin who follows the Laws may still be cruel, predatory, secretive, or strange; the Laws do not require kindness, only restraint at specific thresholds.

## Punishment structure

The compact's enforcement is severe and clear:

- **First violation**: warning. The offender is marked. The community knows.
- **Second violation**: death.
- **No warning**: some offenders receive no warning if they are already considered too dangerous (predators in blood, repeat offenders from beyond a local jurisdiction, beings whose first violation is so severe that warning would endanger others).

> ***"Fae-da'Nar does not ask whether the individual is precious. It knows the individual is precious. Then it asks whether keeping them alive will kill everyone else."***

The harshness reflects the compact's structural condition: a hidden multi-species society lacks the institutional capacity for long-term containment of dangerous individuals across diverse territories and instincts. Exile may move the danger to another border. Imprisonment requires consenting jailers and consenting captives across incompatible species. Death is finite and clear.

The harshness creates internal fear inside Fae-da'Nar. **This is deliberate**. A compact whose violation has no real consequence has no power. The fear is the law's teeth.

## Who is in Fae-da'Nar

The compact binds:

- **Fey** of many types â€” court-aligned fey, wild fey, individual fey-spirits
- **Woodfolk / Woodkin** â€” the mortal-adjacent forest peoples (hidden-village dwellers, fey-touched lineages, partial-fey hybrids)
- **Nature spirits** â€” local water-spirits, tree-spirits, hill-spirits operating in the wildlands
- **Some hidden populations** â€” antlered, bog, mushroom, dream, and other deeply non-human kinds that exist in small numbers across the Frontier

The compact does **not** bind:

- **The [[the-shunned|Shunned]]** â€” predatory races that reject the compact
- **[[druids|Druids]]** themselves â€” Druids are *judges of* Fae-da'Nar, not subjects of it; their grounds are sacred peace territory, and their judgments bind Fae-da'Nar members rather than vice versa
- **Ordinary humans** â€” though frontier humans have their own [[frontier-hospitality|hospitality customs]] that mirror the compact from the human side

The compact does **not require uniform interpretation**. A trollish Woodkin's reading of *unnecessary killing* differs from a moth-spirit's reading, a thorn-child's reading, or a river-hag's reading. The Druids translate **thresholds across these differences**.

## The Druids' role

[[druids|Druids]] are the load-bearing institution that makes Fae-da'Nar workable. They are **not rulers**. They are **trusted judges, communicators, healers, mediators, and keepers of neutral ground**.

A Druid's chosen ground is **sacred peace territory**. No fighting there. Enemy races stand under the same silence. Predator and prey speak without tooth. Fey feud pauses. Human envoys, if invited, may stand without being glamoured into a cautionary tale.

> ***"The Fourth Law makes the Druid's ground the place where instinct must sit down and listen."***

The Druids' authority depends on **trust, not force**. Fae-da'Nar protects Druids fiercely *because* their existence is what keeps Fae-da'Nar from collapsing into endless inter-species conflict. Killing a Druid is not merely insult â€” it is an attack on the **possibility of settlement**.

This is also why Druids survived the [[anti-magic-crusade|Anti-Magic Crusade]] comparatively intact: anti-magic crusaders who attacked Druid-held wildlands were not merely killing rural priests. They were attacking the *judicial infrastructure of hidden coexistence*. Fey retaliation was disproportionate and structurally explicable: Fae-da'Nar was defending its courts. Many mortal histories of *haunted woods*, *failed crusader expeditions*, *monster attacks*, or *divine punishment* during that period may actually record Fae-da'Nar legal-defence actions and Fey retaliation against the Crusade.

## The Anti-Magic Crusade survival

Druids endured the Crusade because:

- Many lived in **rural, liminal, or distant wild-lands** the Crusade's organised mobs and armies could not safely reach
- They did **not depend on urban academies or centralised institutions** that the Crusade could target
- They served as **judges of Fey society**, and Fey trusted and protected them fiercely
- Attacks on Druids triggered **disproportionate Fey retaliation**, which the Crusade's recruitment material did not anticipate

Border Druids and settlement-adjacent grove-judges *did* suffer. Druids were not untouched â€” but their survival was specific and structurally explained, not random luck.

The Crusade is a load-bearing test case for Fae-da'Nar: the compact's purpose is to survive human fear-of-magic at scale, and the Crusade is the moment it was directly tested. The compact held.

## What Fae-da'Nar is not

- **Not a state** â€” no sovereign, no capital, no army, no taxation
- **Not a religion** â€” though Druids serve some religious functions, the compact itself is secular survival logic
- **Not a sect or movement** â€” membership is not chosen by individuals; it is inherited by being Woodkin in the Frontier
- **Not a moral framework** â€” the Laws are survival thresholds, not virtues
- **Not friendly to humans by default** â€” the compact protects humans only insofar as not-frightening-them serves Woodkin survival
- **Not protective of all Woodkin** â€” Shunned races are explicitly outside, and the compact authorises action against them

## The core paradox

Fae-da'Nar's laws exist to coexist with humans, but they are built on **fear of humans**. All frontier peace is built on fear of what war would cost. Humans fear monsters. Woodkin fear mobs. Fey fear boredom, iron, betrayal, and being explained.

> ***"Fae-da'Nar keeps the forest from becoming a nightmare. Frontier hospitality keeps the village from becoming a provocation."***

The compact succeeds by preventing any side from proving the other side's nightmare correct. It is mature, not warm.
