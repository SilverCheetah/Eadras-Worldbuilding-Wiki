# Wizards

**Summary**: Wizards are the scholarly practitioners of structured arcane magic â€” the largest of the four magical orders by population, and the most familiar to ordinary people. A wizard works through **mental focus and physical grimoires**: spells held in the mind during the working day are cast quickly and fluidly; spells not currently memorized are cast by reading directly from a personal grimoire, which takes longer. The arcane tradition is **multi-source** â€” a single wizard's spell list draws on the [[magic-in-eadras#Quintessence, Eldritch, Mana|Eldritch / mana]] family of energies, on positive and negative energy, and on alien mana from beyond Eadras â€” and most modern wizards depend on the [[magic-in-eadras#The Weave|Weave]]'s conductive infrastructure to deliver those varied energies, even though they are not native users of the Weave the way [[order-sharanel|Netharim]] are.

## What a wizard does

A wizard practices **structured scholarly magic** through two complementary modes:

- **Mental focus** â€” the wizard holds a working set of spells in mind, prepared and ready to cast quickly. The size of this working set is bounded; see [[#Mental capacity|Mental capacity]] below.
- **Physical grimoire** â€” the wizard maintains a written record of spells, in their own personal arcane notation. Spells not currently held in mind can still be cast, by **reading directly from the grimoire**.

Both modes are real wizardry. A wizard whose memorized set is exactly what the situation calls for will outperform a wizard pulling out a grimoire; a wizard with a complete and current grimoire will outperform one whose memorized set is wrong for the situation. Most working wizards balance the two: a small set of frequently-needed spells held in mind continuously, the wider repertoire kept in the grimoire for less-routine work.

Per the [[true-language|Language Hierarchy]], wizards work in the **Arcane Language** â€” the mortal-tier referential approximation, three tiers below the True Language of the Omniversal Creator. Wizardry is the most disciplined and successful mortal use of that tier: where ordinary mortals' speech has no magical force at all, a wizard's structured formulas â€” refined, precise, scaffolded by runic notation â€” can compensate enough to reach across the gap and exert leverage on reality.

## Mental capacity

A wizard's working set of memorized spells is bounded. Hold too many at once and the precision of any one of them suffers; hold too complex a working at the upper edge of capacity and the wizard cannot also hold a routine cantrip alongside it.

In-universe, this cap is real â€” wizards know they cannot memorize unlimited material, they know that more powerful (higher-circle) spells eat more of their available capacity than smaller ones, and they know that with practice and study the cap rises. The cognitive science of *why* the cap exists is a Velrathi research interest of long standing; the practical fact of it is part of every apprentice's first year of training. (For the splat-book mechanical formula governing this, see [[rpg/wizard-mechanics]].)

The cap is the structural reason **grimoires exist at all**. A wizard who could simply memorize their entire repertoire would not need a written record. Because the cap is real and the repertoire is potentially much larger than the cap, the grimoire is not optional equipment â€” it is the wizard's working library, the durable record that holds everything not currently in mind.

## Grimoire casting

When a wizard wants to cast a spell **not currently memorized**, they cast it by **reading directly from the grimoire**. This is real spellcasting â€” the spell will work, the energies will obey â€” but it takes **noticeably longer** than casting a memorized version of the same spell.

The longer prep time scales with the spell's complexity. A simple spell read from a grimoire takes only a little extra; a complex high-circle working can take substantially longer to prepare, because more of the formula must be read, parsed, and held in mind at once than a memorized version requires.

This is why a wizard who knows what kind of working a coming day will demand spends time the night before reviewing their grimoire and **memorizing fresh material into the working set**. Choosing today's memorized spells is an act of forecasting; getting it wrong means falling back to grimoire-reading at the moment of need.

### Personal shorthand

A wizard who has written enough of their own material into their grimoire eventually develops **personal shorthand** â€” a unique system of magical notation, tailored to their own habits of thought, that condenses the formal arcane formulas into a compressed personal cipher.

When casting from their own grimoire using their own shorthand, the prep time is **dramatically reduced** â€” a wizard reading their own shorthand can cast nearly as quickly as casting from memory, regardless of the spell's level.

The cost is that **personal shorthand is nearly impossible for other wizards to read**. The same compression that makes the notation efficient for the original author makes it inscrutable to anyone else. A junior wizard given a senior's grimoire to study will spend weeks or months working out the shorthand before they can cast from it usefully â€” and even then, their casting from the deciphered notation will be slower than the original author's was, because the reader must translate-and-cast rather than read-and-cast.

This is one of the great quiet tensions of wizard culture. Personal shorthand is a wizard's most efficient working tool **and** the single biggest barrier to the transmission of arcane knowledge. The [[velrathi|Velrathi]], for whom intergenerational transmission of magical knowledge is a load-bearing cultural value, have a long tradition of master wizards leaving behind translated *fair-copy* grimoires alongside their personal-shorthand working copies â€” but the practice is far from universal, and many a wizard's death has produced a library of priceless spells that nobody can read.

(Mechanically, in the splat, *Personal Shorthand* is a feat. See [[rpg/wizard-mechanics]].)

## Grimoires as treasure

A wizard's personal grimoire is **one of their most prized possessions** â€” frequently the single most valuable object in a working wizard's possession, ranking above magical implements, magitek devices, and even significant amounts of coin. The grimoire is the wizard's life's work, in a way that no other category of professional artifact can match.

Several practices follow from this:

- **Multiple backup copies.** Most experienced wizards maintain **multiple copies** of their grimoire, hidden in secure locations. A senior wizard might have three copies â€” one carried, one in a home library, one in a deep-vault or banking-house deposit â€” with the carried copy regularly updated to match the master record.
- **Magical traps and wards.** Working grimoires are routinely **trapped and warded** against unauthorized opening. The trap is rarely lethal-by-design (a wizard who books a grimoire that kills its reader has no way to grant a friend access), but a stranger reading an unauthorized grimoire will commonly trigger an alarm, a glyph-binding that locks the book until the wizard releases it, an arcane shock that drops the unauthorized reader unconscious, or â€” for the most carefully-warded books â€” magical effects that punish the reader more severely.
- **Personal shorthand as security.** The same shorthand that speeds casting also protects the contents from casual decipherment. A captured wizard's grimoire, even fully readable, is in practice useful only to the captured wizard until someone has invested significant time in cracking the shorthand.

Acquiring **another wizard's grimoire** is, accordingly, considered a **major treasure** in any context where it is acquired â€” looted from a dead wizard, recovered from a ruin, captured from a defeated enemy, or purchased from a willing seller. Even a partial or fragmentary grimoire is genuinely valuable; a complete grimoire of a master wizard's working repertoire is the kind of find that founds dynasties.

The grimoire-as-treasure practice has produced its own small industry: wizards-for-hire who specialize in **deciphering** captured grimoires, **disabling** grimoire wards without setting them off, and **producing fair-copies** of recovered shorthand for resale. The trade is small, well-paid, and quietly competitive; the most accomplished decipherers are themselves senior wizards who have spent decades developing the meta-skill of cracking other wizards' notation.

## Spell ingredients

Many spells â€” especially powerful or complex ones â€” are cast with **ingredients**: physical objects that act as **specific sources of magical energy** and shape the spell's effect.

The mechanism follows directly from the [[magic-in-eadras#Quintessence, Eldritch, Mana|three-state model]] of magic. **Mana** is the matrix-bound state of magical energy â€” Quintessence held within the structure of matter, organic or inorganic. Different matter holds mana in different concentrations and with different **resonances**, sometimes called *flavors* in casting parlance. A spell that is naturally aligned with a particular flavor of mana is cast more powerfully and reliably when ingredients of that flavor are introduced into the working.

Some examples:

- A **feather**, particularly from a mana-rich bird (a hippogriff feather; a primary from a wind-touched roc) carries air-aligned mana and is ideal for **air or wind spells** â€” flight, gust workings, message-on-the-wind divinations, weather-bending.
- **Mana-rich water** â€” water from a sacred spring, water condensed in a high-magic zone, water drawn from a [[seven-conduits|conduit]]-near sea â€” carries water-aligned mana and enhances **water, ocean, healing, and life-tide spells**.
- **Mana-rich crystals** ([[crystalmancy|crystalmancy]] lineages) carry the specific signature their lineage was cultivated for, and any spell that resonates with that lineage benefits from incorporating them.
- **Mana-metals** ([[mana-metals|mithril, orichalcum, kylum, valthyr]] and the Magisterium tier above them) carry their own magical signatures and are the standard ingredient class for permanent enchanted-object workings.
- **Organic ingredients** with magical biology â€” a dragon scale, a unicorn's discarded mane-hair, a fae creature's blood freely given â€” carry extremely strong specific flavors and are correspondingly rare and expensive. These are the ingredients of major spellwork; using them casually marks a wizard as either spectacularly wealthy or about to attempt something they really mean.

Some spells can be **cast without ingredients**, particularly low-circle work that does not strain the wizard's structured-resonance precision much. **Many powerful or complex spells require specific mana-heavy components to function properly** â€” the working is too far above mortal-tier capability to land cleanly without an ingredient resonance to guide it. A wizard whose grimoire contains a high-circle ritual will typically also list its required ingredients in the same entry; collecting and preparing the ingredients is part of preparing the spell.

The ingredient market is a major part of the broader Eadran magical economy. **[[goblins|Goblin]] salvage networks** quietly run substantial inventories of unusual ingredients (see [[goblins#Hoarding and the goblin network]]); [[gnomes|gnome]] workshops produce mana-bearing components on demand; [[dwarves|Shada'din]] mining clans control most of the mana-metals and many crystalmancy lineages; the [[bold|Bold Conduit Republic]] and the [[orden|Orden Conduit Compact]] both run major ingredient-trade flows along the conduit network. A working wizard does not need to forage personally for ingredients in most settled territory â€” the supply chain for them already exists.

## Distinction from the other three orders

- **[[priests|Priests]]** call upon their gods, who use the **Divine Language** on the priest's behalf. Priests do not need grimoires; their workings are mediated through the god, not stored in personal notation. A priest's working capacity is not bounded by personal mental capacity but by the god's willingness to grant the petition. Priests do not generally use spell ingredients â€” though offerings, sacred water, anointing oils, and other ritual components serve a parallel function in some priestly traditions.
- **[[lexanari|Lexanari]]** speak the **Primordial Language** directly. They do not use grimoires; they cannot, because the Primordial Language cannot be written down meaningfully â€” the act of speaking it is the act of using it. A Lexanari's "memorized capacity" is irrelevant; what bounds them is the specificity their mortal mind can hold during invocation, and that bound is much harder than a wizard's. Spell ingredients have no place in Lexanari practice.
- **[[netharim|Netharim]]** work intuitively from a deeply-studied internal model of reality. They do not memorize spells, they do not maintain grimoires, and they do not use ingredients in the wizard sense. What a Netharim does carry, instead, is a personal *body of knowledge* â€” books read, theories internalized, models refined â€” that constitutes the substrate their magic operates from. A Netharim's library is full of books. A wizard's library is full of grimoires. The two libraries look similar from outside and are doing different work.

Wizardry is therefore the **most mechanically external** of the four orders. The wizard's power is held outside the wizard, in books and ingredients, and structured into formal procedures that can be taught, written down (with shorthand caveats), and refined across generations. This is why wizards are the order with the strongest **academy and apprentice tradition**, and why every major civilization on Eadras has produced wizard schools.

## Open canon

- The full **mechanical formula** for a wizard's mental capacity (Intelligence + Wisdom-modifier-doubled + level-doubled, per the splat) is canonical for the splat side; whether the in-universe canon needs a wizard-known *folk version* of the same equation, or whether wizards know only the rough qualitative principle, is open.
- Whether **wizard schools** maintain **standardized teaching grimoires** that use a shared institutional notation rather than personal shorthand, easing transmission within the school at the cost of slower casting for graduates. The tradeoff is plausible; whether it is canonical is open.
- Whether **wizard guilds** have ever attempted to standardize a *universal* arcane shorthand â€” and what the political consequences of such a project would be (the shorthand-as-security function is real, and a universal shorthand would dissolve it).
- The full **inventory** of ingredient flavors and their primary spell affinities. The examples given above are illustrative; the complete catalogue is presumably written somewhere in the deep [[katharim|Katharim]] archives but has not been ingested into the wiki at the level of detail a working wizard would require.
- The **ingredient supply chain**'s formal political economy. The actors are sketched here; the institutional structure (guilds, regulated markets, smuggling routes for restricted ingredients, the [[black-hand|Black Hand]]'s likely involvement) is open canon.
