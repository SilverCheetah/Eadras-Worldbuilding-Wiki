# Mythic Recitations of the Foundational Catastrophes

**Summary**: A canonical compendium of the specific verbal forms in which the major peoples of Eadras remember the **three foundational catastrophes** â€” the [[arathi-cataclysm|Fall of Ssar'veth]] (~10 million years ago), the [[chaos-crusades|Chaos Incursions]] (~6 mybp and ~2 mybp), and the [[demon-war|Demon War]] (~10,000 years ago). The texts are short on purpose: they have been worn smooth by ten thousand or two million years of retelling, and each is written to be **recited around a fire, declaimed in temple, or sung at a gathering**. Each text takes the voice of the people who tell it and reflects what *that people* most wants the catastrophe to teach. The divergences among the forms are not historical errors; they are the *point*. The page also canonizes the **institutional mythic voices** â€” dragon, unicorn, humanoid, orcish, [[katharim|Katharim]], scholarly â€” as load-bearing registers for any future Eadran mythic-recitation material.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-13.

## The principle: ancients remember through myth, not history

Ancient peoples do not remember through history; they remember through **story and ritual recitation**.

The principle was articulated in the 5-11 source chat in response to the specific puzzle of how anyone on modern Eadras could still know about [[kudur-etir|Kudur-eá¹­ir / Ssar'veth]] two million years after the [[arathi-cataclysm|Cataclysm]] he caused. They don't *know* about him the way we know historical facts. Two million years is an incomprehensible amount of time. No one has records, books, or accurate history from that far back. **What survives is myth.**

The mechanism:

- A foundational catastrophe is so large that it leaves a permanent imprint on every culture that survives it.
- The imprint takes the form of **myth, ritual, ritual reenactment, and recitation** rather than detailed factual record. The specifics blur; the *moral shape* of the event endures.
- Names drift. Specific details change in transmission. The arc of the event â€” *one mortal reached too high, the heavens answered, the world broke* â€” survives across millennia even when no one alive can name the original participants.
- Different peoples remember the *same event* in characteristically different forms, each form expressing what that people most needs the event to teach.

The wiki's scholarly-historical canon â€” [[kudur-etir|Kudur-eá¹­ir]], the [[chaos|First Breach]], the [[worldender|Worldbreaker]] as alien Falling Star, the [[spear-of-crystallized-entropy|Spear of Crystallized Entropy]], [[xarvion|Xar'vion]] as Velrathi archmage, the [[black-hand|Black Hand]] â€” lives in the dedicated pages and is what *a player-character with sufficient scholarly contact* could recover in research scenes. This page records what the **non-scholars say at the campfire and at the altar**.

The principle is also a craft instruction. Whenever a future Eadran historical event is developed and the question arises of how distant peoples or distant ages remember it, the answer should default to **mythic recitation in characteristically-different cultural voices** rather than to historical-record-style summary. The wiki's scholarly-historical apparatus exists; in-world recitation does not duplicate it.

## 1. The Fall of Ssar'veth â€” the Cataclysm myths

The [[arathi-cataclysm|Arathi Cataclysm]] survives in the mythic memory of most peoples as the **Fall of Ssar'veth** â€” the story of a great mortal Netharim-king who tried to make himself a god, succeeded, and was struck down by a falling star that broke the world.

The scholarly-historical version (held in the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome of Baldaran]] and in a handful of ancient archives) preserves the original name â€” *[[kudur-etir|Kudur-eá¹­ir]], "Seal-Opener"* â€” and the actual mechanism. For everyone outside the deepest scholarly circles, the name is **Ssar'veth** and the mechanism is *the gods dropped a star on him because he tried to become one of them*.

### 1.1 The core myth â€” what almost everyone believes

> *In the Time Before, there was a being of immense pride called Ssar'veth. He was the greatest mage who ever lived, and he sought to become a god. On the day he succeeded, the heavens themselves rejected him. A star of wrath fell from the sky and struck his city, shattering the world. The seas rose, the earth split, and fire rained for a year. This is why the world is broken â€” because one man tried to steal godhood.*

The common core â€” the version told by every culture that has any version of the story at all. Short, foundational, didactic. *Pride invited cosmic punishment; the world is broken because of it.* In practice it functions as a **proverb-anchor**: people invoke "Ssar'veth" the way a real-world speaker invokes *Icarus* or *the Tower of Babel*. *"Don't be a Ssar'veth"* is recognizable across most of Eadras.

### 1.2 The standard bardic recitation

> *In the First Age, when the world was still young, there lived a Netharim-king named Ssar'veth.*
> *He was the greatest mage who ever walked the earth. His pride was without limit.*
> *He looked upon the gods and said, "Why should I serve them, when I could become one?"*
> *On the day of his ascension, Ssar'veth reached up and tore open the sky itself. The gods were furious. They took a star from heaven and hurled it down upon him in wrath.*
> *The star struck his city of [[altea|Altea]] and broke the world. The earth split open. The seas rose up and drowned the land. Fire fell from the sky for a year.*
> *The world has never been whole since that day.*
> *This is why we do not reach too high. This is why we do not challenge the gods. For when Ssar'veth tried to become their equal, the heavens answered with a star, and the world still bears the scar of his arrogance.*

The canonical recitation form â€” the version a bard would perform, the version a priest would invoke in sermon, the version that survives in fragmentary chronicles across many cultures. The named place â€” **[[altea|Altea]]** â€” is the only city-name almost any cultural version remembers. Modern Eadran cultures invoke *the fall of Altea* as a synonym for catastrophic divine judgment.

### 1.3 The dragon version â€” told with grim satisfaction

> *In the dawn of the world, a mortal named Ssar'veth grew so mighty and so proud that he believed himself the equal of the gods. On the day he tried to steal godhood, the heavens opened and a star of judgment fell upon him.*
> *The star struck his city and shattered the world. Mountains were thrown down, seas swallowed the land, and fire rained for a year. Thus did the sky itself rebuke his arrogance.*
> *Let every dragon remember the fate of Ssar'veth. Even the greatest mortal is still mortal. The heavens do not suffer equals.*

The [[dragons|dragon]] recitation reads the story as **cosmic order correcting a violation** â€” and the dragons are *pleased* by the correction. The interpretive frame: mortals have a station, the station has limits, Ssar'veth was a mortal who tried to climb past the limits, and the heavens responded as the heavens correctly should respond. The tone is **grim satisfaction**. The voice carries over into the [[dragon-empire|Dragon Empire's]] official mythic register; the closing line â€” *the heavens do not suffer equals* â€” is delivered with the implication that the dragons have their own seniority in the cosmic order.

### 1.4 The unicorn version â€” told with sorrow

> *Long ago, a being called Ssar'veth tore a wound in the world itself. He reached beyond the veil of what should be, and something answered that should never have been awakened.*
> *The thing that answered took the form of a star, and it fell upon his city. The world cracked open that day. The wound he made has never truly healed. Even now, the land still bleeds where the star fell.*
> *We unicorns remember. The scar remains. And some wounds do not close â€” they only wait.*

The [[unicorns|unicorn]] recitation reads the story as **something genuinely alien intruding from outside reality** â€” and the unicorns *grieve* over it. This version is the closest of all the mythic forms to the scholarly-historical truth: the [[worldender|Worldbreaker]] *was* an alien object pulled in from outside the [[mythos|Mythos]], and the unicorns' eldritch sensitivity gives them a memory that retains the *wrongness* of it. The unicorns do not see Ssar'veth as a properly-punished sinner; they see him as the *door* through which something outside the Mythos came in, and they see the door as still potentially open. *Some wounds do not close â€” they only wait.*

### 1.5 The humanoid version â€” the common widespread myth

> *In the ancient days there was a king named Ssar'veth, the greatest mage who ever lived. His pride grew so vast that he challenged the gods themselves, seeking to become one of them.*
> *The gods answered him with a star. It fell from heaven and struck his city, breaking the world in its fury. The oceans rose, the earth split apart, and fire fell from the sky for a year.*
> *This is why we do not reach too high. This is why we do not challenge what the gods have ordained. For when Ssar'veth tried to become more than mortal, the heavens dropped the sky upon his head, and the world still bears the wound of his folly.*

The humanoid recitation is the **default version told across most humanoid cultures** â€” humans, half-elves, half-orcs, the various mixed lineages of the modern era, and most settled humanoid populations in the [[azure-basin|Azure Basin]], the [[orden|Orden]], and the post-imperial successor states. The voice is **didactic and plain**: the moral is loud, explicit, and repeated. *We do not reach too high.* This is the standard tavern, temple, and household version of the story â€” what a humanoid child first hears about the Cataclysm.

### 1.6 The scholarly version â€” Tome of Baldaran, Fragmentary History of the First Age

> *The being known in later legend as Ssar'veth was in truth an archmage of the [[arathi|Arathi]] civilization named [[kudur-etir|Kudur-eá¹­ir]]. On the day called God's Day, he achieved ascension through the use of a powerful and unstable artifact. Immediately thereafter, a celestial body of unknown origin â€” referred to in contemporary records only as "the Falling Star" â€” struck the Arathi capital of [[altea|Altea]].*
> *The impact triggered a global catastrophe of unprecedented scale. The continental mass known as the [[altean-highlands|Altean Highlands]] was shattered, the world's crust was destabilized, and a prolonged period of firestorms, flooding, and volcanic activity followed. Approximately sixty percent of all life on the planet perished during this event.*
> *While popular tradition holds that the gods themselves cast down the star in judgment, more measured scholarship suggests that the arrival of the Falling Star was an unintended consequence of Kudur-eá¹­ir's ascension. The precise mechanism remains unknown.*
> *The event is considered the definitive end of the First Civilization. All subsequent ages have measured time in relation to this catastrophe.*

The scholarly version preserved in the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome of Baldaran]] and adjacent in the deepest archives of the [[order-sharanel|Order Sharanel]] and a handful of [[velrathi|Velrathi]] exile scholars in [[veyr-keth|Veyr'Keth]]. It preserves the original Arathi name, the named ritual day, admits the existence of the artifact, and admits the alien character of the Falling Star without endorsing the popular interpretation that the gods threw it. **Measured, cautious, and incomplete** â€” the form of an institution that does not know everything but does know it does not know.

The Tome's open-chapter scholarship is allowed to know that there was a name, a day, an artifact, and an alien Falling Star, but is not allowed to know the [[worldender-crystal|Worldender Crystal]] mechanism, the First Breach geometry, the Spear's full role, or the post-event Spear-prison sequence; those remain sealed in the closed chapters.

## 2. The Chaos Incursions â€” myths of the Breaches

The two great [[chaos-crusades|Chaos Incursions]] survive in mythic memory as *the Wound*, *the Time the Sky Bled*, or *the Unraveling*. The mythic-recitation versions do not preserve the deep mechanics; they preserve the **shape** of the event: *the world was nearly devoured by something formless, the gods fought to seal it, the seal holds â€” for now*.

### 2.1 The common mortal myth â€” "The First Wound"

> *Long ago, the world was nearly destroyed by a terrible darkness that poured through a hole in the sky. This darkness had no shape, no mind, and no mercy â€” it simply unmade everything it touched. The gods fought a desperate war against this horror for years until they finally sealed the wound.*
> *But the world was never the same. This is why there are still places where reality frays and strange things are born.*

The simplest and most widespread form. It identifies the chaos as a *terrible darkness* (the actual [[chaos|Chaos]] is, in canon, more accurately *formlessness* than darkness â€” mortal vocabulary defaults to darkness when describing the unimaginable), identifies the gods as the actors who fought and sealed it, and identifies the lingering consequence: the modern existence of [[aberrations|Aberrations]] and reality-fray zones as the downstream of the imperfect seal. This is the version told to children who ask why the marshes near a certain village are *wrong*.

### 2.2 The dragon myth â€” "The Time the Sky Bled"

> *When Ssar'veth tore open the heavens, he left a wound that never fully closed. Through that wound came the Chaos â€” a mindless, formless enemy that sought to dissolve all order. The greatest dragons of the age flew into that wound to fight it.*
> *Many did not return.*
> *The dragons still remember this as the greatest test their kind has ever faced. They speak of it with both pride and sorrow.*

The [[dragons|dragon]] version explicitly **connects the Wound to Ssar'veth's act** â€” the dragons remember that the breach Kudur-eá¹­ir drilled is the same breach the chaos came through, even if they do not remember the full Spear / Total Icon Binding mechanism. The dragon version is also the only version that **claims dragon agency in the resistance**: the dragons remember themselves as the great combatants who flew into the wound to fight the chaos. The casualties are mourned; the triumph is claimed. The story is told **with pride and sorrow** â€” a more mixed register than the dragons' grim satisfaction over Ssar'veth himself, because in the Chaos Incursions the dragons themselves paid heavily.

### 2.3 The unicorn myth â€” "The Unraveling"

> *The Chaos was not merely an enemy â€” it was the absence of meaning itself. It was the world trying to forget its own name.*
> *The unicorns say the gods did not truly defeat it. They only sealed it awayâ€¦ and the seals are weakening. Some elder unicorns claim they can still hear the Chaos whispering through the cracks in reality.*

The most ominous of the recitations. The [[unicorns|unicorns]] do not believe the chaos was defeated; they believe it was *contained*, that the containment is *failing*, and that the chaos is *waiting*. This is consistent with the unicorn mythic register established in Â§1.4 â€” *some wounds do not close â€” they only wait*. The unicorn interpretive frame extends across catastrophes: every great evil the gods supposedly sealed is, in the unicorn understanding, only sealed for the moment, and the seals are wearing thin.

The unicorns are the mythic voice of *the seals are weakening*. They are correct often enough that other peoples take them seriously when they raise an alarm.

### 2.4 The scholarly / Lirran version â€” "The First and Second Breaches"

> *There were two major incursions of Chaos into the Mythos. The first, far larger, nearly consumed the world. The second was smaller but still catastrophic. Both were enabled by the original wound created during the [[arathi-cataclysm|Cataclysm]]. The [[order-sharanel|Order Sharanel]] considers the sealing of these breaches to be among the greatest acts of the Elder Gods.*

The scholarly version preserves the **count** (two breaches), the **relative scale** (first far larger than the second), the **causal chain** (both enabled by the original Cataclysm wound), and the **institutional credit** (the Order Sharanel reads the sealings as among the [[gods-of-eadras|Elder Gods']] greatest achievements). This is the version preserved in Order Sharanel theological literature, in Lirran temple records, and in the deeper Tome of Baldaran chapters.

## 3. The Demon War â€” myths of the Still King

The [[demon-war|Demon War]] (~10,000 years ago) is the most recent of the three foundational catastrophes and the one most cleanly preserved in living cultural memory across many peoples. The scholarly-historical version is well-known: [[xarvion|Xar'vion]], a Velrathi archmage of the final Firethorn Empire centuries, sought to resurrect his murdered sister [[velena|Velena]]; achieved Unrestricted Elder God status through the Spear; summoned a [[demon-prince-of-the-open-wound|Demon Prince]] in pursuit of resurrection magic; lost his motive to [[dao|Dao]]'s curse; the Demon Prince and its legions ravaged Eadras for twenty years; half of all living creatures died; Xar'vion eventually turned against his summoned Demon Prince and joined the alliance against it; the Elder Gods bound him in [[icons|Total Icon Binding]] as the **Motionless King**; the demons were sealed away.

### 3.1 The common myth â€” "The Demon War"

> *Long ago, a Netharim-king named Xar'vion opened a gate to Hell. A demon prince and its legions poured into our world. For years, the world burned. In the end, the gods themselves bound Xar'vion in eternal stone and sealed the demons away.*
> *This is why we say: "Some doors should never be opened."*

The shortest and most widespread version. It captures the essential arc (Netharim-king, opened gate, demonic invasion, gods bound him in stone, sealing) and supplies the proverb that the story produces: *some doors should never be opened*. The proverb is in active use in modern Eadran speech, the way a real-world speaker invokes *Pandora's box*.

### 3.2 The longer common version â€” "The War of the Still King"

> *Long ago, a Netharim-king named Xar'vion opened a gate to the abyss in his mad quest to bring his sister back from death. From that gate came a demon prince and its legions, and for years the world burned.*
> *It was the closest any living memory has come to the end of all things. Every race, every nation, every hero stood together against the horde. In the end, the gods themselves were forced to intervene. They bound Xar'vion into an eternal prison of stone, condemning him to eternal stillness, and drove the demons back into the void.*
> *This is why we say: "Grief unchecked invites ruin."*
> *This is why we fear those who would bargain with things from beyond the world.*
> *Some say the Still King's statue still exists somewhere, hidden away. Some say there are those who still worship him in secret.*
> *But most people simply call the whole affair The Demon War, and are glad it is over.*

The bardic-recitation form â€” the version a traveling bard would perform in an evening hall, the version a priest might deploy in a sermon on grief and restraint. Canonically known as *The War of the Still King*. It preserves the grief-as-originating-cause, the bargain with the abyss, the universal stand, the divine intervention, and the fading rumors of his cult. The two proverbs it produces â€” *grief unchecked invites ruin* and *fear those who would bargain with things from beyond the world* â€” are both in active use in modern Eadras.

A canonical constraint on this form: **the [[black-hand|Black Hand]] is not commonly known to be associated with Xar'vion**. Cult-of-the-Still-King rumors exist; the connection to the modern, visible Black Hand criminal-political organization is hidden, and modern Eadran characters who know of the Black Hand do not by default associate it with the Motionless King. The Black Hand's actual relationship to Xar'vion is wiki canon and is held by the Inner Hand and the deepest historical scholarship only.

### 3.3 The orcish myth â€” "The Night of the Gray Wall"

> *When the demons came, the world stood on the brink of annihilation. Three races made the Final Stand at the Broken Conclaves: the noble [[orcs|Orug]], the mighty [[aetherins|Aetherin]], and the wise [[el-mer|El'mer]]. They held the line for three days and three nights against impossible odds.*
> *They were betrayed.*
> *The reinforcements that were promised never came. The three races were slaughtered to the last. Only the diluted blood of those three peoples remains today â€” in orcs, humans, and the smaller folk.*
> *This is why orcs still hold the Night of the Gray Wall every year. They mark their bodies with ash and remember the betrayal. They remember that the world was saved, but their ancestors were abandoned.*

The most emotionally charged of the Demon War myths and the version preserved with the greatest specificity by any modern people. Where the common myth speaks in broad strokes, the orcish version focuses with painful detail on the [[battle-of-the-broken-conclaves|Battle of the Broken Conclaves]] â€” the three-day stand in which the Orug, [[aetherins|Aetherin]], and [[el-mer|El'mer]] held the [[battle-of-the-broken-conclaves|Final Wall]] against the Demon Prince's hordes long enough for sealing rituals to complete, were promised reinforcements that did not come, and were exterminated to the last individual.

The orcish version is the **myth of betrayal**. The reinforcements were the Tarran Emperor's responsibility; his failure to send them is the cause the orcs remember; and the modern orcish festival of the **Night of the Gray Wall** is the annual ritual reenactment of that mourning. [[orcs|Orcs]] and [[beast-folk|beast-folk]] together mark themselves with **gray ash** (a symbolic echo of Orug skin) and recite the names of the fallen.

### 3.4 The Katharim version â€” "The Breaking of the Three"

> *The [[katharim|Katharim]] remember what others have forgotten.*
> *They remember that the Orug, Aetherin, and El'mer were not merely brave â€” they were the last true children of the Three Progenitor Gods. They remember that these three races died defending the world while others held back their strength.*
> *They keep the true names of the fallen. They keep the memory of the betrayal. They do not forgive, and they do not forget.*

The mythologically precise version. The [[katharim|Katharim]] â€” the Rememberers of the Dead, the modern post-Rathari priesthood of memorial and underworld matter â€” are the institutional keepers of the names and the truths of the Demon War's most painful moment.

Two specific theological claims: **the three fallen races were "the last true children of the Three Progenitor Gods"** ([[the-three|Melania, Mark, Rand]]), connecting the three exterminated peoples directly to the three creator-gods of the modern humanoid races in a way that holds them as *sacred* â€” the direct descendants of the progenitor gods in a way the modern diluted lineages no longer are; and **"others held back their strength"** â€” the Katharim's explicit naming of the failure that the orcs call betrayal. The Katharim do not soften the accusation. They keep the memory institutionally.

*They do not forgive, and they do not forget.* The Katharim are not vengeful in the sense of plotting revenge; they are vengeful in the sense of refusing to let the memory dim.

### 3.5 What different peoples emphasize

The full pattern across the Demon War mythic forms reveals each people's characteristic emphasis:

- **Humans** tell it as **a tragedy of grief and hubris** â€” [[xarvion|Xar'vion's]] love for his sister, his refusal to accept her death, the cascade of catastrophe that followed.
- **[[rathari|Rathari descendants]]** (functionally extinct as a coherent people, but where their memory survives â€” primarily through the [[katharim|Katharim]] and the post-Rathari scholarly traditions) tell it as a **cautionary tale about unchecked ambition and forbidden magic** â€” Xar'vion was one of their own, the greatest archmage of the final Firethorn centuries, and his fall is the cautionary tale about what magical brilliance unchecked becomes.
- **[[dragons|Dragons]]** tell it with **contempt** â€” *"A mortal who thought he could control what he could not understand."* The dragons' grim-satisfaction register applied to the Demon War reads Xar'vion's specific failure as the predictable consequence of mortal arrogance about cosmic forces.
- **[[unicorns|Unicorns]]** tell **a darker version** â€” they claim the Demon Prince is not truly gone, only waiting *for the next heartbroken fool to open the door again*. Consistent with the unicorn mythic register of *some wounds do not close â€” they only wait*.
- **[[orcs|Orcs]]** tell **the Night of the Gray Wall** version (Â§3.3) â€” the emotional center of the modern orcish religious calendar.
- **[[katharim|Katharim]]** tell **the Breaking of the Three** version (Â§3.4) â€” the most precise version, the institutional version.
- **[[beast-folk|Beast-folk]]** â€” the source-chat notes that beast-folk *do not remember* the Demon War much because they have *tried to forget*. Whether the forgetting is collective amnesia, deliberate institutional silence, the slow erosion of memory by [[sylph|Sylph]]'s Veil influence, or some combination is open canon. The forgetting is itself a piece of beast-folk character worth integrating. (See [[open-questions]].)

## 4. The institutional mythic voices

The pattern across the three catastrophes reveals a consistent set of **mythic voices** that each major people maintains. These voices are load-bearing canon for any future Eadran mythic-recitation material: when a new ancient event is developed and its mythic preservation is needed, the voices below are the established forms.

### 4.1 The dragon voice â€” grim satisfaction over hubris

The [[dragons|dragons']] interpretive register reads catastrophes as **vindications of cosmic hierarchy**. Mortals who overreach get what they deserve. The world breaking is the world correctly resisting violation. The dragons take grim satisfaction in the punishment of pride.

When the dragons themselves are the casualties (as in the Chaos Incursions, Â§2.2), the register softens into *pride and sorrow* â€” the dragons' own sacrifice is honored, but the framework of cosmic-order-correcting-violation remains intact.

The voice is **conservative, hierarchical, and self-confirming**. It is the voice the [[dragon-empire|Dragon Empire]] uses officially.

### 4.2 The unicorn voice â€” sorrowful, watchful, alarmed

The [[unicorns|unicorns']] interpretive register reads catastrophes as **intrusions from outside reality that were never fully closed**. The seals weaken. The wounds wait. The next iteration of the catastrophe is always approaching.

The unicorns are right often enough that this voice carries weight. The [[worldender|Worldbreaker]] did come from outside the Mythos; the Chaos Incursions did exploit a wound that was never fully healed; the Demon Prince is not, in fact, fully gone. The unicorns are the canary in the cosmic shaft.

The voice is **alarmed, perceptive, and unwelcome**. Other peoples find unicorn warnings exhausting and prefer not to hear them. They are nonetheless almost always correct, and the smarter Eadran institutions ([[order-sharanel|Order Sharanel]], the deepest Lirran temple traditions) take unicorn alarm-raising seriously.

### 4.3 The humanoid voice â€” didactic, plain, proverb-producing

The humanoid interpretive register reads catastrophes as **moral lessons for the listener**. *We do not reach too high. Grief unchecked invites ruin. Some doors should never be opened.* The story serves the proverb; the proverb serves the social-instructional function of warning the next generation.

The voice is **practical, mnemonic, and broadly conservative** â€” not in the dragons' cosmic-hierarchy sense but in the everyday folk-wisdom sense of *we have learned this hard lesson; remember it*. It is the voice most stories take when most cultures tell them.

### 4.4 The orcish voice â€” emotionally charged, ritually reinforced, grief-rooted

The [[orcs|orcish]] interpretive register reads the Demon War specifically â€” and possibly other catastrophes by extension, though this is open canon â€” as **ancestral grief that must be ritually held**. The Night of the Gray Wall is the canonical expression; the grief is felt collectively, marked on the body with ash, and recited aloud in the names of the fallen.

The voice is **fierce, mourning, and refusal-of-forgetting**. The orcs do not let pain dim with time. They hold it in ritual.

### 4.5 The Katharim voice â€” institutional precision, the keeping of true names

The [[katharim|Katharim's]] interpretive register reads catastrophes as **specific named events with specific named dead** whose memory the Katharim are *institutionally responsible* for maintaining. The Katharim mythic voice is the most accurate to the historical record because the Katharim's entire institutional purpose is the accurate keeping of the dead.

The voice is **precise, unflinching, and immune to the softening that other peoples apply over centuries**. The Katharim know who was there, what happened, who failed, and who paid for the failure. They preserve this knowledge for as long as their order endures.

### 4.6 The scholarly voice â€” Tome of Baldaran, the measured admission of uncertainty

The scholarly version preserved in the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome of Baldaran]] (and adjacent in the [[order-sharanel|Order Sharanel's]] deepest theological texts, the [[velrathi|Velrathi]] exile archives in [[veyr-keth|Veyr'Keth]], and the most rigorous Lirran temple traditions) reads catastrophes as **events with discoverable mechanisms whose details are partial or unknown, recorded with appropriate epistemic restraint**.

The voice is **cautious, factually careful, and explicitly admitting what is not known**. The Tome does not pretend to a certainty it does not have. It records names where names survive, mechanisms where mechanisms are documented, and explicit lacunae where the record is sealed or lost. The Tome is the voice that says *the precise mechanism remains unknown*.

This is the voice of institutional history in Eadras at its best, and it is rare. Most peoples tell the louder, more emotionally satisfying versions documented above.

## 5. How this material is meant to be used

The recitation forms in Â§1, Â§2, and Â§3 are **quotable artifacts**. They are written to be deployed as-is in:

- **Bardic performance scenes** â€” a traveling bard performing *The War of the Still King* in an evening hall while characters listen.
- **Priestly sermons** â€” a temple recitation of *the fall of Altea* invoked in a sermon on pride.

### Why the recitation texts are short
A myth that survives ten thousand or two million years is short â€” it has been worn smooth by retelling. The full [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome of Baldaran]] scholarly version can be longer because the scholarly tradition is institutional and supports paragraph-length precision; the popular versions cannot afford to be long, because they have to be carried in mouths and ears and ritual reenactments. The texts above are written at the length they would actually be recited at.

### Why they deliberately differ
Same event, six surviving recitations. Each tells the modern listener something different about *who is doing the telling*. This is how the world's cultures earn their individual voices. The dragons' grim satisfaction differs from the unicorns' watchful sorrow differs from the humanoid didactic *didn't they deserve it* differs from the Katharim's institutional precision. The divergences are load-bearing.

### The principle generalizes
Any future Eadran historical event that survives long enough to be remembered through myth should be expected to produce **multiple cultural recitations** in the established voices documented in Â§4. The [[anti-magic-crusade|Anti-Magic Crusade]], the [[great-banishment|Great Banishment]], the [[lost-zenith|Lost Zenith]], the [[thousand-year-dimming|Thousand-Year Dimming]] â€” each would benefit from being given dragon / unicorn / humanoid / orcish (where relevant) / Katharim / scholarly versions when written.

### The category of deliberate forgetting
The beast-folk note in Â§3.5 â€” that beast-folk have *tried to forget* the Demon War â€” introduces a category of mythic-relationship beyond *remembering accurately* and *remembering in mythic form*: the category of **deliberately forgetting**. This is worth keeping in mind for other Eadran peoples and other events. The [[velrathi|Velrathi]] exiles' relationship to the [[firethorn-empire|Firethorn Empire]] may have aspects of this; the [[gray-matriarchy|Gray Matriarchy's]] relationship to the [[great-betrayal|Great Betrayal]] almost certainly does. The mode of forgetting matters as much as the mode of remembering.

### Mythic recitation as exposition
When a player or reader needs to learn about Xar'vion, the writer's choice is between (a) a narrator's history-textbook summary, (b) a character delivering a research-style infodump, or (c) a bard reciting *The War of the Still King* at an evening hall while characters listen.
