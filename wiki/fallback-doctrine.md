# The Fallback Doctrine

**Summary**: The **Fallback Doctrine** is the [[first-rebrightening|First Rebrightening]] civilizational ethic articulated during the [[baldaranic-council-cycle#Phase II â€” The Arathi Council|Arathi Council]]: *no life-supporting system without mortal-scale fallback*. It is the formal anti-Arathi-pattern principle, derived from the diagnosis that the [[arathi|Arathi]] civilization fell catastrophically because its citizens became dependent on systems no living authority fully understood, with no mortal-scale recovery path. The doctrine does **not** reject advancement. It rejects **irreversible dependency on incomprehensible systems**.

**Sources**: arathi-council-01/02/03 (2026-05-15 ingest from Elder Gods chat 1.txt).

**Last updated**: 2026-05-15.

---

## The principle

The doctrine's compressed form:

> ***No life-supporting system without mortal-scale fallback.***

Every system on which ordinary survival depends must have **a mortal-scale recovery path** â€” a way for ordinary mortals, using ordinary capabilities, to maintain the function the system provides if the system fails. The function may degrade. The function may become slower, harder, or more expensive. But the function must remain available without the system.

This applies to:

- **[[seven-conduits|Conduits]]** â€” magical infrastructure that should not be the only source of magical service to a region
- **Food systems** â€” agriculture, granary networks, distribution, preservation
- **Healing systems** â€” medical, magical, and ritual healing capability
- **City wards** â€” magical protection over urban populations
- **Civic energy** â€” heating, lighting, water, sanitation
- **Transit** â€” magical and conventional transportation
- **Weather regulation** â€” any system that artificially controls atmospheric conditions
- **Military defence** â€” anti-incursion, anti-demonic, anti-arcane-threat capability
- **Magical communication** â€” long-distance message-carrying

The list is illustrative, not exhaustive. The doctrine applies to *every system whose absence would catastrophically harm the population that depends on it*.

## Why the doctrine

The [[arathi|Arathi]] civilization at its Stage 5 peak had reached a level of integrated magical sophistication where ordinary citizens could not survive the absence of their civic systems. Food required Ishkara Vein-Substrate distribution. Healing required Thamrite Mirror-Substrate diagnostic systems. Cities required Veyruun Bone-Substrate structural maintenance. Communication required Saelith Choir-Substrate networks.

When the [[arathi-cataclysm|Cataclysm]] broke this integration, the Arathi did not merely lose comforts. They lost the conditions for ordinary survival, almost instantly, across the world. The post-Cataclysm two-million-year silence is partly because the Arathi died too quickly to teach successors anything; the depth of integration was the depth of their vulnerability.

The Fallback Doctrine articulates the lesson: a civilization with no mortal-scale fallback is *one bad day from extinction*. The Arathi were one bad day from extinction for tens of thousands of years before the Cataclysm; the Cataclysm was simply the day.

## What the doctrine permits

The doctrine is **not anti-magic, anti-technology, anti-Conduit, or anti-magitek**. It is not a rejection of advancement. It explicitly allows:

- **Magical infrastructure that improves life** as long as fallback exists for what it provides
- **Sophisticated dependencies** as long as they are reversible at mortal scale
- **Specialised expertise** as long as the loss of the specialist does not collapse the function entirely
- **Long supply chains** as long as they are not the only supply chains
- **Magical industry** subject to the same fallback test

The doctrine's test is *not* "could this be done without magic?" The test is "if this system fails tomorrow, can mortals using ordinary capability maintain the function it provides, even if more slowly or more expensively?"

## Political and institutional impact

Different political actors receive the doctrine differently:

- **[[bold|Bold]]** resists it. Bold's [[conduit-cities|conduit-tied]] urbanism is precisely the integration the doctrine warns against. Bold's response strategies include redefining *mortal-scale fallback* dishonestly (designating wholly-magical backup systems as fallback because they are *operated by mortals*) and arguing that the doctrine's strict reading would prevent any modernisation.
- **[[orden|Orden]]** codifies it. Orden's audit theology fits the doctrine perfectly: every system gets a fallback audit, every audit is recorded, every record is reviewed.
- **Craft guilds** defend it. The doctrine validates exactly the kind of hand-skill craft training the guilds have been losing relevance from. The guilds become unexpected doctrinal allies.
- **Poor villages** preserve it as common sense. Frontier and subsistence communities never built the integrated dependencies the doctrine warns against; they receive the doctrine as recognition of their existing practice.
- **Ambitious states** define "fallback" dishonestly to evade the doctrine's force. Whether the dishonest definitions hold across institutional scrutiny is a recurring political question.

## Derived institutions

Several post-Arathi institutions and customs derive directly from the Fallback Doctrine or were retroactively re-grounded in it during the Council:

### Hand-Tool Requirement

Essential civic processes must retain non-magical or low-magical fallback methods. Bread must remain bakable without magitek ovens. Water must remain drinkable without Ishkara substrate filtration. The requirement applies to ordinary daily life, not just emergency planning.

### Open Root Laws

Cities near major wildlands must maintain unbuilt corridors for water, migration, regrowth, and ecological continuity. The principle is biological-fallback: the wildland is the system that maintains the city's ecological substrate; cutting it off entirely is a Fallback Doctrine violation against the system you didn't realise you were depending on.

### Dead Switch Doctrine

Autonomous defences or war-machines must have **multiple mortal shutdown methods**. The Arathi-era Oru-Sha War-Substrate is precisely the failure of this doctrine: the Arathi built defensive systems they could not shut down, and the systems are still defending against threats from ten million years ago. Modern military magitek must be inspectable, interruptible, and recoverable by ordinary command.

### Entropy Taboo

**Research into entropy nullification, decay abolition, or permanent stasis is forbidden** in most serious magical traditions. The taboo derives from [[kudur-etir|Kudur-eá¹­ir's]] catastrophe â€” his attempt to reverse entropy was *exactly* the Fallback-Doctrine violation taken to its cosmological extreme. The taboo applies in the magical academies, in [[order-sharanel|Order Sharanel]] research priorities, and in mainstream priestly doctrine.

### Festival of Broken Certainties

Some cities **deliberately interrupt public systems once per year** to practice resilience and humility. The Festival is a controlled drill: civic water, lighting, healing, or transit is shut down for a day. The city must function without it. The Festival is partly ceremony (acknowledging dependency), partly training (so the actual loss of the system would not produce panic), and partly civic theology (refusing the Arathi-pattern conceit that integration is invulnerable).

### Conduit Humility Clauses

No state may claim **sovereign ownership** of a [[seven-conduits|conduit]] â€” only stewardship over access and maintenance zones. The Clauses derive from the Fallback Doctrine's stricture against irreversible dependency: a state that claims to *own* a conduit has bound its sovereignty to a system it could lose. The Clauses keep conduit-state relationships flexible.
