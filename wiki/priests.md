# Priests

**Summary**: Priests are the divine practitioners of the four magical orders — clergy of the [[gods-of-eadras|Elder Gods]], the [[three-last-lights|Ascended Mortal Gods]], and (in some doctrinal traditions) other recognized divine beings. A priest does not personally hold magical capacity in the wizardly sense; instead, the priest **calls upon their god**, and the god's response is mediated by the **[[#The Divine Bureaucracy|Divine Bureaucracy]]** — the celestial administration each god maintains to manage divine favor and the granting of magical power on the deity's behalf. The system runs on **Favor**, a fluctuating state of standing with the priest's god that the Bureaucracy continuously monitors and adjusts.

**Sources**: Compiled from prior canon (the Priests section of [[magic-in-eadras]] and the [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand]]); user direction 2026-05-09 evening on Divine Bureaucracy capacity, Favor states, and restoration timing.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-09

## What a priest does

A priest serves a god. The service has two faces:

- **Pastoral and ritual** — the priest tends the temple, leads the faithful in worship, performs sacraments, marks births and deaths, advises rulers and the troubled, mediates community disputes in the god's name, and lives a life consistent with the god's tenets. This is the larger and older part of priestly life.
- **Magical (channeling)** — the priest **channels divine magic on the god's behalf**: healing the wounded, blessing the willing, warding the threatened, smiting the impenitent, divining the obscure, and the other workings their god's portfolio supports.

The two faces are inseparable. A priest who does the magical work without the pastoral work loses the standing that grants the magical work; a priest who does only pastoral work without ever channeling is still a priest, but the god's magical hand reaches them only when their pastoral standing earns it.

Per the [[true-language|Language Hierarchy]], priests do **not** themselves speak the **Divine Language** — that is the language of the gods, used by the gods themselves. A priest invokes in their own [[true-language#The Generational Degradation Model|tier-3 Arcane]] mortal tongue, and the **god** responds with the Divine Language on the priest's behalf. The priest is the petitioner; the god (through their Bureaucracy) is the actor.

This makes priestly magic structurally distinct from wizardry, Netharim practice (in its [[order-sharanel|former canonical form]]), and the new [[lexanari|Lexanari]]/[[netharim|Netharim]] orders. The wizard is the worker; the priest is the petitioner. A priest with a perfect formal record and a god who has paused their attention will find themselves quietly powerless until that attention returns.

## The Divine Bureaucracy

The gods do **not** personally answer every follower's prayer. There are too many followers, the gods have too much to do at their own scale, and the [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Doctrine of the Unheld Hand]] places strict limits on direct divine intervention in mortal affairs in any case. Instead, the gods collectively are served by a **single shared [[divine-bureaucracy|Divine Bureaucracy]]** — the vast, ancient, notoriously dysfunctional institution located entirely within [[solarion|Solarion]] (the divine city in [[celestia|Celestia]], called *the Eternal City* in Bureaucratic usage) that handles the day-to-day business of divinity.

The aspects of the Bureaucracy that priests interact with most directly:

- **Tracks every priest's [[#Favor|Favor]] continuously**, adjusting it as the priest's behavior changes — the work of the [[divine-bureaucracy#Bureau of Favor|Bureau of Favor]]
- **Grants magical capacity** to each priest each day in proportion to their current Favor and standing — also Bureau of Favor
- **Manages divine power as a shared pool** — *Godpower* — drawn from the cumulative power each god has accumulated through worship and structural standing
- **Routes the rare prayer-petitions** that genuinely require the god's personal attention up to the god, while handling the ordinary day-to-day requests at the Bureaucracy level — the work of the [[divine-bureaucracy#Bureau of Prayer|Bureau of Prayer]]
- **Approves or denies major miracle requests** — the work of the [[divine-bureaucracy#Bureau of Miracles|Bureau of Miracles]], the operational gatekeeper of the [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Doctrine of the Unheld Hand]]
- **Enforces each god's tenets** by adjusting Favor downward when a priest acts against the deity's interests, and upward when a priest serves them well

This is why two priests of the same god, channeling the same spell at the same moment, can produce different results. The Bureaucracy is granting them differently because their standing differs. It is also why a priest's day can suddenly *expand* — a Favor adjustment after a particularly good sermon, sacrifice, or successful ministry — or *contract* — a quiet Favor reduction the priest may not even feel until they reach for power and find less of it than they expected.

The Bureaucracy is not visible to the priest in any direct way. Most priests think of their Favor as their *standing with the god*, period — a relational fact, not a tracked metric. The clerical fact that there is an administration making the calls is preserved in theological literature but rarely surfaces in everyday devotional life. (See [[divine-bureaucracy]] for the full institutional treatment, including the twenty-plus Major Bureaus, the Ascended-Mortal-Gods-run-it-while-Elder-Gods-stay-hands-off hierarchy, the eon-bored celestial-spirit workforce, and the systemic dysfunction.)

## Daily channeling capacity

A priest's **daily channeling capacity** — the total magical work they can do in one day before exhausting their connection — depends on three things:

- **Their wisdom** (the depth of their understanding of their god and the path of devotion)
- **Their current Favor** (the Bureaucracy's standing-adjustment, see [[#Favor|below]])
- **Their experience** (how long and how seriously they have served — capacity grows with standing earned over a career)

Priests do **not memorize spells** the way [[wizards|wizards]] do. They may cast any spell they know — any working their god's portfolio supports and that they have learned to channel — as long as they have sufficient remaining capacity for it. The capacity is the constraint; not the contents of any working set.

When the daily capacity is exhausted, the priest must **rest and restore their connection through prayer and proper conduct** before they can channel again. The restoration is not automatic; a priest whose conduct has fallen short during the day will find their next-morning prayer producing less than expected.

(For the splat-side mechanical formula governing priestly capacity, see [[rpg/priest-mechanics]].)

## Favor

**Favor** is the priest's current standing with their god, as continuously tracked and adjusted by the [[#The Divine Bureaucracy|Divine Bureaucracy]]. It is **not a fixed score**. It fluctuates based on the priest's behavior, devotion, and service.

The four canonical states of Favor are:

### Neutral Favor (most common)

The default state of an ordinary priest in good standing. The priest is doing their work — pastoral and magical — at the level the god expects, no more and no less. They receive their full ordinary capacity. This is where the great majority of priests live their entire careers, and there is no shame in it; the gods built priestly orders precisely because the world needs many ordinary priests doing ordinary good.

### Positive Favor

Earned by priests who **go above and beyond in service, piety, or in fulfilling their god's will** — undertaking dangerous missions in the deity's name, ministering to the unministered-to, holding fast under pressure that would have justified retreat, performing the sacrament with extraordinary care, or simply doing the long quiet work of devotion better than is expected.

A priest in Positive Favor has measurably greater capacity than they would otherwise. The bonus is real and the priest will notice it; spells they would normally find at the edge of their day's reach now come within it.

Positive Favor is not permanent. It must be earned again with continued good service. A priest who slacks back to ordinary effort drifts back to Neutral, gently and without punishment.

### Negative Favor

Earned by priests who **violate their god's tenets, act against the deity's interests, or grow lax in their duties.** The Bureaucracy adjusts Favor downward in proportion to the violation: a small lapse produces a small reduction; a serious betrayal produces a serious reduction; a deeply incompatible act can produce a Favor collapse that effectively cuts the priest off from divine magic until they undertake formal restoration.

A priest in Negative Favor has measurably reduced capacity, and may find specific spells refusing to come at all. Severely-fallen priests sometimes describe the experience as the god *turning away from them* — accurate, structurally, even if the felt experience is a bureaucratic adjustment rather than a personal divine snub.

Restoration from Negative Favor is possible and is a recognized part of priestly life. Most religious orders maintain formal procedures for it: confession, penance, restitution, retreat, supervised return to service. Priests who refuse restoration after serious violations are eventually defrocked, at which point the Bureaucracy stops tracking them and the magic stops entirely.

### God's Favorite (extremely rare)

The exalted state. **Only a handful of priests ever reach it.**

A God's Favorite is a priest the deity has personally noticed and elevated. The Bureaucracy adjusts their Favor in a way that goes beyond ordinary Positive Favor — they receive expanded capacity, sometimes access to spells outside their ordinary tradition, and (rarely) experiences of direct divine attention that ordinary priests are taught not to expect.

Reaching God's Favorite is not a path one can deliberately walk. The standard texts agree on this: the priest who *aims* at God's Favorite is by definition not in line for it, because aiming at the title centers the priest rather than the god. The priests who reach the state are typically the ones who never asked for it, often did not realize they had reached it for some time, and continue serving with the same temper they had before.

The Tome of Baldaran preserves the names of historical Favorites where they are known. They are few enough that the list, across all eras of all the gods, is short.

The state is not necessarily permanent — a Favorite who falls into serious violation drops the same way any priest does, and the fall is in some traditions considered structurally worse because of how high the priest had been raised. The traditions that emphasize this point sometimes counsel against the elevation itself, preferring a stable Neutral or Positive priest to a precariously high Favorite.

## Restoration: when the priest's day begins

Divine magic restores during **dedicated prayer**. The timing of that prayer is set by the **god's temperament**, not the priest's preference, and is part of the doctrine of each priesthood:

- **Sunup** is the most common restoration time. Most gods of order, light, work, and structure restore their priests at the rising sun. Priests of [[ioa|Ioa]], [[vos|Vos]], [[raja|Raja]], [[nara|Nara]], and most of the order-aligned pantheon take their daily restoration here.
- **Sundown** is the second most common. Gods of reflection, twilight gentleness, hearth-and-home in the evening sense, and certain crafts restore at sunset. The priest finishes their day's work, prays as the sun goes down, and begins the next "channeling day" as evening turns to night — not at sunrise.
- **Noon** is rare. Reserved for the gods whose temperament aligns with the sun's height — direct, hot, fully present, decisive. [[daj|Daj]] (war, strife, the sharpener of blades) is the canonical noon-restorer; a few warrior-aligned smaller traditions follow the same pattern.
- **Midnight** is rare and significant. Reserved for gods of secrets, the underside, the thoughtful deep — [[nox|Nox]] is the canonical midnight-restorer; [[shani|Shani]] (the stern half of Death) is sometimes also so reckoned, though the formal placement varies between traditions.

The restoration moment is not flexible within a god's tradition. A priest of Vos who tries to restore at noon will simply not restore. The connection runs on the god's clock, not the priest's; the discipline of waking, sleeping, and praying around the deity's appointed hour is part of the ordinary work of being that deity's priest.

This produces visible cultural patterns. Priests of dawn-restoring gods are known for being awake before everyone else; priests of midnight gods keep night hours and are sometimes treated with mild social wariness because of it; priests of noon-restoring [[daj|Daj]] are notoriously direct and hate to be interrupted at their prayer hour. The hour the priest restores at is part of their identity, and most laypeople can guess a priest's god — or at least the temperament of their god — from when they pray.

## Distinction from the other three orders

- **[[wizards|Wizards]]** hold their power inside themselves and in their grimoires. Wizardry depends on personal mental capacity, structured formulas, and ingredients; it is the most external, the most technical, and the most teachable of the four orders. Wizards do not need a deity's good will to work.
- **[[lexanari|Lexanari]]** speak directly in the **Primordial Language** the creator deities used. They do not petition; they invoke. The cost is the catastrophic specificity rule: the mortal mind cannot hold deific complexity, and vague invocation kills.
- **[[netharim|Netharim]]** work intuitively from a personal model of how reality functions. They do not invoke and they do not petition; they reshape directly through comprehension. Their power scales with the accuracy of their internal model rather than with any external standing.

A priest is **none of these**. A priest works **only and entirely through the relationship with their god**. The relationship is what produces the power; the relationship is what bounds the power; the relationship is what restores the power. A priest who has lost the relationship is no longer a priest at all, regardless of training, capacity, or ritual knowledge.

## Druids as priests

Per the 2026-05-09 magic-system rework (see [[log]] and [[druids]]), **[[druids|Druids]] are now a specialized sub-order of priesthood** — clergy serving the three nature-and-fey-aligned Elder Gods ([[qoxli|Qoxli]], [[sylph|Sylph]], and [[nox|Nox]]) — rather than a fourth standalone magical order. Everything on this page applies to Druids as it does to other priests, with the additional expectation that Druids be self-sufficient in wilderness skills and serve as mediators between mortal societies and the [[fae|fae realms]].

**Druids are unique among divine casters in that they may serve up to three deities simultaneously** — [[qoxli|Qoxli]], [[sylph|Sylph]], and [[nox|Nox]]. The choice is explicit and made during initial training: one, two, or all three. Most druids choose one (single-patron); two-patron and tri-patron combinations are real and recognized practices, with each combination producing a distinct flavor (Qoxli + Sylph = wild-fae; Sylph + Nox = fey-and-secret; the rare Qoxli + Nox combination is typically mediation-specialist; tri-patron druids are particularly suited to mediation work). See [[druids#Status as a sub-priesthood]] for the full treatment of the choice.

Rather than maintaining separate Favor tracks for each chosen deity, druids have a single unified score called ***Druidic Favor***. **Druidic Favor represents how well the druid is maintaining the balance between the gods they serve.** It fluctuates based on their actions, decisions, and ability to honor the interests and domains of all chosen patrons without unduly favoring one over the others. When a druid's actions promote natural balance and harmony between their patrons' domains, their Druidic Favor rises. When a druid neglects one deity's domain, shows clear favoritism, or disrupts the balance between the three, their Druidic Favor falls.

This single unified Favor score reflects the fundamental nature of druidism: **the maintenance of balance between multiple divine forces.**

For a single-patron druid, this is functionally identical to an ordinary priest's Favor with their one god — there is no balance to maintain because only one set of interests exists. For two-patron and tri-patron druids, Druidic Favor is genuinely harder to keep high: every action that serves one patron must be weighed against the others, and the Bureaucracy adjusts the unified score based on whether the overall balance is being kept. This is the structural reason multi-patron druids are rarer than single-patron ones — they have *more interests to balance, on a single shared track*, and any one of those interests slipping pulls the whole Favor down.

The deeper philosophical character of Druidic Favor — including the role of high Druidic Favor in producing the practical authority that makes some druids respected and feared by other clergy, the way the gods themselves rarely interfere directly with a druid's Favor (preferring to let the [[#The Divine Bureaucracy|Bureaucracy]] manage it so druids learn balance through trial and experience), and the recognizable Druidic temperament that this produces — is at [[druids#The nature of Druidic Favor]].

The Druidic restoration time generally follows the chosen patron's standard hour: **sunrise** for [[qoxli|Qoxli]]-aligned druids, **midnight** for [[nox|Nox]]-aligned druids, and a varied tradition for [[sylph|Sylph]]-aligned druids (since Sylph is patron of foolishness and indulgence, her priests' restoration timing varies by individual tradition — some at sunrise, some at sundown, some at the moment of a personally significant celebration). **Two-patron and tri-patron druids** negotiate their restoration timing among their tracked deities; in practice most either pick one of the patrons' hours as their primary devotional time, or alternate days. The negotiation is itself part of the Druidic mediation skill, and traditions vary on whether the choice can be revisited later in life.

## Open canon

- The full **theological literature** on the Divine Bureaucracy. The structural fact is canonical; the way priests are taught about it (or not taught about it, in some traditions that prefer the simpler relational framing) is open canon for individual orders.
- Whether **God's Favorites** can be **named publicly** during their lifetime, or whether the most respectful approach is to recognize them only posthumously. The Tome's record contains the historical list; living-Favorite identification varies between traditions.
- The **maximum severity** of a Favor reduction. Whether a god can adjust a serving priest's Favor all the way to *no Favor at all* without formally defrocking them, leaving the priest cut off from magic but still administratively part of the order, is open canon — and is theologically interesting because some traditions hold that *yes* (the god is sovereign), others hold that *no* (the formal connection requires either presence or absence with no middle).
- Whether **multi-deity priests** exist. The clean Druid case (Qoxli + Sylph + Nox) is canonical; whether a priest can simultaneously serve two unrelated gods (Vos + Raja, say) and have two separate Favor tracks is an open theological question. Most traditions say no.
- The relationship between **priest Favor** and the [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Doctrine of the Unheld Hand]]. The doctrine restricts direct divine intervention; Favor is the metric by which the Bureaucracy adjusts the priest's *delegated* capacity. They are compatible but the boundary cases (when does an extraordinary Favor adjustment count as direct divine intervention?) are not fully worked out.
