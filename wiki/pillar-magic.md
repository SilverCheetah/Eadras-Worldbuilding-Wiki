# Pillar Magic

**Summary**: The shared underlying grammar of [[lexanari|Lexanari]] and [[netharim|Netharim]] magic. Every working in either tradition is constructed by combining **one Command Pillar with one Substance Pillar** — the Command names *what is being done*, the Substance names *what it is being done to*. Five of each, twenty-five canonical combinations. Practitioners do not gain spell points in the wizardly sense; they gain **mastery in individual Pillars**, and the more deeply they have mastered a Pillar, the more powerful and refined their magic becomes when that Pillar is part of the working.

**Sources**: User direction 2026-05-09 evening (in the same session that produced [[wizards]], [[priests]], and the magic-system rework).

**Last updated**: 2026-05-09

## What Pillar Magic is

Pillar Magic is **the shared core magic system** of the [[lexanari|Lexanari]] and the [[netharim|Netharim]] — the two innately-mortal magical orders that emerged in the [[log|2026-05-09 magic-system rework]]. It is not used by [[wizards|wizards]] (who work in the Arcane Language formula tradition) or [[priests|priests]] (who call upon gods), but it is the load-bearing structure beneath both Lexanari invocation and Netharim comprehension.

Every working in either tradition is **a combination of two Pillars** — one drawn from the Command set, one drawn from the Substance set. The Command Pillar names what kind of action is being taken; the Substance Pillar names what category of thing is being acted upon. Together they specify a magical effect.

The same Command + Substance combination is **the same magical effect at the underlying level**, regardless of whether a Lexanari is invoking it through the [[true-language#The Generational Degradation Model|Primordial Language]] or a Netharim is shaping it through their internal model of reality. The two orders reach the same grammar by different paths. (See [[#How the two orders use the Pillars|below]].)

## The Command Pillars

There are **five Command Pillars** — the *verbs* of magic. They name the kind of action being taken:

| Pillar | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Control** | Acting on something without fundamentally changing what it is — holding, moving, directing, restraining, commanding. The pillar of *exerting will over*. |
| **Transform** | Changing what something is — shifting it from one form, state, or condition into another. The pillar of *altering*. |
| **Create** | Bringing into existence what was not there before — manifesting, generating, calling-into-being. The pillar of *adding to reality*. |
| **Destroy** | Removing from existence — unmaking, banishing, dispelling, ending. The pillar of *subtracting from reality*. |
| **Perceive** | Knowing — sensing, observing, reading, divining, discerning. The pillar of *understanding without acting on*. |

The five together exhaust the canonical typology of magical action. A working is always one of: holding-things-as-they-are, changing-them, adding-them, removing-them, or knowing-them. There is no sixth Command Pillar; magicians who try to invent one find the working collapses into one of the existing five regardless of how it is framed.

## The Substance Pillars

There are **five Substance Pillars** — the *nouns* of magic. They name the category of thing being acted upon:

| Pillar | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Essence** | Raw magical energy itself — the substrate. What [[magic-in-eadras#Quintessence, Eldritch, Mana\|Quintessence/Eldritch/Mana]] *is*, treated as the working material. The pillar of *acting on the magical layer of reality*. |
| **Matter** | Non-living physical material — stone, water, air, metal, wood, the inanimate substrate of the world. The pillar of *acting on the physical*. |
| **Flesh** | Living biological matter — bodies, blood, organs, growth, organic life. The pillar of *acting on the biological*. |
| **Mind** | Thought, consciousness, memory, perception, will. The pillar of *acting on the mental*. |
| **Spirit** | Souls, ghosts, the spiritual layer of reality, the part of a being that persists beyond flesh and mind. The pillar of *acting on the spiritual*. |

The five together exhaust the canonical typology of magical target. A working acts upon one of: the magical substrate, the physical world, living things, thinking minds, or persistent souls. As with the Command Pillars, magicians who try to invent a sixth Substance Pillar find their working collapses into one of the existing five.

## The twenty-five combinations

Every working combines **one** Command with **one** Substance. The full canonical grid:

| | **Essence** | **Matter** | **Flesh** | **Mind** | **Spirit** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Control** | Channel raw magic; redirect mana flows | Telekinesis; shape physical objects | Body control; paralysis | Domination; mental command | Spiritual binding; possession |
| **Transform** | Convert magical energy types | Alchemy; transmutation | Healing; shapeshifting | Memory alteration; reshape personality | Soul-reshaping; spiritual conversion |
| **Create** | Generate raw magical energy | Conjure objects | Create life; grow bodies | Plant thoughts; suggest ideas | Summon spirits; create new souls |
| **Destroy** | Dispel magic; negate workings | Disintegrate; demolish | Cause damage; kill | Erase memory; cause madness | Banishing; destroying spirits |
| **Perceive** | Sense magic; read mana | Detect objects; survey materials | Sense health; medical sight | Read thoughts; sense emotion | Commune with the dead; see spirits |

The cells in this grid are not exhaustive descriptions — each combination supports a wide range of specific workings within its category — but the cells indicate the *kind* of effect each combination produces.

The grid is **complete**. Every magical effect a Lexanari or Netharim can produce fits somewhere in it. A working that does not fit any cell is, by definition, not Pillar Magic — and cannot be cast by either order. Whether wizards' or priests' workings can be mapped onto the grid by analogy is a scholarly question with limited practical importance; their access methods are different, and the analogy does not buy them anything.

## Mastery, not spell points

Practitioners of Pillar Magic do **not** gain spell points in the wizardly sense. Instead, they **increase their mastery in individual Pillars**.

A practitioner has a current mastery level in each of the ten Pillars (five Command + five Substance). Each Pillar's mastery is independent. As a practitioner studies, practices, and works with a given Pillar, their mastery in that specific Pillar deepens; their mastery in the others does not change unless they also work with those.

The higher a practitioner's mastery in a given Pillar, the **more powerful and more refined** their magic becomes when that Pillar is part of the working. A practitioner with high Control mastery and low Flesh mastery will produce body-control effects (Control + Flesh) that are strong on the *control* dimension and weak on the *flesh* dimension — the will is firm, but the precision of what the will is acting upon is rough. A practitioner with the reverse profile produces effects that are *exquisitely targeted* but *weakly held*.

### Why specialists exist

Mastery in any given Pillar **becomes harder to deepen as it deepens**. Each step beyond the early levels of mastery costs the practitioner more effort, more study, more dedicated work than the previous step did. The result is that a practitioner who tries to be uniformly strong across all ten Pillars finds themselves uniformly mediocre; the same effort, concentrated in two or three Pillars, produces extraordinary mastery in those Pillars at the cost of leaving the other seven or eight largely undeveloped.

This is not a limitation imposed from outside — it is the structural reality of how Pillar Magic deepens. Mortal cognition, mortal lifespan, and the very nature of the Pillars themselves all conspire to reward depth over breadth. A working practitioner has a real choice between:

- **Deep specialization** — extraordinarily powerful in a small handful of Pillars, significantly deficient in most others. The path of the renowned healer-Netharim, the trademark Lexanari known for one signature working-type, the elder mage whose specialty defines the order's reputation in their region.
- **Balanced generalism** — competent across many Pillars, exceptional in none. The path of the working investigator, the diplomatic mediator, the scholar-of-the-whole-grid whose strength is the breadth itself rather than any single point.

Both paths are honored. Most working practitioners settle somewhere on the middle of the spectrum — meaningfully specialized in two to four Pillars, broadly competent in the rest. **True specialists become extremely powerful in their chosen area while remaining significantly deficient in most other forms of magic**, and the orders' culture has long since accepted this as the natural shape of Pillar Magic practice. The strongest combinations of working power and breadth across the whole grid are reached only by collaboration between specialists, not by any single practitioner being everything at once.

### The cap

There is a canonical **upper limit** on a practitioner's mastery in any single Pillar. The cap is high enough that no working magician approaches it in the course of an ordinary career — it is the territory of centuries-long elder practitioners, the named legendary Lexanari and Netharim of historical record. Reaching the cap in even one Pillar is the work of a lifetime; reaching it in multiple Pillars is mythic territory; reaching it across all ten is the kind of achievement that defines a handful of named figures across all of Eadras's history.

(For the splat-side mechanical treatment of mastery progression — the cumulative point cost per mastery level, the level-12 cap, the per-character-level point gain — see [[rpg/pillar-magic-mechanics]].)

## How the two orders use the Pillars

The Pillar grammar is the same for both orders; **the access method is different**.

### [[lexanari|Lexanari]] — speaking the Pillars

A Lexanari **invokes** a Command + Substance combination by speaking the [[true-language#The Generational Degradation Model|Primordial Language]]. The Primordial Language has the Command and Substance Pillars built into its structure as fundamental concepts — speaking them with correct pronunciation, tone, and intent invokes the working.

The Lexanari's mortal-mind specificity rule (see [[lexanari#The mortal-mind specificity rule]]) operates *within* the chosen combination: the Lexanari has selected, say, *Transform + Flesh*, and the question is now how precisely they can specify which transformation, on what flesh, with what limits. Vague specification within the chosen combination produces unpredictable outcomes within that combination's scope. A Lexanari attempting Transform + Flesh with inadequate specification might heal a wound while accidentally triggering rapid hair growth, transmuting the patient's blood, or shapeshifting a finger into something bony — all valid Transform + Flesh outcomes, the language having selected from among them what the speaker did not specify.

This explains the precise nature of the danger Lexanari face. The Pillars don't help; if anything, they *delineate* the danger — a misspoken Lexanari working stays within the Pillar combination they invoked, but anywhere within that combination is fair game.

### [[netharim|Netharim]] — comprehending the Pillars

A Netharim **shapes** a Command + Substance combination through their internal model of reality. They have studied, deeply and continuously, what each Pillar *is* and how the Pillars *combine*. When a Netharim wants to produce an effect, they bring their understanding of the relevant Command and Substance to bear on the situation, and the magic flows according to the accuracy and depth of that understanding.

A Netharim with deep Transform + Flesh understanding — perhaps a senior healer-Netharim who has spent decades studying living tissue and the mechanisms by which it changes — produces healing magic of extraordinary precision and power. The same Netharim might produce *Control + Mind* magic that is competent but not extraordinary, because their study has been in a different direction.

This is why Netharim specialize. The order benefits from distributed depth: senior Netharim hold deep mastery in different Pillars, and a Netharim community covers the grid by collaboration rather than by any single practitioner needing to know everything. (See [[netharim#The lifelong study]].)

### What this changes about the previous distinction

The previous canon-level distinction *"Lexanari speak; Netharim know"* remains correct — it just operates over the **same** underlying grammar rather than two different ones. Both orders are working with the Command + Substance Pillar system. The Lexanari path makes the working faster and more direct (speaking is faster than comprehending) at the cost of catastrophic failure modes. The Netharim path makes the working slower and more flexible (comprehension scales smoothly) at the cost of being bounded by what the practitioner has actually understood.

### Why Lexanari magic kills practitioners and Netharim magic does not

The asymmetry between the two orders is real and load-bearing. Both orders draw on the same number of practitioners-worth of magical strength when they cast a working — both of them are bringing their Pillar masteries to bear on the same Command + Substance combination. What differs is the **failure mode**.

When a Netharim working fails, the world simply does not change much. The practitioner's understanding of the Pillars in play was inadequate to the task; the working fizzles, produces a weaker effect than intended, or does nothing at all. The practitioner is no worse off than before (perhaps a little tired); the cost is opportunity, not blood. Netharim magic is in this sense **fundamentally safe** — wrong answers are *no answer*.

When a Lexanari working **goes wrong**, the world changes anyway, and not gently. The Primordial Language has been spoken, the Pillar combination has been invoked, but the speaker's specificity or the speaker's pronunciation or the speaker's intent has slipped — and the language renders something. What it renders is rarely what the speaker intended. It might be subtly wrong; it might be gravely off-target; it might be lethally wrong; it might be all three at once. Lexanari face a **catastrophic-failure deck** of possible bad outcomes when their workings go wrong, and the deck contains genuinely lethal entries.

The catastrophic risk is **not uniform across all Lexanari workings**. It is gated by the **weakest Pillar in the working** — whichever of the two Pillars (Command or Substance) is at lower mastery. A Lexanari working entirely in their high-mastery Pillars is essentially safe; a Lexanari working that stretches into a Pillar where their mastery is low faces a steep risk gradient toward catastrophic backlash. The lower the weak Pillar, the higher the backlash exposure on every cast.

This produces the canonical Lexanari discipline: **work only in your specialties, never in your weak Pillars**. A Lexanari with deep mastery in Control and shallow mastery in Spirit can theoretically attempt *Control + Spirit* (banishing, soul-binding) workings — and their working might even succeed — but the working's weak link is the Spirit Pillar, and that weak link is what gates the catastrophic deck. Discipline says: don't attempt that working. Find a way to do what's needed with your strong Pillars, or accept that the working is not for you.

This is the structural mechanism behind the canonical observation that **most Lexanari candidates die in training** (see [[lexanari#Why so few survive training]]). Trainees have low mastery in *every* Pillar at first, so every working they attempt has a low weak-Pillar; every working faces high catastrophic exposure; many of those backlash outcomes are lethal. The candidates who survive are the ones who specialize fast — raising one or two Pillars to safe mastery before they touch most of the others. The Netharim equivalent — a young practitioner whose understanding has not yet reached the mastery the working required — simply produces *no result*, and the candidate goes back to study. Same wrong answer; very different consequence.

It also explains why Netharim are *common* (relative to Lexanari) and why Lexanari are *rare*. A Netharim training program is dangerous in the long run (overestimating one's understanding can produce real-world harm in some workings) but is **survivable**. A Lexanari training program filters its candidates through the early-mastery catastrophic gauntlet. The Lexanari order's rarity is not an accident; it is built into the mechanism of how Lexanari magic fails.

## Distinction from wizard and priest magic

Pillar Magic is **not** how [[wizards|wizards]] or [[priests|priests]] work.

Wizards build structured formulas in the Arcane Language that produce specific named effects (e.g., *Fireball*, *Mage Armor*, *Detect Magic*). The wizard's magic is **discrete**: each spell is its own thing, learned individually, memorized or read from a grimoire, cast as a unit. There is no Pillar-grid grammar beneath wizardry. A wizard's spell list has spells in it; it does not have Pillars. Whether any given wizard spell can be analogized to a Command + Substance combination is a scholarly curiosity (most can be roughly mapped); it does not change how the wizard casts it.

Priests call upon their gods, and the god's response is structured by **the god's portfolio and the priest's [[priests#Favor|Favor]]** — not by Pillar grammar. A priest of [[lirra|Lirra]] performs healing through Lirra's grace, not through Transform + Flesh; the resulting effect may resemble what a Netharim healer produces, but the *mechanism* is petitionary and the magic flows from the god's hand, not from the priest's mastery.

The Pillar system is therefore **specifically the substrate of Lexanari and Netharim magic**, and one of the structural reasons those two orders are conceptually paired even though their access methods are very different.

## Open canon

- **Velrathi or Arathi technical names for the Pillars.** The English-tongue names ("Control," "Matter," etc.) are working translations; whether the Velrathi scholarly tradition uses these (in translation) or older technical terms is open canon. A Velrathi name for "Pillar Magic" itself is not yet established.
- **Whether the Pillar grid is canonically a *deity-given* structure or an emergent one.** Two readings: (a) The Pillars are how reality is made (the [[primordial-duo|Primordial Duo]] built the world such that magic divides this way), in which case the grid is built into the cosmos and Lexanari + Netharim simply discovered it; or (b) the Pillars are how the *mortal mind* divides magic, an artifact of cognitive structure rather than reality, in which case different intelligent species might find different but equally valid grids. The first reading is more common in current Velrathi scholarship; the second is held by a quiet minority.
- **Whether [[dragons|dragons]] use Pillar Magic when they cast.** Dragons access magic through the [[magic-in-eadras#The Dragon Truth|Dragon Truth]] — the unified-Sourceshine-and-Eldritch view that does not distinguish raw and reflected. Whether dragon magic also fits the Command + Substance grid (or whether dragons work in a different grammar that only resembles the grid from outside) is open.
- **Whether a sixth Command or Substance Pillar can be discovered.** Current canon says no — workings that try to invent one collapse into the existing five. Whether this is a hard rule or a current limit on mortal cognition is open; the Velrathi are quietly skeptical that the grid is truly closed.
- **The interaction between Pillar Magic and the [[seven-conduits|Seven Conduits]]'s Eldritch flow.** Pillar workings touching the *Essence* substance presumably interact with the planetary magical infrastructure; whether they can disrupt conduit flow at scale, and whether the [[divine-bureaucracy#Bureau of Arcane Regulation|Bureau of Arcane Regulation]] formally monitors high-mastery Essence-pillar practitioners, is open canon.
- **Specific high-mastery effects.** The grid above describes the *category* of each combination. The full taxonomy of specific effects available at each mastery tier within each combination is a splat-design open question.
