# Varkhul

**Summary**: Varkhul is the **southwestern modern continent** of Eadras — a harsh, fractured land roughly the size of two ordinary major continents combined, dominated by the Scorched Waste, encircled offshore by the [[continental-ward|Continental Ward]], and used by the rest of the world as a permanent prison continent for the dangerous, the irredeemable, and the unwanted.

**Sources**: Eadras Cannon Expansion.txt; varkhul_society_and_native_elves_ingest.txt (2026-06-02, Game of Status, dwarf hidden-valley economy, mercenary economy, native elf tribes; dragonkin removed)

**Last updated**: 2026-06-02

## Position and scale

Varkhul is one of the **modern post-supercontinent continents** — a landmass that emerged when the [[eldravath|Eldravath]] supercontinent broke apart. It is **not** a sundered fragment of the Altean Highlands and is not directly descended (or not yet documented to descend) from any single one of the three pre-supercontinent continents ([[avalon|Avalon]], [[damedes|Damedes]], [[culethor|Culethor]]).

Its modern position is **southwestern** in the present-day continental layout. In sheer area it is **roughly the size of two ordinary major continents combined**, making it the largest contiguous landmass on Eadras outside of pre-Cataclysm survivors.

## Surface

Roughly **one quarter** of Varkhul's surface is the **Scorched Waste** — a hostile, lethal expanse that dominates the continental interior and serves as the natural defensive depth of the prison. Beyond the Waste, the continent is "harsh" and "fractured": broken terrain, shifting climates, and few habitable corridors.

## The Continental Ward

Twenty-five miles offshore, encircling Varkhul in its entirety, stands the **[[continental-ward|Continental Ward]]** — the strongest magical barrier on Eadras. It is the cumulative work of nearly every major nation and power on the planet, fed continuously by hundreds of rotating casters. The Ward is what makes the prison-continent function: nothing in the Ward's design is meant for keeping things *out*. Every aspect of it is meant for keeping things *in*.

The nations of Eadras maintain this Ward with grim, sustained determination. They really do not want anything to get out.

See [[continental-ward]] for the full structural specification.

## Inhabitants

Varkhul holds three populations of note, plus an undifferentiated mass of exiles.

### Native elves

The **[[varkhul-native-elves|native elf tribes]]** are Varkhul's only genuinely indigenous population — full communal [[elves|elven]] society, rooted to the continent's own magical convergence points, present long before Varkhul was ever used as a prison. Sealed inside the Ward along with the world's exiles, they survived by going hidden and by turning the *atavist* into an institution: freely individual scouts and wanderers who range the continent and bring knowledge home by choice. Outsiders meet only the wandering atavists and rarely suspect the hidden tribes exist at all.

### Turkorcs

**[[turkorcs|Turkorcs]]** are wholly individualistic, powerful warriors and casters, **demon-manufactured during the [[demon-war|Demon War]]** and exiled here after it. They have no concept of honor and consider honor stupid. They are amoral and extremely dangerous, and they are **strictly contained on Varkhul** — the Continental Ward exists in significant part to prevent them from reaching the wider world.

### Exiled clans

Exiled clans of [[dwarves|dwarves]], [[goblins|goblins]], [[orcs|orcs]], and other outcasts have been deposited on Varkhul over the millennia. The dwarf clans are the clearest worked example of how a settled, communal people survives a no-trust prison continent (see [[#The dwarf clans and the hidden valleys|below]]). The numbers, distribution, and current status of the other exile communities are open canon.

## Society — the Game of Status

Varkhul's internal life is organized around a single pervasive social system best named the **Game of Status** — the prize is standing, not instrumental control. Two principles govern it on different time horizons:

- **"Might makes right"** — the present-tense rule: whoever holds power in the moment sets the terms.
- **"Whoever wins was right"** — the retroactive rule: victory itself confers righteousness, applied backward, and the loser is recast as having deserved to lose. No moral truth survives defeat.

The consequences are distinctive. The logic **launders the betrayals** that put people on Varkhul in the first place — betrayal is not shameful, only a move that succeeded or failed, and the only sin is losing. The society **cannot be shamed from outside**, because "wrongness" is not a category that survives contact with the worldview. Poison, treachery, and betrayal are fully **legitimate** instruments; there are no fouls, because "cheating" requires illegitimate moves and that category has been erased. And because nothing is owed and treachery is expected, the society runs on a strange transparency: no one is owed trust, no one is surprised by betrayal, and there are few illusions left to shatter — which makes it more stable than it sounds.

The Game is not frivolous. It is the only entertainment available, **everyone plays**, and it functions as a continuous **fitness system**: it occupies time, mandates skill-gain under real stakes (lose and you may die), rehearses both direct and indirect conflict, and sorts the capable from the dead generation after generation. Varkhul is fundamentally a **no-trust society**; the only exception is the clans, and even there trust is brutally instrumental — a resource input kept only where survival mechanically requires it, never a moral residue.

The [[turkorcs|Turkorcs]] play the Game as cold individual supremacy (see [[turkorcs#The Game of Status]]).

### The dwarf clans and the hidden valleys

The exiled [[dwarves|dwarf]] clans survive the no-trust continent by **sealing into clanholds** — the dwarven answer to a world where you do not survive by trusting outsiders but by closing a door in the rock. A clanhold cannot feed a clan, though, so the dwarves solve agriculture with **hidden valleys** rather than open fields: a valley that cannot be found cannot be raided. Concealment, not exposure-plus-defense, is the strategy.

Clans always maintain **more than one** hidden valley and dig new ones as needed. A single valley is a single point of failure, and on Varkhul every secret is eventually a found secret; a rotating portfolio turns discovery from a death into a setback, which also makes finding any one valley nearly worthless. Valleys are concealed with camouflage netting and concealment magic — and here **precision matters more than strength.** A powerful veil is a *loud* veil that punches a detectable hole in the magical landscape; the highest concealment skill is making a hidden valley read exactly like ordinary, unconcealed empty terrain. The deepest clan secret is therefore not *where* the valleys are but *how* they are hidden: a betrayed location costs one valley, but a betrayed technique costs all valleys, present and future. The "little trust" the clans retain is exactly as much as farming and shared concealment mechanically require, and not a drop more.

### The mercenary and seeker economy

A market of mercenaries hires out to the dwarf clans for concealment and detection work, drawn from **dragons, [[turkorcs|Turkorcs]], and [[varkhul-native-elves|native elves]]**. Because the concealment craft is a clan's crown jewel, a clan cannot freely train a large in-house cadre without multiplying the number of people who hold the technique; a mercenary lets it rent *capability* for a single job without inducting an outsider into the permanent secret-keeping circle — trust narrowed to one transaction rather than extended for life.

Each kind of mercenary brings a different edge: **dragons** offer raw perceptual and magical class beyond what a clan can field, a luxury asset; **Turkorcs** of the precise rather than the brute sort sell seeker and concealer services, and are notable as a rare point of actual contact between Turkorc and dwarf cultures; **native elves** are the natural concealment and counter-seeker specialists. The central tension is that a mercenary who hides or finds a valley *knows where it is* — and over many contracts the best seekers accumulate a portfolio of everyone's locations, which makes them the most dangerous individuals on the continent: not the strongest, but the ones who quietly know where everyone lives. Reputation for **not** selling a former client is the only thing that keeps them employable, yet on a continent that admires the well-timed betrayal, one perfect sellout might be worth more than a lifetime of reliable contracts. The economy is always one spectacular betrayal away from a shock, held together mainly by the market's punishment of known traitors.

## The Obsidian Sanctum

An ancient [[arathi|Arathi]] [[deep-vaults|Deep Vault]], the **[[obsidian-sanctum|Obsidian Sanctum]]**, lies deep within the Scorched Waste. It is the **only sealed Deep Vault on dry land** anywhere on Eadras — every other unopened vault is underwater. No formal guardians watch it. Access is blocked by the lethal environment, the roaming Turkorcs, and the sheer difficulty of reaching the interior of a continent sealed behind the world's strongest magical prison.

See [[obsidian-sanctum]] and [[deep-vaults]].

## Open canon

- **Pre-Eldravath ancestor.** Whether Varkhul descends from a specific pre-supercontinent continent (e.g., [[damedes|Damedes]]'s post-Eldravath fragment) is undefined.
- **Climate, regions, and named geography** beyond the Scorched Waste.
- **Population sizes** of the native elf tribes, Turkorcs, and exile communities.
- **Internal political structure.** The [[#Society — the Game of Status|Game of Status]] now describes Varkhul's governing social logic, but formal governance (if any) above ad-hoc affiliation remains unspecified.
- **The connoisseurship layer** — whether any Varkhul group (e.g. the dwarf clans) cultivates betrayal admired as a fine art with a shared canon of taste, or whether all Varkhul status is cold, objective fitness-ranking.
- **What holds the mercenary economy together** beyond the market's punishment of known traitors — whether any structural check exists, or it is genuinely one perfect betrayal from collapse.
- **Settlement and exile history** — when the Ward was raised, when continuous exile shipments began, and what existed on Varkhul (including the native elf tribes' own pre-prison history) before it became the prison continent.
