# Halflings

**Summary**: Halflings are small, sociable folk who began as a surface-dwelling subrace of the [[mitans|Mitan]] and stabilized as their own people thousands of years before the [[demon-war|Demon War]]. They are defined today by **"The Change"** — a recurring restlessness that alternates them between settled life and a life of travel — which is **not an ancient biological cycle but a post-Demon-War psychological adaptation** to the trauma of starvation and displacement during that war.

**Sources**: Halflings.txt, Peoples of Eadras.txt, Naming conventions.txt, Halfling Origin & the Change 5-10-26.txt, Mitans.txt, Notes for Changes for 5-10-26.txt

**Last updated**: 2026-05-10

## Overview

Halflings are small, cheerful folk with two opposing sides to their nature — a deep love of home and hearth on one side, and a happy restlessness on the road on the other. They are easygoing, sociable, and well-liked by other races. They descend from the [[mitans|Mitan]] — specifically, from a Mitan agricultural subrace that diverged onto the surface many thousands of years before the [[demon-war|Demon War]] (see [[#Origin as a Mitan subrace]]). Their dwarven kin (see [[dwarves|Shada'din]]) descend from the same Mitan trunk; halflings and dwarves are biologically the closest of cousins, despite the surface-vs.-underground gulf that separates their cultures today.

## Anatomy

Small of stature with a friendly, rounded appearance. Their short legs give them a small stride length — when traveling with taller races, they typically ride a horse, beast, or wagon to keep up.

## Origin as a Mitan subrace

Halflings did not emerge as an independent race; they began as a **subrace within the [[mitans|Mitan]]**, formed by deliberate Mitan policy long before the Demon War. (Source: Halfling Origin & the Change 5-10-26.txt.)

The mechanism was practical, not dramatic. The underground-dwelling Mitan wanted reliable access to **fresh agricultural products** — surface-grown grains, fruits, vegetables, herbs — to supplement the deep-hold provisioning that their fully-subterranean lifestyle could not produce on its own. Mitan governance accordingly **sent a contingent of their people to the surface** to serve as farmers, with the expectation that the surface-farmers would maintain communications with the underground holds, deliver harvests on a regular cycle, and otherwise live as Mitan-on-loan to the sun.

The surface-farmers liked it.

Living above ground, with weather and growing seasons and the daily presence of the sky, suited a portion of the Mitan population in a way no one had quite anticipated. They built **hole-houses** near their fields — partial-underground homes that retained the subterranean architectural instinct while opening the upper rooms to surface light and air. They formed **permanent surface communities** that no longer needed to rotate back underground. Over generations the surface-farmers diverged from the deep-hold trunk in body and culture: smaller-statured (the deep-Mitan robustness was not needed for agriculture), rounder of build, with a stronger sociability than the disciplined Mitan warrior tradition encouraged. The Mitan as a people did not stop them; the underground holds continued to receive surface harvests, and the relationship persisted on cooperative terms for many thousands of years.

By the time the [[demon-war|Demon War]] broke out, halflings had been a recognizably distinct folk for **many thousands of years** — separate communities, separate architecture, separate aesthetic, separate craft tradition, and an entirely separate cultural orientation from their deep-hold cousins. The agricultural origin had been long absorbed into the broader halfling identity as **a people of the table, the hearth, the field, and the surface road**.

## The Change

Halfling society today is built around a recurring restlessness called **The Change**. Every halfling periodically feels a growing unrest that builds over roughly six months. When it becomes too strong, they must leave their current life, shut everything down, and switch to the other side — either heading out to travel or returning home.

The Change is famously inconvenient — it ends careers, abandons shops mid-business, separates lovers, breaks routines that the halfling in question genuinely enjoyed. And yet every halfling experiences it; every halfling structures their life around it; the entire society has organized itself to make the cycle workable rather than catastrophic.

### Origin in Demon-War trauma

The Change is **not an ancient biological cycle**. It manifested **a few years after the [[demon-war|Demon War]] ended**, and it is **a racial psychological adaptation to specific Demon-War trauma**. (Source: Halfling Origin & the Change 5-10-26.txt; canon clarification 2026-05-10.)

During the war, **halflings lost their food supply**. Surface farms were destroyed or overrun by demonic incursions; the supply chains to the underground Mitan holds collapsed; many halflings became **refugees** during the war years, fleeing across landscapes they had no underground retreat from, sleeping under skies that were suddenly the unsafe direction. The compound trauma was specific:

- **Open-sky terror.** Halflings had evolved-by-divergence from underground stock. Their deepest racial body-memory was of the secure underground; surface life had been a chosen comfort, not a biological requirement. Living as refugees under terrifyingly open skies — where demons could and did descend — produced a racial-scale agoraphobic stress reaction that no individual halfling lifetime could fully discharge.
- **Famine and the absence of safe storage.** The certainty of food and the certainty of a place to keep food were, for halflings, the foundational securities of identity. Losing both, simultaneously, across an entire racial population, while also losing the skies they had once trusted, produced a layered trauma that no prior halfling generation had any framework for processing.

A few years after the war ended — when surviving halflings had returned to surface communities, rebuilt their farms, restored a precarious version of their pre-war lives — **The Change began to manifest**. It was not present in the older generation that had personally lived through the war years; it was present in their children and their children's children, and in every halfling generation since. The mechanism is understood (by halfling theologians and by the [[divine-bureaucracy#Bureau of Souls|relevant celestial offices]]) as a **psychological coping mechanism encoded into the racial baseline** — a behavioural adaptation to ensure that **no future halfling generation will ever again find itself trapped without food or shelter**.

The constant movement, the inability to stay anywhere too long, the cyclical abandonment of accumulated property and the fresh restart elsewhere, are all expressions of one underlying answer to the trauma: *if we never settle, we cannot be unsettled; if we never store more than we can carry, we cannot be starved out of a stockpile*. The Change is what the racial body learned from the war and refused to forget.

### Modern halflings are not agoraphobic

This is an important present-day clarification: **modern halflings are quite comfortable under the open sky**. The acute open-sky terror of the war years has not persisted as a phobic response; the racial mechanism encoded the *behavioural lesson* (move, don't stockpile), not the *acute fear*. Halflings travel happily through fields and on open roads, sleep under the stars, and hold daylight festivals without any trace of the wartime body-memory their ancestors carried.

What persists is the cycle. The Change is the body's lasting remembrance of what was once needed; the open-sky comfort that surrounds modern halfling daily life is what was learned in the long peace afterward.

### How The Change manifests today

**Property**: Halfling property ownership is semi-communal. When a halfling feels The Change coming, they give their property to the community to redistribute before leaving. Shops, homes, and belongings are offered to the next arriving halfling who needs them.

**Homebound halflings**: Focus on farming and traditional village life.

**Traveling halflings**: Join caravans or crew ships. They can pack up camp faster than most races and have extensive experience living on the move. Their wagons look ordinary from the outside but contain **two full floors of living space** inside.

Pure halfling caravans are rare — they prefer mixed-race merchant trains for protection, as they are not strong warriors.

## Family

The family is the most stable unit in halfling society. Parents do not feel The Change while raising children. Once children are grown, the whole family often experiences The Change simultaneously. Halflings **nearly always give birth to twins**, which helps keep their population stable despite constant movement.

## Society

Halflings have no single homeland. They live in small villages or towns, never in large concentrations. Their settlements adapt to wherever they are granted guest rights — resembling cozy hobbit-holes in human lands or built into roots of giant trees beneath elven cities.

## Trades and Relations

Halflings are respected farmers and courteous hunters. Other races grant them guest rights because their villages provide reliable agricultural goods in exchange for manufactured items. They are known for being reasonable, easy to get along with, and **excellent cooks** — any caravan lucky enough to have a halfling eats very well.

## Naming and the cognomen system

Halfling formal address is **given name + clan name + (optional) cognomens**.

- **Given names** are practical and sturdy — *Daric, Thomen, Garrick, Miraeth, Alden, Brenna.*
- **Clan names** typically end in *-ric, -bar, -well, -den, -field, -carver* — *Deepbar, Stonewell, Rootcarver, Hillric.*

When a halfling feels The Change and takes to the road, they begin earning **cognomens** — descriptive honorifics granted by others, never self-chosen. A traveling halfling can accumulate many over a lifetime, and lists their most relevant or prestigious when introducing themselves formally on the road. Upon returning home after The Change, most halflings set their cognomens aside (though family and close friends may still use them affectionately). Cognomens are a source of quiet pride: when two traveling halflings meet, exchanging cognomens functions as a form of respect — much like warriors comparing battle scars. A halfling with many respected cognomens is treated with deference even by taller races.

**Formal address examples:**

- *Settled phase*: *Thomen of Deepbar Hillric.*
- *Traveling phase*: *Thomen Farwalker, Stormrider of Deepbar Hillric* — or simply *Thomen Farwalker Stormrider* among those who know them.

**Common ways cognomens are earned**: surviving a dangerous journey (*Farwalker, Stormrider, Riftcrosser*), skill in trade or negotiation (*Brightcoin, Fairdeal, Longbarter*), helping a community (*Rootfinder, Bridgewarden, Hearthfriend*), notable personality or quirk (*Quicklaugh, Silentstep, Twicebold*).

A well-traveled halfling might eventually carry 4–7 cognomens, though they typically use only 1–3 at a time depending on context. See [[naming-conventions#halfling-naming|Racial Naming Conventions — Halfling]].
