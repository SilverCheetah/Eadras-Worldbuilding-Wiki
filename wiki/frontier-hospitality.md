# Frontier Hospitality

**Summary**: **Frontier Hospitality** is the unwritten human customary law governing how mortal frontier settlements treat strangers who walk out of the Frontier — particularly Woodkin and Fey-adjacent visitors from [[fae-da-nar|Fae-da'Nar]] territories. The core rule, **treat the stranger as family, for you never know with whom you deal**, is older and better-obeyed than most formal laws. The hospitality customs (**First Bowl**, **No Iron on the Table**, **Fair Measure Under Witness**, **No Mocking the Mask**, **Guest-Fire Peace**) and the [[#Peace-Brass and Guest-Bronze|peace-brass infrastructure]] together form the human half of the coexistence compact whose Woodkin half is the [[four-laws-of-fae-da-nar|Four Laws of Fae-da'Nar]]. *Where Fae-da'Nar keeps the forest from becoming a nightmare, frontier hospitality keeps the village from becoming a provocation.*

## The core rule

In many mortal frontier settlements, when a stranger walks out of the Frontier to trade, sell, purchase, ask news, seek tools, or pass through, the rule is:

> ***"Treat the stranger as family, for you never know with whom you deal."***

The rule is **half kindness, half terror**. The stranger may be Woodkin, Fey-touched, [[druids|Druid]]-sent, cursed, sacred, dangerous, lost, exiled, judging, testing, or merely old and hungry. Family is fed before being interrogated. Family is also remembered — the host's behaviour at the threshold may matter later, in ways the host cannot predict.

Frontier humans understand the corollary:

> ***"The forest folk are our neighbours, and both respect and peace are earned."***

This is not naïve universal welcome. It is **practical border diplomacy**, conducted at the scale of soup, and frequently more reliable than formal treaties.

## The customs

Five customs recur across frontier traditions, with regional variation:

### The First Bowl

A stranger from the Frontier is offered **food or drink before questions** of business, name, race, or origin. Hospitality precedes interrogation. The First Bowl is offered without expectation of payment; refusing it is also possible and not insulting (some Woodkin do not eat human food for ritual or biological reasons).

The First Bowl serves multiple functions: it establishes peace at the table, it gives the host a moment to assess the stranger, it gives the stranger a moment to assess the host, and it buys time for recognition before commitment.

### No Iron on the Table

In communities aware of the iron-taboos of certain Woodkin, **iron weapons, nails, horseshoes, and iron charms are not placed between host and guest** unless danger has already been declared. Iron in the kitchen is fine. Iron in tools is fine. Iron *between host and guest* is a declared threat.

Even communities without specific iron-aware customs often instinctively follow this in the presence of a clearly-strange visitor.

### Fair Measure Under Witness

Trade with Frontier strangers is conducted **openly**, often with an elder, priest, [[druids|Druid]], or respected market-keeper watching. Cheating a Frontier stranger is dangerous — some strangers count value in ways not immediately obvious, and the consequences of a bad-faith trade can ripple for generations.

The witness's presence is the cheat-deterrent. Many frontier markets have informal **fair-witness customs** — a respected community member who attends Frontier trade and validates the exchange's good-faith conduct.

A frontier saying:

> ***"One spool of blue thread for seven years of mildly inconvenient dreams"*** *remains a poor bargain.*

### No Mocking the Mask

If the stranger **hides their face, voice, feet, shadow, horns, teeth, or other nature**, one does not expose them publicly. Many Woodkin choose to appear partially human-passing when in human settlements; their concealment is not deception in the offensive sense but a form of respect — they are minimising the discomfort their presence creates.

Mocking the Mask is more than rude. It is the kind of insult that does not heal. Hosts teach their children early: *do not point at the hooves*.

### Guest-Fire Peace

A guest welcomed to **hearth or market-fire is under peace** unless they break it first. The fire is the visible boundary. Once across it, the guest is owed protection from the household, the host is owed restraint from the guest, and violation is recognised by the wider community as a serious offence.

Guest-Fire Peace has exceptions, but exceptions require **witnesses**. A claim that the guest broke peace must be sustained against testimony.

## Frontier sayings

The customs are reinforced by recurring frontier proverbs:

> ***"Feed first. Ask second. Accuse last."***

> ***"A stranger from the trees may be only hungry. Do not make them more."***

> ***"The forest folk are our neighbours; respect and peace are earned."***

> ***"Never cheat what came by moon-road."***

> ***"A closed door teaches the woods to close theirs."***

These are taught to children before any formal explanation of why. Adults expand the reasoning when asked; children absorb the practice first.

## Taboos

Frontier communities teach specific taboos to children alongside the customs:

- Do **not demand** a true name
- Do **not count** a stranger's teeth, fingers, shadow, or reflection aloud
- Do **not touch** a hood, mask, veil, antler, tail, horn, or cloak without invitation
- Do **not offer** iron as a first gift
- Do **not follow** the stranger back into the Frontier unless invited twice and warned once
- Do **not ask** whether they are human in front of children
- Do **not laugh** at unfamiliar hunger
- Do **not bargain** for things you cannot understand how to pay

The last taboo is load-bearing. Mortals keep buying songs and then acting surprised when Thursday disappears. Bargaining for *what you do not understand how to pay* is the way frontier folktales end badly.

## Peace-Brass and Guest-Bronze

In smithing and mining frontier towns adjacent to Woodfolk territories, iron is common and necessary, but **hospitality is conducted with non-iron implements**. The reason is the [[#No Iron on the Table|No Iron rule]]: many Woodfolk feel iron as grit-under-the-soul, and the table is the threshold where the felt-presence of iron matters most.

The materials used:

- **Brass utensils**
- **Bronze serving bowls**
- **Copper or ceramic cookware**
- **Tin spoons**
- **Wooden, horn, bone, or clay dishes**
- **Bronze/brass table fittings**
- **Non-iron guest-room hardware**

Iron is **not absent** from these towns — it remains in the forge, the workshop, the plough, the nail, the hinge. The distinction is **symbolic and practical**:

> ***"Iron at the table is threat. Iron in trade is consent."***

A wrapped, priced, named, openly-traded iron knife is *consent*. A bare iron knife between guest and host is *threat*. The categories are taught early and understood by all parties.

### Canonical terms

- **Guest-Bronze** — household or inn utensils reserved for strangers from the Frontier
- **Peace-Brass** — public-facing market utensils, cups, bells, measures, hinges, and table fittings used in trade with Woodfolk or Fey-adjacent visitors
- **Civic Guest-Bronze** — town-maintained hospitality sets funded by peace levies or trade taxes
- **Green Door** — a guest entrance fitted without iron, used for Frontier visitors
- **Peace-Stall** — a market stall certified or customarily maintained without exposed iron at the counter

### Import economy

Most iron-mining frontier towns **import** brass and bronze cookware and tableware rather than producing it locally. They regard the cost as **prudent civic investment**, because peaceful Woodfolk trade brings:

- Meat from wild herds humans cannot safely hunt
- Furs from deep ranges
- Mana-rich plants
- Eldritch-fed mosses
- Living soil
- Animal tissue with alchemical or ritual properties
- Bone, horn, hide, venom, silk, fungus, resin, rare seeds
- Spirit-touched bark, boundary-bird feathers
- Glands from beasts that only Druids know how to handle safely

Some Woodfolk in turn purchase iron, steel, tools, weapons, traps, nails, finished metal goods, salt, cloth, ceramics, lamp oil, glass beads, paper, brass bells.

The trade is **mutually dependent**. A town that imports bronze bowls for strangers is saying: *this peace is worth money before profit arrives*. The Woodfolk notice. So do the Fey. Fey adore costly manners — they prove the apology has bones.

### Smithing town etiquette

In smithing and mining communities, children are taught:

- Do not set **iron knives** at the guest-place
- Do not leave **nails, tongs, horseshoes, or filings** on a hospitality table
- Do not pass food on an **iron blade**
- Do not hang **fresh-forged tools** above a guest's head
- Do not seat a Frontier stranger **facing the forge** unless they choose it
- Do not joke about **"cold iron"** unless you want every adult in the room to go silent

Mining towns may have **public wash-places** where miners clean iron dust from hands and sleeves before entering guest-houses. Some Woodfolk feel iron dust on hands as grit-under-the-soul; the wash is courtesy.

Architectural conventions follow: inns near Woodfolk routes may have **iron-banded back doors and brass-latched front guest doors**. A smithy may have two rooms: the iron-working forge and the guest room, with the guest room appointed in bronze hooks, ceramic lamps, wooden benches, copper kettle, brass cups. Markets may maintain **peace-stalls**: trading booths certified free of exposed iron at the counter.

Sayings:

> ***"Iron for the field, bronze for the guest."***

> ***"A brass spoon buys a quiet night."***

> ***"The forge may roar, but the table must not threaten."***

> ***"Keep iron in the hand of work, not the hand of welcome."***

And the children's version:

> ***"Nails off the table, or the woods eat your name."***

## Mutual benefit

Frontier settlements with strong hospitality customs may receive:

- Safer trade
- Warnings of Shunned activity
- Healing herbs
- Lost children returned
- Fey restraint
- [[druids|Druid]] mediation
- Seasonal blessings
- Strange but useful goods

Settlements that **violate hospitality** may suffer:

- Loss of trade
- Fey mischief or retaliation
- Withdrawal of warning networks
- Druid judgment
- Increased vulnerability to [[the-shunned|Shunned]] predation
- Becoming **"closed"** to the Frontier — paths grow inconvenient, dogs bark at empty corners, milk sours

The worst consequence of broken hospitality is often **absence**:

> *No warning before winter. No herb for fever. No one leading the lost child home.*

Withdrawal is more punishing than retaliation. Settlements that close themselves to the Frontier discover the Frontier closes back, and the closing is mutual until trust is rebuilt — if it can be.

## Relationship to the Four Laws

Frontier Hospitality is the **human half** of the coexistence compact whose Woodkin half is the [[four-laws-of-fae-da-nar|Four Laws of Fae-da'Nar]]:

- **Fae-da'Nar First Law** (don't frighten humans) ↔ **Hospitality** (don't frighten Woodkin)
- **Fae-da'Nar Second Law** (don't interfere) ↔ **Fair Measure** (don't manipulate)
- **Fae-da'Nar Third Law** (oppose Shunned threats) ↔ **Warning network** (humans receive Shunned warnings via Woodfolk trade)
- **Fae-da'Nar Fourth Law** (obey Druids) ↔ **Druid witness** (Druids witness hospitality breaches)

Neither half is sufficient alone. Together they form a **living frontier treaty** maintained by kitchen practice, market courtesy, and the daily refusal of both sides to provoke the other's nightmare.

## Open canon

- The **historical origin** of specific customs — when did "First Bowl" first appear in a recognisable form?
- **Regional variations** — sea-frontier hospitality customs (where the Frontier is across water rather than across forest); mountain variations; desert variations
- The **first major hospitality violation** that established precedent — case law for what constitutes breach versus mere rudeness
- Whether **non-frontier** human communities have developed analogous customs for visiting fey/Woodkin in non-Frontier contexts
- The **first city** to formalise hospitality customs in a written charter (loses some of the "unwritten law" power but gains enforceability)
- The **Black Hand's** documented attempts to exploit hospitality customs — agents posing as Frontier strangers to gain access to hospitality privileges
