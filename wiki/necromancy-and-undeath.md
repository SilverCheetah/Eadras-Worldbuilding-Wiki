## Core Philosophy

Necromancy is a **technical discipline**, not a moral one. It requires three things:

1. A dead body (or suitable vessel)
2. An animating spirit (which can be the original soul, a different intelligent spirit, or even a nature/elemental spirit)
3. The correct spell or ritual

Low-level or non-sentient undead are common and often neutral â€” used for labor, guarding, construction, or other practical purposes. Sentient undead are rarer and more complex, but still not automatically evil.

## Historical Practice

Among the [[velrathi|Velrathi]] (pre-Firethorn Empire), necromancy was a standard tool with no moral stigma. Non-sentient undead (skeletons and constructs) were commonly used for construction and maintenance. Knowledge was considered sacred, and all forms of magic â€” including necromancy â€” were studied with equal passion.

In the [[verdant-scar|Verdant Scar]], necromancy is used as a morally complex archaeological and funerary practice â€” the high concentration of trapped bodies and spirits makes it uniquely effective and uniquely treacherous.

## Necromancy is not resurrection

Necromancy and **true resurrection** are different categories. Necromancy operates on body, soul, and ritual structure within ordinary metaphysical bounds; it does **not** automatically violate Death's sovereignty. Most necromantic work is reanimation, spirit contact, or body-spirit restoration in narrow circumstances when death has not fully settled â€” none of which conflicts with the Fifth Omniversal Law.

True resurrection â€” fully restoring the dead as they were, returning a soul whose jurisdiction has passed to Death â€” is **distinct, far rarer, and far harder**. It requires the **lawful transition of jurisdiction back across the threshold**, which on Eadras requires three conditions:

1. **Consent of both death goddesses.** [[shali|Shali]] and [[shani|Shani]] together hold death's jurisdiction in this Mythos. *Either alone is insufficient.*
2. **Consent of the returning soul.** A soul at rest in [[solarion|Solarion]] or any other afterlife state may decline. Souls who have moved on to peace often do.
3. **Appropriate metaphysical structure.** A vessel adequate to receive the soul, a working that does not violate other Omniversal Laws, and conditions under which the return is metaphysically *legible* rather than torn against the world's grain.

This rule applies **specifically to Eadras**. Other Mythoi may operate differently â€” the death-jurisdiction system of any Mythos depends on which Elder-tier deities hold that domain there. (source: Worldbuilding Session Ingest â€” Eadras Cosmology, Cultures, and Metaphysics.txt)

### Xar'vion's tragedy

[[xarvion|Xar'vion's]] deepest desire â€” to truly resurrect his murdered elder sister [[velena|Velena]] â€” pressed against this rule. **True resurrection was difficult, not impossible.** What he attempted was a **unilateral override** of the lawful death structure â€” to force the return without seeking the goddesses' consent and without confirming Velena's own willingness. The [[spear-of-crystallized-entropy]] did not grant his desire because the Spear is not a jurisdictional authority; it simply removed the desire that had driven him there.

A subtler tragedy follows from this rule: had Xar'vion ascended and *then* approached Shali and Shani with the question, Velena might have answered for herself. The current canon is that **Velena is at peace in Solarion and does not (yet) wish to return** â€” but that answer was never asked. (See [[velena]].)

## Stigma

Necromancy's neutral reputation was damaged by [[xarvion|Xar'vion]], who weaponized grief-magic and soul-theory â€” techniques originally developed to honor and potentially restore the dead â€” as tools of domination:

- Soul-chains instead of restoration
- Memory-breaking instead of remembrance
- Corpse-legions instead of mourning rites

Xar'vion is a primary reason certain cultures distrust necromancy even today, despite its technical neutrality.
