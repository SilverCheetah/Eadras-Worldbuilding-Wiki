# Creation Myth

**Summary**: The narrative account of how the [[omniversal-creator]] imposed the bounded structure of the [[mythos|Eadras Mythos]] within the surrounding [[chaos|Chaos]], called forth [[primordial-duo|Evye and Fryr]], and how the two creators birthed the ten [[gods-of-eadras|Elder Gods]] before sacrificing themselves into the world.

**Sources**: Creation Myth.txt, The Primordial Duo.txt, Gods of Eadras.txt

**Last updated**: 2026-04-29

---

## The Cleaving of Chaos

In the beginning there was only [[chaos|Chaos]] — a seething sea of possibility without structure or meaning. The [[omniversal-creator|Great Creator]], in his boundless wisdom and mercy, forged a bubble of rational void, cleaving Chaos from the not-chaos. This place he named **Eadras**, a [[mythos|Mythos]] (source: Creation Myth.txt).

The Mythos was at first boundless emptiness — devoid of light, love, laughter, and all that makes life worth living. So the Great Creator shaped his two children: **Evye**, the feminine, and **Fryr**, the masculine, to birth everything else (source: Creation Myth.txt).

## The Conversation Between Evye and Fryr

Evye looked across the endless black and spoke first, her voice "like dawn breaking" — calling for the physical: solid ground, seas, skies. Fryr answered with the unseen: realms of spirit, quiet places where minds could wander, where wonder could grow and magic could spark. Without the spirit, he said, the land was just dirt; without the land, the spirit had nowhere to dream itself alive (source: Creation Myth.txt).

Together they decided to weave the two together — *earth underfoot, spirit overhead, like lungs and heart.* But they recognized the cost: pouring everything they were into the work would burn them out. They needed help. Not copies — children. Strong enough to guide what they began (source: Creation Myth.txt).

### The Question of Fear

Before the great act, Fryr asked Evye if she was afraid. She did not answer right away. Then:

> *"Afraid? Of fading? Yes. Of being nothing? Yes. But more... of leaving them without a hand to hold. Still — yes. Let's do it."*

Fryr's reply: *"Then no half-measures. We empty. All of it."* (source: Creation Myth.txt).

This exchange is canonized in Lirran liturgy as the first sacred admission of fear by the divine.

## The Birth of the Ten

Joining together, Evye and Fryr birthed ten [[gods-of-eadras|Elder Gods]] into the void. They emerged "not summoned, not forged — like the first breath after silence, rising from the dark's own quiet heart." Forms took shape: vast, luminous, edges still trembling with what their parents had poured (source: Creation Myth.txt).

The tenth split in the moment of coming into being: one half gentle, offering rest to the dead; the other stern, demanding endless reckoning. **Death, born torn.**

The ten claimed their names in turn:

| Order | Declaration |
|---|---|
| 1 | *"I am Ioa — god of rulership."* |
| 2 | *"I am Nara — goddess of femininity."* |
| 3 | *"I am Raja — god of masculinity."* |
| 4 | *"I am Vos — god of civilization."* |
| 5 | *"I am Qoxli — goddess of the wild."* |
| 6 | *"I am Daj — god of war."* |
| 7 | *"I am Nox — god of night."* |
| 8 | *"I am Sylph — goddess of foolishness."* (grinning) |
| 9 | *"I am Lirra — goddess of magic."* |
| 10a | *"I am Shali — goddess of death."* (chin high) |
| 10b | *"No, you're not. I'm Shani — goddess of death."* |

### The First Argument

The twin death goddesses began bickering immediately:

> Shali: *"See? I'm Shali, goddess of death, and she's a mistake."*
> Shani: *"No, I'm not!"*
> Shali: *"Yes, you are!"*
> Shani: *"No, I'm not—"*

Until Ioa cut through, low and final: *"Be quiet, both of you."* The twins froze, glaring sideways. The void went still (source: Creation Myth.txt).

This first scene establishes Ioa's authority over the pantheon and the eternal rivalry of [[shali|Shali]] and [[shani|Shani]] — a rivalry [[ioa|Ioa]] has been managing ever since.

## The Great Sacrifice

After Evye approved — *"You know yourselves. Good."* — Fryr called the Elder Gods to the work. Each of them was to guide the power their parents pushed out. *"Make it well. Make it real."*

Then Evye and Fryr turned to the void and emptied themselves. The text gives no answer to how long it took: *"time wasn't invented until the end of the creation of the mythos... it took either the blink of an eye or an eon."* (source: Creation Myth.txt).

They became the world. No trace, no echo. The ground, the wind, the quiet between stars are Evye and Fryr.

## What the Ten Did With What Was Poured

The Elder Gods caught and shaped what poured out:

- [[ioa|Ioa]] set the laws.
- [[nara|Nara]] warmed the homes.
- [[raja|Raja]] forged the tools.
- [[qoxli|Qoxli]] ran the forests.
- [[daj|Daj]] sharpened the blades.
- [[nox|Nox]] hid the secrets.
- [[sylph|Sylph]] laughed at the rules.
- [[lirra|Lirra]] wove the magic.
- [[shali|Shali]] and [[shani|Shani]] — still bickering — split the first soul, one to light, one to dark.

(source: Creation Myth.txt)

The result was not perfect, not finished — *just alive.* "A place where things grow, break, love, fight, die, and start again. This is how the mythos came to be."

## Theological Significance

The Creation Myth is the foundational text of nearly every Eadras religion that acknowledges the [[primordial-duo|Primordial Duo]]. Several elements carry doctrinal weight:

- **Total sacrifice**: Evye and Fryr did not rule what they made — they *became* it. This is the canonical justification for the [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Doctrine of the Unheld Hand]] (the gods do not rule mortals; the original creators chose not to).
- **Magical density**: Because the Duo poured *everything* in, Eadras has orders of magnitude more magical energy than other Mythoi. (See [[primordial-duo]].)
- **The fear admission**: The dialogue in which Evye admits fear is the first divine admission of weakness in the cosmology — heavily quoted in [[order-sharanel|Lirran]] mourning rites.
- **Death's torn nature**: The split of the tenth Elder God explains why mortuary tradition in Eadras includes both kind and cruel rites — neither is sufficient alone.
