# The Three Bells of Intervention

**Summary**: An ancient escalation precedent from the [[battle-of-the-broken-conclaves|Broken Conclaves]] period, originally military-religious, formalised during the [[baldaranic-council-cycle|Baldaranic Council Cycle]] as standard doctrine under the [[doctrine-of-open-palm|Open Palm]] reform. The Three Bells specify when divine intervention becomes **permitted** (not guaranteed): mortals first identify and act (First Bell); mortal institutions fail despite good-faith effort (Second Bell); the crisis threatens continuity beyond the immediate victims (Third Bell). At the Third Bell, divine intervention may occur. The doctrine's wound is that *permitted does not mean guaranteed* — the gods cannot promise rescue every time a threshold is crossed.

**Sources**: open-palm-01/02 (2026-05-15 ingest from Elder Gods chat 1.txt).

**Last updated**: 2026-05-15.

## Origin

The Bells originated as a military-religious precedent in the late [[demon-war|Demon War]] — specifically formalised in the aftermath of the [[battle-of-the-broken-conclaves|Broken Conclaves]], when the [[orugi|Orug]], [[el-mer|El'mer]], and [[aetherin|Aetherin]] held the Final Wall without expected Tarran reinforcement and were destroyed in three days. The Bells emerged from the question that conflict left unresolved: *at what point should divine intervention have replaced the missing mortal aid?*

The original framing was tactical: when does an ally's failure to arrive trigger a different ally's obligation? The doctrine generalised over the millennia that followed, and the [[baldaranic-council-cycle|Baldaranic Council Cycle]] formalised it as standard divine-intervention escalation during the [[first-rebrightening|First Rebrightening]].

## The three thresholds

### First Bell — Mortals identify the crisis and act

The First Bell is rung when **mortals recognise a crisis and respond to it**. The threshold is low: any organised mortal action against the crisis suffices. The Bell is rung internally by the divine bureaucratic offices receiving prayer traffic and miracle requests from the affected region; mortals do not directly ring the Bell, and most never know it is ringing on their behalf.

At the First Bell, divine intervention is **not appropriate**. Mortal agency must have its space. This is the [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Unheld Hand]] in its strict form: the gods step back so mortals can step up.

The First Bell rings most often. Most crises stop here. The Bell records and waits.

### Second Bell — Mortal institutions fail despite good-faith effort

The Second Bell rings when **mortal action has been earnest but insufficient**. The threshold is harder to assess: it requires distinguishing failure-despite-effort from failure-because-of-insufficient-effort. The [[divine-bureaucracy|Divine Bureaucracy]]'s assessment offices spend considerable resources on this judgment, because the doctrine's integrity depends on the distinction.

At the Second Bell, divine intervention remains **inappropriate** under the doctrine's strict reading — but the bureaucracy begins **preparing**. Inventory of available interventions is taken; relevant Ascended Mortal Gods are notified; the [[katharim|Katharim]] are alerted to the possibility that the situation may become Third-Bell-class.

Many Second Bells de-escalate as mortal institutions recover, reorganise, or recruit allies. The Bell rings; the preparation begins; the preparation is most often unused.

### Third Bell — Threatens continuity beyond immediate victims

The Third Bell rings when **the crisis threatens continuity beyond the immediate victims** — Weave stability, soul-cycle continuity, reality stability, species survival, or irreversible civilizational collapse. The threshold is cosmologically significant: the categories listed are not exhaustive, but the doctrine treats *threat to a category of being or reality, not just to individuals* as the test.

At the Third Bell, **divine intervention becomes permitted**.

*Permitted does not mean guaranteed.* This is the doctrine's wound, and the [[baldaranic-council-cycle|Open Palm Council]] was explicit about it. The gods cannot promise rescue every time a threshold is crossed, because such a promise would:

- Encourage mortal recklessness (manufactured crises to summon divine aid)
- Encourage dependency (mortal institutions building around the assumption of rescue rather than around their own resilience)
- Encourage manufactured threshold-crossing by adversaries (the [[black-hand|Black Hand]] would engineer Third-Bell-class crises whenever it wanted divine attention drawn elsewhere)

So the Third Bell *opens the possibility* of intervention; the gods retain discretion about whether to act, how, when, and at what cost.

## Permitted does not mean guaranteed

The phrasing is doctrinally exact. *Permitted* means *the [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Unheld Hand]] no longer forbids*. *Guaranteed* would mean *rescue is contractually owed to mortals*. The latter is what the doctrine refuses.

The refusal has hard consequences. Some Third-Bell crises are not intervened against, and the resulting losses are real. Families of the unintervened-for are exactly the population the [[petition-of-abandoned-circumstance|Petition of Abandoned Circumstance]] gives a voice to: *the Bell rang; no help came; the dead arrive before [[shali|Shali]] and [[shani|Shani]] asking why*.

There is no satisfying answer to that question when the truth is *we could have, and we did not*. The doctrine is honest about this. The Open Palm reform's anti-secrecy push permits the absence to be named even when it cannot be justified.

## Application in priestly practice

The Three Bells may evolve from divine procedural framework into mortal-side priestly doctrine — and the [[first-rebrightening|First Rebrightening]] is the period in which that transition is happening. Anticipated mortal applications:

- **Priestly discernment doctrine**: clergy learning to recognise which Bell the crisis they face has rung. A priest who incorrectly perceives a First-Bell crisis as Third-Bell-class produces *miracle inflation* — desperate prayers escalating cases that should have been handled mortally. A priest who incorrectly perceives a Third-Bell crisis as First-Bell-class produces *bureaucratic cowardice* — institutional refusal to escalate cases that genuinely required it.
- **Emergency escalation protocol**: temple-internal procedures for moving prayer traffic up the divine routing tree. Open Palm reformist temples are already developing these.
- **Miracle triage standard**: when multiple Bell-rung crises occur simultaneously, the doctrine guides which receives priority.

## The Crusade-class exception

The [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Doctrine of the Unheld Hand]] has historically recognised an exception for *Crusade-class threats* — cosmologically severe situations that warrant direct divine action regardless of where the Bells stand. The [[chaos-crusades|Chaos Crusades]], the [[anti-magic-crusade|Anti-Magic Crusade]] avatar intervention, and the [[arathi-cataclysm|Cataclysm-era]] [[worldender|Worldender]] dispatch all operated under this exception.

The Three Bells doctrine **integrates the Crusade-class exception** rather than replacing it: Crusade-class threats are *automatically* Third-Bell-class, and the doctrine's Third-Bell language preserves the existing exception's operational latitude.

## Open canon

- Whether mortals can **ring the Bells themselves** through some ritual or institutional procedure, or whether Bell-ringing remains entirely a divine-bureaucratic internal event.
- How the [[hearth-witness-mandate|Hearth-Witness Mandate]] interacts with Bell-ringing — do Hearth-Witnesses certify Bell assessments?
- Whether **specific Bells become culturally famous** — e.g., "the Third Bell of the Sixth Plague" as historical reference.
- Whether [[bold|Bold's]] proposed automated petition-routing engines can read the Bells, and what that implies for divine sovereignty.
- The first **mortal weaponisation of the Three Bells** — the [[black-hand|Black Hand]] is likely to attempt manipulation around the threshold-crossing logic.
