# Closed-Time-Bubble Chaos Crusade

> **Working-title note (2026-05-09):** The title *Closed-Time-Bubble Chaos Crusade* is a **placeholder** that will be changed before publication. The user has flagged that it spoils the climactic twist in its own marketing â€” a campaign whose central reveal is *"the timeline you were on was an unactualized branch"* should not give that away in the title that appears on the splatbook spine. **Summary**: Flagship Crystalline Age splatbook campaign concept. Players take elite Arathi troubleshooter characters at the height of post-scarcity [[arathi|Arathi]] civilization, several centuries before the [[arathi-cataclysm|Cataclysm]]. A [[chaos|Chaos]] incursion begins for which the empire's cosmology has no precedent and against which its arsenal cannot defend. The campaign progresses from "minor anomalies" to existential crisis to heroic loss. At the climax, **the actualized timeline of Eadras diverges away from the path the players have been on** â€” the events of the campaign turn out to have been an unactualized inflection-point potential the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome]] recorded but the world's actual history does not walk. The player characters alone remember, having been deliberately preserved as the universe's designated observers of the unactualized branch. The Tome records the branch (because the players observed it) but seals the chapter as a non-actualized potential and refuses to open it for anyone in this age.

This is **splatbook material, not canonical wiki history**. The Crystalline Age era does not contain a remembered Chaos Crusade. The Tome contains the sealed chapter for the unactualized branch; the world does not.

## The campaign arc

### Setup â€” the height of the Crystalline Age

Players take **elite Arathi troubleshooter characters** during the height of [[arathi|Arathi]] civilization, several centuries before [[kudur-etir|Kudur-eá¹­ir]] is born. The empire is at its absolute peak: living crystal cities sing, [[magitek]] runs flawlessly, the [[seven-conduits|conduits]] are perfectly tuned, the empire's understanding of its own magical infrastructure is total and complete. The world is geologically fixed (no plate tectonics yet â€” though earthquakes and the subterranean hydrological cycle are active background, see [[worldbreaker-mechanism]]). The conduits sit exactly where the Arathi expect them to.

The players' characters are part of an elite team that handles **anomalies, edge cases, and irregularities** that ordinary imperial systems miss. They are powerful, well-equipped with the best magitek and crystalmancy the empire produces, and woven into the highest tiers of imperial society. They have access to [[concordance|the Concordance]], [[heart-of-the-goddess|Lirra's Heart]], [[nexus|the Nexus]], and the [[limina|Limina]]-level infrastructure that lets a party simply step out of the Prime Material and walk into adjacent planes.

The empire is at its brightest. The audience knows it is doomed. The *characters* do not know â€” Kudur-eá¹­ir is a child somewhere else in the empire, the [[worldender|Worldbreaker]] has not been pulled through, the Cataclysm is an unimaginable abstraction. The **dramatic irony is the engine of the entire campaign.**

### Act 1 â€” minor anomalies

The campaign opens with **small, localized glitches**. Things that should not be possible under Arathi magical law:

- A crystal spire that starts growing wrong
- A [[seven-conduits|conduit]] that briefly outputs raw [[eldritch|Sourceshine]] instead of regulated Eldritch
- A [[magitek|magitek]] train that arrives before it departed
- A single street where time runs slightly backwards for six seconds
- A statue whose carved expression changes overnight, with no record of who altered it
- A scholar's notes that are fully written in a language the scholar does not know

The Arathi response is calm and arrogant: *this is clearly a malfunction in our systems; we will study and correct it.* The player characters' early job is investigation and containment.

The audience knows by Act 1 that this is not a malfunction.

### Act 2 â€” accelerating

The incursions get **bigger, more frequent, and more violent**. Entire districts where physics stops behaving. Living geometry that shouldn't exist. Things that look at the players with **too many eyes**.

Empire-level resources are mobilized. The greatest Arathi crystalmancers in history are brought in to study the phenomena. They cannot stop them. The empire's institutional response is competent, staggering in its scale, and inadequate to what is actually happening.

The player characters work harder, accomplish more, and watch more parts of the empire fall to the spreading anomalies. By the end of Act 2, the **Chaos** nature of the incursion is recognized â€” and the empire discovers that it has no concept of how to defend against it. There is no [[magic-in-eadras|Weave]] yet. There are no [[order-sharanel|Netharim]]. There are no [[netharim|Netharim]] or [[lexanari|Lexanari]] orders. There is **only crystalmancy and magitek** â€” both of which assume a stable substrate of named reality, and Chaos is the absence of names. The empire is fighting an infection of its own metaphysical premise.

### Act 3 â€” the war

By Act 3 the incursion is a **war**. The greatest empire in history is fighting on its home ground for its own existence, and the player characters are at the center of every major engagement.

The campaign's set-pieces unfold here. Players burn through resources. Beloved NPCs die. Cities fall. The empire's institutional confidence collapses; the Arathi's deepest assumption â€” *we understand reality* â€” fractures publicly.

The player characters' final acts are **heroic**. They fight harder than they have ever fought, they accomplish things that should be impossible, they save what can be saved, and they keep fighting after everything is lost. This is the part of the campaign players will remember as the high water mark of their characters' lives.

And they lose.

The empire is going to fall to Chaos. The player characters cannot stop it. Their final stand buys time, takes a respectable bite out of the largest Chaos manifestations they can reach, and ends in their imminent obliteration.

### The climax â€” the timeline shifts away

At the moment the player characters are about to die â€” the empire crumbling around them, Chaos pressing into the heart of [[altea|Altea]], everything they tried to save burning â€” the actualized timeline of Eadras **does not, in fact, follow this branch**.

The events of the campaign have been an **inflection-point potential**: a path the world could have taken, that the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome of Baldaran]] recorded as a possibility (see [[tome-of-baldaran#Potential events at inflection points|the Tome's potential-events recording mechanism]]), but that the actualized history of Eadras does not walk. The campaign was always a *might-have-been*. At the climax, the world's actual timeline diverges from the path the players have been on; the inflection resolves the other way; the path the players were walking falls away and is sealed.

From the perspective of canonical Eadras history, the empire was never threatened. The dead never died. The cities were never sacked. There is nothing to be unmade because nothing actualized in the first place â€” only one branch did, and that branch was the one in which the Crystalline Age proceeded toward its [[arathi-cataclysm|known eventual fate]].

The **player characters retain their memories** because they were the [[observer-and-naming|designated observers]] of the unactualized branch. The Tome's record of the branch is sealed; the players' memories are not, but they have nothing in the canonical world to corroborate against.

### Coda â€” the survivors

The players wake at the moment of the inflection point, before the first small anomaly appeared, with all their memories, all their scars, all their knowledge of what was coming on the path that didn't actualize, and **no one else who remembers anything**.

Their characters become â€” for the rest of their lives â€” the **only living witnesses** of a path the world might have walked but didn't. The empire goes on with its golden age intact. Kudur-eá¹­ir grows up. The Cataclysm waits in its century. The player characters age out of the events of the campaign as figures whose later lives, in canonical Crystalline Age history, look subtly off-key â€” they founded orders no one understands the purpose of, they wrote books in coded language, they took stances in court politics that don't quite track with their stated reasoning.

In the wider canon, none of this is recorded as a war that *occurred*. The [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome of Baldaran]] records the unactualized branch (because the players observed it, and the Tome's [[tome-of-baldaran#Potential events at inflection points|inflection-point recording mechanism]] preserves potentials of that scale), but **the chapter is sealed and never opens in this age**. The Tome's stance is essentially: *this is in the records, but this is not history.*

## Why this works in canon

### It does not break Time Is Absolute

The campaign **does not violate** the [[omniversal-laws|Time Is Absolute]] [[omniversal-laws|Omniversal Law]] â€” and unlike earlier rougher framings of this concept, **no fine-print loophole is required**. The law is preserved cleanly.

Time was always absolute, the actualized timeline always proceeded forward without interruption, and cause-and-effect were never violated. What happened during the campaign is simply that an inflection point in the early Crystalline Age had multiple possible resolutions; the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome]] recorded the possibilities; only one resolution actualized; the others (including the one the players experienced) became sealed unactualized records. **Nothing was undone, surgically excised, rewound, or stitched back together** â€” those framings were earlier, dramatically-overworked attempts to describe a phenomenon that turns out to have a simpler explanation. Branching potentials at inflection points are entirely compatible with absolute time; only one branch is real, and the others are real only as records of what could have been.

The PCs' memories survive because they were **deliberately positioned as designated observers** of the unactualized branch â€” the same kind of cosmic-observer status that the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome]] itself holds. Their memories are real; the events those memories correspond to are real-as-potentials but not real-as-actualized-history.

### It uses the [[observer-and-naming|observer-requires-reality]] principle

The PCs' memories are not a side effect; they are **structurally necessary**. Eadras runs slightly **observer-hungry** because the [[primordial-duo|Primordial Duo]] sacrificed themselves into the Mythos and left no ultimate observer behind ([[observer-and-naming]]). For a potential timeline of this scale to be entirely uncwitnessed â€” no observer, no remembered consequence â€” would risk it not even counting as a *coherent potential* the Tome could record. The Tome and the cosmic order cooperate to keep the players in observer position so that the unactualized branch is preserved at all.

The event remembered by them satisfies the observer-and-naming requirement on the unactualized branch; the actualized timeline simply went a different way. Both halves are needed: the actualized timeline proceeds (Time Is Absolute is preserved), and the unactualized potential is preserved on the Tome's pages because the players witnessed it.

### It uses the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome's curator role]]

The [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome of Baldaran]] is not a passive archive. It records reality after the fact and through observer-witnessing â€” including potential events at inflection points â€” and **chooses what to reveal and what to keep sealed**. The Tome's seal of the unactualized branch is consistent with its general curatorial mandate: *this is in the records, but this is not history.* The book that should know everything will, in this case, voluntarily refuse to open the relevant page for anyone in this age.

This sets up the **deepest gut-punch** of the campaign. The players spend the entire campaign fighting like legends on a path that, it turns out, the world never walked. They lose on a branch that wasn't even the actualized branch. They come out of it as the only people in history who carry memories of a Crystalline Age outcome that did not happen â€” and the Tome, the book that records everything, holds the sealed record of the same branch and refuses to open it. The myth of Arathi perfection that every later civilization worships is, from the players' perspective, accurate to the actualized timeline; it is just that they alone know what the world *could have been*, on a branch that the universe did not walk.

## What the campaign requires from the splat

This is a **flagship adventure**, not a one-shot. To run cleanly it needs:

- The **Crystalline Age splat's** full setting material: Arathi society, [[crystalmancy|crystalmancy]] mechanics, [[magitek|magitek]] mechanics, imperial structure, the [[seven-conduits|conduit]] geography of the pre-Cataclysm world, the High Court of Altea, the great Houses, the magitek transit network, and the planar-access patterns that high-level Arathi parties exploit.
- A **Chaos manifestation toolkit** that does not draw from the modern era's Chaos vocabulary. The PCs are encountering Chaos centuries before [[kudur-etir|Kudur-eá¹­ir]] drills the [[chaos-crusades|First Breach]]; the modern terms ([[neverborn|Neverborn]], [[aberrations|Aberrations]]) do not yet exist as concepts. Chaos in the Crystalline Age looks like *reality breaking* without conceptual precedent.
- A **PC mortality framework** that supports late-campaign character death without ending the campaign. The default tabletop pattern of "if you die, you make a new character" needs adjustment, because the campaign's coda specifically requires the players to *survive into the post-bubble timeline* to deliver the gut-punch ending.
- A **GM playbook for the inflection-point climax** itself. Telegraphing the divergence too early ruins the surprise; revealing it too late ruins the payoff. The pacing of Act 3's heroic-loss arc and the timing of the timeline's divergence away from the players' branch are the two highest-skill GM tasks in the campaign.
- **A real title.** The current working title is a placeholder that openly spoils the climactic twist (see [[#Closed-Time-Bubble Chaos Crusade|the working-title note at the top of this page]]). A final title needs to evoke the campaign's themes â€” doomed Crystalline-Age splendor, designated cosmic observers, the gap between actualized and unactualized history â€” without giving away that the events of the campaign are themselves an unactualized branch. This is genuinely difficult marketing work and is left to the eventual splat designer.

These are the responsibilities of the eventual splatbook, not the wiki. The wiki's job is to keep the campaign concept coherent with the broader setting; the splat's job is to make it runnable.

## What players take away

If this campaign runs well, what players should walk away with:

- **The high-water mark of their characters' lives**, fought legendarily, on a stage they will not match in any later campaign.
- **A truth nobody else in the setting knows.** They are now, in canon, the only people in any era who hold this piece of metaphysical history. Any later campaign by the same group can use this knowledge as background.
- **A real understanding of why Eadras runs observer-hungry**, learned not as a metaphysical lecture but as the structural fact that saved their characters from being forgotten.
- **A complicated relationship with the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome of Baldaran]].** The book that records everything chose, in their case, to refuse to record the most important thing they ever did. They will think about this for years.

That is the design payoff. The campaign concept exists to deliver it.

## Open canon (splat-side)

- The exact **mechanism** by which Chaos breaches the Crystalline Age Mythos in the absence of the [[chaos-crusades|First Breach]] aperture. The conversational suggestion is that *Chaos doesn't experience time linearly* â€” the same point Kudur-eá¹­ir will eventually drill is "thin" across all the time it will ever be drilled, including before. Whether this is the load-bearing mechanism for the splat or whether some entirely different mechanism is preferred is a splat-design call.
- The **identity** (if any) of the closed-time-bubble's *agent*. The current framing is that "the universe" does it; whether the splat names a specific agent (the [[voroik|Voroik]]? the Tome itself? a higher Concordance authority?) or leaves the mechanism abstract is a splat-design call.
- Whether **other** closed-time-bubble events are implied to exist in Eadras's history â€” events the Tome has similarly sealed and the player characters of this campaign are simply the ones who broke through the seal. The hint is *yes, there are others, and the players don't know about them*; whether the splat foregrounds this hint or leaves it implicit is a splat-design call.
