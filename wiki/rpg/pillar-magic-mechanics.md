# Pillar Magic Mechanics

**Summary**: RPG-side mechanical treatment of the [[pillar-magic|Pillar Magic]] system shared by the [[lexanari|Lexanari]] and the [[netharim|Netharim]]. Per-Pillar mastery progression in place of spell points; mastery points awarded each character level and freely distributable across the ten Pillars. **Splat-book material**, not in-universe canon â€” the in-universe lore lives at [[pillar-magic]].

**Sources**: User direction 2026-05-09 evening (in the same session that produced [[rpg/wizard-mechanics]] and [[rpg/priest-mechanics]]).

## What this page is

The full in-universe canon of Pillar Magic â€” the five Command Pillars (Control / Transform / Create / Destroy / Perceive), the five Substance Pillars (Essence / Matter / Flesh / Mind / Spirit), the twenty-five canonical combinations, and how the [[lexanari|Lexanari]] and [[netharim|Netharim]] each access them â€” is at [[pillar-magic]].

This page is the splat-side treatment: how a [[lexanari|Lexanari]] or [[netharim|Netharim]] character actually levels up, distributes points, and casts at the table.

## Mastery, not spell slots

Lexanari and Netharim characters do not gain spell points or spell slots in the wizardly sense. They gain **mastery in individual Pillars**.

Each character has a current **mastery level in each of the ten Pillars** (5 Command + 5 Substance). Each Pillar's mastery is independent. Casting a working uses the masteries in **the two Pillars that compose the working** (one Command + one Substance) â€” the working's power, precision, and reliability all scale with those two specific masteries.

A character with high mastery in *Control* and low mastery in *Mind* will cast Control + Mind workings (domination, mental command) with a strong-but-imprecise feel: the will is firm, but the targeting and finesse on the mental side is rough. The reverse profile produces working that is *exquisitely targeted* but *weakly held*. The most powerful casters in either order are those who have built up high mastery in multiple Pillars on both sides; an elder Lexanari or Netharim with high mastery in many Pillars is correspondingly extraordinary.

## Mastery point gain per level

When a Netharim or Lexanari **gains a character level**, they receive a number of **mastery points** equal to **the total spell levels a [[wizards|Wizard]] gains at that character level**. These points can be **freely distributed** among any of their Pillars to increase their mastery in those specific Pillars.

**At character level 1, a Pillar Magic user receives 6 mastery points.** This is the starting allocation â€” the level-1 application of the per-level rule (i.e., a normal Wizard, in this system's spell-progression chart, gains 6 total spell levels at character level 1). At each subsequent character level, the Lexanari or Netharim receives whatever total-spell-levels number the Wizard's progression chart specifies for that level â€” typically larger numbers at higher character levels, as the Wizard's slot-progression unlocks new spell tiers.

The conversion is parallel and 1:1: whatever per-level resource a normal Wizard receives in spell-level-equivalent capacity at character-level-up, the Lexanari or Netharim receives the same numeric value as freely-distributable mastery points. The splat may tune the Wizard's progression chart later; the conversion stays parallel however that chart is set.

### Distribution rules

Mastery points are **freely distributable**. The character chooses, at each level-up, where to spend each point. They may:

- **Concentrate** all the level's points into a single Pillar, advancing that Pillar's mastery quickly
- **Spread** the points across multiple Pillars, advancing each more slowly
- Save no points â€” they should typically be spent at level-up rather than banked, though GMs may allow short-term banking for narrative reasons

There is no requirement that points be spent on Pillars the character has used recently; the discipline of *which* Pillars to advance is part of the character's developing personal magical identity.

### Cost to raise a Pillar

The cost to raise a Pillar's mastery is **cumulative and rising** â€” each successive mastery level costs more than the previous one. The canonical schedule, with **maximum mastery capped at level 12**:

| Pillar Mastery Level | Cumulative Total Points to Reach | Marginal Cost from Previous Level |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 | 1 |
| 2 | 3 | 2 |
| 3 | 6 | 3 |
| 4 | 10 | 4 |
| 5 | 15 | 5 |
| 6 | 21 | 6 |
| 7 | 28 | 7 |
| 8 | 36 | 8 |
| 9 | 45 | 9 |
| 10 | 55 | 10 |
| 11 | 66 | 11 |
| **12 (max)** | **78** | **12** |

The cumulative cost to reach mastery level **N** is the **N**th triangular number â€” `N Ã— (N + 1) / 2` â€” so reaching level 5 costs 15 points, level 8 costs 36 points, level 12 (the cap) costs 78 points. The marginal cost to advance from level N-1 to level N is simply N points. **Mastery cannot exceed level 12** â€” the canonical cap on a single Pillar's mastery for any practitioner.

This curve is **deliberately punishing for deep specialization**, in the precise sense that it rewards the trade-off rather than refusing it. A character with 30 points to spend can:

- Reach **mastery 5** in **two** Pillars (cost 15 each = 30 total), with everything else at level 0
- Reach **mastery 7** in **one** Pillar (cost 28) with two leftover points to put into a level-1 or level-2 spread
- Reach **mastery 3** in **five** Pillars (cost 6 each = 30 total)
- Reach **mastery 1** in **all ten** Pillars (cost 10) with 20 leftover points for further investment somewhere

Across a long career, a character cannot be all things to all working-types. The cost curve forces a real choice between **deep specialization** (extraordinarily powerful in a small handful of Pillars, weak in most others) and **balanced generalism** (competent across many Pillars, exceptional in none). This is the canonical reason [[netharim|Netharim]] communities **specialize and collaborate** ([[netharim#The lifelong study]]) and the reason elder [[lexanari|Lexanari]] are typically known for one or two trademark working-types rather than being uniformly capable.

Reaching the **mastery cap of level 12** in a single Pillar costs **78 points** â€” a substantial career investment. The exact number of character levels required depends on the splat's per-level point-gain chart, but the position is the same regardless: **level-12 mastery is the kind of attainment elder NPC characters can plausibly reach across a long career; starting and mid-career PCs almost certainly cannot reach it in any one Pillar within standard play, let alone multiple Pillars**. Reaching the cap in multiple Pillars is the kind of legendary achievement that defines a handful of named Lexanari and Netharim across all of Eadras's history.

### Starting profile (character level 1)

A first-level Lexanari or Netharim begins with **6 mastery points** to distribute across the ten Pillars (per the level-1 application of the [[#Mastery point gain per level|per-level rule]] above). The 6 points are freely distributable. Sample starting profiles a player could choose:

- **Narrow specialist**: mastery 3 in one Pillar (cost 6 â€” entire budget). Strong in one specific Command + Substance grid cell, untrained in the rest.
- **Dual specialist**: mastery 2 in two Pillars (cost 3 each = 6 total). Two Pillars at moderate strength; if the player picks one Command and one Substance, the resulting combination is the character's signature.
- **Specialist with breadth**: mastery 2 in one Pillar (cost 3) + mastery 1 in three others (cost 1 each = 3 total). One Pillar at moderate strength, three at minimum.
- **Balanced generalist**: mastery 1 in six Pillars (cost 6 total). Minimum mastery across most of the grid, no Pillar above level 1.
- **Half-spread**: mastery 1 in five Pillars (cost 5) + 1 banked or saved point.

Starting characters cannot yet reach mastery level 4 in any Pillar (cost 10) â€” that requires a few character-level-ups of additional points first.

## Casting a working

To cast a Pillar Magic working, the character:

1. **Selects the working's Command + Substance combination** â€” one of the [[pillar-magic#The twenty-five combinations|twenty-five canonical cells]].
2. **Specifies the desired effect** within the chosen cell. (For Lexanari, this is the [[lexanari#The mortal-mind specificity rule|specificity rule]] â€” vague specification produces unpredictable outcomes within the chosen cell. For Netharim, this is the [[netharim#How Netharim magic works|comprehension-shapes-effect]] mechanism â€” the working takes shape according to the depth of the character's understanding.)
3. **Rolls the dice pool**: a number of dice equal to **the sum of the character's mastery in the two Pillars being used** (Command mastery + Substance mastery). The dice type and success threshold depend on the order â€” see below.
4. **Counts successes** against the working's difficulty (set by the GM based on the effect's scope, scale, and ambition).

### Resolution by order

The two orders use the **same number of dice** for any given working but differ in **dice type, success threshold, and failure mode**:

| | **[[netharim\|Netharim]] â€” The Little Gods** | **[[lexanari\|Lexanari]] â€” The Name-Bound** |
|---|---|---|
| **Dice type** | d10 | d12 |
| **Success on** | 8â€“10 (3 outcomes per die; ~30% per die) | 9â€“12 (4 outcomes per die; ~33% per die) |
| **Backlash trigger** | none | number of 1s rolled in the pool **â‰¥** the rating of the **lowest** Pillar used in the working |
| **Failure mode** | Gentle â€” fizzles, produces a weak effect, or simply does nothing | If backlash triggered: **catastrophic backlash** â€” draw from the **Catastrophic Failure Deck**. Otherwise gentle, like Netharim |

A character with Control mastery 5 and Matter mastery 3 attempting a *Control + Matter* working rolls **8 dice** in either order â€” eight d10s as a Netharim, or eight d12s as a Lexanari. The Lexanari has a slightly higher per-die success rate (4/12 = 33% vs 3/10 = 30%). The backlash threshold for that Lexanari working is **3** â€” the rating of the lower Pillar (Matter, at 3). Catastrophic backlash triggers if the Lexanari rolls **three or more 1s** among their eight d12s.

### The backlash rule in detail

The backlash rule is **gated by the working's weakest Pillar**:

- The threshold for triggering backlash is set at the **rating of the lowest Pillar used in the working** â€” the Command rating or the Substance rating, whichever is lower.
- Backlash triggers when the number of 1s rolled in the dice pool **equals or exceeds that threshold**.
- A working in Pillars where both ratings are high is correspondingly safe; a working stretching into a low-rated Pillar is correspondingly dangerous.

Probabilities for an 8-die pool (8 d12s) by lowest-Pillar rating:

| Lowest Pillar | Backlash trigger | Approx. P(backlash) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | any 1 rolled | ~50% |
| 2 | 2+ 1s rolled | ~13% |
| 3 | 3+ 1s rolled | ~1.4% |
| 4 | 4+ 1s rolled | <0.1% |
| 5+ | 5+ 1s rolled | vanishingly rare |

This produces an **extremely steep risk gradient**. A Lexanari working at the very edge of their mastery (one of the two Pillars at rating 1) faces near coin-flip odds of catastrophic backlash on every cast. The same Lexanari working in their high-mastery Pillars is functionally safe.

### The Catastrophic Failure Deck

When a Lexanari working triggers backlash (1s rolled â‰¥ lowest Pillar rating), the character draws from the **Catastrophic Failure Deck** â€” a curated splat-side deck containing a variety of **nasty, unpredictable, and potentially lethal** consequences. The deck includes both subtle and brutal entries; outcomes range from "the working produces something unintended within the chosen Pillar combination" (softer end) through "the Lexanari takes serious magical damage" to "the working hits the wrong target catastrophically" or "reality in the immediate vicinity becomes briefly unstable" (harder end), and the worst entries can kill the caster or others outright.

(Specific deck entries are splat-design open canon; see [[#Open mechanical questions|open mechanical questions]] below. The deck-as-mechanism is canonical; the deck-as-content is for the splat designer to populate.)

### Why this makes Lexanari significantly more dangerous

The Lexanari and Netharim **roll the same number of dice** for any given working (combined-Pillar mastery), and the Lexanari per-die success rate is fractionally **higher** than the Netharim's (4/12 vs 3/10). A naive read suggests Lexanari magic is just better.

What flips the comparison is the catastrophic-backlash rule, **gated by the working's weakest Pillar**. The risk gradient is extreme:

- A Lexanari working entirely in **high-mastery Pillars** â€” both ratings at 4+ â€” is essentially never at backlash risk. Catastrophic exposure is vanishingly rare.
- A Lexanari working that includes any **mid-mastery Pillar (rating 2 or 3)** faces noticeable but manageable backlash exposure.
- A Lexanari working that **stretches into a low-mastery Pillar (rating 1)** faces near coin-flip odds of catastrophic backlash on every single cast.

The structural consequence is that **specialist Lexanari are safe and generalist Lexanari are dead**. A Lexanari who works only within their two or three high-mastery Pillars rarely sees the catastrophic deck; a Lexanari who tries to be broadly capable across many Pillars must repeatedly stretch into Pillars at low mastery and face the catastrophic gradient on each such working.

This is the mechanism behind the in-universe canonical line that **most Lexanari candidates die in training** ([[lexanari#Why so few survive training|see Lexanari]]). Training necessarily involves attempted workings across many Pillars before any have been raised to safe levels; trainees have most or all of their Pillars at rating 1 or 2; many of those workings trigger catastrophic backlash; many of those backlash draws are lethal. Surviving Lexanari are the small fraction who got through the early-mastery danger zone â€” and the survivors emerge as specialists almost by necessity, since survival required focusing investment narrowly to escape the rating-1 backlash gauntlet quickly.

This is also the canonical reason **Lexanari are warned not to extend beyond their established specialties even after surviving training**. A Lexanari with mastery 8 in Control and mastery 1 in Spirit has a *Control + Spirit* (banishing, soul-binding) working available to them in principle, and their per-die success rate is fine â€” but the lowest Pillar gates the backlash threshold at 1, and the working is therefore catastrophically dangerous in a way the same character's Control-and-something-else workings simply are not. The discipline of Lexanari practice is, to a substantial degree, **the discipline of refusing workings that involve any low-mastery Pillar**.

### What the two access methods feel like at the table

- **[[lexanari|Lexanari]]** speak the Primordial Language to invoke. Workings are typically fast and direct â€” the time to cast is roughly *the time it takes to say the words with adequate specificity*, which scales with how much specification the character is trying to pack into the invocation. Failure modes are catastrophic-when-they-strike: insufficient specification means the language lands somewhere within the chosen cell, but if the dice betray the caster the deck steps in.
- **[[netharim|Netharim]]** shape workings through their internal model of reality. Workings are typically slower and more contemplative â€” the character draws on what they have studied about the relevant Pillars and bends reality to match. Failure modes are softer: an inadequate model produces a weaker effect, an off-target effect, or no effect at all, never the catastrophic Lexanari-style runaway.

## Combined mechanical reference

For quick GM reference at the table:

- **Pillar Magic users** are [[lexanari|Lexanari]] and [[netharim|Netharim]] only; not wizards, not priests, not druids
- **Ten Pillars total**: 5 Command (Control / Transform / Create / Destroy / Perceive) + 5 Substance (Essence / Matter / Flesh / Mind / Spirit)
- **Working** = one Command + one Substance; 25 canonical combinations
- **Mastery is per-Pillar**, not per-character-level; cap is **mastery level 12** in any single Pillar
- **Cost to reach mastery level N** = `N Ã— (N + 1) / 2` (cumulative; triangular numbers â€” 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21, 28, 36, 45, 55, 66, 78)
- **Starting mastery points at character level 1** = **6**
- **Points awarded per character level** = total spell levels a normal [[wizards|Wizard]] gains at that character level (6 at character level 1; scales up with character level per the Wizard's progression chart)
- **Points freely distributable** â€” the player chooses
- **Resolution**: roll a number of dice equal to combined mastery (Command + Substance); count successes against difficulty
- **Netharim**: d10s, success on **8â€“10**; gentle failure (fizzle / weak effect)
- **Lexanari**: d12s, success on **9â€“12**; **backlash if 1s rolled â‰¥ lowest Pillar rating in the working** = draw from the Catastrophic Failure Deck

## Open mechanical questions

- ~~**Starting mastery profile.**~~ **Resolved 2026-05-09**: a level-1 character has **6 mastery points** to distribute. See [[#Starting profile (character level 1)|Starting profile]] above. The exact starting-Pillar choices are entirely up to the player; the splat does not prescribe them.
- ~~**Resolution mechanic for combined-Pillar workings.**~~ **Resolved 2026-05-09**: dice pool of size `Command mastery + Substance mastery`, with order-specific dice (d10 for Netharim, d12 for Lexanari) and order-specific success thresholds (8â€“10 for Netharim, 9â€“12 for Lexanari). - **Catastrophic Failure Deck contents.** The deck-as-mechanism is canonical (Lexanari failures with at least one 1 in the pool draw from it); the deck's specific entry catalogue is open canon for the splat designer. Entries should range from "soft" (an unintended-but-recoverable outcome within the chosen Pillar combination) through "moderate" (serious magical damage to the caster, off-target effects, regional reality instability) to "severe" (lethal outcomes for caster and/or others, lasting magical wounds, permanent loss of mastery in the Pillars involved). The deck should be large enough that drawing the same entry twice in a session is rare.
- **Whether backlash triggering is independent of the working's success/failure.** The canonical rule (resolved 2026-05-09) is that backlash triggers when 1s rolled â‰¥ lowest Pillar rating, **regardless of whether the working otherwise succeeded or failed**. A Lexanari can successfully cast their working AND simultaneously trigger backlash on the same roll. Whether the working still produces its intended effect when backlash fires alongside success is a small open question â€” the working interpretation is that the working *does* succeed (the speaker's specificity carried it through the language) but the catastrophic backlash fires *as well* (the language extracted its toll for the flawed invocation). The splat may tune this differently.
- ~~**Whether mastery scales linearly or with diminishing returns.**~~ **Resolved 2026-05-09**: cumulative triangular-number cost curve (level N total cost = N(N+1)/2). Aggressive specialization-rewarding curve; deeply punishing for breadth. See [[#Cost to raise a Pillar|Cost to raise a Pillar]] above. Whether the *effect strength* on a working scales linearly or sub-linearly with mastery level (a separate question from cost) is still open: the canon says higher mastery produces "more powerful and refined" magic but does not specify the precise per-level effect curve.
- **Whether [[lexanari|Lexanari]] and [[netharim|Netharim]] use the same conversion ratio** (mastery points per character level) or different ones. The user-direction text uses one rate; whether the two orders should mechanically diverge here (e.g., Lexanari fewer-but-deeper points to reflect their rarer profile) is a tuning question.
- ~~**Maximum mastery cap.**~~ **Resolved 2026-05-09**: cap is **mastery level 12** in any single Pillar. Reaching it costs 78 points (roughly 39 character levels at the working 2-points-per-level rate), which positions the cap as elder-NPC-attainable but PC-unreachable in standard 1-20 character-level play. Mastery at the cap in **all ten Pillars simultaneously** would cost 780 points â€” the work of millennia and the kind of mythic achievement that defines a handful of named legendary practitioners across all of Eadras's history.
- **Multiclass interactions.** Whether a character can be both Lexanari and Netharim (the user-direction has not addressed this), and how that would interact with the shared Pillar grammar, is open.
- **Cantrip-equivalents.** Whether a Lexanari or Netharim has any free / unlimited / very-low-level workings outside the mastery-point economy is open. The wizard tradition has cantrips; whether the Pillar tradition has an analogue is a splat-design question.
