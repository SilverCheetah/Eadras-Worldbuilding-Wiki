# The Lexanari

**Summary**: The Lexanari — *the Name-Bound* — are an incredibly rare order of mortal mages who can innately reach for and speak the **Primordial Language**, the tongue of the [[primordial-duo|creator deities]]. Their power is enormous and their failure rate catastrophic: most candidates are destroyed during their training, and even surviving Lexanari live with the constant knowledge that a single mistake in pronunciation, tone, or intent during invocation can erase them from existence.

## What they are

A Lexanari is a mortal who can speak the **Primordial Language** — the language [[primordial-duo|Evye and Fryr]] used to shape Eadras itself. It sits one tier above the Divine Language of the [[gods-of-eadras|Elder Gods]] and one tier below the [[true-language|True Language]] of the [[omniversal-creator|Omniversal Creator]]. (See [[true-language]] for the full hierarchy.)

Mortals are not built for this language. The [[true-language|generational degradation model]] places ordinary mortal speech three tiers below True Language; the Primordial tier is two tiers above where mortals naturally function. The Lexanari are the rare exceptions whose minds were structured at conception to reach that high.

The gift is **innate and never learned**. No amount of study, training, or ritual will produce a Lexanari from a person not born one. There is no academy that can manufacture them. There is only the chance of being born with the capacity — and then the question of whether the candidate survives the training.

## Use, not danger

Casual speech is **not** dangerous. A Lexanari can hold normal conversations all day in any mortal language without consequence. The danger arrives only when the Lexanari **consciously chooses to speak the Primordial Language**, which is a deliberate, willed act distinct from ordinary speech.

When they do invoke it, they must speak with **perfect pronunciation, perfect tone, and perfect intent**. Any one of those three failing is potentially catastrophic.

## The mortal-mind specificity rule

The deepest constraint on Lexanari practice is not the difficulty of pronunciation. It is the structural one: **mortal minds cannot hold the full complexity of a command the way a deific mind can.**

A god in the act of creation can simply think *create a plate of food* and receive exactly what was imagined — because the deific mind contains the full specification of every parameter, every contingent quality, every intersection of cause and effect, all at once. The Primordial Language receives that complete imagination and renders it.

A mortal Lexanari cannot. The mortal mind does not contain that whole at once. So when a Lexanari speaks the Primordial Language, the language receives whatever the mortal mind *did* hold — whatever happened to be present in the moment of speaking — and renders that.

This produces the rule that defines Lexanari practice:

> *The more specific and complete the command, the more likely the desired result.*

A Lexanari in danger who shouts *"Stop!"* may stop the attacker's heart. They may stop all motion in the room. They may stop time itself in their immediate vicinity. They may stop their own breathing. The single word, with no specifying context, is too small a frame for the Primordial Language to know what was meant — so the language fills the gap with whatever resonates closest, and any of those readings is a valid execution.

A Lexanari who is hungry and says *"I want a plate of food"* may receive food, but it could be moldy, half-rotten, stolen mid-bite from an inn fifty miles away, or appear inside someone else's body. The vagueness is the killer.

A Lexanari who says, instead, *"I want to create — to bring into existence here in my hands, beginning now, a fresh plate of hot stew, made just now from good ingredients, the stew newly out of a kettle over a fire, served at safe eating temperature — produced immediately upon the completion of these words"* — is far more likely to receive exactly that.

Lexanari training is, in practice, **the discipline of holding the entire specification of an effect in mind, in correct order, with all relevant qualifiers, while pronouncing it in a language that punishes any drift**. It is the most demanding mental work that any mortal practice has ever required.

## Why so few survive training

The training cannot be done in safety. Lexanari learn the Primordial Language by **using** it, because it cannot be taught any other way — there is no abstract theoretical mode separate from invocation. Each training exercise is a real act of speaking the Primordial Language with whatever specificity the candidate currently has.

Most candidates discover, in the course of training, that the specificity they can hold in mind is not yet adequate to the effects they are attempting to produce. The discovery is fatal. There is no consolation prize for almost.

Survivors of Lexanari training are therefore exceptionally disciplined, exceptionally careful, and exceptionally rare. The order has never been numerous. In any given era, the entire surviving Lexanari population of Eadras can typically be listed by name without exhausting the page.

## Pillar Magic — the underlying grammar

Lexanari and Netharim share **the same core magic system**: every working is constructed by combining one **Command Pillar** with one **Substance Pillar**. The Lexanari path is to **invoke the chosen Command + Substance combination through the Primordial Language** — the language has the Pillar grammar built into its structure as fundamental concepts, and speaking the right pillars together with adequate specificity produces the working.

The five Command Pillars (the verbs of magic): **Control, Transform, Create, Destroy, Perceive.**
The five Substance Pillars (the nouns of magic): **Essence, Matter, Flesh, Mind, Spirit.**
Twenty-five canonical combinations.

The [[#The mortal-mind specificity rule|specificity rule]] above applies *within* the chosen combination. A Lexanari has selected, say, *Transform + Flesh*, and the question is now how precisely they can specify which transformation, on what flesh, with what limits — not whether they have the Pillars in hand at all (they do, by training and innate gift). Insufficient specification within the chosen combination produces unpredictable outcomes within that combination's scope; the Pillars don't help with vagueness, they merely *delineate* its scope.

Lexanari mastery progression is **per-Pillar**, not a unified spell-points pool — each of the ten Pillars has its own mastery level, deepened independently as the practitioner studies and works with that Pillar. A Lexanari with high Control mastery and low Mind mastery will produce *Control + Mind* workings (domination, mental command) that are firm-of-will but rough-on-mind, and so on across the grid. The very rare Lexanari who approaches high mastery in all ten Pillars is correspondingly extraordinary — and dangerous.

The catastrophic-backlash risk that defines Lexanari practice is **gated by the working's weakest Pillar** — whichever of the two Pillars used in a given working is at lower mastery. A Lexanari working entirely in their high-mastery Pillars is essentially safe; a Lexanari working that stretches into a Pillar at low mastery faces a steep risk gradient toward catastrophic backlash. This produces the canonical Lexanari discipline: **work only in your specialties**. A Lexanari with deep Control mastery and shallow Spirit mastery can theoretically attempt *Control + Spirit* workings, but the working's weak link is Spirit, and Spirit is what gates the catastrophic deck. Discipline says: do not attempt that working.

For the full canonical treatment of Pillar Magic, see [[pillar-magic]]. For the splat-side mechanics (mastery point gain per character level, the d12 dice pool, the weakest-Pillar backlash rule, the Catastrophic Failure Deck), see [[rpg/pillar-magic-mechanics]].

## Distinction from the [[netharim|Netharim]]

The Lexanari and the [[netharim|Netharim]] are sometimes confused by laypeople because both work outside the formula-driven structure of [[wizards|wizardry]] and outside the deity-mediated structure of [[priests|priesthood]]. They are not similar — though they do share **the same underlying [[pillar-magic|Pillar Magic]] grammar** (above). What differs is how they access it.

- **Lexanari** invoke the Pillars by speaking the Primordial Language. Workings are typically fast and direct — the time to cast is the time it takes to say the words with adequate specificity. Failure modes are catastrophic: insufficient specification means the language lands somewhere within the chosen combination, but exactly *where* is up to the language.
- **[[netharim|Netharim]]** shape the Pillars through their internal model of reality. Workings are typically slower and more contemplative — the character draws on what they have studied about the relevant Pillars and bends reality to match. Failure modes are softer: an inadequate model produces a weaker effect, an off-target effect, or no effect, but rarely the catastrophic Lexanari-style runaway.

A common formulation: *Lexanari speak; Netharim know. The first rewrites reality, the second nudges it.* Both are working with the same grammar; they just access it differently.

## Distinction from the old [[order-sharanel|Netharim]] tradition

The Lexanari fill the niche the old [[order-sharanel|Netharim]]/Druid axis used to occupy as *"a magical tradition with overwhelming power and severe consequences for failure."* They are not, however, mechanically the same as either. Netharim and Druids in their previous canonical form drew from external structures (the Weave, the All); Lexanari draw from no external substrate at all. They simply speak.

The mapping from the old tradition to the Lexanari is conceptual, not literal. See [[log]] and [[inspirations]].

## Open canon

- Whether Lexanari training has any institutional structure (a master-apprentice model, a school, a hidden order), or whether the gift is so rare that each surviving Lexanari trains the next one privately.
- Whether Lexanari are recognized formally by the [[order-sharanel]] or by major nation-states, or whether they tend to remain anonymous because the gift is more dangerous as a public identifier than as a secret.
- Whether speaking the Primordial Language can be done **silently** (an internal articulation) — the working canon assumes spoken articulation only, but the question is not closed.
- The geographic and bloodline distribution of the gift. The implication that the gift is born, not made, raises but does not answer the question of whether certain lineages produce Lexanari more often than others.
- Whether a Lexanari can refuse to use the gift entirely — and whether that refusal is honored by the cosmos or whether the gift will eventually surface against the holder's will.
