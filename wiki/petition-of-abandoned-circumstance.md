# Petition of Abandoned Circumstance

**Summary**: A formal afterlife procedure by which dead souls may **accuse the divine offices** of negligence, routing failure, or institutional delay that contributed materially to their suffering or death. Filed in the afterway halls between death and final placement; processed by the [[katharim|Katharim]] and the death-priests of [[shali|Shali]] and [[shani|Shani]]. **Most petitions are denied** — not because the pain is false, but because divine responsibility is difficult to assign — but the existence of the procedure is itself the [[doctrine-of-open-palm|Open Palm's]] institutional accountability mechanism. The Petitions preserve theological tension: suffering is real, negligence can be real, responsibility remains difficult.

## When a Petition arises

A soul filing a Petition of Abandoned Circumstance has typically just died under conditions where:

- A prayer was sent and not answered in time
- A divine miracle was procedurally available but did not arrive
- A Hearth-Witness's consequence-file flagged the case but the office did not respond
- A [[three-bells-of-intervention|Third Bell]] should have rung but didn't
- A divine office's routing visibly misallocated resources during the dying's window
- Some other documentable institutional failure contributed materially to the death

The Petition is not appropriate for *ordinary* deaths. Old age, accident, ordinary illness, ordinary violence — these are part of the world's regular pain. The Petition is reserved for deaths where *the institutional structure that was supposed to be there visibly failed to be there*.

## Where Petitions are filed

In the **afterway halls** between death and final afterlife placement. The traditional locus is the **Hall of Last Hearings**, a specific institutional space within [[shali|Shali]] and [[shani|Shani]]'s shared administrative geography where newly-arrived souls may, briefly, raise grievances before being placed.

The Hall is staffed by:

- **[[katharim|Katharim]] death-priest auditors** who collect the soul's testimony and the accompanying record
- **A panel of recent dead** who have served their afterlife sentences as Hearing-witnesses (distinct from [[hearth-witness-mandate|Hearth-Witnesses]] in the living sense)
- **A senior [[shani|Shani]]-clergy mediator** who oversees the hearing
- *No representative of the accused divine office* in the initial hearing — the office is summoned only if the Petition is provisionally accepted

A soul has roughly the equivalent of three days of mortal time to file. The window is firm: after placement, the soul is in the cycle and may no longer file. (This produces a recurring tension — souls who realise their grievance only after rest, judgment, or partial reincarnation can no longer access the Petition.)

## What happens to a Petition

### Stage 1 — Provisional review

The Katharim auditor reviews the soul's testimony against available records: the divine office's procedural log, the [[hearth-witness-mandate|Hearth-Witness]] consequence-files (if any), the relevant prayer routing, the medical/military/civic records of the death event. The review is procedural; it asks whether the Petition has *any institutional basis*.

Most Petitions are dismissed at this stage as **grief-petitions without institutional grounds**. The soul's pain is real; the institutional case is not. The soul is informed gently, given the option of brief counsel with a death-priest, and proceeds to placement.

### Stage 2 — Office summoned

Petitions that pass provisional review summon the accused divine office to the Hall. The office sends a representative; the office's procedural log is read aloud; the office's response is recorded. The Mediator may ask questions of both sides.

This stage is *uncommon*. Most Petitions do not reach it.

### Stage 3 — Ruling

The Mediator renders a ruling. Outcomes:

- **Petition Denied**. The institutional case does not support the soul's claim. The office is not at fault. The soul is placed.
- **Petition Partially Sustained**. Some institutional failure occurred; full negligence is not established. The office receives a formal record of the partial sustainment. The soul receives an acknowledgement in the official record. The soul is placed.
- **Petition Sustained**. Institutional negligence is established. The office is formally censured. A correction is required at the bureaucratic level. The soul is placed *with prejudice* in the official record — meaning future audits of related petitions will weight the office's responses accordingly.
- **Petition Escalated**. The Mediator finds the case exceeds the Hall's authority — typically because the alleged failure implicates [[gods-of-eadras|Elder Gods]] directly. Escalation routes to the [[baldaranic-council-cycle|Baldaranic Council]] or, in extreme cases, to the [[tome-of-baldaran|Tome of Baldaran]] for sealed-chapter recording.

## Why most are denied

The Council was explicit about why the denial rate is high — and why it must remain so. The doctrinal logic:

> If every tragedy becomes litigation against heaven, divine administration collapses under accusation. If no tragedy can accuse heaven, divine restraint becomes immune from judgment.

The Petition system preserves the tension. Suffering is real. Negligence can be real. Responsibility remains difficult to assign. The denial rate's height is a feature: it forces the Hall to refuse most claims, which preserves divine action's legitimacy when it does occur — and it forces the gods to acknowledge the cases that *do* sustain, which preserves the doctrine's accountability.

The most uncomfortable form of denial is the **acknowledged-but-denied**: cases where the Katharim auditor recognises that the soul has been wronged but the institutional structure does not produce assignable responsibility. The death was not natural; the failure was real; but no specific office can be censured because the failure was systemic, distributed, or below the threshold of any single office's mandate.

For these cases, the death-priest tradition has developed quiet counsel: *we cannot give you justice; we can only listen to your testimony before you go on*. This is not satisfying. It is what the doctrine permits.

## The Mediator's burden

The Hall's [[shani|Shani]]-clergy Mediators bear unusual weight. They must:

- Hold institutional structure (cannot validate every petition, or the system collapses)
- Hold mortal pain (cannot dismiss legitimate cases, or the doctrine becomes a sham)
- Hold the Decree of Shali-Shani-Ioa (cannot disclose sealed material even when the petitioner asks)
- Hold the [[katharim|Katharim]] auditor's institutional discipline
- Hold the soul's mortal urgency in the small window before placement

The position is a death-priest specialisation. Mediators serve specific tenure cycles; the role is rotated to prevent burnout, which is real and observable. Burnt-out Mediators reportedly lose the capacity to track mortal urgency, which is exactly the failure mode the [[hearth-witness-mandate|Hearth-Witness Mandate]] addresses for the living-world bureaucracy.

## Doctrinal pressure

The Petition's existence is what makes the [[doctrine-of-open-palm|Open Palm reform]] real rather than rhetorical. Without an accountability mechanism, *the Hand shall not be absent* is aspiration. With the Petitions, the gods are formally accountable to the dead.

But the Petition does not solve the underlying tension. The denial rate ensures most pain is denied institutional remedy. The sustainment rate ensures the institution remains capable of being wrong. The doctrine lives in the tension between the two.

[[shali|Shali]] is sceptical of the Petition system — she finds the death-cycle litigation crude, and she would prefer that judgment happen at her tribunal not in a hearing hall. [[shani|Shani]] is more sympathetic — she sees grief as needing acknowledgement even when institutional remedy is denied, and the Hall provides exactly that acknowledgement. The pairing of their offices in administering the Hall is part of the doctrine's calibration.

## Examples (illustrative, not all canonical)

Cases the Council discussed as illustrative of the Petition's range:

- **Plague-village petitions**: a village died of a plague that priestly intervention could have arrested. The Hall almost always sustains these partially — the priests acted in good faith but the routing failed.
- **Battle-medic petitions**: a wounded soldier prayed for a battlefield miracle that did not arrive. Hall typically denies — the battlefield is the [[three-bells-of-intervention|First Bell]] tier and divine intervention is structurally inappropriate.
- **Childbirth petitions**: a child died in labour after a midwife prayed for divine assistance. Hall sometimes sustains — depending on the office's routing record and the local Hearth-Witness's file.
- **[[anti-magic-crusade|Anti-Magic Crusade]] petitions**: every soul killed during the Crusade who can establish that they were *not* a magic-user filing a Petition. The volume of these flooded the Hall in the aftermath; the doctrinal response is still being worked out.
- **[[unreceived|Unreceived]] petitions** — the souls of those wholly consumed by demons *cannot file*, because they never arrive at the Hall. This is one of the doctrine's hard limits: the most severely wronged souls have no procedural recourse.

## Open canon

- The **flood of Anti-Magic Crusade petitions** in the millennia after the Crusade — how the Hall processed them, how many were sustained, what doctrinal precedents emerged.
- **The first Petition sustained against an Elder God** (rather than against a divine office at a junior administrative tier). Whether any have been sustained at all is a matter of secret knowledge — most cases that would reach this tier are escalated to the [[baldaranic-council-cycle|Council]] rather than ruled on at the Hall.
- **How Hearth-Witness consequence-files feed into Petition rulings** — the procedural integration is being formalised through the [[first-rebrightening|First Rebrightening]].
- **The Petition's relationship to the [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Unheld Hand]] coexistence** — can a Petition allege failure of restraint? Or only failure of intervention? The doctrinal answer is technical and emerging.
- **Whether a [[katharim|Katharim]] mortal-side advocate can file a posthumous Petition** on behalf of a deceased relative who didn't reach the Hall in time. Currently not permitted, but the reform pressure is real.
