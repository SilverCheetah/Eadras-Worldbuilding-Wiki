## Worship patterns

How a god receives worship varies by tier (source: May 1 revisions.txt):

- **Elder Gods** do not crave worship. They accept it when freely given and have allowed their priesthoods to develop their own traditions.
- **Elderborn** actively desire and encourage worship; their faiths tend to be more personal and fervent.
- **Ascended Mortal Gods** (such as Inki, Baldaran, Din'shadar, Aramet, Korvath, Vexara, the Three Last Lights, the Dragon Gods, and the Unicorn Gods) actively desire and encourage worship; their faiths are typically the most personal and fervent of all.

Worship in Eadras is **not race-restricted**. A single city may contain temples to many different gods, with priests and devotees of many races mingling in shared rites. See [[religion-in-eadras|Religion in Eadras]] for the full account, including the dragons' early Source-only worship and the post-Weave theological upheaval.

## Source of godly power

The power that gods exercise â€” sometimes called **Godpower** in cross-Mythos theological writing â€” has a single underlying source across the [[omniverse|Omniverse]]. Every Mythos contains a **Well of Creation**, a reservoir of the **Creative Power** the [[omniversal-creator|Omniversal Creator]] put into that Mythos at its making. Most of that power was spent creating the Mythos itself â€” its planes, its laws, its substance, the conditions for life within it. What remains *leftover* in the Well after creation is finished is what the gods of that Mythos draw on. A deity's power, anywhere in the Omniverse, is in the most basic sense **a tap on the local leftover Creative Power**.

**All gods hold a direct link** to their Mythos's Well. The connection is unmediated â€” there are no intermediaries between deity and Creative Power, no priestly conduit required, no permission needed beyond divinity itself. A god is, by definition, *something that holds a direct link to its local Well*. Mortal-tier beings cannot establish such a link; the link is the threshold of divinity, not a privilege granted by it.

One notable **non-deity entity** in Eadras also holds a direct link to the Well: the **[[spear-of-crystallized-entropy|Spear of Crystallized Entropy]]**. This is the canonical reason the Spear operates at god-tier power and is so catastrophic when wielded by a mortal â€” it has the same unmediated tap that a god has. In this very specific sense, the Spear is a god, though a *thing* rather than a person. The existence of the Spear is the reason a mortal can, briefly and disastrously, exercise something resembling divine power: the Spear is the conduit, and through it, the mortal touches Creative Power directly. ([[dao]]'s curse on the Spear is what keeps that touch from being merely beneficial; see [[spear-of-crystallized-entropy]] for the full treatment.)

### Worship as a secondary source of godpower

In addition to the Well-link that constitutes their divinity, gods across the [[omniverse|Omniverse]] derive a **secondary stream of power from worshippers**. *More worshippers means more godpower*; loss of worshippers shrinks the god's effective capacity. The relationship is canonical but underdocumented in mortal theology, which usually frames worship as a duty *to* the gods rather than as a substrate *of* their power.

In Mythoi with finite, slowly-draining Wells, worship-power is **load-bearing** for godly capacity. Pantheons grow by accumulating worship and decline when worship shrinks. This is the structural reason most pantheons are **expansionist**: they push their worship outward into neighbouring Mythoi, contest other pantheons for converts, and conduct competing-for-souls business in the **Outer Realms** (which encircle the [[concordance|Concordance]]) and in **[[nexus|Nexus]]** (the megacity where gods of every Mythos can meet). For most gods, expansion is not ambition; it is survival.

The Eadras pantheon is the canonical exception. Their [[#Position in the multiverse|Bastion-fed Well]] makes Well-power effectively unlimited, and the positional advantage of where Eadras sits in the Omniverse is already sufficient for them on its own. Worship-power still flows â€” Eadras has thriving priesthoods, and the [[divine-bureaucracy|Divine Bureaucracy]]'s Bureau of Prayer and Bureau of Favor process worship and reward devotion through institutionalised Favor â€” but the Eadras pantheon does *not need* worship for survival or growth the way most pantheons do. This is part of why they can afford to be inward-turned and absorbed in the [[#The Game of the Gods|Game]] rather than expansionist. Worship in Eadras is something gods receive because gods are worshipped; it is not the substrate of their existence.

This is **universal architecture**. It is not Eadras-specific. Every god in the Omniverse, regardless of which Mythos they call home, takes their power from their local Well of Creation in the same way. Variation between Mythoi â€” why some are magic-rich and others thin, why some pantheons are mighty and others marginal â€” is variation in **how much leftover Creative Power each Well holds**.

The Well's contents are the OC's creative substance, distilled into the form that animates the local Mythos. Mortal scholars who have studied the metaphysics conjecture that the Well also feeds mortal magic in some attenuated form, and that the connection between godly power and the magic mortals work is closer than mortal practice usually acknowledges. The full relationship is open canon; see [[magic-in-eadras]] for the Eadras-specific magical mechanisms that may or may not be Well-derived.

A theological consequence worth noting: **gods are not the source of their own power.** Whatever a god creates, builds, threatens, or grants is ultimately a borrowing from the OC's prior creative act â€” channelled through the Well, into the deity, and out into the Mythos. Pantheons that grasp this are quieter than pantheons that do not. The Eadras pantheon's understanding of this is mixed, varies by individual god, and is not a question they spend much time on while the [[#The Game of the Gods|Game]] is available.

## Position in the multiverse

The pantheon of Eadras occupies an unusually privileged cosmic position, resting on **two distinct features** that together set Eadras apart from every other Mythos in the Omniverse. These features are independent of each other and should not be conflated.

### Eadras is intrinsically magically rich

**Intrinsic magical richness is a property of the Mythos itself** â€” of its substance, its laws, its cosmological architecture, what is woven into its underlying fabric. Eadras is intrinsically rich: its reality is saturated with magic at the level of what the Mythos *is*. Both mortal spellcraft and divine working draw on a magically dense ground.

This is **distinct from the size of the Well of Creation**. The [[#Source of godly power|Well]] regulates how much Creative Power is available to the gods of a Mythos â€” a power-budget concept. Intrinsic magical richness regulates what reality is *like* â€” what is possible, what is woven into the substance, what spellcraft can find to work with.

The two properties are **conceptually independent variables**. In sacrifice-Mythoi (the [[primordial-duo#Why they sacrificed|seven closest to the Bastion]]) they nonetheless **co-vary by shared cause**: pouring out both Well-allotment and Primordial essence into the substance produces intrinsic richness *at the cost of* the Well that remains afterward. The typical *consequence* â€” small remaining Well â†’ modest pantheon â€” follows in most sacrifice-Mythoi. Eadras's exception is the [[bastion-of-light|Bastion]] feed, which **decouples the consequence from the cause**: the small Well never empties, so the consequence does not follow.

### The Primordial sacrifice â€” rare in the Omniverse, shared by the seven closest Mythoi

The second feature is **rare in the Omniverse but not unique to Eadras**: its Primordial creators, [[primordial-duo|Evye and Fryr]], **sacrificed themselves into the Mythos**. They dissolved their essence into the Well of Creation and into the substance of the world, paying their own existence as the price of an exceptionally bountiful, diverse, magically rich creation.

In the infinite Omniverse, this pattern occurs only in a small handful of Mythoi: **the seven Mythoi closest to the [[bastion-of-light|Bastion of Light]]** (Eadras among them), and possibly in some Mythoi of the **second cosmographical ring** outward from the Bastion (canonically uncertain). Across the rest of the Omniverse â€” across the vast majority of Mythoi â€” primordial creators are *still present*, still ruling, still in possession of their Wells; primordial sacrifice is the **extraordinary exception**, restricted (so far as is known) to a small inner cluster around the Bastion.

The motivation for the Primordials' choice is documented at [[primordial-duo#Why they sacrificed|Primordial Duo Â§ Why they sacrificed]]: Evye and Fryr had **no desire to rule**, wanted to create the most bountiful and richly magical Mythos they could, and **trusted the Elder Gods** (their own children) to shape creation alongside them and to rule after their departure. The sacrifice was a deliberate handoff, not a forced ending.

The consequences for Eadras of the sacrifice are several:

- **There is no primordial-tier active divinity within the Mythos's pantheon.** The [[#Tier Structure|Elder Gods]] are the highest active tier â€” by design, since the Primordials handed rulership to them. ([[dao]] operates *outside* the Mythos's divinity entirely: he is not a Mythos divinity, holds no portfolio within the Eadras pantheon, and does not regulate the Mythos. His function is as an [[omniversal-creator|OC]] Avatar â€” a tier-prior cosmic layer that does not enter into the Mythos's internal divinity ordering.)
- **The Well is small.** Most of the OC's substantial original allotment, *and* most of the Primordial Duo's added essence, was expended creating the intrinsic magical richness of Eadras's substance.
- **The Mythos itself is exceptionally rich.** The expenditure produced the saturated reality Eadras enjoys.

The six other Mythoi closest to the Bastion are presumed to follow similar patterns â€” sacrificed primordials, Elder-Gods-tier active divinity, intrinsically rich worlds. Their specific histories are not documented in Eadras canon; they exist at the edge of mortal scholarship as cosmographical facts more than as known histories. Across the rest of the Omniverse, the typical pattern of primordial-still-ruling Mythoi prevails.

### Why the pantheon is still powerful

The Eadras pantheon draws Godpower from the small remainder Well. By depth measure alone, they should be operating on a thin supply. They are not, because the **small Well is always full**.

Eadras sits **immediately adjacent to the [[concordance|Concordance]]** â€” the vast outer plane at the center of the Omniverse â€” and through that proximity, the [[bastion-of-light|Bastion of Light]] continuously feeds Eadras's Well. The Bastion is the absolute center of the Omniverse and the home of the [[omniversal-creator|OC]]; the Creative Power it radiates passes through the [[concordance|Concordance]] and seeps directly into the nearest Mythos. That nearest Mythos is Eadras. The Well **never runs dry**.

The mechanic is precise: **Well size regulates total power available to gods *only when the Well is not being refilled*.** A large Well slowly draining bottlenecks its pantheon eventually. A small Well that is always full does not. In Eadras the size-as-cap framing simply does not apply; the pantheon has **effectively unlimited Well availability**.

Well availability is, however, only one of three axes that bound divine action. The full mechanic:

- **Well size** â€” the reservoir gods draw from. Effectively unlimited in Eadras (Bastion feed); finite-and-draining in typical Mythoi.
- **Per-god draw capacity** â€” the rate and total quantity a single god can channel from the Well in any given working. **Bounded everywhere, including Eadras.** A god can exhaust themselves on a single great effort: [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Auralis and Thamior]] spent their full draw plus their personal life-force trying to save the Arathi, and dwindled to faint avatars as a result. They did not drain the Well; they hit their own draw limit and pushed past it into self-consumption.
- **Bureaucratic channeling** â€” the [[divine-bureaucracy|Divine Bureaucracy]]'s institutional speed, competence, and availability for translating divine intent into mortal-world effect. The Bureau operates on **rules** (doctrine, Favor, procedure) â€” not on power scarcity. Miracle denials are doctrinal or procedural, not capacity-based. This is a separate bottleneck from raw Godpower and applies regardless of how full the Well is.

The Eadras pantheon is **unbounded on the first axis and bounded on the other two**. They can never run out of Well-power to draw on; they can exhaust themselves on any single working; and the Bureaucracy continues to filter what actually reaches mortals through the rules-based process every Bureau in the Omniverse uses.

Combined with the intrinsic magical richness of the Mythos itself, this gives the Eadras pantheon what only the **seven closest Mythoi** possess: **unbounded Godpower in a magically saturated Mythos, with no primordial-tier divinity above them**. The deities native to Eadras are correspondingly powerful â€” **among the most powerful gods in the multiverse**, by the simultaneous gift of cosmic geography, intrinsic Mythos richness, and the vacancy left by the Primordial sacrifice.

Their power is *positional* rather than intrinsic to themselves. A god of similar nature in a Mythos farther from the Bastion, with active primordials in place to outrank them, drawing from a Well that drains and is not refilled, would be a far smaller being. The Eadras pantheon's strength is **a gift of three rare circumstances aligned in this Mythos** â€” shared with a handful of close cosmic neighbours and found nowhere else.

In most other Mythoi, gods of such power would be **expansionist**. Because [[#Worship as a secondary source of godpower|worshippers are a secondary source of godpower]] and the local Well slowly drains, expansion is *survival* â€” pantheons grow by accumulating worship and decline when they don't. The standard high-tier deity pattern across the [[omniverse|Omniverse]] is therefore to push worship outward into neighbouring Mythoi, contest other pantheons in the Outer Realms (which encircle the [[concordance|Concordance]]) and in [[nexus|Nexus]], recruit or convert populations across realities, and gradually accrue influence beyond the bounds of the home plane. This is what powerful gods generally do.

The Eadras pantheon does **not** do this. They are turned almost entirely inward, absorbed in their own affairs and (especially) in the [[#The Game of the Gods|Game of the Gods]]. This inward orientation is not coincidence. The Game is the mechanism that maintains it: deeply addictive, complex enough to occupy immortal attention indefinitely, and a perfect recreation of *Eadras itself* rather than any other Mythos â€” playing it draws the gods deeper into Eadras, not outward toward anywhere else.

Crucially, the Game keeps the pantheon busy **without harming them**. It is not a chain, not a binding, not a diminishment. It is **benign containment**: the gods are absorbed and content, and they remain at their full power; they simply spend that power inward. From [[dao|Dao's]] [[dao#Role|monitor frame]], this serves *both* roles 1 and 2 of his triple function: it ensures the smooth running of Eadras (since gods absorbed in the Game don't over-intervene in mortal affairs), *and* it preserves the macro balance of the Omniverse (since the most powerful pantheon in a magic-rich Mythos isn't pushing into neighbouring Mythoi to destabilise them). The Game is, among other things, the reason this exceptionally-powerful pantheon has never become an Omniversal problem.

The pantheon themselves do not experience the Game as containment. From inside it, they experience it as the most interesting thing in existence â€” which it largely is, for them. The system is stable because it is genuinely satisfying to the contained.

## The Game of the Gods

Created by [[dao]] in secret, one day it simply appeared in [[solarion]]. The Game is **a perfect recreation of the Eadras Mythos in miniature, gamified** â€” an exact-scale model of the world below, with all important mortals as pieces on a vast board in the divine realm. Its rules are intricate, layered, and *godly* â€” appropriate to immortal players and beyond the capacity of any mortal mind to hold in full. The gods take turns moving tokens, much as mortal players in a game of D&D do, but the moves available, the rules governing each, and the strategic depths involved are of an entirely different order. The Game is deeply addictive, which is why most gods spend their time in the divine realm rather than intervening directly in mortal affairs.

### The board and the play

The board is **live**. It updates in real time, continuously reflecting the state of Eadras-below as that state changes. There is no separate "input" or "rendering" step; the board *is* a divine-realm view of the same world the mortals inhabit. Its display is **pictographic**: every piece, region, current, and trend is rendered visually, in symbols and shifting iconography that the gods read fluently and that no mortal could parse. A scholar shown the board would not see "kings and cities and armies." They would see an unintelligible mass of moving signs whose underlying grammar is divine.

This is not an accident of representation; it is a structural fact about the Game. The information density of Eadras at any instant is far beyond any mortal mind's capacity to hold, let alone interpret. The pictographic form is what permits an immortal player to take in the whole board at once. Reduce it to anything a mortal could read, and the Game's grain disappears.

**Play unfolds across turns at depths mortals cannot trace.** A god following the rules can make moves whose immediate effect on the board is far too subtle for any mortal observer to register â€” a nudge here, an inflection there, a small adjustment to one piece's standing in a system of relationships â€” and the consequences land *turns later*, often as major events whose causal chain has long since vanished from observable history. The clichÃ© of divine plans operating across centuries is, in the Game, mechanical truth: a god setting up a delayed payoff fifty turns out is **playing the rules as designed**, not bending them.

This deepens the causation question (below) significantly. Even if a mortal scholar accepted the *Game-causes-Eadras* position, they could not trace any particular mortal event back to its originating divine move â€” the moves are too subtle and the chains too long. The causation, if it exists, is **real but unrecoverable**. Even if a god *told* a mortal which Game move produced a particular outcome, the mortal would have no way to verify it: the move would have been one of many on its originating turn, and the chain to the present is opaque.

The aesthetic implications are significant. Divine plans in Eadras are not theatrical announcements followed by visible miracles. They are quiet, layered, and almost invisibly long-term. A god who suddenly intervenes openly is doing something *outside* their usual play â€” and (as the next section discusses) such interventions further disrupt the Game and are themselves regarded as disruptive even by the intervening god.

### Causation and reality

The board moves and Eadras appears to move with it. A god moves a piece, and the mortal-world correlate of that piece *also* moves: a king's army marches, a lover changes their mind, a city's fortunes turn. The correspondence is reliable enough that the gods plainly believe they are playing reality.

But the **direction of causation** is one of the great philosophical questions of Eadras, and from the mortal side it cannot be resolved. Does the Game *cause* Eadras â€” every meaningful mortal event directed by some god's move? Does Eadras *cause* the Game â€” the board reflecting mortal choices made freely below? Are both true at once, the Game and Eadras isomorphic in a way that makes the question ill-formed? Or is the relationship asymmetric, with some moves causing and others reflecting?

Philosophers argue. The debate is approximately as old as recorded scholarship; mortal schools have lined up on every position. **And â€” critically â€” the gods themselves do not know.** This is not divine secret-keeping or coyness. The Game was made by [[dao]] *in secret*, and Dao did not explain it. It simply appeared one day in [[solarion|Solarion]], complete and already playing, and the current pantheon has learned its rules through millennia of play rather than from instruction. They play the Game competently â€” they have been at it long enough to be very competent â€” but they did not design it and cannot interrogate its designer. When mortal scholars have managed to ask a dispatched divine agent about the causation question, the answer has varied by the god, and (more tellingly) the gods who give different answers do not regard their disagreement as serious; they are reporting their own private theories formed during long play, not authoritative knowledge. The Game's nature is, from any single vantage *including the divine one*, opaque.

Mortal scholarship has accumulated **canonical examples on each side** of the debate, none of which fully resolves the question.

**Evidence for the Game causing Eadras** comes from **direct-effect actions**: cases where a god has plainly moved a piece on the board and the corresponding mortal has acted accordingly without prior mortal cause. The flagship example is the moving of **Heroes** â€” the Game's term for the rare mortal pieces that have personal divine attention. A god moves a Hero across the board; the mortal Hero, in Eadras, sets out on a journey. A god moves a Hero into the territory of an enemy; the mortal Hero confronts and engages. The correspondence is direct, the timing is tight, and the Hero's own retrospective account often acknowledges *not knowing why* they made the choice they did. Mortal traditions inclined to the *Game-causes-Eadras* reading point to these cases as canonical.

**Evidence against the Game causing Eadras** comes from **emergent events with no deific cause**: major Eadras-events that no god initiated through Game-play. The canonical example is the **[[demon-war|Demon War]]**. The [[demon-prince-of-the-open-wound|Demon Prince]] was summoned into Eadras by [[xarvion|Xar'vion]] through a ritual operating *outside the Game's mechanics* â€” not through a Game move. No god in [[solarion|Solarion]] played the piece that produced the war; the board *updated* to reflect the demonic incursion after the fact, with pieces dying, regions clearing, and narrative cycles destroyed by an event no Game move could be traced to. If the Game caused Eadras in a pure form, no such emergent event should have been possible. The Demon War is the most-cited counterexample in the *Eadras-causes-Game* and *isomorphism* traditions.

Together, these two bodies of evidence make pure versions of both *Game-causes-Eadras* and *Eadras-causes-Game* difficult to maintain. A growing number of modern scholars hold an **asymmetric** reading: divine moves cause downward for pieces the gods are actively playing (Heroes and similar), while emergent Eadras-events update the board reflectively (the Game accommodates what it did not initiate). This hybrid position is not yet a canonical fifth school, but its adherents are increasingly common in [[lirra|Lirran]] and Tarran philosophical circles.

The traditions usually shake out into four positions:

- **Game-causes-Eadras** â€” every meaningful mortal event is the consequence of a divine move; free will is an illusion mortals tell each other; the real action happens above and is enforced below.
- **Eadras-causes-Game** (the *reflective* reading) â€” the board is an observer, a mirror, a divine instrument that reflects what mortals do but does not direct it. Mortal moral responsibility is preserved. [[lirra|Lirran]] clergy generally favour this reading.
- **Isomorphism without direction** â€” the Game and Eadras are isomorphic at every moment; to ask which causes the other is to misunderstand the relationship. Some Tarran philosophers, with their traditional taste for formal ontological investigation, hold this position.
- **Principled refusal to speculate** â€” mortals cannot resolve the question from inside Eadras, and asking is not piety. The [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Doctrine of the Unheld Hand]] is the canonical example; it is a piety position, not a metaphysical one.

None of these positions are settled, and the debate is expected to persist as long as both mortals and gods exist.

### Why the gods hate demons

The gods' hatred of [[demons|demons]] is real, intense, and **petty**. It is not a noble cosmic opposition to invaders from a collapsed Mythos, nor a protective rage over mortals threatened by predatory aliens. It is a grudge.

Demons disrupt the Game. When the [[demon-war|Demon War]] began and the [[demon-prince-of-the-open-wound|Demon Prince of the Open Wound]] tore his way into Eadras through [[xarvion|Xar'vion]]'s summons, the Game in [[solarion|Solarion]] became unplayable. Important mortals â€” Game pieces â€” began dying in volumes the rules had never been built to handle. Whole regions cleared off the board. The carefully-balanced narrative cycles the gods had spent millennia cultivating were torn up in months. The addictive cycle was *broken*, and the gods could not simply ignore it.

Worse: the demons forced the gods to **leave the Game and intervene directly** in the war. Intervention is itself a disruption â€” moving a god's own pieces, breaking the turn-by-turn discipline of the Game, distorting the board with direct action that the Game's rules treat as a last resort. The gods who entered the war did so reluctantly, knowing each direct action further degraded the Game they were trying to defend.

The lasting consequence is that **most of the divine pantheon's loathing of demons is rooted in this grudge**, not in moral horror. A god who has seen mortal suffering on a scale that would shatter mortal minds is usually unmoved by mortal suffering. A god whose addictive entertainment has been ruined for centuries by an alien predator is *furious*, in a small petty way that runs deeper than the cosmic frame would suggest.

The [[divine-bureaucracy|Divine Bureaucracy]] has had to manage this. The bureau heads â€” themselves outside the Game in the way the Bureaucracy is outside divine politics generally â€” have spent millennia routing around the grudge, fielding divine demands that demons be hunted, ritualised, anathematised, and framed within mortal-facing doctrine as cosmic evils. The underlying motive was always *they broke our game*. The doctrine reads better. A reflective theologian â€” among the Lirran clergy especially, who maintain a tradition of asking awkward questions about divine motivation â€” might privately note the gap; few would say so openly.
