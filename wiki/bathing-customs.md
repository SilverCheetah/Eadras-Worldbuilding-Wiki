# Bathing Customs of Eadras

**Summary**: Cross-cultural compendium of bathing and cleanliness practices across the major peoples of Eadras. The communal-bathhouse tradition is **a Velrathi invention** that propagated downstream through the [[rathari|Rathari]] and the [[beast-folk|Beast-Folk]], producing a recognizable family of practices that share a structural core (magical purification + auto-scrub stations + communal soak) while differing dramatically in tone and discipline.

**Sources**: Extra large grok chat 5-9-2026.txt

**Last updated**: 2026-05-09

## The Velrathi origin of communal bathing

The communal-bathhouse tradition is **a Velrathi invention**. Early in their building-up of urban infrastructure, the [[velrathi|Velrathi]] discovered that ordinary communal bathing spread disease catastrophically — water in shared pools accumulated whatever every bather brought into it, and large communities suffered from outbreaks they could trace directly to the bath.

Their solution was characteristically Velrathi: they engineered the problem out of existence with magic. Two innovations:

1. **Magical purification of all water in the building.** Permanent enchantments treat every drop of water entering or already inside a Velrathi bathhouse, rendering it incapable of carrying disease. This is not a once-applied charm but a continuously-maintained property of the building's water-system.
2. **Mandatory auto-scrub cubicles.** Before any bather is allowed into the communal soaking pools, they pass through a small individual cubicle that magically subjects them to water, soap, and a brisk **automated scrubbing**. The cubicle does the cleaning work for them; the bather steps in dirty and steps out clean. Only after this protocol is the bather permitted into the shared pools.

The two innovations together turned communal bathing from a public-health hazard into a social institution. Modern Velrathi treat the daily bath as **an hour-long social opportunity** as much as a hygienic one — the soak-pool is where intellectual gossip is exchanged, alliances are formed, and minor disputes are mediated. Bathing is not a chore. It is a feature of well-organized Velrathi life.

This is the structural ancestor of every other communal-bath tradition in the wider downstream cultural family.

## How it propagated

### [[rathari|Rathari]]

The Rathari inherited the full Velrathi pattern — magical water purification, auto-scrub cubicles, communal soak — and brought it into their own underground cities. **In structure**, Rathari bathhouses look essentially identical to Velrathi ones.

In **practice**, however, the Rathari lean strongly toward the auto-scrub side of the system and away from the social soak. Most Rathari treat the bath as **hygiene, not socializing**. They go through the auto-scrub, and then most of them simply leave. The communal pools see use, but far less than in Velrathi culture.

This is partly cultural temperament (the Rathari are more inwardly-focused than the Velrathi) and partly schedule — the typical Rathari scholar is so deeply invested in their work that the social hour the Velrathi pencil in feels like a distraction. A few Rathari subcultures preserve the soak as a serious daily practice, but they are the minority.

A more notable Rathari quirk: **the most absent-minded scholars sometimes forget to bathe entirely**, going days or even weeks without setting foot in the bathhouse. When this becomes obvious, **family members or close colleagues are expected to physically intervene** — to walk into the scholar's office, observe the situation, and say some variant of *"You smell like a tomb. Bath. Now."* Junior Rathari learn early that being on the receiving end of this intervention is mortifying; it is therefore a common social pressure that prevents most scholars from going entirely feral.

### [[beast-folk|Beast-Folk]]

The Beast-Folk inherited the **communal core** of the tradition and dropped the formality. Beast-Folk communal bathing is:

- Loud
- Joyful
- Emotionally open
- Often chaotic
- A primary venue for inter-Circle bonding, child play, courtship, gossip, and laughter

A magical tub is conjured at every settlement, even temporary ones — the working is part of standard Beast-Folk camp craft and any Beast-Folk adult of moderate magical talent can produce it. **An elder is always present** to keep some semblance of order in the chaos: not to enforce silence (silence has no place in a Beast-Folk bath) but to watch for the children, intervene when play-fighting is about to become real fighting, prevent injuries, and gently restrain over-rambunctious behavior.

The auto-scrub elements of the Velrathi tradition have largely disappeared from Beast-Folk practice — the Beast-Folk are not concerned about disease in the same way the Velrathi were, and the bath is treated more as a community ritual than as a hygiene-engineering problem.

#### The wagon-pool

Beast-Folk who travel as part of larger mixed-race caravans carry **a magical pop-up communal pool** on a single wagon. The pool is:

- Sealed at one end to a **portal connecting to a warm region of the [[eadras-cosmology|Plane of Water]]**
- Compact when stowed; commands open it to full-sized configuration in the field
- About **3 feet deep** when fully open
- Fills at approximately **1 foot per minute** (a full-pool fill takes about three minutes)
- Drains at the same rate, with the water returning to the Plane of Water slightly dirtier than when it came

Every Beast-Folk wagon in a caravan carries one of these pools. Not every Beast-Folk has a wagon — many travel as part of someone else's caravan rather than running their own — but those who have wagons consider the pool a non-negotiable part of the kit. It is, in effect, the **portable cultural embassy** that lets Beast-Folk in any caravan keep a piece of home with them.

#### Open invitation

The Beast-Folk are generous with the bath. **Caravan companions of any race are openly invited** to use the wagon-pool, unless a particular companion has insulted the Beast-Folk and not apologized — in which case they are quietly not invited.

But the Beast-Folk are also **quick to forgive**. A sincere apology is genuinely forgotten; the bath is open again the next night. Insult-and-recovery dynamics through the bath are a small but real component of mixed-caravan social life.

### [[orden|Orden]]

The Orden have their own distinct tradition that does not descend from the Velrathi pattern. Each Orden village maintains a **massive cast-iron communal hot-tub** — a single enormous pot, large enough for many adults at once, that doubles as **the heaviest object in the village**.

The pot is:

- Cast as a single piece of iron, in molds capable of producing what is essentially a dwarven-scale soup-cauldron sized up to bath dimensions. (This is genuinely an engineering marvel and represents one of the village's largest collective investments.)
- Heated over a controlled **fire** beneath it
- Filled with water (or, in winter, snow) and brought to a high temperature
- **Cooled as needed** with cool water or fresh snow until the temperature is bearable for everyone
- Used by the whole village in rotation — there is no separate household tub culture

#### Double duty as a child-shelter

The cast-iron pot has a second function in Orden life that other peoples find startling but the Orden consider perfectly sensible: in the event of a sudden attack on the village, **the children are dropped into the empty (or hastily emptied) tub, a heavy iron lid is dropped over the top, and the pot becomes a makeshift bunker.** The combination of the iron's mass, the curve of the pot's wall, and the lid's weight make the inside genuinely defensible — children inside are protected from arrows, stray spear-thrusts, and most casual attacks by raiders not equipped to deal specifically with the pot.

This is why the village considers it the heaviest thing they own. It is not merely a bath; it is also a piece of village infrastructure. The bath function is the daily-use case; the shelter function is the rare-but-load-bearing case.

### [[gnomes|Gnomes]]

Gnome attitudes to bathing are blunt: they do not particularly care.

A typical gnome bathes **once or twice a week** and considers that perfectly adequate. They are not anti-bathing — they don't *like* being dirty in a really objectionable way, and they will wash hands and faces casually as needed — but they consider the question of being squeaky-clean to be **fundamentally less interesting** than whatever invention or theoretical problem they're currently chewing on.

This produces a recognizable gnome-workshop atmosphere: dishes pile up, soap is several months old, the bath is sort of around but only thought about when something forces the issue. A gnome who has been working on a fresh problem for a week may notice their state and shrug.

There are exceptions — gnome diplomats, public-facing officials, and certain professional cooks all maintain higher standards as part of their work — but the cultural baseline is *eh, that's good enough*.

### [[goblins|Goblins]]

Goblins enjoy bathing **after work**. During work, they do not care: goblin work involves grease, machinery, dust, the inside of broken-down magitek, and other genuinely dirty things, and a goblin in the middle of a job is going to be filthy. Goblins do not consider this a problem and do not feel they smell offensively to themselves.

But once the work is done, they like a hot soak. A goblin who has spent the day deep in a workshop will sit in a tub for an hour afterward, scrub thoroughly, change into clean clothes, and pour themselves a small cup of [[goblin-ancestor-liquor|Ancestor Liquor]]. The end-of-day bath is its own small ritual.

The disconnect between work-mode and post-work-mode is one of the things that confuses other races about goblin life — outsiders see the dirty goblin in the workshop and assume goblin culture is filthy. The same goblins, two hours later, are clean and pleasantly dressed. Both states are legitimate; goblins simply don't waste energy on cleanliness during the part of the day when work will dirty them again immediately.

### [[elfen|Elfen]] and modern [[elves|elves]]

Elfen bathing customs are not yet documented in the wiki at the depth the downstream cultures are. The implication of the Velrathi-origin frame is that the Elfen had their own pre-existing nature-aligned bathing practices that did not conform to the urban-bathhouse pattern. (Open canon.)

### Bird-lineage [[beast-folk|Beast-Folk]]

The bird-bodied Beast-Folk love bathing. Bathing for them is closely tied to **preening** — a natural grooming behavior that water plus oiling enables in a way that dry-grooming alone does not. Bird-bodied Beast-Folk often have their own scented preening oils that they apply post-bath, and bathing-and-preening together is a serious social practice in their Circles. Pairs and small groups will often help each other with hard-to-reach areas.

### Amphibian-lineage [[beast-folk|Beast-Folk]]

The amphibian-bodied Beast-Folk are basically always wet. They live half-submerged in or adjacent to water as a matter of biology, and the question of *going to bathe* doesn't really apply to them in the same way it does to other peoples. Their cultural rituals around water are more likely to be about **drying out** — controlled periods of being out of water for skin health and ritual purposes — than about getting wet.

### Note on scaled humanoids

There are **no scaled [[beast-folk|Beast-Folk]]**. Scaled humanoids are not part of the [[rathari|Rathari]]-descended Beast-Folk family at all — they belong to the [[dragonfolk|dragon-created lineages]] (the [[lizardfolk]], [[kobolds]], [[dragonborn]], [[snakefolk]]) created by the dragons after the [[the-three|Three]]'s humanoid creation work. Any bathing customs they have come from the dragon-lineage tradition rather than from the Velrathi-Rathari-Beast-Folk family documented above.

## The structural family

The downstream tradition (Velrathi → Rathari → Beast-Folk) shares:

- A **communal-bathing core** — bathing is a group activity, not just a private one
- A **mandatory cleanliness protocol before the soak** — auto-scrub or its equivalent
- An **expectation that adults of the community will do this**, not a question of whether anyone bothers

What changes between branches is **tone, formality, and the degree to which the protocol survives intact**. The Velrathi maintain the full structure with social-hour disposition; the Rathari maintain the full structure with hygiene-only disposition; the Beast-Folk preserve the communal soak and lose most of the formal protocol.

The Orden, gnome, and goblin practices are **not part of this downstream family** — each developed independently and reflects its own cultural temperament rather than being a degraded version of the Velrathi original.

## Open canon

- The bathing customs of the [[tarans|Taran]], the [[katharim|Katharim]], the [[dwarves|Shada'din (Dwarves)]], the modern [[humans|humans]], the modern oceanic peoples (which is an interesting case given they live in water already), and the [[halflings|halflings]].

- Whether the [[gray-matriarchy|Gray Matriarchy]] preserves the Velrathi bathing tradition (since they are descended from exiled [[tarans|Tarans]] who would have known the imperial bathhouse system at the height of [[firethorn-empire|Firethorn]] cultural integration).
