# Philosophy of Contribution

## The shared core

Every culture in the [[velrathi|Velrathi]] â†’ [[grugi|Grug]] â†’ [[goblins|Goblin]] descent line believes that **every capable person must contribute to society**, and **freeloading is not tolerated** in any form. Each culture works the principle out differently, but the underlying value is the same.

It is **not extremist**. All three cultures recognize **sensible exceptions**:

- **Injured and ill** people are expected to recover, not to keep producing through the recovery period
- **Disabled** people are expected to do work appropriate to their abilities, with the community finding work that suits â€” or, when no appropriate work exists, supporting them outright
- **Elderly** members of the community are honored and supported, not pressured to keep producing past their reasonable working years
- **Children** are expected to grow up, not to work as adults

What is **not** tolerated is the able-bodied, capable adult who can contribute and chooses not to. *If you are perfectly capable of working and choose not to, there will be consequences.* The consequences vary by culture (social pressure for the Velrathi, more severe sanction for the Goblins) but the principle is consistent.

This shared value distinguishes the descent line from many other Eadran cultures. The [[elfen|Elfen]], the [[tarans|Taran]], and most modern races have their own work ethics, but the specific *every capable person contributes / freeloading is not tolerated / sensible exceptions for inability* synthesis is recognizably Velrathi-Grug-Goblin in structure.

## [[velrathi|Velrathi]]: contribute meaningfully

In Velrathi culture, contribution is **not** specifically physical labor. The Velrathi were and are a scholarly civilization; their concept of meaningful contribution covers:

- **Intellectual contribution** â€” research, scholarship, archive-keeping, magical theorization, philosophical work, mathematics, history, all the work that produces and preserves knowledge.
- **Artistic contribution** â€” the arts the Velrathi cultivate (architectural design, formal music, verse forms, the visual arts of stone-and-spell-lattice ornamentation) all qualify.
- **Skilled labor** â€” craft work, magical practice, civic administration, teaching, other professional labor.

A Velrathi doing any of these is contributing in the way the culture expects. **Physical labor is not required** of every Velrathi â€” the [[grugi|Grug]] were created precisely to handle the physical labor that the empire's magitek infrastructure required, and Velrathi citizens are not expected to duplicate Grug work themselves.

But the Velrathi are deeply intolerant of citizens who **contribute nothing**. A Velrathi who does no scholarly work, no artistic work, no skilled labor, and no civic role becomes an object of social discomfort and is gently but persistently pressured back into productive activity. The pressure rarely escalates to formal sanction â€” the social discomfort itself is usually enough â€” but Velrathi who refuse to engage will eventually find themselves quietly excluded from circles of consequence.

The Velrathi pragmatic exception is fully respected. A Velrathi scholar laid up by illness for months is fed, cared for, and welcomed back when they recover; a Velrathi artist whose work is genuinely inaccessible to the broader public still receives full membership in society as long as the work is real. The philosophy is anti-freeloader, not anti-anyone-who-isn't-currently-producing.

## [[grugi|Grug]]: physical labor as cultural foundation

The [[grugi|Grug]] take the principle further. They were **created** by the Velrathi specifically to perform the heavy physical labor and engineering work that the empire's vast magical infrastructure required â€” they were the maintainers of the Velrathi machines, the workers in the deep tunnels, the engineers who kept the magitek running.

Because labor was their **literal purpose** for existing, the Grug developed a culture in which **physical work is fundamental respect**. A Grug who shies from hard work is failing at the most basic level of being Grug. A Grug who keeps doing hard work into old age is honored; a Grug who carries a difficult job to completion is praised; a Grug who solves a stubborn engineering problem with sustained effort is admired across the wider community.

What distinguishes Grug culture from a pure work-ethic is that even **high-ranking** Grug â€” chiefs, master engineers, senior council members â€” are expected to **regularly work with their hands**. Leadership does not exempt one from labor. A Grug authority figure who never picks up a tool is suspect. The expectation is structural, not just rhetorical: senior Grug are expected to spend real working hours alongside the rank and file, not as ceremony but as ordinary continuing work.

This produces the recognizably Grug texture: a culture that **respects effort over status**, where authority is held by people who continue to do the work themselves, and where idle command is considered a corruption of proper Grug governance.

The Grug pragmatic exceptions match the broader pattern â€” injured, ill, disabled, elderly Grug are supported and given work appropriate to ability. But within the able-bodied population, the cultural pressure to **stay in the labor** is strong.

## [[goblins|Goblins]]: radical egalitarianism

The Goblins, descended from the [[grugi|Grug]] (alongside their sibling people the [[gnomes|gnomes]]; see [[lost-primer]]), inherited the Grug's deep respect for physical labor and **pushed it further than any other culture on Eadras**.

In Goblin society, **nobody is exempt from manual labor**. The labor roster is regularly published â€” typically every week or so â€” and **everyone** is on it. **Kings, archmages, high priests, master merchants, ordinary workers** â€” all of them, when their name comes up on the roster, show up for their shift and do the work alongside everyone else. There is no separate noble track. There is no "I'm too important for this." There is only the rotation.

This is **not** ceremonial. Goblin work-gangs do real work â€” engineering jobs, magitek maintenance, infrastructure repair, mining, hauling, all the essential labor that keeps Goblin cities running. The king does not get a special light shift; he gets the same work as the goblin standing next to him.

### The underlying philosophy

The cultural reasoning is direct:

- **Everyone is a goblin first.** Whatever role you have, your fundamental identity is goblin. The role is just a current job description, not who you are.
- **Different people just have different responsibilities.** A king is a goblin who has been entrusted with the job of making decisions for the whole people. A wizard is a goblin who has been entrusted with the job of doing magical work that requires study. A common laborer is a goblin who has been entrusted with the job of doing the work that doesn't require those specializations. **None of these makes one goblin better than another.** They just have different responsibilities.
- **A king worked to get where he is.** Goblin society is a strict **meritocracy**. Kings are not born; they are chosen for the role from the broader population based on demonstrated capability. Status is **earned and provisional**, never inherent and never permanent. A goblin who stops earning their position will eventually be replaced.

This is the philosophy that makes the work-gang rotation possible. If the king were *intrinsically* better than the common goblin, putting them on the same shift would be absurd. Because the king is just a goblin with a different responsibility â€” and one who earned the responsibility through demonstrated work â€” putting them on the same shift is **the most natural thing in the world**.

### How outsiders see this

Goblins find the rigid hereditary hierarchies of other races mildly **ridiculous**. The idea that a person could be born into authority â€” and be unable to lose it through inadequate performance, and never be expected to do real work alongside the people they govern â€” strikes Goblins as a kind of polite madness. They are usually too pragmatic to make a public point of this, but in private goblin commentary on, say, the [[firethorn-empire|Firethorn Empire's]] aristocratic structure or modern human nobility is often dryly amused.

### Sensible exceptions

The Goblin pragmatic exceptions are the same as the broader pattern. Injured, ill, elderly, and disabled goblins are not on the work-gang rotation; the community supports them and finds them work appropriate to their abilities. The principle is rigorously applied to the able-bodied, not enforced cruelly against people who cannot work.

## How the philosophy propagated to the [[gnomes|gnomes]]

The [[gnomes|Gnomes]], the Goblins' sibling people in the descent from the [[grugi|Grug]] (see [[lost-primer]]), inherited a softer version of the same philosophy. Gnomes do **not** put kings on work-gang rotation â€” gnome civic governance is not as strictly egalitarian as goblin governance â€” but they do strongly believe that **everyone should be doing meaningful work**, that **freeloading is not tolerated**, and that **even high-status gnomes are expected to keep their hands in their craft** rather than dropping into pure administration.

The gnome variant is closer to the Velrathi position than to the goblin one. Gnome contribution is heavily intellectual and creative (the inventor culture, the gnome love of complex problems), but the underlying anti-freeloader axis is recognizably the same.

## Open canon

- The full enforcement structure of the goblin work-gang rotation. The principle is canonical; the procedural detail (who maintains the roster, what happens if someone refuses a shift, how shifts are scheduled around specialized work) is open.
- Whether the **[[gnomes|gnomes]]'s** version of the philosophy survived intact in their modern civilization or has degraded under the gnomish vault-archaeology obsession (which is so culturally strong it sometimes overrides other values).
- Whether **mixed gnome-goblin cities** in the modern era (where they exist) blend the two work-philosophies, alternate them, or maintain separate work-gang systems for the two populations.
- Whether the **[[katharim|Katharim]]** (descended from the Rathar, who are descended from the Velrathi) preserve any version of the philosophy of contribution, or whether the Rathar/Katharim path inherited a different facet of Velrathi culture entirely.
