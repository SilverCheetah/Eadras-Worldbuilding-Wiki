# Druids

**Summary**: Druids are a specialized sub-order of [[priests|priesthood]] who serve the three [[gods-of-eadras|Elder Gods]] most closely tied to the natural and fey worlds â€” [[qoxli|Qoxli]], [[sylph|Sylph]], and [[nox|Nox]] â€” and who are expected, beyond the ordinary obligations of priesthood, to be **highly self-sufficient** and competent in practical wilderness skills. They serve as guardians of the natural world and as mediators between mortal societies and the [[fae|fae realms]].

## Status as a sub-priesthood

Druids are not a separate magical order. They are a specialization within [[priests|priesthood]] â€” clergy who happen to serve the three Elder Gods whose domains overlap on the natural and fey side of Eadras's cosmology. The three patrons are:

- **[[qoxli|Qoxli]]** â€” Elder Goddess of the wild, animals, the bow, forests, freedom, and the chase.
- **[[sylph|Sylph]]** â€” Elder Goddess of foolishness, indulgence, and independence; patron of all [[fae|Fae]].
- **[[nox|Nox]]** â€” Elder God of night, secrets, and the loathsome; the second great fey patron.

These three are not a single coordinated trinity â€” Qoxli and Nox are at active odds (Qoxli's *strong dislike* of Nox is canonical), and Sylph's relationship with both varies by individual question â€” but their domains overlap enough that a single practitioner can plausibly serve any combination of them.

A druid **chooses** which of the three to follow. The choice is explicit and may be:

- **One** of the three. The druid serves a single patron and orients their entire practice around that deity. This is the most common pattern. A Qoxli-druid is a wild-and-hunt-aligned mediator; a Sylph-druid leans fae and indulgence; a Nox-druid leans secret-and-shadow. Each single-patron druid acknowledges the others as appropriate to circumstance, but their primary discipline and Favor track runs through the one god they chose.
- **Two** of the three. A meaningful and recognized pattern. A Qoxli + Sylph druid is wild-fae nature-and-mischief â€” a common combination given the two goddesses' close friendship. A Sylph + Nox druid is a fey-and-secret hybrid â€” less common but not rare. A Qoxli + Nox druid is unusual given the two deities' active hostility, and the few druids who walk it tend to be specifically engaged in mediating *between* the two domains, often as part of the broader [[#Mediators between mortal and fey|mediation function]] of the role.
- **All three.** Tri-patron druids exist and are particularly suited to mediation work, negotiating among the three patrons in their daily practice. Tri-patron druids are correspondingly hard to predict from outside, since their actions in any given moment can be drawing on any of three different domains.

The choice is made during the druid's initial training and is rarely changed afterward, though traditions exist for **adding** a patron to an existing practice (the new patron must accept the addition; the existing patron must consent to share). **Removing** a patron is theologically tricky and typically requires formal renunciation through the leaving deity's priesthood; it is uncommon enough that most druid orders treat it as a once-in-a-career event when it happens at all.

## Druidic Favor

**Druids are unique among divine casters in that they may serve up to three deities simultaneously.** Rather than maintaining three separate Favor tracks, druids have a **single unified score called Druidic Favor.**

Druidic Favor represents how well the druid is **maintaining the balance** between the gods they serve. It fluctuates based on their actions, decisions, and ability to honor the interests and domains of all chosen patrons without unduly favoring one over the others. When a druid's actions promote natural balance and harmony between their patrons' domains, their Druidic Favor rises. When a druid neglects one deity's domain, shows clear favoritism, or disrupts the balance between the three, their Druidic Favor falls.

This single unified Favor score reflects the fundamental nature of druidism: **the maintenance of balance between multiple divine forces.**

For a single-patron druid, this is functionally identical to an ordinary priest's Favor with their one god â€” there is no balance to maintain because only one set of interests exists. For two-patron and tri-patron druids, Druidic Favor is genuinely harder to keep high: every action that serves one patron must be weighed against the others, and the [[priests#The Divine Bureaucracy|Divine Bureaucracy]] adjusts the unified score based on whether the overall balance is being kept. This is the structural reason multi-patron druids are rarer than single-patron ones.

(For the splat-side mechanical formula `WIS + Druidic Favor + (level Ã— 2)`, see [[rpg/priest-mechanics#Druidic Favor (druid-specific case)]].)

### The nature of Druidic Favor

Druidic Favor is both **mechanical and deeply philosophical**. Unlike priests who serve a single deity, a druid is expected to act as a **living embodiment of balance** between their patron gods. The Divine Bureaucracy judges them not on devotion to any one god, but on how well they uphold the equilibrium between all chosen patrons.

**High Druidic Favor** is earned when a druid consistently demonstrates **wisdom, restraint, and fairness** between the domains of their gods. A druid who skillfully mediates between conflicting natural forces, protects all aspects of nature equally, or finds elegant solutions that satisfy multiple gods will see their Favor rise. Such druids are often **respected â€” and sometimes feared â€” by other clergy**, because the discipline they have mastered is harder than ordinary single-deity devotion and produces a kind of practical authority that single-patron priests cannot quite match.

**Low Druidic Favor** occurs when a druid becomes **unbalanced** â€” showing clear favoritism toward one god, neglecting another's domain, or making decisions that disrupt the natural harmony between their patrons. Severe or prolonged imbalance can cause Favor to drop sharply, limiting the druid's magical power and (in the worst cases) cutting them off from divine channeling entirely until the imbalance is corrected.

**The gods themselves rarely interfere directly with a druid's Favor.** They generally allow the [[priests#The Divine Bureaucracy|Divine Bureaucracy]] to manage it, only stepping in during truly exceptional circumstances. Many druids believe this distance is intentional â€” that the gods want their druids to **learn balance through trial and experience** rather than constant divine guidance. A druid corrected by the gods directly is, in this reading, a druid who has failed at the very practice their order was created for; the gods would rather a druid struggle and recover than be corrected and never learn.

This philosophical framing produces the recognizable Druidic temperament: thoughtful, slow to judge, suspicious of single-source moral certainty, attentive to the consequences of any decision across multiple competing concerns. A druid who picks a side too quickly is doing the wrong job; a druid who endlessly equivocates is doing the wrong job too. The right job is *upholding the balance* â€” and the discipline of doing it well is the discipline that produces high Druidic Favor.

## Druidic magic in the broader priesthood

Because druids are priests, the magic they wield is **divine magic**. They call upon their patrons to act through them, the same way every other priest does. The Divine Language used in their invocations belongs to the gods, not to the druid; the druid speaks the [[true-language|tier-three Arcane formula]] of priestly invocation, and the god responds.

What distinguishes druids from other priests is **what they do with the access**, not how they got it.

## The self-sufficiency expectation

Where most priests of urban-domain gods spend their lives in temples, courts, and city ministries, druids are expected to be **highly self-sufficient**. The expectation comes from the patrons themselves â€” Qoxli's wild, Sylph's preference for the fae who do not live in cities, and Nox's preference for places nobody else watches.

A trained druid is competent in:

- **Wilderness survival** â€” making camp, finding food and water, weathering storms, navigating without roads, recognizing hazards.
- **Herbalism** â€” identifying useful plants, preparing tinctures, recognizing toxic species, knowing what to grow and where.
- **Animal handling** â€” moving safely among large wild animals, working with semi-tame livestock, recognizing animal communication, avoiding predator territories.
- **Practical nature magic** â€” small workings of healing, weather-reading, seed-quickening, beast-calming, and similar, performed without need for a temple or extensive ritual setup.

The self-sufficiency is **not** an alternative power source. It is an expectation about competence: a druid who needs to be looked after by the temple administration is, by their patrons' standards, failing at the role.

## Mediators between mortal and fey

The druids' most distinctive function is **mediation between mortal societies and the [[fae|fae realms]]**.

The fae are not extinct, retreated, or background â€” they are real, present, and locally dangerous wherever wild magic is dense. They are also, by [[sylph|Sylph]]'s patronage, a peoples in their own right, not "monsters" to be cleared. When a settlement and a fae court need to negotiate (over land use, grievance, marriage compact, ritual obligation, or any of the other things fae and mortals occasionally need to settle), the druid is the one who can speak in both directions without insulting either.

This makes druids unusually political within their settlements. A village druid is the person sent when a child wanders into the wrong grove and the [[fae|fey]] decline to send her back without recompense. A town druid handles seasonal compacts about which forest paths are safe to cut, which springs not to dam, which animals not to hunt this year. A regional druid manages the running treaties with the larger fae courts and with the Sylph-touched [[beast-folk|Beast-Folk]] [[flyting|Laughing Circles]] when their territory abuts mortal civilization.

Druidic mediation is also an inter-divine working. Druids are often called on to mediate between followers of [[qoxli|Qoxli]], [[sylph|Sylph]], or [[nox|Nox]] who have disputes that other priests cannot adjudicate without bias. The druid's tri-patron orientation makes them structurally suited to it.

## Distinction from the previous canonical Druid

The druids in their previous canonical form were a fourth standalone magical order, parallel to wizards / priests / Netharim, who drew on a different magical substrate at considerable risk. That arrangement was a [[inspirations|borrowing]] from another author's work, and was retired in the 2026-05-09 ingest (see [[log]]).

Under the current canon, druids are not a fourth magical order; the four orders are [[wizards]], [[priests]], [[lexanari|Lexanari]], and [[netharim|Netharim]]. Druids are a specialized form of priesthood within the second of those four. The role and the title persist; the magical mechanics underneath the role have been folded into ordinary priesthood with the patrons listed above.

The previous canon's druidic concept of "tapping The All directly" is no longer the druidic mechanism. The All â€” the leftover creation energy of [[primordial-duo|Evye and Fryr]] â€” remains canonical as the deep reservoir that surfaces as the [[magic-in-eadras|Source/Eldritch/Mana]] family of states; it is not, however, a substrate that mortal druids draw on directly. *(See [[magic-in-eadras]] for the substrate-state model and the [[magic-in-eadras|Note-and-defer]] banner about the larger rework.)*

## Open canon

- Whether [[beast-folk|Beast-Folk]] communities recognize their own [[flyting|Laughing Circle]] elders as performing a function structurally identical to druid mediation, even when the elders never adopt the druid title.
