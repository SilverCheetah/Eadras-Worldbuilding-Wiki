# Demons

**Summary**: Demons are an invasive alien race from a different [[mythos|Mythos]] — known in the Concordance tongue as **Nethar** or **Vorath**, a Mythos whose other planes its own demons have already consumed, leaving only their hellish Jupiter-like Prime. Demons are the **ecological endpoint of a collapsed Mythos**: survival stripped of every balancing structure except predation. They are energivores whose unbounded mutability, magical inventiveness, and inability to be permanently destroyed make them an existential threat to the omniverse, and whose continued permitted existence by the [[omniversal-creator|Omniversal Creator]] is the greatest mystery in creation.

**Sources**: More corrections.txt, Playground generation 2nd part.txt, Commentary Ingest — Demon War, Chaos Crusades, Demons, and Aberrations.txt, gods-hate-demons-2026-05-11.txt

## Origin

Demons come from an entirely different [[mythos|Mythos]] — a reality so alien to ours that **their very existence is poisonous to this Mythos**.

### The demon Mythos: Nethar / Vorath

The demon Mythos's true name in the demonic language is **unpronounceable by humanoid mouths** — there is no clean way to render it. In the [[concordance|Concordance]] tongue, the cross-Mythos lingua franca, the names that have been recorded for the Mythos are **Nethar** or **Vorath** (used roughly interchangeably by different Concordance scholars). Eadras texts default to *Nethar* in formal writing and *Vorath* in older sources. (source: More corrections.txt)

### Just the Prime Material — the ecological endpoint of a collapsed Mythos

The demon Mythos is in a **terminal state**. It once had the full structure of a normal Mythos — multiple planes mirrored across positive and negative axes, planar reflections, the usual cosmological architecture. The demons **ate all other planes**. Only their Prime Material remains. Whatever celestial, infernal, fey, shadow, or transitional planes once existed in Nethar / Vorath have been consumed by their own occupants over deep time, leaving the Prime as the last surviving region.

This is what the omniverse contains demonkind to: a Mythos that is itself food, in which the predators have already finished everything except themselves and each other and the surface they stand on. They have no fey realm to retreat to, no celestial homeworld watching over them, no negative-plane storage of their dead. The Prime is all that remains, and it is what produces them.

In the Tome's philosophical synthesis, demons are not *evil incarnate*, not *Chaos entities*, not *moral monsters*, and not *infernal punishers*. They are **survival stripped of every balancing structure except predation** — the ecological endpoint of a Mythos that has consumed itself. Their civilization (such as it is) is *post-metaphysical-collapse, ecologically exhausted, structurally cannibalized*. The harshness of the Prime they live on is what shaped them into the energivores, extreme adaptive predators, and radically mutable entities they are. (source: Commentary Ingest — Demon War, Chaos Crusades, Demons, and Aberrations.txt)

### The Prime they live on

Their Prime Material is **Jupiter-like and hellish**: a realm of never-ending electrical storms, crushing atmospheric pressure, toxic clouds, and violent chaos. The conditions there are so extreme that no being from Eadras's reality could survive there for even a single moment. (source: More corrections.txt)

Conversely, when demons enter Eadras, they bring something of that toxicity with them — and Eadras is hostile to their existence in ways that the larger [[mythos|Mythos]] system mostly contains, but never fully neutralizes.

## Physiology and abilities

### Energivores — ontological predation

Demons are **energivores**. They do not eat food. They consume **raw energy** — mana, life force, raw matter, magical structure, anything that contains energy. Whatever has energy in it is potential demon-fuel. This relationship to existence is best framed as **ontological predation**: demons are not predators of bodies, they are predators of the *substance* underlying coherent existence.

The more a demon consumes, the **larger, stronger, and more intelligent** it becomes. This is not a metaphor or a slow growth: a demon that has fed for a century is fundamentally a different order of being than the one it was a century before.

- **Young demons** may appear as insubstantial shadows — barely there, easily dispersed, weak.
- **Mature demons** take coherent monstrous forms with definite bodies.
- **Ancient demons** — those that have fed for centuries — can grow to **colossal size**, sprouting dozens of limbs, thick armored plates, razor-sharp bone weapons, or even completely new organs designed specifically for killing whatever they have learned to kill.

### Mutable bodies — accelerated ontological adaptation

Demon bodies are **incredibly mutable**, but this is not ordinary shapeshifting — it is **accelerated, survival-driven ontological adaptation**. A demon confronted with armor, magic, environmental threat, or a specific enemy type may spontaneously develop new organs, weapons, defensive systems, or magical adaptations within the engagement itself. Anatomy that worked yesterday may be replaced today by anatomy designed against last night's enemy.

This mutability makes them difficult to categorize, difficult to permanently counter, and terrifying to rigid magical systems.

### Spell invention — deforming magical assumptions

Demons are also capable of **spontaneously creating new spells**, often twisting the laws of magic in ways that **native mages cannot replicate or even comprehend**. The defining characteristic is not that they learn local spellcraft and improvise on it — it is that they **deform magical assumptions dynamically**. They do not study the local magical system; they bend the system around their predation as they encounter it.

This makes them especially dangerous to the [[order-sharanel|Order Sharanel]] and other formal magical institutions, whose strength lies in disciplined, repeatable spellcraft. Against demons, repeatable spellcraft is a known quantity that the demon has already adapted past. The thematic implication, much-quoted in modern Sharaneli teaching: **rigid magical systems are vulnerable to adaptive ontological improvisation**. This is why the Order's relationship with [[beast-folk|Beast-Folk]] improvisation, [[sylph|Sylphic]] flexibility, and [[windspeakers|Windspeaker]] adaptive technique runs deeper than one might expect from a formal academy of Netharim practice — the rigid form's defenders need the unrigid forms' habits.

## Threat level

Demons can be **killed** in battle, and they can be **banished**. Neither produces permanent destruction:

- **Killing a demon** in this Mythos appears to destroy it locally, but the rules governing what "destruction" means for a being whose substance is alien to the Mythos are not fully understood. Many killed demons stay killed; some return.
- **Banishment** does not destroy the demon at all — it sends them back to their home Mythos, **from which they can eventually return**. There is no banishment that is final.

For these reasons, **many scholars consider demons to be an existential threat to the entire omniverse** — not because any one demon is worldending, but because the species as a whole cannot be permanently removed from the omniverse by any known means.

## The Great Mystery

> *Why does the [[omniversal-creator|Omniversal Creator]] permit these beings to exist at all?*

This is openly called **the greatest mystery in creation**. Theological and philosophical responses range from:

- **The Growth Hypothesis** — some scholars speculate that demons exist to drive growth and advancement: that civilizations, species, and gods only develop the capacities they need by being pressed against threats they cannot eliminate. Demons are the irreducible pressure.
- **The Failed Mythos Hypothesis** — that the demon Mythos is a creation that went wrong, and the Omniversal Creator does not destroy failed creations.
- **The Test Hypothesis** — that the omniverse is, in some sense, *for* demonstrating who and what survives the demonic.
- **The Refusal-to-Speculate Position** — common among Lirran clergy and the [[doctrine-of-unheld-hand|Doctrine of the Unheld Hand]] — that the Creator's reasons are not knowable to creatures within creation, and asking is not piety.

(source: More corrections.txt)

## Eadras's encounters with demons

Demons have entered Eadras most prominently through the [[demon-war|Demon War]], which began when [[xarvion|Xar'vion]] summoned the [[demon-prince-of-the-open-wound|Demon Prince of the Open Wound]] — itself an ancient, immensely-fed demon — using true-name summoning. The War ended with the Prince's defeat and the closing of major demonic incursion, but **demons have never been entirely eliminated from Eadras**.

In modern Eadras, demonic influence has been **almost entirely removed** following the War's conclusion. The exceptions are deliberate: the [[black-hand|Black Hand]] maintains a small cadre of Warlocks (with demon pacts) and Magic Users (who summon demons by true-name) for selective deployment. See [[black-hand]].

## Why the gods hate them

The [[gods-of-eadras|gods]] hate demons with an intensity that mortal cosmologies tend to read as moral horror or cosmic righteousness. The actual motive — well-documented in inner-pantheon traditions if not in mortal religious texts — is **petty**.

Demons disrupted the [[gods-of-eadras#The Game of the Gods|Game of the Gods]], the addictive board-game of mortal-piece-management that occupies most of the divine pantheon's attention; the [[demon-war|Demon War]] forced the gods to leave the Game and intervene directly, which further disrupted it. The hatred is rooted in this grudge — not in horror at mortal suffering, which the gods are generally unmoved by, but in fury that their entertainment was ruined and they were dragged off the board to fix it.

See [[gods-of-eadras#Why the gods hate demons]] for the full treatment.

The practical consequence for mortal-facing doctrine is that godly hostility to demons is real and intense, and is preached as such. Whether the doctrine accurately represents the *reason* is a question theologians of certain reflective sects (Lirran clergy especially) have noted but never resolved openly.

## Open canon

- Whether **Nethar** and **Vorath** are merely two Concordance approximations of the same name, or whether they distinguish the Mythos at different points in its history (e.g., before and after the consumption of its other planes)
- The original planar structure of Nethar/Vorath before the demons consumed it — what was the demonic equivalent of Celestia? Of Nyxar? When did each fall?
- Whether demons have any internal social structure, or are purely individualistic
- Whether the Omniversal Creator has ever spoken to the question of why demons are permitted
- Whether other Mythoi are also invaded by demons (i.e., is Eadras's demon problem a local affliction, or universal?)
