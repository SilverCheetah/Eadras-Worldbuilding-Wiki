HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT
================================================================

Part I extracts setting-wide PRINCIPLES this material surfaces or reinforces. Part II is LIVE WIRES — open story prompts, productive ambiguities, hooks the council set up or generated by accident. The contradiction-flag taxonomy applies as in the companion files but is used sparingly here.


================================================================
PART I — SETTING-WIDE PRINCIPLES
================================================================

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 1 — GODS CAN COMMIT CRIMES.

  Ixthal's Forbidden Kindling is a divine being violating divine law and getting away with it for ~8 million years. This is the most theologically aggressive single fact in the Eadras pantheon. The Elder Gods believe dragons and unicorns arose naturally. They are wrong. The agency they think was distributed in natural Source surges was actually concentrated in one god's amplification. The Doctrine of the Unheld Hand has a hole punched in it that nobody knows is there.

  Generalization: divine law in Eadras is REAL but ENFORCEABLE ONLY WHEN VISIBLE. A god who commits a violation in a manner indistinguishable from natural phenomena, never speaks of it, and accepts the strategic cost of permanent silence can in principle persist within the pantheon while violating its codes. This is a setting where divine law is not omniscient — it requires perception and inference like mortal law, and can be defeated by sufficient concealment.

  Story consequence: characters who learn of the Forbidden Kindling face a structural problem. Telling the Elder Gods would destabilize divine law, dragon religion, unicorn self-understanding, and mortal faith in cosmic order. Keeping the secret makes them complicit in Ixthal's crime. There may be no clean response. This is the kind of moral problem the setting prefers — unstable, asymmetric, and resistant to heroism.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 2 — RACES INHERIT TRAITS FROM THE OUTSIDE OF THEIR OWN NATURE.

  Dragons are individualistic apex predators. They built a city because they learned cooperation from unicorns. The Council of 99 is a dragon institution that runs on a unicorn-given premise: cooperation without domination or hierarchy. Ratharil is, structurally, "what dragons did with unicorn instinct."

  Unicorns are herd-mind collectives. Some of them now walk as individuals. The walk-the-Dragon-Path tradition exists because dragons taught them individuality. Far-Wanderers, Border-Friends, and Lead-Minds are unicorn social roles that owe their structure to the Great Exchange.

  Generalization: SAPIENT SPECIES IN EADRAS REACH THEIR MATURE FORMS BY ACQUIRING TRAITS FROM OTHER SPECIES. Each species' most civilized achievements are inheritances, not native inventions. The setting rewards reading civilizations as cultural genome — what they got from whom and when.

  Story consequence: a character who studies one race's history will eventually encounter another race in the lineage. The setting does not have racial purity at the level of culture. The closest things to "pure" cultures are the FAILURE MODES — the Flesh-Eaters who reject everything dragons learned from unicorns, the early pre-Exchange unicorn herds that rejected individuality as sickness. Cultural purity in Eadras correlates with cultural regression.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 3 — SILENCE CAN BE STRATEGIC.

  Ixthal's silence is the deepest example: he is fully capable of speech and chooses concealment. Dragons honor him with the Court of the Unanswered Sun. Unicorns remember him only as sensation, not as a named god. The Sealed Hall of First Flame in Ratharil holds the truth incompletely.

  Generalization: information control is a divine art in Eadras, not just a mortal political tool. Gods conceal. Truths are preserved deliberately incompletely. The Tome of Baldaran has sealed chapters. The Three Last Lights' deepest theological implications are not openly preached. Even the Elder Gods are operating with limited information.

  Story consequence: scholars and inquisitors in Eadras face an information landscape where institutional silence is not always evidence of absence. Things are NOT WRITTEN DOWN because someone decided they should not be. A research character who finds the boundaries of recorded knowledge in this setting is finding traces of curatorial choice, not gaps in the record.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 4 — INSTITUTIONS ARE CORRECTIONS TO INSTINCT.

  Ratharil exists because dragon-instinct alone produces the War-Recovery-Stability-Overcrowding-War cycle. The Treaty of Ratharil and the Council of 99 are corrections to the dragons' own nature. The 999,999 population cap is not natural to dragons; it is law layered over biology to prevent biology from destroying itself.

  Generalization: civilization in Eadras emerges WHERE SPECIES LEARN TO ACT AGAINST THEIR DEFAULT TENDENCIES. Dragons against territorial pride. Unicorns against herd-mind absorption. Later humanoid civilizations against their own equivalent default failures. The setting takes a pragmatic view of civilization: not as natural flowering but as deliberate restraint.

  Story consequence: societies in Eadras are most fragile at the points where they correct their own instincts. The dragon Council can be undermined by appeals to dragon pride. Unicorn herds can be unsettled by reverting to undeliberated Chorus reactions. The Flesh-Eaters appeal to "true dragon nature" — they are advocating for the cycle Ratharil was built to break. Pre-civilization is always one cultural collapse away.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 5 — THE PRECURSORS OF MORTAL HISTORY ARE NOT MORTAL.

  Dragons emerged 8 mybp. Unicorns 6 mybp. Humanoid races emerged ~39 kybp. The first 99% of post-Cataclysm sapient history happened without humanoid involvement. By the time humanoids appear, the Compact of Horn and Flame is ancient, Ratharil is established, the Council of 99 has been governing for a long time, two Chaos Crusades have been repelled, and dragon-unicorn culture is mature.

  Generalization: HUMANOID HISTORY IS A LATE LAYER ON TOP OF DEEP NON-HUMANOID HISTORY. Most of what shaped Eadras predates humans, elves, dwarves, gnomes, orcs, halflings, goliaths, and beast-folk by orders of magnitude. The world humanoid civilizations inherit is one built by precursor species.

  Story consequence: this is a setting where mortal characters routinely encounter older agencies that pre-date their species. A dragon mercenary in a tavern has a worldview shaped by 8 million years of dragon-unicorn cultural exchange. A unicorn encountered in the wild may be a Far-Wanderer who has been walking individual paths longer than human civilization has existed. Mortal characters are juniors. The setting expects them to be.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 6 — DESIGN IS HIDDEN INSIDE BIOLOGY.

  Dragon age-deference is an Ixthalic engineering feature — divine regulation built into species psychology to prevent first-generation extinction. Unicorn herd-mind is similarly engineered (the species was "born collective," not assembled into collectivity over generations). Both species carry their creator's design choices in their default behaviors.

  Generalization: SPECIES IN EADRAS REVEAL THEIR DESIGNERS THROUGH PSYCHOLOGICAL DEFAULTS. The instincts a species cannot help following are fingerprints of the intelligence that shaped them. This applies to The Three's humanoid races, to Ixthal's dragons and unicorns, and to anyone else who has been quietly shaping species into existence.

  Story consequence: a scholar who studies enough species behaviors carefully could in principle trace divine engineering. The age-deference instinct is one such trace for Ixthal. Other species likely have their own fingerprints. The Forbidden Kindling could theoretically be exposed not through finding records but through inferring designers from designed behavior. This is a more difficult investigation than finding documents and a more dangerous one to succeed at.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 7 — THE SETTING FAVORS DELIBERATE OVER REACTIVE.

  The Chorus is preferred over the herd-mind in motion for important decisions. The Council of 99 is preferred over individual dragon pride. The Compact of Horn and Flame is preferred over post-war hatred. The Great Exchange is preferred over the assumption that dragons and unicorns must remain other to each other. Across this material, the council consistently rewards characters and species who can act DELIBERATELY rather than REACTIVELY.

  Generalization: this is a CONTEMPLATIVE setting. The crafts the setting rewards are pause, deliberation, restraint, careful study, patient cooperation. The failure mode the setting punishes is impulse — Kudur-eṭir reaching uncontrollably, Tarloth taking the Spear shortcut, the Flesh-Eaters reverting to immediate appetite, dragon clans entering the War-Recovery cycle.

  Story consequence: heroes who act fast and decisively can succeed at tactical scale in Eadras stories, but the strategic logic of the setting favors the patient. The most successful divine actor in the topic (Ixthal) is the one who waited two million years between flares and has stayed silent for the eight million years since. The most successful institutional actor (Ratharil) emerged from generations of accumulated restraint. Heroism in Eadras is more often a long discipline than a single brave act.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 8 — CULTURAL EXCHANGE IS THE ENGINE OF MAGICAL DEVELOPMENT.

  Dragons did not develop psionics on their own. Unicorns did not develop personal-power Eldritch magic on their own. Each species acquired a major new branch of magic through the Great Exchange. The setting's magic systems are not species-bound; they cross-pollinate through cultural contact.

  Generalization: NEW MAGIC IN EADRAS USUALLY APPEARS THROUGH TRANSMISSION, NOT INDEPENDENT DISCOVERY. The Weave was a divine intervention transmitted to mortals (not invented by them). Crystalmancy was transmitted from Arathi survivors through their material legacy. Necromancy is described in this material as "archaeology with moral consequences" — knowledge inherited from the dead.

  Story consequence: a character who wants to learn a new branch of magic must find someone to learn it FROM, more often than they must invent it. The setting has a strong preference for apprenticeship and pedagogy as magical-learning pathways. Self-taught geniuses in Eadras tend to end badly (Kudur-eṭir is the archetypal example).

PART II — LIVE WIRES
================================================================

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 1 — DOES IXTHAL EVER GET CAUGHT?

  The wiki's load-bearing fact is that Ixthal has not been caught. He still holds his place in the divine pantheon. His silence still works. But the wiki phrasing — "Ixthal has not been caught" — implies tense: someone could catch him. The story space of an investigation into the Forbidden Kindling is wide open. Who would investigate? Lirra (most able to recognize Source-flare manipulation)? Vos (most procedurally inclined toward divine law)? Nox (most attuned to secrets)? An ascended mortal scholar whose research crosses too close to the truth?

  This is one of the highest-stakes investigations the setting allows. The investigator's success would destabilize four institutional structures. Their failure might mean their own death or divine binding.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 2 — THE SIX UNNAMED EMBERS, AGAIN.

  Same live wire as Topic 1, now seen from the dragon side. The six dragon-gods who withdrew are present in draconic esoteric lore and the Sealed Hall of First Flame but absent from mortal religion. Their relationship to mortal affairs is undefined. Their relationship to IXTHAL is also undefined — do they know what he is? They are Source-born from his first flare. They may have intuitions about their origin that mortal dragons do not.

  Story scaffolding: a quiet sect of dragons devoted to one of the Six who claims to receive subtle guidance about the species' true history. The Sealed Hall of First Flame would presumably contain partial evidence. A young dragon admitted to the Hall and reading too carefully could be the protagonist of an Ixthal-uncovering story.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 3 — THE FLESH-EATERS' DOCTRINAL CACHES.

  The Flesh-Eaters survive their own destruction because of buried doctrinal caches that allow the cult to be reborn from a single finder. This is an unusual cult-survival mechanic. The wiki phrase is "Always reborn." Where are the caches? How many exist? Who would know? Are there caches the Council of 99 has destroyed and others that remain hidden? Is there a finite number, eventually exhaustible, or are new ones being made?

  This is a setting-internal counter-organization with a renewable mechanic. Stories that involve cleaning up Flesh-Eater cells need to address whether the cleanup is permanent or just suppression. A character whose career is hunting Flesh-Eater caches is structurally available.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 4 — THE CHORUS DURING THE GREAT EXCHANGE.

  The Chorus made the cold-calculated decision to fight the Unicorn Plains War. What did it say about the Great Exchange? The bridge-individual unicorns who first stepped outside to talk to young dragons were doing something the Chorus might have FORBIDDEN. Either the Chorus debated and approved the exchange, or the bridge-individuals acted against the Chorus's deliberation and were tolerated rather than punished.

  Either path opens story space. If the Chorus approved, then it has been governing through Walking-the-Dragon-Path tolerance for a very long time and may have institutional sub-modes that handle individuals. If the Chorus did NOT approve, then there is a long history of Far-Wanderers acting outside Chorus authority — and the present-day Chorus may have a fraught relationship with its own dissidents.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 5 — PURPLES AND FAYE.

  Purple dragons were "deeply influenced in early history by the Elder Goddess known in draconic tradition as Faye. Theologians may identify Faye with Sylph under an archaic draconic name, though dragons often insist their own name is older and more accurate."

  This is open canon. Purple draconic theology may preserve aspects of Sylph that mainstream pantheon worship lost. The wiki does not have a dedicated article on Faye as a Purple-specific name (as far as I have not yet checked). A Purple scholar character is structurally available — chaotic, unpredictable, with a theological argument that they have the older name and the mainstream pantheon has the corruption.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 6 — DRAGON CHILD RACES AND THE INHERITANCE QUESTION.

  Dragonborn legalists' proverb: "Made by the flame does not mean owned by the fire." This implies an active legal/cultural debate among dragon child races about whether they owe anything to dragons. The wiki canon mentions that some honor dragons, some resent them, some deny being "children." Status as of present-day Eadras: undefined.

  Story scaffolding: a Dragonborn or Lizardfolk character whose civic identity centers on this proverb. A legal proceeding in which a dragon claims hereditary authority over a kobold settlement and the kobolds counter-argue from independence. The wiki frames the child races as DRAGONS CREATED IN RESPONSE TO HUMANOID RACES — meaning they are themselves a kind of imitation of The Three's humanoid creation. Their independent status is therefore overdetermined.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 7 — THE STILL-LIVING SIX UNICORN... WAIT, THERE ARE THREE UNICORN GODS.

  Wiki canonizes three ascended unicorn gods: Starwyn (Prophecy), Faylune (Mischief and Laughter), Valenor (Truth). The playground did not develop unicorn theology beyond Ixthal as Silent Brightness. Starwyn-of-Prophecy is theologically interesting: a unicorn god of prophecy is a successor to Ixthal's pattern-sight gift. Did Starwyn inherit foresight, ascend through demonstrating it, or develop it independently?

  The playground left unicorn theology entirely open. The wiki canonized three gods but did not fully develop them. Stories that center on unicorn religion have substantial open space.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 8 — THE FIRST MEADOW AS UNICORN-DRAGON SHARED SACRED SPACE.

  The First Meadow may exist as an original physical location "now protected by both unicorns and dragons together." This is unusual — most sacred sites in Eadras are species-specific. A jointly-defended sacred space implies an ongoing institutional cooperation between unicorns and Council-of-99 dragons that the wiki does not detail.

  Story scaffolding: a pilgrimage that requires permissions from BOTH the Chorus and the Council. A character who has been granted access by one and rejected by the other. An attempt to reach the First Meadow that triggers active defense from both species.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 9 — THE COURT OF THE UNANSWERED SUN AS PUZZLE.

  The court occasionally produces a faint Source resonance, never on command. What does the resonance MEAN? A faint signal from a god who refuses to speak is theologically rich. Is it acknowledgement? Apology? Random radiation from divine attention partially turned? Recognition of a specific dragon's specific grief?

  The setting deliberately leaves this open. Story-wise, the court is a place where attentive characters might receive a private signal from a god who otherwise will not respond. Each instance could be different in meaning. The court is functioning as a low-bandwidth divine channel for the very few who can read it.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 10 — RHTRAKHADARA AND DRACONIC PRONUNCIATION.

  Draconic carries metaphysical weight when spoken with true intent and can subtly influence the Fire. Mortals find correct pronunciation extremely difficult. Even master Weavespinners only approximate. This implies a hard ceiling on mortal-spoken Draconic magic: nobody can match the dragons' own resonance.

  Story scaffolding: a mortal mage who spends a career on the Draconic-pronunciation problem. A dragon mentor who is willing to teach but cannot transmit what is biologically impossible for the student to produce. A breakthrough that costs the student their humanoid speech ability. This is the linguistic version of "trying to be a dragon" — a path with structural limits the setting has built in.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 11 — THE 999,999 LIMIT AS METAPHYSICAL CONTAINMENT.

  Wiki adds: "this number may also serve as a metaphysical containment limit: too many Source-born dragons may disturb ley systems and the Weave." This is HYPOTHETICAL in canon ("may also"). If true, exceeding the limit could damage the Weave itself. The Council of 99 may know this. The Flesh-Eaters might not care, or might consider it a feature.

  Story scaffolding: a Council inquiry into dragon population approaching the limit. A Flesh-Eater plot to overload the count specifically to harm the Weave. A dragon birth that pushes the count to exactly 999,999 and the political stakes that surround the next death. The number has fewer than seven digits of slack between routine population and metaphysical danger; the system runs close to its limit.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 12 — IXTHAL'S MORAL POSITION AS PROTAGONIST-VOICE.

  The wiki preserves his private conviction: "The law forbade interference. But the law also permitted a world where only gods remembered speech." His defining possible statement: "There were no mortals. I changed that." His philosophical objection to the Doctrine of the Unheld Hand: before mortals exist, whose destiny is being protected? Noninterference can become divine abandonment of the very possibility of mortal history.

  This is a sympathetic case. Ixthal's argument is not obviously wrong. The Elder Gods would reject it because creating races determines destiny at the grandest scale, but the argument has force. A story that puts a character in dialogue with the case for the Forbidden Kindling (knowing or not knowing it was committed) is structurally available. Ixthal is the most morally sophisticated criminal in the pantheon, and his crime produced ~8 million years of mortal life.

  This live wire pairs with Principle 1. The setting wants you to think hard about whether the divine law Ixthal broke was the right law. It does not give a clean answer.
