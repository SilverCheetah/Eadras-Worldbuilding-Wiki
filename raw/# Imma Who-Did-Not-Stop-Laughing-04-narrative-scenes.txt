## Scene 1: The Cup in the Niche

The small office is quiet. The day's flyt-blessings have been signed; the ledger sits closed. The goddess Imma Who-Did-Not-Stop-Laughing rises from her desk and walks to the niche on the south wall — a small carved hollow set at standing height, lined with a square of dark cloth, holding a single silk-wrapped clay cup.

She does not unwrap it. She knows what is inside. A plain clay vessel, fired in a Brella caravan's small wagon-side kiln nearly a century ago, with her mother's thumbprint preserved on the rim from the day the clay was wet.

The cup is the load-bearing anchor of who she is — the small physical detail that holds the self-perception that holds the form. Without the cup in the niche, the form might shift. With the cup, the goddess is still Brella's daughter.

She rests one hand on the silk. *"I will be back, little one,"* she says, to nothing in particular. The lantern overhead glows softly. The office is full of the small assembly of cups she has gathered from her past soul-lives across the months since the Tome began curating: a half-bowl for the Keeper, a wooden cup for the Decree-keepers, a glass treaty-vial empty and faintly clouded with a Velrathi trace. The desk is becoming populated.

She returns to her seat. The next blessing has already begun to arrive — a flyting in a goblin tunnel, three witnesses on the floor laughing. She signs it. Somewhere far below, in a cave the goddess has never visited, the Weave warms by one quiet thread.

## Scene 2: The Corridor Nod

It is a divine-morning in Solarion. The light through the gold-domed central district's stained windows falls in long shafts across the divine main street. Imma walks her usual route from the small wagon-style office at the corridor's quiet end toward the central bureaus where the day's cross-pantheon work-traffic is processed.

Three doors before her own building, she pauses very briefly outside the Office of Velrathi Affairs.

Velveth — the Velrathi goddess of love, betrayal, and duty — stands in her own open doorway, holding a tea-cup in her gray hand. The cup is Clarity Thorn; the goddess's red eyes glow in the formal Velrathi-pale way. She is dressed in the dark layered robes that have not changed in ten thousand years. The cup in her hand is the same kind of cup the Velrathi treaty-mages use for sealed witnessing.

The two goddesses look up at each other for half a heartbeat.

Imma gives the precise Velrathi-precision nod — a nod she had no reason to know how to give until the night the Tome of Baldaran showed her a soul-life named Meleth Vaethari-of-the-Crimson-Spire, who drafted a treaty long ago in a vertical chamber stained with crimson morning light.

Velveth returns the nod. There is a fraction of additional warmth in it that no spirit-clerk passing in the corridor would notice.

Neither goddess speaks. Neither will ever speak of it. The Decree of Shali, Shani, and Ioa binds them both, and the silence is not a burden but a kind of intimate working language.

Imma continues down the corridor. The bureaus await. The day's flyt-blessings will be signed with the same hand she has always used. Velveth raises her teacup, takes a small sip, and turns back to her own desk.

The whole exchange has taken less time than a breath.

## Scene 3: The Porch in the Morning

A small wagon-style office, with a beast-folk wagon-wheel sigil set into the wood beside the door. A porch with a single bench. On the railing: a loaf of dark bread and a bowl of clean water, set out as if anyone passing might be hungry.

For the first time in the long span of her tenure, Imma Who-Did-Not-Stop-Laughing sits on the porch in the divine-morning. She does not have a working agenda. She is simply *present*. The ledger inside can wait an hour.

The corridor is mostly quiet. A junior celestial-spirit clerk passes with an armful of prayer-scrolls bound for the Bureau of Souls. The clerk glances up at the wagon-wheel sigil, notices Imma on the porch, and stops in mid-stride. The clerk is androgynous, pale-glowing, distinctly nervous in the way most celestial-spirit juniors are when an Ascended Mortal looks at them directly.

Imma smiles, a small fox-folk smile that the spirit-clerk has never seen before in this corridor. She gestures, just slightly, at the loaf. *"Some bread for the road?"*

The clerk hesitates. The bread is real. The bowl is real. The goddess is real. The clerk has never been invited to anything by any office on this corridor in two hundred years of running prayer-scrolls.

The clerk takes a small piece of the loaf — barely a bite. Bows once. Continues down the corridor toward the Bureau.

Imma watches the clerk go. *"That is what the loaf is for,"* she says, to no one in particular. The Weave is one thread warmer.

Down the corridor, three doors away, Velveth in her own office hears the very faint chime of a porch-loaf-being-shared. She does not look up from her work. But she smiles.

## Scene 4: The Tome Reads Aloud

The Tome of Baldaran lies open on the desk in Imma's office. It has appeared, today, as a great brown-leather book — three feet long, twenty inches wide, ten inches tall, bound in rich brown leather with intricate filigree and softly glowing crystals. It is not the form a mortal would see; the form is calibrated for an Ascended Mortal Goddess who has earned the privilege of consultation.

The page glows faintly gold. The ink swirls.

*"You are strong enough to bear this truth,"* the ink writes, slowly, in a hand that is the Keeper's-own and not Baldaran's.

Imma is sitting very still. Her hands are flat on the desk. The cups arranged around her — Brella's, the Keeper's half-bowl, the Decree-keepers' wooden cup, the glass treaty-vial empty-and-clouded with Meleth's trace — sit in their places. The office is quiet.

*"Chaos unmakes souls as easily as any other real thing,"* the ink continues. *"It did so in the Crusades. The decree came during the Arathi Cataclysm."*

The goddess does not flinch. The Keeper, watching from somewhere in the pocket-dimension where the Tome resides outside of time, has paced the revelations carefully across the long evening. The goddess is steady. She has been steady all night.

But the trembling — the tiny trembling she had thought she had stilled — has returned in her hands.

The Keeper notices. The ink swirls more gently. *"I will pause if you wish."*

Imma breathes out. *"No,"* she says aloud, to the empty office. *"Continue. I am ready."*

The Tome continues. Somewhere down the corridor, Velveth in her own office puts down her teacup very quietly, in a gesture only the Tome perceives.

## Scene 5: The Vision of Raj at the Crossing

The morning is clear and cold. A wagon rolls down a long slope toward a river-crossing in alder-and-birch country. The river is high — the wet-stone smell of spring-snowmelt has been hanging in the air since dawn.

The wagon is small by mortal standards but large by Brella standards: two floors, the upper one opened on the south side to a writing-slope, the lower one packed with rosewood-spice cargo and the folded magical wagon-pool rig that every beast-folk Caravaneer carries as the cultural infrastructure of his people.

On the driver's bench sits an old otter-folk male, white at the whiskers, with a long-healed scar across the back of his left hand. He hums to himself, a tune that has been with him for thirty years — a tune he never learned from anyone, that he is not aware was given to him by a cousin named Tenneth who died twenty-three winters ago.

The wagon rolls down toward the ferry. Old Bren the ferryman shouts an overcharge-price from the far bank. The otter-folk Caravaneer waves back, smiling — he will let himself be overcharged today, and he will overcharge a Bold merchant in the next town to make it back, and the road's small economy will balance one more season.

He does not know that, far in his soul's future, the goddess who his soul will eventually become is resting attention on him from a small office in Solarion. He does not know that the warm amused quiet pride he occasionally feels when he is cataloguing his hoard or humming his dead cousin's tune is *not* Sylph, however much it has Sylph's shape.

He knows only that the road is his, the wagon is his, the poem is in his head and on the writing-slope, the cart-oxen are good lads, the wrist clicks, and the river will be high, and the ferryman will overcharge.

*He is happy.*

It is a Tuesday morning in late spring in a year no mortal calendar would later remember, and the goddess who will one day be his soul's resting form sits in a small office on a divine main street and *carries his happiness back into the Weave*, where the laughter he is about to give to old Bren on the far bank will warm one quiet thread.
