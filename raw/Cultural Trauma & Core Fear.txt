Beastfolk: Cultural Trauma & Core Fear
The Beastfolk were once Rathari — scholarly, sober, stern, and emotionally reserved. The horrors they endured during the Demon War were so devastating that the survivors came to despise everything about their former lives as Rathari.
They rejected their entire previous identity.
In their desperation, the first generation prayed to the goddesses to take it all away — their memories, their identities, their entire existence as Rathari. The goddesses answered. These Rathari willingly sacrificed every memory of who they had been, and the goddesses physically transformed them into the first Beastfolk.
Modern Beastfolk are not merely afraid of remembering the Demon War.
They are terrified of remembering who they used to be.
Their wild joy, emotional intensity, and passionate revelry are not frivolity. They are a desperate, ongoing rejection of their entire previous existence. To become quiet, reserved, or scholarly again would feel like the first step toward becoming the very people they sacrificed everything to escape.

Orcs: Cultural Trauma & Core Fear
The deepest wound in Orcish cultural memory is the Battle of Three Bloods during the Demon War.
In that battle, three entire progenitor races — the Orug, the Aetherin, and the El'mer — were completely wiped out. The Orug formed the central shield wall and held the line until the very end, waiting for reinforcements that never arrived.
This event still lives in the bones of every Orc. Their intense honor codes, their refusal to break oaths, and their deep cultural terror of abandonment all trace back to this moment — when their ancestors were left to die while the rest of the world failed to show up in time.
To fail your kin when they need you is not merely shameful to an Orc.
It is the one sin they will never forgive.

Humans: Cultural Trauma
Humans generally carry very little cultural trauma from the Demon War itself.
To most humans, the war is ancient history. The name “Aetherin” is either unknown or treated as dusty legend. Ten thousand years is simply too long for most humans to feel any real emotional connection to what was lost.
However, humans do carry deep cultural trauma from more recent human wars. Their scars tend to be generational and personal rather than mythic.

Gnomes: Cultural Trauma & Core Fear
The deepest trauma in Gnomish culture is the discovery of the Arathi civilization.
When the Gnomes first uncovered the ruins and records of the Arathi, they realized they were looking at what they considered a near-perfect civilization — a society that had achieved a level of magical and technological harmony they could only dream of.
This discovery filled them with both wonder and profound grief.
They could study the Arathi. They could learn from their techniques and reverse-engineer some of their devices. But they could never truly know them. They had missed the living civilization by ten million years.
That unreachable knowledge became a wound that never fully healed.
To this day, Gnomes are obsessively driven to recover every scrap of lost Arathi knowledge. Every recovered device is both a triumph and a painful reminder of what they can never fully have.
Their greatest cultural fear is that there will always be some truth, some beauty, some perfection just beyond their reach.

Goblins: Cultural Trauma & Core Fear
The deepest trauma in Goblin culture is the Great Scattering.
After the Velrathi were exiled following the Demon War, the Grug, Gnomes, and Goblins suddenly found themselves without purpose. The great machines they had maintained for centuries no longer needed tending. The structured society they had belonged to had vanished.
The three groups split apart and went their separate ways. For the Goblins, this loss of purpose was devastating. In a single generation, they went from being an essential part of something greater to being rootless and purposeless.
This event left a permanent mark on the Goblin soul.
Their greatest cultural fear is being cast out — of being exiled, abandoned, or told they no longer have a place or a purpose. To a Goblin, there is almost nothing more terrifying than being rejected by their own people.
This is why Goblin society, despite its chaotic reputation, is fiercely loyal and tightly knit. Having a role — any role — is sacred to them.

Katharim: Cultural Trauma & Core Fear
The Katharim are the descendants of Rathari who were so deeply scarred by the horrors of the Demon War that they completely reinvented themselves.
They turned away from arcane magic, embraced divine magic, swore an oath of absolute pacifism, and dedicated their lives to the dead. What began as a desperate vow to never forget the victims of the Demon War gradually expanded until the Katharim became the undertakers and memorialists of the entire world.
They remember every name. They tend every grave. They ensure that no soul is forgotten.
Though they do not know it, the Katharim serve a vital metaphysical role. In Eadras, to name something is to reinforce its existence. By preserving and speaking the names of the dead across millennia, the Katharim unknowingly strengthen the very fabric of reality itself.
Their greatest cultural fear is forgetting.
To the Katharim, allowing the dead to be forgotten is not merely disrespectful — it is a violation of the fundamental order of existence. They believe that if the dead are forgotten, the living will inevitably repeat the sins that led to the Demon War.
