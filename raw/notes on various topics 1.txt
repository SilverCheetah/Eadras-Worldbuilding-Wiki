Orcs
Orcs are a hardy, nomadic people primarily inhabiting the vast grasslands beyond the southern mountain ranges. They are descended from one of the three original humanoid races created by the sibling gods. Orc society is split between plains orcs, who follow great herds across the steppe, and city orcs, who live as master artisans and traders in humanoid cities across the known world, including among humans, elves, and dwarves.
Despite the distance, the two groups maintain strong cultural unity through a long-standing tradition of exchanging adolescents for several years so that each generation learns both the nomadic and settled ways of life.
Orc culture is defined by practicality, mastery, and personal honor. They value skill above almost everything else and channel their competitive nature into constant contests. Nearly every craft, trade, and martial discipline becomes a competition. Grandmasters — those who have proven themselves supremely skilled — retire from competition to become teachers, advisors, and judges.
Each clan maintains a permanent clan-hold, usually built in hills or mountainous terrain so the open plains remain reserved for grazing herds. Most orcs spend the majority of the year following the herds. Leadership is strictly merit-based. Potential chieftains undergo years of testing and apprenticeship. A wise chieftain steps down when they are no longer capable, and the designated assistant takes over. The elders and warriors together can remove a failing chieftain in extreme cases.
The high point of orc life is the Great Gathering, held every three years. Nearly every member of every clan attends, leaving only a small guard behind. It is a massive event of trade, diplomacy, matchmaking, skill demonstrations, and above all, competition in every conceivable discipline.
Orcs are fearsome warriors when their clans are threatened, but they are not a conquering people. Their honor is rooted in self-mastery, protecting the clan, and proving superiority through skill rather than domination.

Dwarves
Dwarves are descendants of the Mitans, the small-statured sub-race engineered by the Terrans (one of the three original races) for intricate work. They are a dual-natured people with a clear cultural divide between work and play. When working, dwarves are dead serious, focused, and professional. When relaxing, they are loud, colorful, joyful, and unrestrained.
Dwarven society runs on shifts because they operate 24-hour factories producing gnomish inventions. This constant cycle means there is almost always a group of dwarves either coming off work and ready to party or preparing to start their shift.
In “relaxing” mode, dwarves wear bright silks, satins, and brocades — clothing that would seem very un-dwarven to outsiders — and throw massive, raucous parties filled with drinking, feasting, music, dancing, and games.
Dwarves maintain a strong, mutually beneficial relationship with gnomes, whom they genuinely enjoy having around. They handle the mass manufacturing of gnomish designs while the gnomes focus on research and invention.

Gnomes
Gnomes are brilliant inventors and scholars who prefer not to run their own cities or societies. They live among other races (especially dwarves) so they can devote their time to research, invention, and archaeological exploration of Precursor ruins.
Most gnomish technology is not invented from scratch. It is inspired by the study of Precursor artifacts from the ancient civilization destroyed by the Cataclysm. Gnomes analyze the principles behind the old technology and create working modern versions using current magic and materials.
They maintain a close partnership with dwarves, who mass-produce their inventions. Gnomish sales teams travel the world with magical catalog crystals that project holographic images of products and teleportation boxes built into carts. Orders and payment are sent back to the dwarven factories, and goods appear almost instantly.

The Spear of Crystallized Entropy
The Spear of Crystallized Entropy is a single crystalline spear with a dark stain running through it. It is an extremely dangerous sentient artifact with a low level of awareness and a desire to be found.
It was created by a mad archmage of the Precursor civilization who sought to freeze entropy itself. The attempt shattered his mind. He discovered that every 5,000 years, on a specific day, the spear can channel power from the primordial “All” — leftover god-power from creation — and turn its wielder into an unrestricted elder god.
The spear carries a terrible curse: the first time a person touches it, it removes their original motivation for seeking it.
The spear has been used four times in the last ten million years:

By the mad archmage, who used it to call down the Worldbreaker asteroid that caused the Cataclysm.
By an ancient dragon who sought godhood after her mate ascended.
By a unicorn (details still developing).
By a human (details still developing).

Each time it is used, it begins to slowly corrupt the surrounding area or plane.

The Fourth Hiding Place
The elder gods’ current (fourth) hiding place for the Spear is one of the most heavily defended locations in the world. It sits in the middle of what would be the Pacific Ocean on Earth.
The defenses include:

A permanent ring of storms created by cold, dry air meeting warm, wet air.
A wide magic-dead zone.
A powerful outward-blowing wind.
A magical shell of unimaginable strength that requires a specific key to pass. Even with the key, it blinds non-magical people completely and temporarily blinds magical users (who can counterspell it).
A hidden society of Rathari guardians living inside.
The Spear itself is inside a volcano, protected by a great red worm that swims through lava as if it were water.
The final ward requires a Weavespinner, a priest, a druid, and a wizard working together to open.


The Rathari Guardians
The Rathari were a scholarly, magic-using child race of the Velerathi. A devout group of them, all worshipers of the goddess of magic, were chosen by the elder gods to guard the Spear after its second use.
They were sealed inside the fourth hiding place before the Demon War and have had no contact with the outside world since. They are now the last true Rathari in existence.
Their society has rotted over the millennia. Cut off from their goddess, they have fallen into decadence and debauchery, encouraged by their leader. The leader is one of the few who remembers what they are guarding. He has become delusional, believing himself to already be an elder god. He murders troublesome individuals and euphemistically calls it “putting them to sleep,” telling himself he will resurrect them once he gains ultimate power.
He is desperately trying to reach the Spear but is missing a druid needed for the final ward.
