## HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT

This document preserves a set of **recitable mythic texts** — the specific verbal forms in which the major peoples of Eadras remember their three foundational catastrophes ten thousand or two million years after the events. The catastrophes themselves are well-canonized in the wiki; what this document adds is the **specific in-world mythic register** in which bards, priests, and storytellers transmit those events to their audiences, plus a piece of **meta-craft canon** about how ancient peoples remember at all.

The texts are short by design. They are written to be **recited around a fire, declaimed in temple, or sung at a gathering**. Each text takes the voice of the people who tell it and reflects what *that people* most wants the catastrophe to teach. The texts diverge from each other and from the scholarly-historical record in instructive ways; preserving those divergences is the point.

Three things in this document carry `[CANONIZED]` status:

1. **The mythic-recitation texts themselves** — these are new canonical artifacts of in-world culture, written to be quoted as bard or priest recitation in story and game material.
2. **The cultural-perspective framing** — the rule that each of the three events has a *common* form, a *dragon* form, a *unicorn* form, a *humanoid* form, and (where applicable) a *scholarly Tome of Baldaran* form, and that these forms differ in characteristic ways that reflect each people's institutional voice.
3. **The meta-craft principle on myth-vs-history** — the load-bearing observation, surfaced in the source chat, that ancient peoples remember through *ritual recitation* and *story* rather than through accurate historical record, and that this rule must be applied across Eadran ancient history for the setting to maintain tonal weight.

One additional piece of `[CANONIZED]` material from the source chat — the **Spear's alien-artifact framing** as the reason the gods could not destroy it — is treated as authorial principle here and developed in full in the companion `spear-containment-diagnosis-and-the-alien-artifact-principle.md`. This document focuses on the mythic-recitation surface; the companion focuses on the cosmological underpinning.

Sections marked `[IN-WORLD]` are the recitations and the cultural framing in which they live on Eadras. Sections marked `[META]` are author craft notes on why these texts work and what they replace. The closing `LIVE WIRES` section enumerates open prompts.

## 1. THE META-CRAFT PRINCIPLE: MYTH IS HOW ANCIENTS REMEMBER

`[CANONIZED]` `[META]`

The framing observation that organizes this entire document is the principle that **ancient peoples do not remember through history; they remember through story and ritual recitation.**

In the source-chat conversation, this principle was applied to the specific puzzle of how anyone on modern Eadras could still know about Kudur-eṭir / Ssar'veth two million years after the Cataclysm he caused. The chat's response — *"They don't 'know' about him the way we know historical facts. Two million years is an incomprehensible amount of time. No one has records, books, or accurate history from that far back. What survives is myth"* — is the load-bearing claim.

The mechanism the principle describes:

- A foundational catastrophe (the Cataclysm, the Chaos Incursions, the Demon War) is *so large* that it leaves a permanent imprint on every culture that survives it.
- The imprint takes the form of **myth, ritual, ritual reenactment, and recitation** rather than detailed factual record. The specifics blur; the *moral shape* of the event endures.
- Names drift. Specific details change in transmission. The arc of the event — *one mortal reached too high, the heavens answered, the world broke* — survives across millennia even when no one alive can name the original participants.
- Different peoples remember the *same event* in characteristically different forms, each form expressing what that people most needs the event to teach.

The principle applies, in this document, to three specific historical episodes:

- **The Arathi Cataclysm** (~10 million years ago), known to almost everyone as the fall of *Ssar'veth*, the corrupted folk-form of the scholarly *Kudur-eṭir*.
- **The Chaos Crusades / Chaos Incursions** (the two great breaches into the Mythos), known to most as *the Wound* or *the Time the Sky Bled*.
- **The Demon War** (~10,000 years ago), known to most as *the War of the Still King* or simply *the Demon War*.

For each, this document records the mythic forms in which the peoples of Eadras transmit the event today. The texts are short, recitable, and culturally specific. They are the **actual words** a bard or priest would use; they are quotable in fiction or game material as the in-universe lore. The scholarly-historical canon — *Kudur-eṭir, the First Breach, the Worldbreaker as alien Falling Star, the Spear of Crystallized Entropy, the Total Icon Binding, Xar'vion as Velrathi archmage, the Black Hand* — lives in the wiki and is not the focus here. The focus here is what the **non-scholars say at the campfire and at the altar**.

The principle is also a craft instruction. Whenever a future Eadran historical event is developed and the question arises of how distant peoples or distant ages remember it, the answer should default to **mythic-recitation in characteristically-different cultural voices** rather than to historical-record-style summary. The wiki's scholarly-historical apparatus exists; in-world recitation should not duplicate it. (See §6 for further craft notes.)

## 2. THE FALL OF SSAR'VETH — THE CATACLYSM MYTHS

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The Arathi Cataclysm survives in the mythic memory of most peoples as **the Fall of Ssar'veth** — the story of a great mortal sorcerer-king who tried to make himself a god, succeeded, and was struck down by a falling star that broke the world.

The scholarly-historical version (held in the Tome of Baldaran and in a handful of ancient archives) preserves the original name — *Kudur-eṭir, "Seal-Opener"* — and the actual mechanism (uncontrolled deific reach pulling the Worldbreaker through the First Breach he had drilled; Total Icon Binding by the Elder Gods; Falling Star striking the Icon and annihilating him with Altea). For everyone outside the deepest scholarly circles, the name is *Ssar'veth* and the mechanism is *the gods dropped a star on him because he tried to become one of them*.

The mythic versions all share a **core structure** — the prideful king, the attempt at ascension, the falling star, the broken world — but each people's version emphasizes what their cultural sensibility most needs from the story.

### 2.1 The Core Myth — what almost everyone believes

> *In the Time Before, there was a being of immense pride called Ssar'veth. He was the greatest mage who ever lived, and he sought to become a god. On the day he succeeded, the heavens themselves rejected him. A star of wrath fell from the sky and struck his city, shattering the world. The seas rose, the earth split, and fire rained for a year. This is why the world is broken — because one man tried to steal godhood.*

This is the **common core** of the myth — the version told by every culture that has any version of the story at all. It is short, foundational, and didactic. *Pride invited cosmic punishment; the world is broken because of it.*

In actual use, this version functions as a **proverb-anchor**. People invoke "Ssar'veth" the way a real-world speaker invokes *Icarus* or *the Tower of Babel* — a single name that carries the whole moral. *"Don't be a Ssar'veth"* is recognizable across most of Eadras.

The longer culturally-specific versions follow.

### 2.2 The Fall of Ssar'veth — the standard bardic recitation

> *In the First Age, when the world was still young, there lived a sorcerer-king named Ssar'veth.*
> *He was the greatest mage who ever walked the earth. His pride was without limit.*
> *He looked upon the gods and said, "Why should I serve them, when I could become one?"*
> *On the day of his ascension, Ssar'veth reached up and tore open the sky itself. The gods were furious. They took a star from heaven and hurled it down upon him in wrath.*
> *The star struck his city of Altea and broke the world. The earth split open. The seas rose up and drowned the land. Fire fell from the sky for a year.*
> *The world has never been whole since that day.*
> *This is why we do not reach too high. This is why we do not challenge the gods. For when Ssar'veth tried to become their equal, the heavens answered with a star, and the world still bears the scar of his arrogance.*

This is the **canonical recitation form** — the version a bard would perform, the version a priest would invoke in sermon, the version that survives in fragmentary chronicles across many cultures. It is the longer-form expansion of the core, with the named city *Altea* preserved (the only city-name almost any cultural version remembers) and the prescriptive moral made explicit at the end.

`[CANONIZED]` — The name *Altea* survives in folk memory specifically because it is the named place struck. Modern Eadran cultures invoke "the fall of Altea" as a synonym for catastrophic divine judgment.

### 2.3 The Dragon Version — told with grim satisfaction

> *In the dawn of the world, a mortal named Ssar'veth grew so mighty and so proud that he believed himself the equal of the gods. On the day he tried to steal godhood, the heavens opened and a star of judgment fell upon him.*
> *The star struck his city and shattered the world. Mountains were thrown down, seas swallowed the land, and fire rained for a year. Thus did the sky itself rebuke his arrogance.*
> *Let every dragon remember the fate of Ssar'veth. Even the greatest mortal is still mortal. The heavens do not suffer equals.*

The dragon recitation reads the story as **cosmic order correcting a violation** — and the dragons are *pleased* by the correction.

The dragons' interpretive frame is that mortals have a station, and the station has limits, and Ssar'veth was a mortal who tried to climb past the limits, and the heavens responded as the heavens correctly should respond. The tone is *grim satisfaction*. The closing didactic line — *the heavens do not suffer equals* — is delivered with the implication that the dragons have their own seniority in the cosmic order and consider the lesson reinforcing of their own position.

This is the **canonical dragon voice** for catastrophic moral instruction. The voice carries over into the Dragon Empire's official mythic register (see companion `dragon-empire-far-eastern-meritocracy.md`) and is the same voice the dragons apply to other tales of overreach.

`[CANONIZED]` — The dragons' mythic register is *grim satisfaction*. This is now load-bearing canon for any future dragon-perspective myth in Eadran material.

### 2.4 The Unicorn Version — told with sorrow

> *Long ago, a being called Ssar'veth tore a wound in the world itself. He reached beyond the veil of what should be, and something answered that should never have been awakened.*
> *The thing that answered took the form of a star, and it fell upon his city. The world cracked open that day. The wound he made has never truly healed. Even now, the land still bleeds where the star fell.*
> *We unicorns remember. The scar remains. And some wounds do not close — they only wait.*

The unicorn recitation reads the story as **something genuinely alien intruding from outside reality** — and the unicorns *grieve* over it.

The unicorn interpretive frame is the closest of the mythic versions to the scholarly-historical truth (the Worldbreaker *was* an alien object pulled in from outside the Mythos), and the unicorns' eldritch sensitivity gives them a memory of the event that retains the wrongness of it. The unicorns do not see Ssar'veth as a properly-punished sinner; they see him as the *door* through which something outside the Mythos came in, and they see the door as still potentially open. *Some wounds do not close — they only wait.*

The tone is **mournful and watchful**. The unicorns are not satisfied by the resolution. They believe the event is not over.

`[CANONIZED]` — The unicorns' mythic register is *sorrowful, watchful, and prone to seeing intrusions from outside reality as the actual mechanism behind cosmic catastrophe*. This becomes load-bearing canon for unicorn-perspective material across Eadran history.

### 2.5 The Humanoid Version — the common widespread myth

> *In the ancient days there was a king named Ssar'veth, the greatest mage who ever lived. His pride grew so vast that he challenged the gods themselves, seeking to become one of them.*
> *The gods answered him with a star. It fell from heaven and struck his city, breaking the world in its fury. The oceans rose, the earth split apart, and fire fell from the sky for a year.*
> *This is why we do not reach too high. This is why we do not challenge what the gods have ordained. For when Ssar'veth tried to become more than mortal, the heavens dropped the sky upon his head, and the world still bears the wound of his folly.*

The humanoid recitation is closest to the canonical bardic form (§2.2) and is the **default version told across most humanoid cultures** — humans, half-elves, half-orcs, the various mixed lineages of the modern era, and most settled humanoid populations in the Azure Basin, the Orden, and the post-imperial successor states.

The interpretive frame is the **didactic one**: the story is told *to instruct the listener not to repeat the error*. The moral is loud, explicit, and repeated. *We do not reach too high.*

The humanoid version is also the version with the **least supernatural texture**. The dragons' version is grimly satisfied; the unicorns' version is hauntedly alien. The humanoid version is plain: *pride got him killed and broke the world; don't be him*.

This is the standard tavern, temple, and household version of the story. It is what a humanoid child first hears about the Cataclysm.

### 2.6 The Scholarly Version — Tome of Baldaran, Fragmentary History of the First Age

> *The being known in later legend as Ssar'veth was in truth an archmage of the Arathi civilization named Kudur-eṭir. On the day called God's Day, he achieved ascension through the use of a powerful and unstable artifact. Immediately thereafter, a celestial body of unknown origin — referred to in contemporary records only as "the Falling Star" — struck the Arathi capital of Altea.*
> *The impact triggered a global catastrophe of unprecedented scale. The continental mass known as the Altean Highlands was shattered, the world's crust was destabilized, and a prolonged period of firestorms, flooding, and volcanic activity followed. Approximately sixty percent of all life on the planet perished during this event.*
> *While popular tradition holds that the gods themselves cast down the star in judgment, more measured scholarship suggests that the arrival of the Falling Star was an unintended consequence of Kudur-eṭir's ascension. The precise mechanism remains unknown.*
> *The event is considered the definitive end of the First Civilization. All subsequent ages have measured time in relation to this catastrophe.*

This is the **scholarly version** — the form preserved in the Tome of Baldaran and in the deepest archives of the Order Sharanel and a handful of Velrathi exile scholars in Veyr'Keth.

It preserves the **original Arathi name** (*Kudur-eṭir*) and the **named ritual day** (*God's Day*). It admits the existence of the *artifact* (the Spear of Crystallized Entropy, though the Tome's open chapters do not name it explicitly). It admits the *alien* character of the Falling Star without endorsing the popular interpretation that the gods threw it. It is **measured, cautious, and incomplete** — the form of an institution that does not know everything but does know it does not know.

The Tome version is the closest to scholarly-historical truth among the surviving mythic-recitation forms, and is the version a player-character with sufficient scholarly contact (Order Sharanel access, deep Lirran temple access, certain Katharim archive access) can encounter in research scenes. It is *not* the popular version, and quoting it in a tavern would produce confused looks.

`[CANONIZED]` — The scholarly version is the form preserved in *Tome of Baldaran, Fragmentary History of the First Age*. The Tome's open-chapter scholarship is allowed to know that there was a name, a day, an artifact, and an alien Falling Star, but it is not allowed to know the Worldender Crystal mechanism, the First Breach geometry, the Spear's full role, or the post-event Spear-prison sequence; those remain sealed in the closed chapters and in author-canon.

## 3. THE CHAOS INCURSIONS — MYTHS OF THE BREACHES

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The two great Chaos Incursions (the *Chaos Crusades* in scholarly terminology, the *First Breach* and *Second Breach* in the dragon and unicorn traditions) survive in mythic memory as *the Wound*, *the Time the Sky Bled*, or *the Unraveling*.

The scholarly-historical reality is that the First Breach was the puncture Kudur-eṭir drilled through the Mythos Shell to call down the Worldbreaker, and the chaotic forces that subsequently came through that breach (during what was later known as the First Chaos Crusade) were both an effect of that breach being open and, per `[CANONIZED]` 5-11 craft canon, *amplified* by the gods' failed first attempt to destroy the Spear — the gods' raw divine power reflected by the alien Spear deflected directly into the Wound and worsened the Crusade significantly. (See companion `spear-containment-diagnosis-and-the-alien-artifact-principle.md` for the full mechanism.)

The mythic-recitation versions do not preserve these mechanics. They preserve the **shape** of the event: *the world was nearly devoured by something formless, the gods fought to seal it, the seal holds — for now*.

### 3.1 The Common Mortal Myth — "The First Wound"

> *Long ago, the world was nearly destroyed by a terrible darkness that poured through a hole in the sky. This darkness had no shape, no mind, and no mercy — it simply unmade everything it touched. The gods fought a desperate war against this horror for years until they finally sealed the wound.*
> *But the world was never the same. This is why there are still places where reality frays and strange things are born.*

The common mortal version is the **simplest and most widespread** form. It identifies the chaos as a *terrible darkness* (the actual Chaos is, in canon, more accurately *formlessness* than darkness, but mortal vocabulary defaults to darkness when describing the unimaginable), it identifies the gods as the actors who fought and sealed it, and it identifies the lingering consequence — that the seal is not perfect and that the world's modern *frayed places* and *strange births* (Aberrations) are downstream of that imperfect seal.

This is the version that explains the modern existence of Aberrations and reality-fray zones in folk understanding. It is the version told to children who ask why the marshes near a certain village are wrong.

### 3.2 The Dragon Myth — "The Time the Sky Bled"

> *When Ssar'veth tore open the heavens, he left a wound that never fully closed. Through that wound came the Chaos — a mindless, formless enemy that sought to dissolve all order. The greatest dragons of the age flew into that wound to fight it.*
> *Many did not return.*
> *The dragons still remember this as the greatest test their kind has ever faced. They speak of it with both pride and sorrow.*

The dragon version explicitly **connects the Wound to Ssar'veth's act** — the dragons remember that the breach Kudur-eṭir drilled is the same breach the chaos came through, even if they do not remember the full Spear / Total Icon Binding mechanism.

The dragon version is also the only version that **claims dragon agency in the resistance**. The dragons remember themselves as the great combatants who flew into the wound to fight the chaos. The casualties are mourned. The triumph is claimed. The story is told *with pride and sorrow* — a more mixed register than the dragons' grim satisfaction over Ssar'veth himself, because in the Chaos Incursions the dragons themselves paid heavily.

`[CANONIZED]` — The dragons remember themselves as the front-line combatants of the Chaos Incursions. This is the dragons' interpretive frame and may not be entirely accurate (other peoples and the gods themselves also fought), but it is what the dragons say at their gatherings.

### 3.3 The Unicorn Myth — "The Unraveling"

> *The Chaos was not merely an enemy — it was the absence of meaning itself. It was the world trying to forget its own name.*
> *The unicorns say the gods did not truly defeat it. They only sealed it away… and the seals are weakening. Some elder unicorns claim they can still hear the Chaos whispering through the cracks in reality.*

The unicorn version is **the most ominous** of the recitations. The unicorns do not believe the chaos was defeated; they believe it was *contained*, that the containment is *failing*, and that the chaos is *waiting*.

This is consistent with the unicorn mythic register established in §2.4 — *some wounds do not close — they only wait*. The unicorns' interpretive frame extends across catastrophes: every great evil the gods supposedly sealed is, in the unicorn understanding, only sealed for the moment, and the seals are wearing thin.

The unicorns are the mythic voice of *the seals are weakening*. They are correct often enough that other peoples take them seriously when they raise an alarm.

### 3.4 The Scholarly / Lirran Version — "The First and Second Breaches"

> *There were two major incursions of Chaos into the Mythos. The first, far larger, nearly consumed the world. The second was smaller but still catastrophic. Both were enabled by the original wound created during the Cataclysm. The Order Sharanel considers the sealing of these breaches to be among the greatest acts of the Elder Gods.*

The scholarly version preserves the **count** (two breaches), the **relative scale** (the first far larger than the second), the **causal chain** (both enabled by the original Cataclysm wound), and the **institutional credit** (the Order Sharanel reads the sealings as among the Elder Gods' greatest achievements).

This is the version preserved in Order Sharanel theological literature, in Lirran temple records, and in the deeper Tome of Baldaran chapters. A scholar-character in the modern era researching the Incursions would encounter it.

`[CANONIZED]` — The canonical count is *two incursions*, the *First Breach* being far larger than the *Second Breach*, both downstream of the Cataclysm wound. (This aligns with the existing wiki canon of the *First Chaos Crusade* and *Second Chaos Crusade*.)

## 4. THE DEMON WAR — MYTHS OF THE STILL KING

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The Demon War (~10,000 years ago) is the most recent of the three foundational catastrophes and the one most cleanly preserved in living cultural memory across many peoples. The scholarly-historical version (per the existing wiki) is well-known: Xar'vion, a Velrathi archmage of the final Firethorn Empire centuries, sought to resurrect his murdered sister Velena; achieved Unrestricted Elder God status through the Spear of Crystallized Entropy; summoned a Demon Prince in pursuit of resurrection magic; lost his motive to Dao's curse; the Demon Prince and its legions ravaged Eadras for twenty years; half of all living creatures died; Xar'vion eventually turned against his summoned Demon Prince and joined the alliance against it; the Elder Gods bound him in Total Icon Binding as the Motionless King; the demons were sealed away.

The mythic-recitation versions emphasize different parts of this story depending on which people is telling it.

### 4.1 The Common Myth — "The Demon War"

> *Long ago, a sorcerer-king named Xar'vion opened a gate to Hell. A demon prince and its legions poured into our world. For years, the world burned. In the end, the gods themselves bound Xar'vion in eternal stone and sealed the demons away.*
> *This is why we say: "Some doors should never be opened."*

This is the **shortest and most widespread** version — the one most mortals on Eadras know, regardless of culture or species. It captures the essential arc (sorcerer-king, opened gate, demonic invasion, gods bound him in stone, sealing) and supplies the proverb that the story produces: *some doors should never be opened*.

The proverb is in active use in modern Eadran speech, the way a real-world speaker invokes *Pandora's box*. It is what people say when a colleague proposes a course of action that seems pointlessly dangerous.

### 4.2 The Longer Common Version — "The War of the Still King"

> *Long ago, a sorcerer-king named Xar'vion opened a gate to the abyss in his mad quest to bring his sister back from death. From that gate came a demon prince and its legions, and for years the world burned.*
> *It was the closest any living memory has come to the end of all things. Every race, every nation, every hero stood together against the horde. In the end, the gods themselves were forced to intervene. They bound Xar'vion into an eternal prison of stone, condemning him to eternal stillness, and drove the demons back into the void.*
> *This is why we say: "Grief unchecked invites ruin."*
> *This is why we fear those who would bargain with things from beyond the world.*
> *Some say the Still King's statue still exists somewhere, hidden away. Some say there are those who still worship him in secret.*
> *But most people simply call the whole affair The Demon War, and are glad it is over.*

This is the **bardic-recitation form** — the version a traveling bard would perform in an evening hall, the version a priest might deploy in a sermon on grief and restraint. It is canonically known as *The War of the Still King*.

It preserves the essential elements: **grief as the originating cause** (Xar'vion sought to resurrect his murdered sister), **the bargain with the abyss** (the demon-summoning, here described as opening a gate rather than the more accurate summoning of a Demon Prince), **the universal stand** (every race, every nation, every hero stood together — which is true; the historical Demon War did produce a near-universal alliance), **the divine intervention** (the gods bound Xar'vion in stone), and the **fading rumors of his cult** (the Motionless King's statue and his worshippers — true, but the Black Hand's specific connection to him is *not* common knowledge; see §4.3 below).

The two proverbs it produces — *grief unchecked invites ruin* and *fear those who would bargain with things from beyond the world* — are both in active use in modern Eadras.

`[CANONIZED]` — The Black Hand is **not commonly known to be associated with Xar'vion**. This is canonical for any common-knowledge depiction of the War of the Still King. Cult-of-the-Still-King rumors exist; the connection to the modern, visible Black Hand criminal-political organization is hidden, and modern Eadran characters who know of the Black Hand do not by default associate it with the Motionless King. The Black Hand's actual relationship to Xar'vion is wiki canon and is held by the Inner Hand and the deepest historical scholarship only.

### 4.3 The Orcish Myth — "The Night of the Gray Wall"

> *When the demons came, the world stood on the brink of annihilation. Three races made the Final Stand at the Broken Conclaves: the noble Orug, the mighty Aetherin, and the wise El'mer. They held the line for three days and three nights against impossible odds.*
> *They were betrayed.*
> *The reinforcements that were promised never came. The three races were slaughtered to the last. Only the diluted blood of those three peoples remains today — in orcs, humans, and the smaller folk.*
> *This is why orcs still hold the Night of the Gray Wall every year. They mark their bodies with ash and remember the betrayal. They remember that the world was saved, but their ancestors were abandoned.*

The orcish version is the **most emotionally charged** of the Demon War myths and is the version preserved with the greatest specificity by any modern people.

Where the common myth speaks of the Demon War in broad strokes (sorcerer-king, demon invasion, gods bound him), the orcish version focuses with painful detail on **the Battle of the Broken Conclaves** — the three-day stand in which the Orug, Aetherin, and El'mer held the Final Wall against the Demon Prince's hordes long enough for sealing rituals to complete, were promised reinforcements that did not come, and were exterminated to the last individual.

The orcish version is the **myth of betrayal**. The reinforcements were the Tarran Emperor's responsibility; his failure to send them is the cause the orcs remember; and the modern orcish festival of the **Night of the Gray Wall** is the annual ritual reenactment of that mourning. Orcs and beast-folk together mark themselves with **gray ash** (a symbolic echo of Orug skin) and recite the names of the fallen.

`[CANONIZED]` — The orcish version is what orcs in the modern era *actually believe and recite*. The orcish identification of the three fallen races as **the Orug, the Aetherin, and the El'mer** is canonically correct. The orcs' identification of themselves and other modern peoples as descendants of *the diluted blood of those three* (orcs from Orug, humans from Aetherin, "the smaller folk" from El'mer) reflects what the orcs say at the festival; the exact diluted-bloodline mapping is supported by the existing wiki canon and is consistent with the orcs' own self-understanding.

### 4.4 The Katharim Version — "The Breaking of the Three"

> *The Katharim remember what others have forgotten.*
> *They remember that the Orug, Aetherin, and El'mer were not merely brave — they were the last true children of the Three Progenitor Gods. They remember that these three races died defending the world while others held back their strength.*
> *They keep the true names of the fallen. They keep the memory of the betrayal. They do not forgive, and they do not forget.*

The Katharim version is the **mythologically precise** version. The Katharim — the Rememberers of the Dead, the modern post-Rathari priesthood of memorial and underworld matter — are the institutional keepers of the names and the truths of the Demon War's most-painful moment.

Their version makes two specific theological claims:

- **The three fallen races were "the last true children of the Three Progenitor Gods."** This connects the three exterminated peoples — Orug, Aetherin, El'mer — directly to the three creator-gods of the modern humanoid races (Melania, Mark, Rand, in the canonical mapping). The Katharim hold that these three were not just brave but **sacred** — the *direct* descendants of the progenitor gods in a way the modern diluted lineages no longer are.
- **Others held back their strength.** This is the Katharim version's explicit naming of the failure that the orcs call betrayal. The Katharim do not soften the accusation. They keep the memory institutionally.

The Katharim **do not forgive, and they do not forget**. This is a load-bearing line of their mythic register. The Katharim are not vengeful in the sense of plotting revenge; they are vengeful in the sense of refusing to let the memory dim. Their institutional function is to *remember*, and they apply that function to the betrayal as steadily as they apply it to every named death of the war.

`[CANONIZED]` — The Katharim's institutional role is to keep the true names of the fallen and the memory of the betrayal. The Katharim do not let the Demon War story drift into the simpler folk versions; they hold the painful specificity.

### 4.5 What different peoples emphasize

The full pattern across the Demon War mythic forms reveals each people's characteristic emphasis:

- **Humans** tell it as **a tragedy of grief and hubris** — Xar'vion's love for his sister, his refusal to accept her death, the cascade of catastrophe that followed.
- **Rathari descendants** (functionally extinct as a coherent people, but where their memory survives — primarily through the Katharim and the post-Rathari scholarly traditions) tell it as a **cautionary tale about unchecked ambition and forbidden magic** — Xar'vion was one of their own, the greatest archmage of the final Firethorn centuries, and his fall is the cautionary tale about what magical brilliance unchecked becomes.
- **Dragons** tell it with **contempt** — "*A mortal who thought he could control what he could not understand.*" The dragons' grim-satisfaction register applied to the Demon War reads Xar'vion's specific failure as the predictable consequence of mortal arrogance about cosmic forces.
- **Unicorns** tell **a darker version** — they claim the Demon Prince is not truly gone, only waiting *for the next heartbroken fool to open the door again*. This is consistent with the unicorn mythic register of *some wounds do not close — they only wait*.
- **Orcs** tell **the Night of the Gray Wall** version, as documented in §4.3 — the emotional center of the modern orcish religious calendar.
- **Katharim** tell **the Breaking of the Three** version, as documented in §4.4 — the most precise version, the institutional version.
- **Beast-folk** — `[FLAG_FOR_REVIEW]` — the source chat notes that beast-folk *do not remember* the Demon War much because they have *tried to forget*. The chat does not develop this further; whether the beast-folk's forgetting is collective amnesia, deliberate institutional silence, the slow erosion of memory by Sylph's Veil influence, or some combination is open canon. The forgetting is itself a piece of beast-folk character worth integrating into their wider profile.

## 5. WHO REMEMBERS HOW — THE INSTITUTIONAL VOICES

`[CANONIZED]` `[META]`

The pattern across the three catastrophes reveals a consistent set of **mythic voices** that each major people maintains. These voices are now load-bearing canon for any future Eadran mythic-recitation material: when a new ancient event is developed and its mythic preservation is needed, the voices below are the established forms.

### 5.1 The dragon voice — grim satisfaction over hubris

The dragons' interpretive register reads catastrophes as **vindications of cosmic hierarchy**. Mortals who overreach get what they deserve. The world breaking is the world correctly resisting violation. The dragons take grim satisfaction in the punishment of pride.

When the dragons themselves are the casualties (as in the Chaos Incursions §3.2), the register softens into *pride and sorrow* — the dragons' own sacrifice is honored, but the framework of cosmic-order-correcting-violation remains intact.

The voice is **conservative, hierarchical, and self-confirming**. It is the voice the Dragon Empire uses officially.

### 5.2 The unicorn voice — sorrowful, watchful, alarmed

The unicorns' interpretive register reads catastrophes as **intrusions from outside reality that were never fully closed**. The seals weaken. The wounds wait. The next iteration of the catastrophe is always approaching.

The unicorns are right often enough that this voice carries weight. The Worldbreaker did come from outside the Mythos; the Chaos Incursions did exploit a wound that was never fully healed; the Demon Prince is not, in fact, fully gone. The unicorns are the canary in the cosmic shaft.

The voice is **alarmed, perceptive, and unwelcome**. Other peoples find unicorn warnings exhausting and prefer not to hear them. They are nonetheless almost always correct, and the smarter Eadran institutions (Order Sharanel, the deepest Lirran temple traditions) take unicorn alarm-raising seriously.

### 5.3 The humanoid voice — didactic, plain, proverb-producing

The humanoid interpretive register reads catastrophes as **moral lessons for the listener**. *We do not reach too high. Grief unchecked invites ruin. Some doors should never be opened.* The story serves the proverb; the proverb serves the social-instructional function of warning the next generation.

The voice is **practical, mnemonic, and broadly conservative** — not in the dragons' cosmic-hierarchy sense but in the everyday folk-wisdom sense of "we have learned this hard lesson; remember it." It is the voice most stories take when most cultures tell them.

### 5.4 The orcish voice — emotionally charged, ritually reinforced, grief-rooted

The orcish interpretive register reads the Demon War specifically — and possibly other catastrophes by extension, though this is open canon — as **ancestral grief that must be ritually held**. The Night of the Gray Wall is the canonical expression; the grief is felt collectively, marked on the body with ash, and recited aloud in the names of the fallen.

The voice is **fierce, mourning, and refusal-of-forgetting**. The orcs do not let pain dim with time. They hold it in ritual.

### 5.5 The Katharim voice — institutional precision, the keeping of true names

The Katharim's interpretive register reads catastrophes as **specific named events with specific named dead** whose memory the Katharim are *institutionally responsible* for maintaining. The Katharim's mythic voice is the most accurate to the historical record because the Katharim's entire institutional purpose is the accurate keeping of the dead.

The voice is **precise, unflinching, and immune to the softening that other peoples apply over centuries**. The Katharim know who was there, what happened, who failed, and who paid for the failure. They preserve this knowledge for as long as their order endures.

### 5.6 The scholarly voice — Tome of Baldaran, the measured admission of uncertainty

The scholarly version preserved in the Tome of Baldaran (and adjacent in the Order Sharanel's deepest theological texts, the Velrathi exile archives in Veyr'Keth, and the most rigorous Lirran temple traditions) reads catastrophes as **events with discoverable mechanisms whose details are partial or unknown, recorded with appropriate epistemic restraint**.

The voice is **cautious, factually careful, and explicitly admitting what is not known**. The Tome does not pretend to a certainty it does not have. It records names where names survive, mechanisms where mechanisms are documented, and explicit lacunae where the record is sealed or lost. The Tome is the voice that says *the precise mechanism remains unknown*.

This is the voice of institutional history in Eadras at its best, and it is rare. Most peoples tell the louder, more emotionally satisfying versions documented in §5.1-§5.5.

## 6. AUTHOR CRAFT NOTES

`[META]`

A few observations on this material as craft, preserved from the source-chat conversation:

**The recitation texts are short on purpose.** A myth that survives ten thousand or two million years is short — it has been worn smooth by retelling. The full Tome of Baldaran scholarly version can be longer because the scholarly tradition is institutional and supports paragraph-length precision; the popular versions cannot afford to be long, because they have to be carried in mouths and ears and ritual reenactments. The texts in §2 through §4 are written at the length they would actually be recited at.

**The texts deliberately differ from each other in characteristic ways.** This is the entire load-bearing point of the cultural-perspective framing. The dragons' grim satisfaction differs from the unicorns' watchful sorrow differs from the humanoid didactic didn't-they-deserve-it differs from the Katharim's institutional precision. Same event, six surviving recitations. Each one tells the same listener (the modern Eadran character or player) something different about *who is doing the telling*. This is how the world's cultures earn their individual voices.

**The principle generalizes beyond these three events.** Any future Eadran historical event that survives long enough to be remembered through myth should be expected to produce **multiple cultural recitations** in the established voices. The Worldbreaker Cataclysm, the Chaos Crusades, and the Demon War are the three foundational catastrophes; further events worth myth-status include the Sorcerer Crusade, the Great Banishment, the Lost Zenith, the Thousand-Year Dimming, and any post-Demon-War continental-scale event large enough to leave a permanent cultural imprint. Each of these would benefit from being given dragon, unicorn, humanoid, orcish (where relevant), Katharim, and scholarly versions when written.

**The relationship to the wiki is layering.** The wiki provides the canonical scholarly-historical version of each event. This document provides the in-world mythic-recitation layer that sits on top of that scholarly version. A character in a story or game scene who encounters one of these events should encounter it *first* through the relevant mythic recitation and only later, if their access permits, through the scholarly Tome version. This is the right experiential order for a fantasy setting: the deep history is mostly *story*, with *fact* available only to those who can earn it.

**Mythic recitation can serve as exposition without breaking immersion.** When a player or reader needs to learn about Xar'vion, the writer's choice is between (a) a narrator's history-textbook summary, (b) a character delivering a research-style infodump, or (c) a bard reciting *The War of the Still King* at an evening hall while characters listen. Option (c) is best. The recitation forms in this document exist to make option (c) genuinely available — they are quotable as-is, in scenes, with appropriate framing.

**Beast-folk forgetting is its own canon point.** The note in §4.5 that beast-folk *have tried to forget* the Demon War is a small detail but a load-bearing one. It introduces a category of mythic-relationship beyond *remembering accurately* and *remembering in mythic form* — the category of *deliberately forgetting*. This category is worth keeping in mind for other Eadran peoples and other events. Some peoples make active institutional choices to *not* keep certain memories alive. The Velrathi exiles' relationship to the Firethorn Empire may have aspects of this; the Tarran Gray Matriarchy's relationship to the Great Betrayal almost certainly does. The mode of forgetting matters as much as the mode of remembering.

## LIVE WIRES

Open story prompts and unresolved questions surfaced by this material:

- **What is the recitation-form myth of the Sorcerer Crusade?** The Sorcerer Crusade (~5,000 years ago) is canonically the catastrophe that brought down the Amunorian Empire, killed three Weavespinners, and contributed to the Thousand-Year Dimming. It is large enough and recent enough to have produced full mythic recitations in the established cultural voices. None has been written yet. The Amunorian diaspora's version (mournful, identifying the fall as a divine sign that the imperial legacy ended), the Velrathi exile version, the Order Sharanel version, the dragon version (presumably more grim-satisfaction-over-hubris), the unicorn version (presumably more *we warned you*), and the scholarly version are all open canon worth developing.
- **What is the recitation-form myth of the Great Banishment?** The Banishment is one of the largest events in modern Eadran history and presumably has its own mythic surface. The Tarran versions (exile, betrayal, the price of imperial hubris) would differ sharply from the Velrathi exile versions and the modern humanoid versions.
- **The beast-folk silence.** Why have the beast-folk tried to forget the Demon War? Sylph's Veil influence? The shame of some specific beast-folk decision during the war? A deliberate religious choice in the post-Rathari beast-folk tradition? The forgetting is itself a piece of canon worth developing.
- **The Velrathi exiles' mythic relationship to Xar'vion.** Xar'vion was a Velrathi. The Velrathi exiles in Veyr'Keth presumably have their own painful relationship to his memory — pride that he was the greatest of theirs, shame that his fall triggered the catastrophe, theological argument over whether he was a true Velrathi or a corruption of the line. None of this is written.
- **The Inner Hand's secret mythic version.** The Black Hand's Inner Hand, who do know that the Motionless King is Xar'vion and that their work is in his service, presumably have their own secret recitation — possibly a *cult-of-Xar'vion* mythic form that inverts the public War of the Still King recitation. *He was not the villain; he was the broken hero whose grief the world refused to permit.* This is the Inner Hand's load-bearing narrative and would benefit substantially from a written recitation text.
- **The unicorn version of the Spear.** The unicorns' interpretive frame for the Cataclysm and the Chaos Incursions is consistent with their broader theological position on the Spear: it is an alien object, not part of the gods' jurisdiction, and not properly destroyed but only contained. A unicorn-perspective recitation on the Spear specifically would tie the whole unicorn voice together.
- **The Three Last Lights.** This canonical wiki entity (the names of the three goddesses Melania/Mark/Rand who created the humanoid progenitor races, also the three races who died at the Final Wall) is a candidate for its own short mythic recitation in a Katharim register. The Katharim are the keepers of the dead and the Three are the load-bearing dead; a Katharim recitation of *The Three Who Were Lost* would be powerful canon.
- **Dragon-Empire-internal version of the dragon voice.** The dragons' grim-satisfaction register is general; the Dragon Empire's official theological version is presumably more elaborated, more institutionalized, and more politically loaded. A Dragon Empire bureaucratic recitation form for the Fall of Ssar'veth — perhaps formally chanted in imperial temple, perhaps annotated by ranked priests with the hashmarks of their interpretive standing — would tie the Empire's official cult to the broader dragon mythic voice. (See companion `dragon-empire-far-eastern-meritocracy.md`.)
- **The humanoid version variants by region.** The "humanoid version" in this doc is the broad average. Different humanoid cultures — Tariq al-Honor, Azure Basin polities, Orden member-states, the Iberian Marches, the Balkan Heights, the Kingdom of Xanar — almost certainly each have their own slight inflections of the standard recitation. Regional flavor work on these would add texture.
- **The lullaby version.** Children in many cultures encounter the foundational myths first through *lullaby* — a sung short form that introduces the moral without the full bardic recitation. A *Ssar'veth lullaby* would be a beautiful piece of texture (frightening on its face, normalizing through repetition, foundational to how Eadran children first encounter the deep past). One should be written.
- **Failed myths.** The framing principle is that myths *survive*. But many do not. The mythic version of the Lost Zenith's hidden truths, of the Three Last Lights' exact deaths, of the Spear's true history — many of these have *failed* as living recitation and survive only in scholarly form, if at all. The category of *what is no longer remembered* is its own piece of canon, and what causes mythic survival vs failure is worth thinking about.
