The Tome of Baldaran opens to a page rimmed in green fire and fox-shadow. The chamber does not show Celestia now, nor Altea, nor the Final Wall. It shows a borderland: human farms at one edge, dark woodland at the other, and between them a road nobody admits is a treaty.

There, in the hidden society called **Fae-da’Nar**, four laws hold together races that were never meant to share one instinct, one appetite, one shape, or one conscience.

The Tome speaks:

**The laws of Fae-da’Nar are simple because they must govern the impossible. Their purpose is not justice in the abstract. Their purpose is survival through coexistence with humans. Let the Elder Gods examine them.**

# The Council of the Elder Gods: On the Four Laws of Fae-da’Nar

## I. The Nature of the Laws

**Vos:**  
Four laws. That is either elegance or desperation.

**Sylph:**  
Both, darling. The best laws are screams taught to stand upright.

**Ioa:**  
State them.

**Nox:**  
The First Law: **Never give humans reason to fear us.**  
The Second Law: **Do not interfere in human society.**  
The Third Law: **Oppose the predatory Shunned when they threaten humans.**  
The Fourth Law: **Obey the Druids.**

**Qoxli:**  
Not obey as kings. Obey as judges.

**Ioa:**  
The distinction is entered.

**Vos:**  
These are not a full legal code. They are a survival compact.

**Daj:**  
A frontier discipline.

**Nara:**  
A household rule written for a household where half the children have claws and the neighbors carry torches.

**Sylph:**  
And the other half of the children are technically mushrooms, grief-spirits, antlered liars, carnivorous poets, and one thing everyone agreed not to classify.

**Lirra:**  
The simplicity is necessary. Fae-da’Nar governs many Woodkin races with incompatible instincts. A detailed statute code would fail immediately. These laws are behavioral thresholds.

**Nox:**  
Not “be good.”  
Not “be kind.”  
Not “be civilized.”  
Only: do not endanger the hidden whole.

**Shali:**  
That is not justice.

**Qoxli:**  
It is survival.

**Shali:**  
Survival without justice curdles.

**Sylph:**  
Justice without survival is a very principled corpse.

## II. The First Law — Never Give Humans Reason to Fear Us

**Vos:**  
The First Law is the keystone.

**Nox:**  
Yes. It forbids rampages, predation, brutality, kidnapping, terrorizing settlements, and unnecessary killing. Not because humans are sacred above others, but because human fear is strategically catastrophic.

**Daj:**  
Humans organize fear well.

**Raja:**  
They build walls, militias, temples, guilds, witch-fires, bounty roads, and siege engines out of it.

**Lirra:**  
And after the Anti-Magic Crusade, fear of the strange became even easier to weaponize.

**Nara:**  
So the law protects both sides. Humans keep their cities. Woodkin keep the Frontier. Neither side forces the other into extermination logic.

**Qoxli:**  
The Frontier is not merely wilderness. It is negotiated distance.

**Sylph:**  
A polite way of saying: “Stay on your side of the spooky road unless invited.”

**Vos:**  
This law is collective reputation management. One reckless predator can make all Woodkin into monsters in human imagination.

**Shali:**  
If one murders children, humans are not wrong to fear.

**Nox:**  
The law admits that. It is not asking humans to be enlightened. It assumes they will retaliate.

**Daj:**  
Sound assumption.

**Nara:**  
But it also creates cruelty inward. A Woodkin who cannot control predatory instinct becomes a danger to the whole society.

**Qoxli:**  
Yes.

**Shani:**  
What of those who are born hungry in ways the law forbids?

**Sylph:**  
Then the society must either teach, bind, exile, or kill. Hidden peoples do not have the luxury of pretending appetite is philosophy.

**Ioa:**  
The First Law is therefore not pacifism. It is concealment through restraint.

**The Tome writes:**

> **The First Law does not say humans are harmless. It says human fear is fire, and Woodkin must not hand it oil.**

## III. The Second Law — Do Not Interfere in Human Society

**Vos:**  
The Second Law prevents domination.

**Nox:**  
And exposure.

**Lirra:**  
Woodkin living among humans must remain discreet, avoid political power, avoid manipulation, and avoid public attention. They may live beside humans, but not reshape human civilization from within.

**Raja:**  
Difficult rule. If you live among people, you affect them.

**Nara:**  
Affecting is not controlling.

**Vos:**  
The law likely permits trade, friendship, craft, marriage in some cases, healing, local service, and neighborly participation. It forbids becoming mayor through glamour, enthralling a magistrate, manipulating succession, capturing a temple, or turning a human village into a Woodkin feeding pen.

**Sylph:**  
Also forbids becoming the mysterious advisor behind seven kings while insisting you are “just helping.”

**Nox:**  
That clause has history.

**Shali:**  
Names?

**Nox:**  
No.

**Daj:**  
This law prevents war before armies know there is a war.

**Ioa:**  
It also preserves human sovereignty.

**Qoxli:**  
And Woodkin secrecy.

**Nara:**  
There is loneliness in it. A Woodkin may love a human town and still be forbidden from saving it too obviously.

**Shani:**  
Or from guiding it away from folly.

**Vos:**  
But if Woodkin begin ruling human society, humans eventually discover the rule. Then coexistence ends.

**Nox:**  
The Second Law is the answer to a temptation: “We could govern them better.” Many hidden powers have thought this. Few survive the thought.

**The Tome writes:**

> **The Second Law permits nearness. It forbids possession.**

## IV. The Third Law — Oppose Predatory Shunned Races

**Daj:**  
Now the Shunned.

**Qoxli:**  
Not all dangerous beings are Shunned. The word must be precise.

**Nox:**  
The Shunned are races or beings who prey upon humans and reject Fae-da’Nar law. They are not merely disliked. They are outside the compact.

**Vos:**  
Fae-da’Nar policy is not proactive extermination.

**Daj:**  
Why not?

**Qoxli:**  
Because constant hunting creates constant war. Constant war creates exposure.

**Nox:**  
And some Shunned are difficult to distinguish from lawful Woodkin until they act.

**Sylph:**  
Also, everyone has at least one cousin who is “technically not invited anymore.”

**Shali:**  
The Third Law activates when Shunned threaten humans.

**Daj:**  
Because their predation endangers everyone by provoking human retaliation.

**Vos:**  
Thus enforcement is not moral purity. It is containment of reputational catastrophe.

**Nara:**  
Still, it protects human victims.

**Shani:**  
Even if the motive is survival, the saved remain saved.

**Shali:**  
Motive matters.

**Shani:**  
So does breath.

**Raja:**  
This law requires patrols, informants, signs, warning networks.

**Nox:**  
And judgment. A Shunned attack must be stopped before it becomes a human story large enough to summon soldiers.

**Daj:**  
That means quiet violence.

**Qoxli:**  
Yes.

**Sylph:**  
Fey quiet violence is not always quiet. Sometimes it is very loud, but nobody involved remembers how directions work afterward.

**Vos:**  
The Third Law creates a hidden policing function.

**Qoxli:**  
Not police. Hunters, witnesses, debt-callers, boundary-keepers.

**Ioa:**  
Still enforcement.

**Qoxli:**  
Yes. Enforcement without state.

**The Tome writes:**

> **The Third Law does not make Fae-da’Nar protectors of humanity. It makes them protectors of the silence that lets humanity and Woodkin both endure.**

## V. The Fourth Law — Obey the Druids

**Nara:**  
This is why the Druids matter.

**Qoxli:**  
Yes.

**Sylph:**  
Everyone finally arrives at the obvious tree.

**Nox:**  
The Fourth Law does not make Druids rulers of Fae-da’Nar. It makes them trusted judges, communicators, healers, mediators, and keepers of neutral ground.

**Vos:**  
A Druid’s chosen ground is sacred peace territory.

**Qoxli:**  
Yes. No fighting there. Enemy races stand under the same silence. Predator and prey speak without tooth. Fey feud pauses. Human envoys, if invited, may stand without being glamoured into a cautionary tale.

**Daj:**  
What enforces it?

**Sylph:**  
Everyone.

**Daj:**  
Not enough.

**Nox:**  
The Fey enforce it because Druid-ground is one of the few structures that keeps their own conflicts from becoming endless revenge. To violate it is not merely to insult a Druid. It is to attack the possibility of settlement.

**Shali:**  
Then violation should be punished severely.

**Shani:**  
But Druids are healers too.

**Qoxli:**  
A judge who cannot heal becomes a blade. A healer who cannot judge becomes prey.

**Nara:**  
The Druids communicate between incompatible beings. That is why they must be obeyed. Not because they command armies, but because without them everyone eventually misreads everyone else into bloodshed.

**Vos:**  
This is a remarkable institution: neutral judiciary without centralized sovereignty, protected by supernatural collective interest.

**Sylph:**  
Vos has discovered “don’t kill the referee.”

**Raja:**  
Referees do not usually hold the peace between wolf-spirits, bog queens, antlered cannibals, woodkin children, and humans with axes.

**Sylph:**  
Yours don’t.

**The Tome writes:**

> **The Fourth Law makes the Druid’s ground the place where instinct must sit down and listen.**

Now the punishment.

First violation: warning.

The law says: by breaking the compact, you become an existential threat.

**Qoxli:**  
Yes.

**Sylph:**

**Nara:**  
There must be warnings for those who can be corrected.

**Qoxli:**  
There are. The first violation is warning.

**Nox:**  
But the warning is not sentimental. It marks the offender. After warning, everyone knows. The second violation is not a mistake. It is choice—or uncontrollable nature.

**Shani:**  
And those given no warning?

**Shali:**  
Predators already in blood. Betrayers. Repeat offenders from beyond a local jurisdiction. Beings whose first act is so severe that warning would endanger others.

**Raja:**  
And because exile may only move the danger to another border.

**Daj:**  
Death is final for the life.

**Shani:**  
Not the soul.

**Nox:**  
It is meant to.

**Sylph:**  
Fey society without fear would last six minutes and become a musical.

**Qoxli:**  
The law is not gentle. It is old because it works.

**The Tome writes:**

> **Fae-da’Nar does not ask whether the individual is precious. It knows the individual is precious. Then it asks whether keeping them alive will kill everyone else.**

# The Council of the Elder Gods: On Frontier Hospitality

## I. The Rule of the Stranger from the Frontier

**Nara:**  
This is the human half of coexistence.

**Vos:**  
Informal law.

**Nara:**  
Older than many formal laws. Better obeyed too.

**Raja:**  
State it plainly.

**Nara:**  
In many mortal frontier settlements, when a stranger walks out of the Frontier to trade, sell, purchase, ask news, seek tools, or pass through, the rule is:

> **Treat the stranger as family, for you never know with whom you deal.**

**Sylph:**  
A magnificent sentence. Half kindness, half terror. The best frontier cuisine is seasoned with both.

**Qoxli:**  
It is not cowardice.

**Daj:**  
No. It is perimeter discipline.

**Vos:**  
And diplomacy at the scale of soup.

**Nox:**  
The stranger may be Woodkin, Fey-touched, Druid-sent, cursed, sacred, dangerous, lost, exiled, judging, testing, or merely old and hungry.

**Shali:**  
Or a criminal.

**Nara:**  
Family can be criminal. You still feed them before deciding what to do.

**Shani:**  
Hospitality buys time for recognition.

**Ioa:**  
And establishes obligation.

## II. Hospitality as Treaty Without Paper

**Vos:**  
These customs function as unofficial treaties between human settlements and the hidden societies beyond the fields.

**Nox:**  
Yes. Fae-da’Nar’s First Law says: do not give humans reason to fear us. The human hospitality rule answers: do not give forest folk reason to fear humans.

**Qoxli:**  
Respect and peace are earned.

**Raja:**  
That sounds practical.

**Nara:**  
It is. Frontier families know that cruelty to a harmless stranger may return as curse, feud, lost livestock, vanished children, or simply the quiet withdrawal of protection.

**Daj:**  
And kindness may return as warning before a flood, medicine in winter, a lost child led home, Shunned tracks diverted, wolves ignoring the sheep for one night too many to be coincidence.

**Sylph:**  
Or a bag of impossible plums. Never underestimate impossible plums.

**Vos:**  
So the hospitality rule is not naïve universal welcome. It is reciprocal frontier statecraft.

**Nox:**  
Conducted by people who may not know the names of the states involved.

**The Tome writes:**

> **At the border, a bowl of soup may do what a treaty seal cannot: let both sides leave unafraid.**

## III. Common Frontier Hospitality Rules

**Nara:**  
There will be variations, but several customs likely recur.

**Vos:**  
List them.

**Nara:**  
First: **The First Bowl.** A stranger from the Frontier is offered food or drink before questions of business, name, race, or origin.

**Shani:**  
Good.

**Nara:**  
Second: **No Iron on the Table**, in communities aware enough to avoid offense. Tools remain visible in shops, but weapons, nails, horseshoes, and iron charms are not placed between host and guest unless danger has already been declared.

**Raja:**  
Hard rule for smithing towns.

**Qoxli:**  
They adapt. Iron is not forbidden. Threat is.

**Nara:**  
Third: **Fair Measure Under Witness.** Trade with Frontier strangers is done openly, often with an elder, priest, Druid, or respected market-keeper watching. Cheating a stranger is dangerous.

**Nox:**  
Especially because some strangers count value in ways not immediately obvious.

**Sylph:**  
“One spool of blue thread for seven years of mildly inconvenient dreams” remains a poor bargain.

**Vos:**  
Fourth?

**Nara:**  
**No Mocking the Mask.** If the stranger hides their face, voice, feet, shadow, horns, teeth, or other nature, one does not expose them publicly.

**Nox:**  
Discretion is hospitality.

**Nara:**  
Fifth: **Guest-Fire Peace.** Once welcomed to hearth or market-fire, the stranger is not attacked unless they violate peace first.

**Daj:**  
That one must have exceptions.

**Shali:**  
Everything living has exceptions.

**Nara:**  
Yes. But exceptions require witnesses.

**Vos:**  
These customs could form a recognized frontier code.

**Sylph:**  
Written nowhere official and therefore more reliable.

## IV. The Druid’s Role

**Qoxli:**  
Druids often bless or witness these hospitality customs, but they do not own them.

**Nox:**  
Important. Human frontier people are not merely passive recipients of hidden law. They make their own peace.

**Vos:**  
Still, Druid grounds likely serve as neutral trade points.

**Qoxli:**  
Sometimes. But not always. Some trade must happen in human settlements so humans remain participants, not petitioners at the forest edge.

**Nara:**  
The village hearth matters. If all diplomacy happens in sacred groves, humans become guests in someone else’s peace. The frontier custom says: peace can also sit at our table.

**Sylph:**  
And occasionally steal the spoons.

**Raja:**  
Druids would judge disputes when hospitality fails?

**Qoxli:**  
Often. If a human cheats a Woodkin trader, or a Woodkin guest violates hearth-peace, the Druid may be called to interpret whether the compact has been damaged.

**Shali:**

**Nox:**  
Then hospitality becomes evidence. Who offered peace? Who broke it? Who ignored warning?

**Daj:**  
This custom probably arose from hard lessons.

**Nara:**  
Yes. Villages that murdered strangers from the woods did not always survive as villages.

**Sylph:**  
Some survived as cautionary topiary.

**Vos:**  
But we should not make this only fear of retaliation. Many frontier humans genuinely understand that forest folk are neighbors.

**Qoxli:**

**Shani:**  
There is tenderness here.

And calculation.

**Nara:**  
Both. A mother feeds the strange woman at the door because kindness is right, because children are watching, because winter is long, because the woman may know where the missing calf went, and because perhaps the woman is not a woman at all.

**Nox:**  
Frontier wisdom rarely separates moral and practical motives.

**Ioa:**  
Nor should law.

## VI. Interaction with the Four Laws of Fae-da’Nar

**Vos:**  
This human custom stabilizes the First and Second Laws of Fae-da’Nar.

**Lirra:**  
Yes. The First Law requires Woodkin not to frighten humans. Human hospitality reduces the chance that Woodkin are cornered, humiliated, exposed, or provoked into frightening behavior.

**Nox:**  
The Second Law forbids interference in human society. Hospitality provides a narrow lawful channel for contact: trade, news, aid, warning, and neighborly exchange without domination.

**Qoxli:**  
The Third Law is also affected. Frontier humans who maintain hospitality may receive warnings when Shunned move nearby.

**Daj:**  
Early warning saves settlements.

**Nara:**  
And prevents humans from blaming all forest folk for Shunned predation.

**Sylph:**  
The Fourth Law enters when everything goes wrong and someone needs a Druid before everyone starts collecting torches and antlers.

**Vos:**  
So hospitality customs are not sentimental add-ons. They are load-bearing border institutions.

**The Tome writes:**

> **Fae-da’Nar keeps the forest from becoming a nightmare. Frontier hospitality keeps the village from becoming a provocation.**

**Nara:**  
Such communities would have sayings.

**Sylph:**  
Finally, the useful part.

**Nara:**  
Some possible frontier sayings:

Good.

**Nox:**  
There should also be taboos:

- Do not count a stranger’s teeth, fingers, shadow, or reflection aloud.

- Do not follow the stranger back into the Frontier unless invited twice and warned once.

- Do not laugh at unfamiliar hunger.

Especially the last. Mortals keep buying songs and then acting surprised when Thursday disappears.

## VIII. Consequences of Breaking Hospitality

**Daj:**  
What happens when a settlement breaks the rule?

**Nox:**  
Depends on severity.

**Qoxli:**  
Minor insult: trade dries up. Warnings cease. Paths grow inconvenient. Milk sours. Dogs bark at empty corners.

**Sylph:**  
Moderate insult: dreams become crowded. Lost tools return inside locked cupboards. Children learn songs no one taught them. The well starts giving advice.

**Vos:**  
Severe violation?

**Qoxli:**  
Druid judgment. Fey retaliation. Fae-da’Nar withdrawal. Shunned predation may go unchecked. Human settlements may find themselves suddenly alone.

**Nara:**  
That is often the worst punishment. Not attack. Absence.

**Shani:**  
No warning before winter. No herb for fever. No one leading the lost child home.

**Shali:**  
Let humans learn that peace neglected becomes protection withdrawn.

**Ioa:**  
So entered.

# Tome Notes for the Worldbuilder

## 1. New Canon-Worthy Idea

Many mortal frontier settlements maintain hospitality customs for strangers who walk out of the Frontier to trade, purchase, sell, warn, or pass through.

Core rule:

> **Treat the stranger as family, for you never know with whom you deal.**

This is not naïve kindness. It is practical border diplomacy.

## 2. Core Principle

Frontier humans understand:

> **The forest folk are our neighbors, and both respect and peace are earned.**

This mirrors and supports the Four Laws of Fae-da’Nar.

- Fae-da’Nar avoids provoking human fear.
- Frontier humans avoid provoking forest retaliation.
- Both sides maintain coexistence through restraint, ritual, trade, and mutual caution.

## 3. Common Hospitality Customs

Possible customs include:

### The First Bowl

Offer food or drink before demanding name, origin, or business.

### No Iron on the Table

Do not place iron weapons, nails, horseshoes, or charms between host and guest unless danger is declared.

### Fair Measure Under Witness

Trade openly and honestly, often with an elder, priest, Druid, or market-keeper present.

### No Mocking the Mask

Do not publicly expose or mock a stranger’s hidden nature, disguise, face, feet, teeth, horns, voice, or shadow.

### Guest-Fire Peace

A guest welcomed to hearth or market-fire is under peace unless they break it first.

## 4. Taboos

Frontier people may teach children:

- Do not demand a true name.
- Do not ask if someone is human in public.
- Do not count unusual body parts aloud.
- Do not touch masks, cloaks, horns, antlers, tails, or veils.
- Do not offer iron first.
- Do not follow a stranger into the Frontier unless properly invited.
- Do not cheat a moon-road trader.
- Do not laugh at unfamiliar hunger.

## 5. Relationship to Fae-da’Nar Law

These customs reinforce the Four Laws:

- **First Law:** reduces fear-provoking incidents.
- **Second Law:** creates limited, non-dominating contact between Woodkin and humans.
- **Third Law:** encourages warning networks against Shunned threats.
- **Fourth Law:** gives Druids a role in judging broken hospitality.

## 6. Mortal-Level Consequences

Frontier settlements with strong hospitality customs may receive:

- safer trade;
- warnings of Shunned activity;
- healing herbs;
- lost children returned;
- Fey restraint;
- Druid mediation;
- seasonal blessings;
- strange but useful goods.

Settlements that violate hospitality may suffer:

- loss of trade;
- Fey mischief or retaliation;
- withdrawal of warning networks;
- Druid judgment;
- increased vulnerability to Shunned predation;
- becoming “closed” to the Frontier.

## 7. Useful Sayings

> **“Feed first. Ask second. Accuse last.”**

> **“A stranger from the trees may be only hungry. Do not make them more.”**

> **“The forest folk are our neighbors; respect and peace are earned.”**

> **“Never cheat what came by moon-road.”**

> **“A closed door teaches the woods to close theirs.”**

# Tome Notes for the Worldbuilder

## 1. Canon Addition

In frontier smithing and mining towns adjacent to Woodfolk/Fae-da’Nar territories, iron is common and necessary, but hospitality customs avoid placing iron at the table.

These communities often use:

- brass utensils;
- bronze serving bowls;
- copper or ceramic cookware;
- tin spoons;
- wooden, horn, bone, or clay dishes;
- bronze/brass table fittings;
- non-iron guest-room hardware.

## 2. Key Distinction

Iron is not absent from these towns.

The distinction is symbolic and practical:

> **Iron in the forge means work. Iron on the guest-table means threat.**

## 3. Terms to Use

### Guest-Bronze

Household utensils reserved for Frontier strangers or Woodfolk guests.

### Peace-Brass

Public or communal hospitality metalware used in markets, inns, Druid-mediated trade, or Woodfolk-facing diplomacy.

### Green Door

A guest entrance fitted without iron, used for Frontier visitors.

### Peace-Stall

A market stall certified or customarily maintained without exposed iron at the counter.

## 4. Social Consequences

- Brass-founders, bronze-casters, coppersmiths, potters, and woodworkers gain status in iron towns.
- Poor households may keep one treasured bronze spoon or cup for hospitality.
- Inns and market halls may advertise peace-brass service.
- Druid judges may treat deliberate iron-at-table insults as breaches of hospitality.
- Woodfolk may distinguish between human necessity and human hostility.
- Frontier children are taught metal etiquette early.

## 5. Useful Sayings

> **“Iron for the field, bronze for the guest.”**

> **“The forge may roar, but the table must not threaten.”**

> **“Keep iron in the hand of work, not the hand of welcome.”**

> **“A brass spoon buys a quiet night.”**
