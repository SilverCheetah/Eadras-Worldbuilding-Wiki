# 1. CORE PREMISE

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The Four Laws of Fae-da'Nar are not a broad moral philosophy. They are a survival code designed to allow many Woodkin and Fey-adjacent races with incompatible instincts to coexist with humans without provoking exterminatory conflict.

Their overriding purpose is survival through coexistence with humans.

They protect:
- Woodkin secrecy;
- human settlements;
- the Frontier;
- Druid neutrality;
- Fey-Druid trust;
- the fragile boundary between hidden society and human retaliation.

---

# 2. THE FOUR LAWS OF FAE-DA'NAR

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] First Law: Never give humans reason to fear us.

This forbids rampages, predation, brutality, kidnapping, terrorizing settlements, unnecessary killing, and behavior that confirms human monster-fears.

Core function: protect the secrecy and stability of the hidden whole.

Key phrase:

"The First Law does not say humans are harmless. It says human fear is fire, and Woodkin must not hand it oil."

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Second Law: Do not interfere in human society.

Woodkin among humans must remain discreet and avoid political power, manipulation, domination, public attention, or reshaping human civilization from within.

Core function: coexistence without domination.

Key phrase:

"The Second Law permits nearness. It forbids possession."

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Third Law: Oppose predatory Shunned races when they threaten humans.

The Shunned are beings or races who prey upon humans and reject Fae-da'Nar law. Fae-da'Nar does not necessarily hunt them proactively, but stops them when their actions threaten humans and therefore endanger everyone.

Core function: collective reputation management through controlled enforcement.

Key phrase:

"The Third Law does not make Fae-da'Nar protectors of humanity. It makes them protectors of the silence that lets humanity and Woodkin both endure."

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Fourth Law: Obey the Druids.

Druids are communicators, healers, mediators, judges, trusted authorities, and keepers of sacred neutral ground. A Druid's chosen ground is peace territory.

Core function: neutral diplomatic and judicial framework preventing endless tribal conflict.

Key phrase:

"The Fourth Law makes the Druid's ground the place where instinct must sit down and listen."

---

# 3. PUNISHMENT STRUCTURE

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The punishment structure is severe.

First violation: warning.
Second violation: death.
No warning: possible if the individual is already judged too dangerous.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The warning is not sentimental. It marks the offender. A second violation is treated as choice, uncontrollable nature, or existential danger.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The law prioritizes communal survival over the individual because one reckless being can expose the entire hidden society.

Key phrase:

"Fae-da'Nar does not ask whether the individual is precious. It knows the individual is precious. Then it asks whether keeping them alive will kill everyone else."

---

# 4. DRUIDS AS JUDGES, NOT LEADERS

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Druids do not rule Fae-da'Nar or Fey society. They adjudicate.

They judge:
- oaths;
- bargains;
- trespasses;
- conflicts between Woodkin races;
- conflicts with humans;
- violations of the Four Laws;
- whether mercy or execution is required;
- whether Shunned action threatens the compact.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Druids translate thresholds, not merely language. They determine when appetite becomes predation, secrecy becomes manipulation, interference becomes domination, and mercy becomes risk.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Their judicial role explains why Fey protect them fiercely. Killing a Druid attacks the judicial infrastructure of hidden coexistence.

---

# 5. DRUIDS AND THE ANTI-MAGIC CRUSADE

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The Four Laws clarify why Druids survived the Anti-Magic Crusade comparatively intact.

Anti-magic crusaders who killed Druids were not merely killing isolated rural priests. They were attacking Fae-da'Nar's neutral judiciary, Fey trusted authorities, sacred peace grounds, and the framework preventing Woodkin-human conflict.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Many mortal histories of "haunted woods," failed crusader expeditions, monster attacks, or divine punishment may actually record Fae-da'Nar legal-defense actions and Fey retaliation.

# 6. FRONTIER HOSPITALITY

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Many mortal frontier settlements maintain hospitality customs for strangers who walk out of the Frontier to trade, purchase, sell, warn, or pass through.

Core rule:

"Treat the stranger as family, for you never know with whom you deal."

This is practical border diplomacy, not naive kindness.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Frontier humans understand:

"The forest folk are our neighbors, and both respect and peace are earned."

This mirrors the Four Laws. Fae-da'Nar avoids provoking human fear. Frontier humans avoid provoking forest retaliation.

---

# 7. FRONTIER HOSPITALITY CUSTOMS

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The First Bowl: offer food or drink before demanding name, origin, race, or business.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] No Iron on the Table: do not place iron weapons, nails, horseshoes, tools, or charms between host and guest unless danger is declared.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Fair Measure Under Witness: trade openly and honestly, often with an elder, priest, Druid, or market-keeper present.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] No Mocking the Mask: do not publicly expose or mock a stranger's hidden nature, disguise, face, feet, teeth, horns, voice, or shadow.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Guest-Fire Peace: a guest welcomed to hearth or market-fire is under peace unless they break it first.

Useful sayings:
- "Feed first. Ask second. Accuse last."
- "A stranger from the trees may be only hungry. Do not make them more."
- "Never cheat what came by moon-road."
- "A closed door teaches the woods to close theirs."

# 8. PEACE-BRASS AND GUEST-BRONZE

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] In smithing and mining frontier towns adjacent to Woodfolk territories, iron is common and necessary, but hospitality is conducted with non-iron implements.

Such towns often use or import:
- brass utensils;
- bronze bowls;
- copper pots;
- tin spoons;
- wooden, horn, bone, clay, or ceramic dishes;
- bronze/brass table fittings;
- non-iron guest-room hardware.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Most iron-mining towns import brass or bronze cookware and tableware rather than producing it locally. They regard it as a wise civic investment because peaceful Woodfolk trade brings rare goods.

Key distinction:

"Iron at the table is threat. Iron in trade is consent."

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Terms:
Guest-Bronze: household hospitality ware reserved for Frontier strangers.
Peace-Brass: public or civic non-iron tableware used in markets and inns.
Civic Guest-Bronze: town-maintained hospitality sets funded by peace levies or trade taxes.
Green Door: guest entrance fitted without iron.
Peace-Stall: market stall with no exposed iron at the counter unless requested.

# 10. GREEN MARKET DAYS AND TRADE ETIQUETTE

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Green Market Days are regular market days when Woodfolk trade is expected and hospitality customs are heightened.

Common regulations:
- no exposed iron on hospitality tables;
- iron goods covered until requested;
- no shouting prices at masked traders;
- dogs restrained unless trained not to chase unfamiliar scents;
- blades wrapped or peace-bound until transfer;
- Druid or market-master witness for sensitive trade.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Druids may certify trade routes, mediate disputes, judge hospitality breaches, witness restricted iron sales, and maintain knot-ledgers: symbolic records that confirm lawful trade without exposing Woodfolk identities.

# 11. THRESHOLD TOWN GROWTH

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Frontier trade naturally snowballs. A reliable exchange point becomes a market; a market becomes a route; a route becomes infrastructure; infrastructure becomes settlement growth; settlement growth becomes political gravity.

Growth stages:
1. Guest-Bowl Settlement.
2. Green Market Town.
3. Threshold Town.
4. Charter City.
5. Severed City.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The central structural tension:

A town grows rich by respecting the border, then becomes large enough to forget the border was a relationship.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Threshold cities may become Faithful Threshold Cities, Ceremonial Threshold Cities, Severed Expansion Cities, Ruined Caution Cities, or Relocated Thresholds.

# 12. SIGNS WOODFOLK TRADE IS DRYING UP

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Trade withdrawal may appear through:
- fewer masked traders;
- lower-quality goods;
- mana-rich plants disappearing;
- meat delivered only through intermediaries;
- Druid witnesses absent;
- Green Market becoming mostly human festival;
- Fey mischief sharpening;
- Shunned warnings arriving late or not at all;
- old paths closing or misleading travelers.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Woodfolk often do not announce withdrawal. The relationship simply dries.

# 13. CITY FACTIONS

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Threshold cities commonly develop factions.

Old Compact Faction: old families, innkeepers, market witnesses, some priests, Druid-allied households, and experienced traders who support hospitality, limits on clearing, Druid mediation, and Green Market protections.

Expansion Faction: settlers, ranchers, lumber guilds, grain investors, nobles, land speculators, and military planners who want farmland, timber, pasture, roads, and human legal control.

Commercial Opportunists: merchants who want both Woodfolk goods and forest clearance.

Fear Faction: anti-magic preachers, crusade-remnant ideologues, frightened settlers, and Black Hand-influenced agitators who claim Woodfolk are arming, corrupting, or spying.

---

# 14. WOODFOLK RESPONSE LADDER

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Fae-da'Nar does not respond as one state. Responses vary, but a general ladder exists.

1. Cooling trade.
2. Warning signs.
3. Selective closure of paths.
4. Fey sanctions.
5. Druid judgment.
6. Woodfolk enforcement.
7. Frontier rupture.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Frontier rupture may lead to open hostility, Shunned predation, human military response, and collapse of coexistence.
