Addition — oldest continuous settlements:

A small number of locations in the world qualify as the oldest continuously inhabited or continuously significant settlements.

These sites can be traced back to the pre-catastrophe era, not through uninterrupted civilization, but through continuous reoccupation and rediscovery of the same geographic points.

Their persistence is not due to cultural continuity, but to underlying planetary and magical structures that make these locations consistently favorable for habitation.

Addition — conduit-linked continuity:

All known oldest continuous settlements are located at or near Eldritch conduit endpoints.

The stable, high-density energy at these locations ensures that they remain attractive for habitation across all eras, including after the Worldbreaker event.

Even after complete ecological and civilizational collapse, these sites naturally draw returning populations due to favorable environmental and magical conditions.

Addition — reoccupation pattern:

Following the Worldbreaker catastrophe, surviving life and later emerging civilizations gradually repopulated the world.

As ecosystems stabilized, populations independently converged on the same locations that had previously supported Ararhi settlements.

This process resulted in multiple layers of occupation at the same sites, often without knowledge of the full extent of prior habitation.

Addition — layered habitation:

Oldest settlement sites contain multiple stratified layers of habitation, including:

- pre-catastrophe Ararhi structures
- early post-catastrophe resettlements
- successive cultural phases

In many cases, modern cities are built directly atop ancient foundations, with deeper layers remaining partially or completely unexplored.

Addition — settlement stability factors:

The persistence of these sites is driven by multiple reinforcing factors:

- proximity to Eldritch conduits
- stable access to water and resources
- favorable climate conditions
- strategic geographic positioning

These factors remain consistent across geological and ecological shifts, making the locations repeatedly optimal for habitation.

Addition — mythic interpretation of continuity:

Many cultures interpret the persistence of these sites as evidence of divine favor or cosmic design.

Common myths include:

- the world was rebuilt around these locations
- these places survived the destruction untouched
- they are anchors of reality or balance

These interpretations reflect observed continuity but do not accurately describe the underlying mechanisms.

Summary rule — continuous settlements:

The oldest continuous settlements in the world are not preserved cities, but recurring centers of habitation driven by stable planetary and magical structures, particularly Eldritch conduit endpoints, resulting in repeated reoccupation across multiple eras.

Addition — catastrophic crustal displacement:

During the Worldbreaker event, the planet’s crust temporarily decoupled from the mantle due to extreme thermal and mechanical disruption.

In this state, the crust behaved as a semi-fluid layer, shifting over the mantle rapidly, analogous to butter sliding across a heated surface.

This resulted in large-scale horizontal displacement of the entire crust relative to the planet’s rotational axis.

Addition — polar stability:

The planet’s rotational axis and magnetic poles remained stable during the Worldbreaker event.

However, because the crust shifted independently of the mantle, surface locations moved relative to the poles.

This caused regions that were previously equatorial to shift toward higher latitudes, and vice versa, dramatically altering climate zones.

Addition — climatic displacement:

The rapid movement of the crust across stable poles resulted in abrupt climatic changes across the surface.

Regions experienced sudden transitions, including:

- temperate zones becoming polar
- polar regions moving into warmer latitudes
- ecosystems displaced into incompatible climates

This contributed significantly to biological collapse during and after the catastrophe.

Addition — settlement displacement:

Pre-catastrophe settlements, including Ararhi population centers, were not destroyed solely by impact effects but were also relocated across the planet’s surface.

Cities originally built for specific climates were abruptly moved into drastically different environmental conditions, further contributing to their collapse.

This displacement explains why some ancient sites are found in locations that appear geographically or climatically inconsistent with their original design.

Addition — conduit displacement effects:

Although the crust shifted during the Worldbreaker event, the deep planetary conduits remained fixed relative to the mantle.

This resulted in temporary misalignment between surface geography and conduit endpoints, followed by gradual realignment as tectonic processes stabilized.

Over time, settlement patterns re-centered around conduit endpoints, reinforcing their role as persistent focal points of habitation.

Addition — geological indicators of displacement:

Evidence of crustal displacement includes:

- mismatched geological strata across regions
- abrupt discontinuities in mountain formation patterns
- fossil and sediment distributions inconsistent with current latitude

These anomalies are recognized by advanced scholars but are not fully understood within most cultures.

Refinement — Worldbreaker unified mechanism:

The large-scale geological effects of the Worldbreaker event were not caused by a single factor, but by the combined interaction of multiple simultaneous processes.

The initial impact introduced extreme kinetic energy into the planet, jarring both crust and mantle and destabilizing their interaction.

At the same time, massive high-pressure subsurface water reservoirs were exposed by fracturing crust. These reservoirs rapidly decompressed and flashed to steam, releasing both pressure and thermal energy into the surrounding material.

The combined effects of impact shock, thermal expansion, and pressure release temporarily decoupled the crust from the mantle, allowing large-scale horizontal displacement.

This state initiated runaway tectonic motion, with crustal plates moving at extreme velocities before gradually slowing over time.

These processes collectively account for the observed planetary restructuring, including crustal displacement, ocean formation, and long-term tectonic stabilization.

Refinement — polar stability interpretation:

During the Worldbreaker event, the planet’s rotational axis and magnetic poles remained stable while the crust shifted over the mantle.

No sentient life survived to observe this directly. The stability of the poles is therefore a conclusion derived from later analysis rather than recorded observation.

Evidence for this includes consistent stellar reference patterns and magnetic alignment preserved in geological and crystalline records.

Addition — post-catastrophe inference:

Knowledge of crustal displacement relative to stable poles is reconstructed by later civilizations through indirect evidence.

This includes:

- alignment of ancient crystalline systems with current stellar positions
- magnetic signatures preserved in arcane metals and geological formations
- inconsistencies between fossil distribution and present-day latitude

These data points indicate that the surface moved while the underlying planetary orientation remained unchanged.

Addition — survival constraint:

As a result, all knowledge of the catastrophe’s detailed mechanics is derived from physical evidence rather than eyewitness accounts.

Refinement — terminology (conduits):

References to “poles” in earlier descriptions of the Worldbreaker event refer to the endpoints of the seven planetary conduits, not to the planet’s rotational or magnetic poles.

Conduit endpoints are fixed relative to the mantle and represent deep structural anchors within the planetary system.

Refinement — conduit stability:

During the Worldbreaker event, the crust shifted over the mantle while the seven conduits remained fixed in position.

Because conduit endpoints are anchored to the mantle, their surface locations changed relative to the moving crust.

This resulted in temporary misalignment between existing geography and conduit endpoints, followed by gradual re-centering of settlement and activity around these stable locations.

Refinement — sapient survival:

All pre-catastrophe civilizations, including the Ararhi, were destroyed, and no direct witnesses to the event exist.

Refinement — conduit reoccupation:

Following the catastrophe, emerging life and later civilizations gradually reoccupied regions near conduit endpoints.

Because conduits remained fixed while the crust shifted, these locations reasserted themselves as centers of habitation independent of prior cultural continuity.

This explains why the oldest continuous settlement sites correspond to conduit endpoints despite complete extinction of earlier civilizations.

Addition — conduit-based reconstruction:

The role of conduits during the Worldbreaker event is inferred through their continued stability and influence on settlement patterns.

Evidence includes:

- persistent alignment of major population centers with conduit endpoints
- localized concentrations of stable Eldritch energy
- structural continuity in deep geological and arcane formations

Refinement — conduit anchoring:

The seven planetary conduits are anchored to the planetary core rather than the crust or mantle.

Because of this anchoring, conduit positions remain fixed relative to the core even during large-scale geological disruption.

During the Worldbreaker event, the crust shifted over the mantle while the conduits maintained their positions, resulting in a temporary misalignment between surface geography and conduit endpoints.

Over time, tectonic processes and settlement patterns re-aligned surface activity with these fixed points.

Addition — layered planetary structure:

The planet operates as a layered system consisting of:

- a stable core, which anchors conduit structures
- a dynamic mantle, which transmits heat and energy
- a mobile crust, which carries surface geography

This structure allows for large-scale displacement of the surface without altering deep planetary anchors such as conduit positions.

Refinement — catastrophe interaction with conduits:

During the Worldbreaker event, crustal displacement occurred independently of the core-anchored conduit system.

As a result, surface locations moved relative to conduit endpoints, causing regions that were previously aligned with high-energy zones to shift away, and other regions to move into alignment.

This contributed to the collapse of pre-catastrophe systems and the later re-establishment of settlements at conduit endpoints.
