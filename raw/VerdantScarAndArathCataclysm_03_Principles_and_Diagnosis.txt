HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT
================================================================

This document has two parts. Part I extracts SETTING-WIDE PRINCIPLES that this material surfaces or reinforces — laws that transfer across topics, not just facts about the Arathi era. Part II is LIVE WIRES — open story prompts the material leaves dangling, productive ambiguities, hooks the council either set up deliberately or generated by accident.

The contradiction-flag taxonomy applies as in the companion files; here it is used sparingly because the focus is forward-looking insight rather than corrections.


================================================================
PART I — SETTING-WIDE PRINCIPLES
================================================================

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 1 — CATASTROPHES ARE BIGGER THAN THEIR CATALYSTS.

  This is the central setting law this material surfaces. Kudur-eṭir wanted to end entropy. He did not want to annihilate the Arathi capital or shatter the planet's crust or boil sixty percent of life away. The Worldbreaker came through because his uncontrolled deific reach had nowhere else to go through the existing First Breach. The catastrophe has moral weight independent of his intent.

  This law has wide application across the setting. Xar'vion wanted his sister back. He did not aim the Demon War. Tarloth wanted to follow her mate into godhood. She did not intend the corruption of the Shadowdark plane. The setting routinely gives world-shaping consequences to acts whose initial intent was specific and personal. The cosmology is structured so that grief, ambition, or compassion can be loaded onto leverage points that scale uncontrollably.

  The story consequence: villains are not measured by what they wanted. They are measured by what they CAUSED. And what they caused is usually disproportionate, accidental, or larger than they understood. This makes the setting tragic by default. It also makes blame difficult — Kudur-eṭir is not pure evil, he is overreach plus cosmic vulnerability. Most of Eadras's biggest disasters look the same way.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 2 — ANTAGONISTS ARE STRUCTURAL INVERSIONS, NOT EXTERNAL OPPOSITES.

  The Verdant Scar's dangers are all distorted versions of the same thing the Scar protects:

    - The Scar regulates magic. Want-Wolves are predators that emerge when self-regulation fails inside a person (uncontrolled fixation).
    - The Scar preserves memory of the dead. Marble Mourners are mourning gone slightly wrong — they pursue thieves with full force, the same energy that helps pilgrims become an active threat.
    - The Scar holds a sealed wound. House Theryx wants to open it. The Scar's protector culture and its opener culture grow from the same root: the existence of the sealed wound.

  This principle generalizes. The Neverborn is not the enemy of Eadras — it is the consequence of TOO MUCH ORDER becoming visible to a Chaos observer. The Spear's curse is not anti-power — it strips the wielder of the motive that made them want power. Kudur-eṭir is not anti-life — he is too much love refusing to accept what love costs.

  Story implication: the most dangerous antagonists in Eadras share an essential structure with the thing they threaten. Defeating them by destruction tends to fail. Containing them, restoring proper proportion, or articulating the missing humility tends to work better than overpowering them.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 3 — REGULATORY INFRASTRUCTURE TENDS TO BE LIVING, NOT BUREAUCRATIC.

  The Verdant Scar regulates magic without an institution. It does the job of a Tower of Lirra without being a Tower. Its compass is the forest's distributed root-mind, not a council, archive, or written code. The forest is described as "a dreaming immune system" — biological metaphor, not administrative.

  The same pattern appears in nearby canon: the Tome of Beldoran is a living archive, not a dead one. The Seven Conduits are anchored in cosmological geometry, not in mortal institutions. The Weave that Lirra wove is a living lattice. Even the Omniversal Laws are described as gears in a machine ("Every fact links to every other fact like gears in a machine") — order, but organic order.

  This is a setting that distrusts pure bureaucratic regulation. When institutions appear in Eadras, they tend to be FAILURE MODES (Arathi crystal mind-regulators that shattered; later, the Sorcerer Crusade) rather than primary infrastructure. Real regulation is alive.

  Story implication: order in Eadras is not authoritarian. It is ecological. The setting punishes characters who try to administer reality and rewards characters who learn to participate in it.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 4 — PRECURSORS LEAVE NO DIARY.

  The Arathi cannot be solved. Their language is fossil. Their cities are pressure-crushed labyrinths. Their architect of destruction is annihilated. They left no testimony, no surviving lineage, no whispering remnant. Even the noble Houses that claim Arathi descent are pretenders across an extinction boundary.

  This is a deliberate cosmological design choice and it has wide consequences. The wiki is consistent about it. "There is no Arathi descent, no Arathi echoes, and no remnants of Altea whatsoever." Pressure Echoes preserve fragments, not personhood. Marble Mourners are grief-animations, not Arathi spirits. The Tome of Baldaran preserves both the canonical name (Kudur-eṭir) and the folk-corruption (Ssar'veth), but no Arathi voice tells you which they preferred.

  Story implication: characters interested in the Arathi must work from inference, fragment, divine residue, and educated guess. There is no recovery scenario where an Arathi survivor explains things. This forces the setting into archaeology-as-detective-work rather than time-travel-as-revelation. It also makes the Arathi era a SHAPE rather than a story — the setting has a hole where its First Civilization was, and modern Eadras works in that hole's absence.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 5 — HIDING TRUTH CAN BE PROTECTION.

  The Pilgrims of the Closed Wound believe some doors should stay shut even if treasure lies behind them. The Root-Crucible is sealed for good reason. The First Breach was sealed (twice) by the Elder Gods. The Tome of Baldaran is curated; not all its chapters open in every age. The Doctrine of the Unheld Hand develops later as a divine principle.

  This is a recurring law in Eadras: some truths the era cannot bear should be sealed. The setting does not endorse forbidden knowledge as automatically liberating. Knowledge in Eadras has weight that can crush the bearer or the world. Curation is a virtue, not a censorship.

  Story implication: the romantic-fantasy default ("forbidden knowledge is freedom; secrecy is repression") fails in Eadras. Characters who insist on opening sealed places tend to share Kudur-eṭir's posture — compassion or curiosity without humility. The setting rewards characters who can leave a door closed.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 6 — JUSTICE IS NOT COMFORT, BALANCE IS NOT MERCY.

  The Lorekeeper articulates this directly in Element II: "Ssar'veth became an unrestricted Elder God, but the Spear's curse stripped away his deepest motivation. He gained divinity and lost the reason he had sought it. Arath gained mastery and lost the world it mastered. Eadras gained deep oceans, mountains, fertile volcanic soils, and new life—but only through catastrophe. That is Eadrasian tragedy: justice is not comfort. Balance is not mercy."

  The Omniversal Law of Justice does correct imbalances. But it does so on geological timescales, through consequence rather than restoration, and without restoring what was lost. The Three Loom Sisters do not undo time. They do not prevent death. They do not bring Arath back.

  This is a setting where the moral cosmology is REAL but COLD. Wrongs are corrected, but the correction does not feel like rescue. Innocents die in the correction. The cosmology is fair without being kind.

  Story implication: protagonists in Eadras cannot rely on cosmic justice to deliver satisfying endings. The setting will balance the books. It will not make characters whole. Stories that lean on the Omniversal Laws for plot resolution should expect them to grind slow and impersonal.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 7 — DESIRE IS DANGEROUS GEOMETRY.

  The Spear strips motive. Want-Wolves hunt fixation. The Root-Crucible holds a motivational absence. The Neverborn was given contour by Kudur-eṭir's obsession ("obsession is structure, and structure is rare outside Mythoi"). Even Tarloth's wielding comes from desire — to follow her mate into godhood by force when the legitimate path closed.

  This material is teaching the setting a vocabulary for treating DESIRE as the thing-shaped-like-a-blade. Not lust, not greed, not even ambition in the abstract — but the specific motivational shape that locks a person into a single pursuit. Fixation is the Eadran failure mode. The Want-Wolves are the most legible expression of this: they cannot find you unless you SPEAK your obsession aloud. Discipline of desire is, in Eadras, more than a virtue. It is a load-bearing magical principle.

  Story implication: characters in Eadras can be undone by what they want most. This is not new in fantasy, but Eadras gives it teeth. The setting's most dangerous artifacts and entities all target the wanting. Heroes who can hold a sealed door, set down a goal, or accept loss without spending themselves are doing magic the setting recognizes as important.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 8 — THE LIVING WORLD REMEMBERS WHAT INSTITUTIONS FORGET.

  The Veyran Wardens preserve trail-songs and warded boots, not academic histories. The Marble Mourners hold names the Arathi state could not preserve. The Glassharts encode magical regulation in their antler-structure. The Verdant Scar itself is memory turned into ecology.

  Across this material, the deepest forms of memory are not archival. They are EMBEDDED. The forest remembers. The dead statues remember. The herds remember. Institutional memory in Eadras is fragile (the Arathi state had massive archives and lost all of them). Ecological memory is durable.

  Story implication: scholars in Eadras who want to know what happened do not work in libraries first. They work with terrain, animals, ruins, and ritual practice. The setting has a strong preference for KNOWLEDGE THAT LIVED THROUGH the thing it remembers.

[DIAGNOSIS] PRINCIPLE 9 — THE COSMOLOGY HAS HARD LAWS, NOT GUIDELINES.

  Multiple times in this material, the council steps back to confirm that proposed mechanics fit the Omniversal Laws. Ithren's foresight is reframed as pattern-perception rather than time-vision. The Tilted Miles are spatial-not-temporal. "For a few hours" is duration, not manipulation. Necromancy follows technical requirements (body, animating spirit, correct ritual). Even the Spear's mechanism, when corrected in canon, fits the law: the Spear cannot violate Omniversal Law of Destruction; it can only weaponize entropy locally.

  This consistency is structural. Eadras's cosmology is REAL — its laws bind everyone, even gods. Even Kudur-eṭir, as an Unrestricted Elder God, still operated within Omniversal Law. The "Unrestricted" qualifier means outside the inter-divine compact, not outside the Omniversal Creator's laws.

  Story implication: the setting punishes worldbuilding shortcuts that would in other fantasy go unchallenged. Time loops are impossible. True resurrection is impossible. Some lines cannot be crossed even by gods. This is a HARDER setting than its surface tone suggests.

PART II — LIVE WIRES
================================================================

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 1 — WHO ASCENDED FIRST? AURALIS OR ITHREN?

  The wiki's Three Last Lights page contains a subtle ordering question that the playground touched but did not resolve. Auralis is described in canon as "the last of the three to ascend, becoming a goddess of refuge in the final days." If she ascended last, then Thamior and Ixthal ascended first or middle. Their order matters: did Ixthal see the catastrophe coming early and ascend first, while the other two ascended later in response to Ixthal's refusal to act? Did Thamior ascend in middle, trying a different strategy than Ixthal's preserve-and-wait?

  This is unsettled. It could matter for stories that try to reconstruct the final age of Arath. Did the three know each other? Did they argue? Did Ixthal try to persuade Auralis and Thamior NOT to spend themselves? Or did he just watch, knowing?

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 2 — WHAT IS THE MOTIVATIONAL ABSENCE BELOW THE ROOT-CRUCIBLE, EXACTLY?

  Playground canon says the Root-Crucible contains "the shape of the desire Kudur-eṭir lost when the Spear's curse stripped him." Wiki canon revises Kudur-eṭir's curse: it shattered his consciousness, didn't cleanly strip his motive. The Root-Crucible's contents need slight retconning to align: what is the SHAPE preserved beneath the Verdant Scar? His entropy-ending motive (lost in the unique-mode break)? The shape of his shattered mind? The shape of his Arathi-civilization love? Open canon.

  Story consequence: anything found in the Root-Crucible has uncertain mechanical effect. The mage who touches it "might lose the emotional boundary that prevents them from using the power they already possess" — but if the absence-shape is mixed in nature, the consequences are unpredictable. Good for stories.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 3 — THE COMPACT OF HORN AND FLAME, MODERN STATUS.

  The first ancient accord between dragons and unicorns. The playground establishes it. The wiki does not have a dedicated article. Is it still in effect? Has any dragon broken it? Did Tarloth's ascension violate it (she was a dragon who attempted ascension — did she have any unicorn-related history?)? Does Darkhoof (a unicorn Spear-wielder) represent a unicorn-side violation of the Compact?

  This is rich territory. The two oldest non-Arathi sapient races, sharing a foundational treaty that predates every humanoid civilization. Stories that put dragons and unicorns in tension would draw on the Compact as the thing being violated or invoked.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 4 — THE FIRST MEADOW'S MODERN LOCATION.

  Playground proposes the First Meadow as PATH-DEFINED — wherever the eldest herd gathers becomes the First Meadow ritually and magically — with an original location protected by unicorns and dragons together. This raises tension: a pilgrimage site that moves. Mortal kings want to find it; unicorns refuse maps; dragons destroy roads built toward it.

  No wiki article confirms or refines this. Open: is the original location still defended? Does any mortal civilization claim to know where it is? What happens if a pilgrim finds the wrong meadow at the right time?

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 5 — THE SIX UNNAMED EMBERS.

  The wiki canonizes that six of the nine original dragons withdrew, are unnamed in mortal-accessible record, and are present in draconic esoteric lore. Their names, colors, individual stories, and current relationship to mortal affairs are open canon. Whether any of them is the patron of any specific draconic clan, lair, or ritual is undefined.

  This is one of the largest deliberately-open spaces in the setting. The Nine Embers ascended legitimately — they are inside the divine compact, unlike Spear-ascendeds. Six of them are still active divine beings, just not addressing humanity. A story about a mortal who somehow gains the attention of one of the six unnamed Embers is structurally available.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 6 — THE ASHEN ACCUSERS AND COAL KEEPERS.

  Playground establishes two named sects with opposing views on Ixthal. The wiki preserves Ixthal as canonically ambiguous (worshipped by some, rejected by others). The specific sect names from the playground (Ashen Accusers, Coal Keepers) are not in the wiki articles I've consulted, which suggests they may be open canon — sect names available for story use, not yet integrated.

  This is high-leverage territory for a humanoid character study. An Ashen Accuser priest and a Coal Keeper priest disagreeing about Ixthal at the funeral of a triage decision — a doctor or general who made an Ixthal-shaped choice — is a story scaffolding that drops directly out of this material.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 7 — THE BATHYRIC MANDATE.

  Element II proposed this as creature, polity, or both — a collective of deep-sea intelligences descended from creatures altered by the Drowning. The Mage Architect deferred it as "too large for this element." No wiki article appears for it. Status: still deferred.

  This is a hook that has waited millions of in-world years and through multiple canon iterations. If/when a future ingest opens it, the Bathyric Mandate is positioned to be either a deep-sea civilization with claims on Arathalassic ruins (politically explosive given the Sea peoples' competing claims) or a non-civic intelligence (a collective consciousness of altered deep life). The choice would matter.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 8 — STILLBEASTS AND ASHSEED FORESTS, AWAKENED.

  Playground canon proposes mineral-stasis-preserved organisms from the pre-Cataclysm world that occasionally wake. These are catastrophe survivors of a different kind than the Three Last Lights — not gods, not bloodlines, just ANIMALS AND PLANTS from a world that no longer exists. They usually die "violently or tragically" in modern Eadras.

  Story-wise, this is highly productive. A Stillbeast revived in a mining accident, a druid awakening an Ashseed Forest seed not understanding what climate it needs — these are immediate plot hooks. The CONSEQUENCE pattern is established: pre-Cataclysm life cannot survive post-Cataclysm climate. This is a quiet form of the principle that catastrophes are bigger than their catalysts: the world is no longer one the awakened creature can live in. The compassion of waking them is itself the harm.

  This pairs naturally with Principle 5 (Hiding Truth Can Be Protection) — Stillbeasts might be safer asleep. A pilgrim of the Closed Wound might oppose Stillbeast revival on doctrinal grounds.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 9 — THE NULL OBSERVATORY.

  Playground locates the Null Observatory at the still center of the largest pre-Cataclysm landmass. The wiki confirms its existence and notes Kudur-eṭir's contact with the Neverborn occurred there. The Cataclysm presumably destroyed it. But its LOCATION — the still center of the largest pre-Cataclysm continent — may correspond to a present-day surface point or buried feature. Did some fragment of the Observatory survive the planetary unzipping? Is it under a modern continent? Under the Arathalassic Sea?

  The First Breach (the Mythos-Shell aperture, not the impact site) was drilled "long before God's Day." Its mortal-side physical location — where the Observatory stood — is distinct from the Worldbreaker Scar (where Altea fell). Open question: where, on a modern map, did the Null Observatory stand? This could matter for any story that approaches Chaos-Eadras contact.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 10 — THE NINE SHELLS, MODERN HOLDINGS.

  Playground introduces the Nine Shells — fragments of the original dragon eggshells, regulated Source resonance, sacred draconic artifacts, "false Nine Shell relics are common." The wiki does not have a dedicated article. Who holds the real ones? Are some lost? Are some in draconic-only sanctuaries (Ratharil, the Sealed Hall of First Flame)? Are humanoid kings collecting false shells believing them real?

  The market for false shells is a story-rich detail. So is the possibility of a humanoid character genuinely possessing a real shell (perhaps through a draconic gift or inheritance from an old dragon-ally bloodline). The Shells regulate draconic magic — that's the canon mechanic. The implications for any humanoid mage who handles one are unspecified. Open.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 11 — THE WORLDBREAKER'S OBJECT-NATURE AS LITERATURE FOR ASTRONOMY.

  The Worldbreaker was an alien object from pure Chaos. Most surviving fragments are in the Worldbreaker Scar and along the LIP plume that built Arathyn. The Scar is "the densest single deposit of alien-origin material on the planet." This is open territory: what does it MEAN for an object to be alien to a Mythos? The Velrathi at Veyr'Keth (per wiki) are foremost living scholars of what the Spear does to a plane. Who studies the Worldbreaker fragments? Dao holds every alien shard, per wiki, so any held outside his custody would be there by his sufferance.

  Story scaffolding: a scholar character whose investigation of Worldbreaker fragments puts them in contact, deliberately or accidentally, with Dao. Or a Velrathi correspondence about Spear and Worldbreaker comparable corruption.
