Lairs and Contingency Planning

Summary:
Flesh-Eater cells do not rely on a single fixed base of operations. Each cell maintains a layered system of primary and fallback lairs, supported by strict movement discipline and contingency planning. Survival depends on concealment, mobility, and the willingness to abandon territory without hesitation.

Primary Lair

The primary lair serves as the cell’s active base of operations.

Location:

Remote wilderness, ancient ruins, or broken terrain
Areas with limited aerial visibility (dense canopy, cliffs, fog-prone regions)
Locations difficult to approach directly from open sky

Structure:

Concealed entrance, not visible from standard approach angles
Multiple exit routes, including vertical shafts or secondary passages
Minimal internal development; no permanent infrastructure requiring upkeep

Internal Use:

Resting area
Temporary feeding and processing space
Observation points near exits

Cleanliness Discipline:

Remains are removed or scattered away from the lair
Bones are broken and dispersed
No accumulation of evidence near the entrance

Failure to maintain this discipline is the most common cause of discovery.

Secondary Lairs

Each cell maintains at least one secondary lair.

Purpose:

Immediate fallback if the primary lair is compromised

Distance:

Typically 200–500 miles from the primary site

Characteristics:

Pre-scouted and accessible
Usable without preparation
Less optimal than primary lairs but more concealed

Secondary lairs are not intended for long-term habitation, only survival.

Emergency Shelters

In addition to established lairs, cells maintain awareness of temporary refuge locations.

Examples:

Abandoned ruins
Collapsed tunnels
Cliffside shelves
Dense forest canopy zones

These are used when both primary and secondary lairs are unsafe.

Movement and Relocation

Flesh-Eater cells relocate when necessary to avoid detection or destruction.

Trigger Conditions:

Confirmed dragon tracking
Repeated sightings
Evidence that their activity has been recognized
Loss of secrecy discipline

Method:

Movement occurs during storms, heavy rain, or low visibility
Travel paths are indirect and unpredictable
Cells may temporarily split and regroup later

Objective:

Break tracking patterns
Avoid confrontation
Re-establish concealment elsewhere

Entry and Exit Protocols

Strict discipline governs movement to and from lairs.

Rules:

No repeated approach paths
No direct flight to or from the entrance
Landing zones are kept at a distance from the lair

Standard Practice:

Land away from the lair
Approach on foot or by climbing
Vary routes with every use

Predictable movement patterns lead directly to discovery.

Deception and Misdirection

Flesh-Eaters actively obscure their true location.

Methods:

Leaving signs of activity away from the lair
Rotating hunting grounds
Using corpse-drops in locations that do not reveal their base

These practices allow them to communicate territorial claims without exposing themselves.

Behavioral Rules

Flesh-Eaters do not form attachments to their lairs.

A lair is a tool, not a home
Territory is expendable
The survival of the cell takes priority over all else

Cells will abandon a lair without hesitation if it becomes unsafe.

Failure Modes

Most lairs are discovered due to internal failures rather than external tracking.

Common Causes:

Repeated use of the same routes
Hunting too close to the lair
Visible activity near settlements
Physical traces such as scales or blood
Overconfidence or delayed relocation

Discovery is almost always the result of degraded discipline.

Special Case: Ruin Lairs

Some cells choose to inhabit ancient draconic ruins.

Advantages:

Natural concealment
Complex internal structure
Multiple entry and exit points

Disadvantages:

Known or historically significant locations
Higher likelihood of search or exploration
Structural instability

Ruin lairs offer strong concealment but increased long-term risk.

Summary Rule

Flesh-Eater cells operate from layered, redundant lair systems and rely on strict secrecy, mobility, and rapid abandonment to survive. A lair is never permanent, and any failure in discipline will eventually lead to detection and destruction.
