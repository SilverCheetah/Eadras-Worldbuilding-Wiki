The sealed chamber opens beneath no mountain and above no sky: a circular hall of black-gold stone suspended in the administrative hush between Celestia’s record vaults and the older, stranger silence where the Elder Gods still remember being young. The Tome of Baldaran is laid open upon a table whose surface shows cities, forests, graves, roads, battlefields, temples, and unanswered prayers. Since no narrower petition has been named, the council gathers around the question that stains all divine discourse in Eadras:

**Has the Doctrine of the Unheld Hand become wisdom, cowardice, or bureaucracy wearing a crown?**

---

## The Council of the Elder Gods

**Vos:**  
The phrasing is deliberately insulting.

**Sylph:**  
Naturally. Otherwise none of you would have attended.

**Vos:**  
We attend because the matter is serious. The Doctrine of the Unheld Hand is not negligence. It is a structural necessity. Mortals cannot develop lawful self-direction beneath the constant shadow of divine command. A city ruled by thunder never learns to build courts.

**Raja:**  
A city ruled by nothing burns before it builds courts.

**Nara:**  
And a city ruled by paperwork starves while waiting for permission to receive rain.

**Lirra:**  
That has happened.

**Vos:**  
In isolated cases.

**Nara:**  
In three famine cycles across the northern terrace states after the Second Salt Winter. Prayer petitions were routed through an obsolete fertility sub-office because the region’s temple names had changed. Mothers boiled leather while Celestia debated whether “grain mercy” fell under hearth, agriculture, or civic stability.

**Sylph:**  
A sacred question. Very important. The children were relieved to know their hunger had been properly categorized.

**Vos:**  
I do not defend incompetence.

**Qoxli:**  
You defend the cage that breeds it.

**Vos:**  
I defend continuity. Without the Bureaucracy, divine action becomes whim. Whim becomes favoritism. Favoritism becomes cultic war. Cultic war becomes empire. Empire becomes divine possession wearing mortal faces.

**Daj:**  
He is not wrong.

**Raja:**  
He is not enough.

**Ioa:**  
Both are true.

*A silence follows, because Ioa has spoken early.*

**Ioa:**  
The Unheld Hand was never meant to be an empty hand. It was meant to be an unclosed fist.

**Nox:**  
An old distinction. Frequently lost in translation. Sometimes deliberately.

**Shali:**  
Mortals prefer accusation to nuance. Gods prefer nuance to accountability. Between the two, much evil survives.

**Shani:**  
And much sorrow.

**Lirra:**  
The Doctrine emerged after the first direct-rule theocracies collapsed. Not from theory. From wreckage. When Elder presence saturates mortal governance, institutions deform. Law becomes prophecy. Craft becomes relic-making. Magic becomes supplication. Even language bends.

**Raja:**  
You say that as if mortals do not deform under kings, councils, guilds, generals, markets, and every other thing with a seal.

**Lirra:**  
They do. But those can be overthrown without tearing the Weave.

**Daj:**  
Usually.

**Sylph:**  
Sometimes the Weave enjoys a little tearing. Airs out the room.

**Lirra:**  
No.

**Sylph:**  
A little?

**Lirra:**  
No.

---

### On the Origin of the Doctrine

**Nara:**  
The mortal churches teach the Doctrine as if it descended complete from the Primordial Sacrifice. That is not true.

**Vos:**  
It is partly true.

**Nara:**  
It is liturgically convenient. The Duo sacrificed themselves into creation, yes. They did not leave us a procedural manual.

**Nox:**  
There was a first version. Not a manual. A warning.

**Raja:**  
You would remember the wording.

**Nox:**  
I remember the absence around it. The phrase most closely rendered was: *“Do not fill the space we opened.”*

**Shani:**  
That is beautiful.

**Shali:**  
That is insufficient.

**Ioa:**  
It was sufficient for children born into inheritance.

**Daj:**  
We were not children for long.

**Sylph:**  
Speak for yourself. I remain magnificently underdeveloped.

**Vos:**  
The earliest mortal priesthoods interpreted divine restraint as reverence. Later empires interpreted it as delegation. After the Arathi, restraint became fear. After the Demon War, it became policy. After the Sorcerer Crusade, it became excuse.

**Lirra:**  
Do not soften that. During the Sorcerer Crusade, divine offices failed. Weavespinner petitions were marked “politically contaminated” in twelve major jurisdictions. Protective miracles were delayed until local priestly authorities could verify that sorcerers were “orthodox persons.”

**Nara:**  
They were murdered while waiting.

**Lirra:**  
Yes.

**Daj:**  
And the Black Hand knew exactly which forms to poison.

**Nox:**  
They did not create the rot. They mapped it.

**Vos:**  
That distinction matters.

**Qoxli:**  
To archives. Not to corpses.

**Shali:**  
It matters to judgment. Corruption without an infiltrator is negligence. Corruption with an infiltrator is negligence made useful.

---

### The Bureaucracy as Shield and Disease

**Vos:**  
The Divine Bureaucracy is the greatest stabilizing institution remaining from the post-Cataclysmic order. It routes prayers across languages extinct before current continents settled. It prevents contradictory miracles. It regulates priestly authority. It keeps ambitious Ascended Gods from turning worship into taxation of the soul.

**Sylph:**  
And it has seven thousand separate stamps for rain.

**Vos:**  
Precipitation is metaphysically complex.

**Qoxli:**  
Rain falls when water rises and returns.

**Lirra:**  
Magically induced rain does not. Not always. If mishandled it strips river spirits, shocks local leybraids, encourages fungal blooms, and causes migratory collapse.

**Qoxli:**  
I know. I am saying rivers manage it better than departments do.

**Raja:**  
There should be fewer departments.

**Vos:**  
Every century someone says that. Every century the reform creates three more.

**Sylph:**  
There is a Shrine of Failed Simplifications in the Lower West Annex. Very popular with junior clerks. They leave offerings of broken quills and quietly weep.

**Nara:**  
Mock it, but the clerks matter. Many of them have carried mortal grief longer than mortal nations have existed.

**Shani:**  
I have seen intake scribes hold the names of dead infants in their minds for ten thousand years because no parent remained to remember them.

**Shali:**  
And I have seen the same offices misfile tyrants as martyrs because their execution rites were politically inconvenient.

**Nox:**  
The Bureaucracy preserves. It also conceals. These are siblings.

**Ioa:**  
Institutions must be answerable.

**Vos:**  
To whom?

**Ioa:**  
To purpose.

**Sylph:**  
Ah, perfect. We shall create a Department of Purpose.

**Raja:**  
Do not joke that into existence.

**Sylph:**  
Too late. Somewhere a seraph just felt inspired and reached for a ledger.

---

### Mortal Theologies of the Unheld Hand

**Nara:**  
Among mortals, the Doctrine has split into at least four living traditions.

**Vos:**  
Five, if one counts the Bold conduit-pragmatists.

**Nara:**  
I count people, not pamphlets.

**Vos:**  
Pamphlets become people with knives.

**Daj:**  
He is right.

**Nara:**  
Fine. Five.

First: the **Orthodox Unheld**, common in old temple cities, who teach that divine restraint grants mortals dignity. Their rites emphasize self-governance: citizens bring tools, keys, bread, and law-tablets to the altar instead of begging for intervention.

Second: the **Empty Palm Lament**, strong among famine-scarred villages and war widows, who teach that the gods abandoned the world after the Demon War. Their hymns are accusations, not praises.

Third: the **Stewardship Compact**, favored in Orden and many civic leagues, which holds that gods provide principles, mortals build institutions, and bureaucracy is sacred if audited.

**Sylph:**  
“Sacred if audited” is the most Orden prayer ever spoken.

**Nara:**  
Fourth: the **Wild Refusal**, found among Qoxli’s deeper circles and some Beast-Folk communes, which says the gods stepped back because life cannot breathe under cosmic architecture. They practice seasonal untempled rites.

**Qoxli:**  
No roofs during spring vows. No written prayers during flood season. No permanent altars in migration paths.

**Nara:**  
Fifth: the **Bold Revision**, which argues that if the gods will not hold the hand, mortals may build machines to do it.

**Lirra:**  
That one worries me.

**Raja:**  
Of course it does. They are building.

**Lirra:**  
They are building automated petition engines tied to conduit grids and semi-living crystal adjudicators. If a civic miracle-machine begins making theological distinctions at scale, it may create artificial Favor loops.

**Vos:**  
Bold claims they have safeguards.

**Lirra:**  
The Arathi had safeguards.

*The table goes quiet.*

**Shali:**  
There is the old grave.

**Nox:**  
There is always the old grave.

### Arath and the Fear of Divine Engineering

**Raja:**  
Do not use Arath as a curse against every ambitious tool.

**Lirra:**  
I am not. I am using it as evidence.

**Raja:**  
The Arathi failed because they believed permanence could be engineered. That does not mean bridges are arrogance.

**Vos:**  
Nor archives. Nor conduits. Nor public sanitation. I tire of every aqueduct being accused of aspiring to Altea.

**Qoxli:**  
Civilization always insists its newest mouth is only drinking a little.

**Vos:**  
And wildness always pretends rot is wisdom.

**Qoxli:**  
Rot feeds forests.

**Vos:**  
Rot also feeds plague.

**Daj:**  
Enough. The Arathi matter here because they collapsed the difference between sacred order and total infrastructure. Their city was temple, machine, road, archive, military grid, weather system, and throne. When Kudur-eṭir reached for entropy itself, every part of Arath helped him stand tall enough to fall on the world.

**Ioa:**  
A sovereign error.

**Nara:**  
A household error, too. Arathi families taught children that doubt was a developmental flaw. Their lullabies promised permanence.

**Sylph:**  
Imagine singing a baby to sleep with administrative certainty.

**Nara:**  
They did. And when the Worldbreaker struck Altea, there were no true ruins. Only absence. That is why modern parents in many lands still avoid naming a child after a living city.

**Vos:**  
A custom born of Cataclysmic displacement.

**Nara:**  
A custom born of mothers watching maps become lies.

**Nox:**  
The taboo is called **The Unfixed Name** in several old tongues. A child may be named for a river, ancestor, virtue, beast, star, or joke—but not for a city until they survive their seventh winter.

**Sylph:**  
Beast-Folk name children after jokes immediately. It has saved many from becoming monuments.

### The Game of the Gods

**Daj:**  
We should speak of the Game.

**Vos:**  
Must we?

**Sylph:**  
Oh yes. Let us discuss how our sacred restraint coincidentally resembles recreational imperialism.

**Raja:**  
That is unfair.

**Nara:**  
Is it?

**Ioa:**  
The Game began as containment.

**Nox:**  
After the early intervention wars.

**Daj:**  
When divine rivalries spilled into mortal banners. Armies marched under our quarrels. Mortals died proving which god could produce better kings, harvests, weapons, walls.

**Shali:**  
Souls arrived bearing prayers like legal briefs.

**Shani:**  
And confusion. Many did not know whether they had died for home, god, lord, or divine wager.

**Vos:**  
The Game redirected direct intervention into symbolic, long-form influence. It reduced catastrophic meddling.

**Sylph:**  
It also made us very good at pretending influence is not meddling if done slowly.

**Lirra:**  
Demons interrupted the Game because they did not play symbolically. They consumed the board.

**Daj:**  
That is why we hate them.

**Qoxli:**  
Partly.

**Daj:**  
Yes. Partly because they killed half of life. Partly because they turned strategy into appetite. Partly because they made every fortress irrelevant until we learned new rules.

**Nara:**  
And partly because they revealed how frivolous we had become.

**Vos:**  
The Game was never frivolous.

**Sylph:**  
No, no. Very serious. Moving cultures like carved stones while actual widows invented grief in new dialects.

**Ioa:**  
The Game remains necessary.

**Nara:**  
Does it?

**Ioa:**  
Yes.

**Raja:**  
Say why.

**Ioa:**  
Because gods denied all contests seek other battlefields. Better a board than a continent.

**Daj:**  
Agreed.

**Sylph:**  
Spoken like men who trust boards not to bleed.

**Shali:**  
Everything bleeds eventually.

### Death’s Complaint

**Shali:**  
The Doctrine burdens death with divine restraint’s unpaid accounts. When intervention fails, mortals come to us. When institutions delay, mortals come to us. When priests preach patience to the doomed, mortals come to us.

**Shani:**  
They ask why no hand came.

**Shali:**  
And there is no dignified answer when the truth is “routing error.”

**Vos:**  
You exaggerate.

**Shali:**  
I do not.

**Nox:**  
She does not.

**Shani:**  
There are halls in the afterways where souls may file a **Petition of Abandoned Circumstance**. Most are denied. Not because their pain is false, but because divine responsibility is difficult to assign.

**Nara:**  
That should change.

**Vos:**  
Into what? Shall every tragedy become litigation against heaven?

**Shali:**  
Perhaps heaven would behave better if it feared judgment.

**Sylph:**  
Careful, sister. If we put the Bureaucracy on trial, it will request discovery and bury us in ourselves.

**Lirra:**  
There is precedent. After the Thousand-Year Dimming, several celestial offices were censured for negligent Weave-risk assessment.

**Raja:**  
Censured?

**Lirra:**  
Their seals were suspended.

**Raja:**  
Did anyone lose authority?

**Lirra:**  
For nine centuries.

**Raja:**  
That is not nothing.

**Sylph:**  
For immortals, that is a stern afternoon.

**Shali:**  
Atonement must be proportional to consequence.

**Shani:**  
And restorative where possible.

**Qoxli:**  
Plant living things in dead offices. Make clerks serve one mortal lifetime in the regions their delays harmed.

**Vos:**  
That could destabilize celestial staffing.

**Nara:**  
Good.

### Proposed Reform: The Hearth-Witness Mandate

**Nara:**  
I propose a mandate. Every major divine office that processes mortal petitions must maintain **Hearth-Witnesses**: rotating mortal or recently mortal auditors empowered to observe consequence, not merely procedure.

**Vos:**  
Ascended Mortal Gods already provide executive oversight.

**Nara:**  
They become divine too quickly.

**Shani:**  
Recently dead souls could serve before final placement, if willing.

**Shali:**  
Dangerous. The dead are not clerks to be exploited.

**Shani:**  
Not exploited. Invited. Some crave meaning before rest.

**Daj:**  
Veterans would do it.

**Nara:**  
Widows too. Farmers. Midwives. Failed magistrates. Repentant tax collectors.

**Sylph:**  
Especially tax collectors. They already understand sin, columns, and being hated.

**Vos:**  
Mortal auditors would lack metaphysical comprehension.

**Lirra:**  
That may be the point. A system comprehensible only to immortals will inevitably forget time.

**Raja:**  
Let them mark delays in heartbeats, not celestial cycles.

**Nox:**  
Some offices will resist. Others will falsify compliance. A few will learn.

**Ioa:**  
Mandate approved in principle.

**Vos:**  
We are not voting.

**Ioa:**  
No.

**Sylph:**  
Ah, sovereign order. The oldest shortcut.

---

### Proposed Reform: The Doctrine of the Open Palm

**Raja:**  
Then say the Doctrine plainly. The hand is unheld, not absent. We need a mortal-facing correction.

**Nara:**  
A new catechism?

**Raja:**  
A practical one. Farmers understand hands. Builders understand hands. Soldiers understand hands.

**Lirra:**  
Theologically risky. If mortals believe gods may intervene more often, petition volume will surge.

**Sylph:**  
Oh no, prayers. In heaven. How unexpected.

**Vos:**  
Surges are not trivial. Prayer routing is not mere correspondence. Intense collective petition can alter divine pressure, strain local miracle channels, and induce priestly mania.

**Qoxli:**  
Then teach them when not to pray.

**Nara:**  
That is already present in rural hearth traditions. They distinguish between **asking**, **thanking**, **witnessing**, and **releasing**. Only asking burdens the divine channel.

**Lirra:**  
That distinction could be formalized.

**Daj:**  
In war temples, we teach three prayers: before battle, after battle, and never during panic unless command has failed. Panic-prayer spreads through ranks like fire.

**Shali:**  
Death-priests teach the Last Economy: do not spend a dying breath begging for what cannot be returned. Spend it naming what must be carried onward.

**Shani:**  
And sometimes spend it saying, “I am tired.” That too is holy.

**Ioa:**  
The corrected doctrine shall be called by mortals if they wish: **The Open Palm**.

**Vos:**  
That name will fracture.

**Nox:**  
All names fracture. Useful ones fracture into tools.

### The Infernal Comparison

**Sylph:**  
We have avoided the embarrassing neighbor below.

**Vos:**  
Nyxar is not a neighbor.

**Nox:**  
It shares more walls with Celestia than Celestia admits.

**Shali:**  
Hell functions.

**Daj:**  
Hell processes.

**Shali:**  
Efficiently.

**Nara:**  
Without mercy.

**Vos:**  
That is precisely why comparison is limited. The Infernal Bureaucracy has one central advantage: it does not care about flourishing. It has measurable outputs, uniform procedures, and no obligation to preserve dignity beyond contract terms.

**Sylph:**  
“Your suffering has been optimized.”

**Raja:**  
Devils know their jobs.

**Lirra:**  
Because their jobs are narrower. Divine administration balances prayer, agency, metaphysics, law, mercy, precedent, and domain conflict. Hell balances extraction.

**Qoxli:**  
A leech is also efficient.

**Nox:**  
Devils tempt divine offices not by cruelty, but by clarity.

**Vos:**  
Yes.

**Nox:**  
A tired clerk sees infernal precision and thinks: if only prayer had cleaner categories. If only mercy were not discretionary. If only souls arrived already sorted.

**Shani:**  
That thought is the first coldness.

**Shali:**  
And sometimes the first honesty.

**Shani:**  
Sister.

**Shali:**  
Mercy without discipline becomes indulgence. Discipline without mercy becomes Hell. The Divine Bureaucracy must fear both.

### On Direct Intervention

**Daj:**  
There are times when the Hand must close around a weapon.

**Vos:**  
Rarely.

**Daj:**  
Rarely is not never. During the Demon War, restraint would have ended the world.

**Lirra:**  
The Weave was intervention beyond precedent. I altered the nature of magic to prevent consumption.

**Qoxli:**  
And every living thing still feels the scar.

**Lirra:**  
Yes.

**Qoxli:**  
I am not accusing. I am remembering.

**Lirra:**  
So am I.

**Raja:**  
This is what mortals do not understand. Divine intervention is not a clean rescue. It leaves infrastructure in the soul of the world.

**Nara:**  
Still, mothers whose children are eaten by demons do not care whether rescue is metaphysically elegant.

**Daj:**  
Nor should they.

**Ioa:**  
There must be thresholds.

**Vos:**  
There are thresholds.

**Nox:**  
Unpublished, inconsistent, historically revised, and frequently bypassed.

**Vos:**  
Because publishing them would invite exploitation.

**Nox:**  
Secrecy also invites exploitation. It merely changes the exploiters.

**Sylph:**  
Perhaps we should publish false thresholds and hide true ones inside festival songs.

**Lirra:**  
That is already done in three fae-adjacent traditions.

**Sylph:**  
And they said I never contribute.

### Divine Precedent: The Three Bells

**Nara:**  
There is an old precedent worth restoring: **The Three Bells of Intervention**.

**Daj:**  
From the Broken Conclaves.

**Lirra:**  
Before the final deaths of the Orug, El’mer, and Aetherin.

**Vos:**  
A military-religious protocol, not general doctrine.

**Nara:**  
Make it general.

**Raja:**  
Remind us.

**Daj:**  
First Bell: mortals identify the crisis and act.  
Second Bell: mortal institutions fail despite good-faith effort.  
Third Bell: the crisis threatens continuity beyond the immediate victims—Weave, soul-cycle, reality-stability, species survival, or irreversible civilizational collapse.

**Shali:**  
Then divine intervention is permitted.

**Nox:**  
Permitted, not guaranteed.

**Nara:**  
That word is the wound.

**Ioa:**  
It must remain.

**Sylph:**  
Because if gods promise rescue, mortals build houses in dragon mouths and call it faith.

**Qoxli:**  
Some already do.

**Vos:**  
The Three Bells could guide priestly discernment. It would reduce frivolous miracle petitions and clarify emergency escalation.

**Lirra:**  
It would also help Weave-risk triage. Magical catastrophes often go unreported until local priests fear doctrinal embarrassment.

**Raja:**  
Make cowardice reportable.

**Shali:**  
Make concealment punishable.

**Shani:**  
Make confession protected.

**Nara:**  
Good. A priest who reports failure before disaster should not be disgraced.

**Daj:**  
In armies, a scout who reports weakness saves lives. Only fools punish the scout.

**Sylph:**  
Many rulers are fools.

**Ioa:**  
Yes.

### Closing Judgment

**Vos:**  
So we preserve the Doctrine, reform the Bureaucracy, clarify intervention thresholds, and invite mortal auditors into divine administration. This is not small.

**Sylph:**  
Nothing is small. Ants have empires. Children have apocalypses. Clerks have stamps.

**Qoxli:**  
And forests have patience.

**Raja:**  
Enough patience becomes rot.

**Qoxli:**  
Enough haste becomes ash.

**Lirra:**  
The Weave can support reform if implemented slowly. Not timidly—slowly. There is a difference.

**Shali:**  
Judgment will require records.

**Shani:**  
Mercy will require faces.

**Nox:**  
Secrets will gather around both.

**Nara:**  
Then let the first Hearth-Witnesses be chosen from those who have suffered delay and still refuse vengeance.

**Daj:**  
Hard candidates to find.

**Nara:**  
Rare things are worth building around.

**Ioa:**  
The Hand remains unheld.  
It shall not be absent.  
Let that be the correction.

**Sylph:**  
I give it six centuries before someone puts that on a banner and invades a neighbor.

**Daj:**  
Three.

**Vos:**  
One and a half, in the Azure Basin.

**Nox:**  
Already drafted.

**Nara:**  
Of course it is.

# Tome Notes for the Worldbuilder

## 1. New Canon-Worthy Ideas Generated

- **The Doctrine of the Open Palm**  
  A theological correction to the Doctrine of the Unheld Hand. It teaches that the gods should not dominate mortals, but divine restraint must not become abandonment.

- **Original Primordial Warning:**  
  A remembered phrase from the Primordial Duo: *“Do not fill the space we opened.”*  
  This can serve as a foundational theological fragment, interpreted differently by various religions.

- **The Hearth-Witness Mandate**  
  Proposed reform requiring divine offices to include mortal or recently mortal auditors who judge divine procedure by lived consequence rather than celestial abstraction.

- **Petition of Abandoned Circumstance**  
  A postmortem appeal filed by souls who believe divine negligence contributed to their suffering or death. Most are denied, but their existence creates theological and bureaucratic tension.

- **The Three Bells of Intervention**  
  A divine escalation doctrine:
  1. Mortals identify the crisis and act.  
  2. Mortal institutions fail despite good-faith effort.  
  3. The crisis threatens broader continuity: Weave stability, soul-cycle, species survival, reality integrity, or irreversible civilizational collapse.

- **The Unfixed Name**  
  A post-Arathi naming taboo in some cultures: children are not named after living cities until surviving their seventh winter, because cities can vanish and maps can become lies.

- **Shrine of Failed Simplifications**  
  A semi-comic but useful Celestian location where failed attempts to streamline divine bureaucracy are memorialized.

- **Prayer Categories in Hearth Traditions:**  
  Rural traditions distinguish between:
  - asking  
  - thanking  
  - witnessing  
  - releasing  
  Only “asking” significantly burdens divine petition channels.

- **Infernal Temptation as Administrative Clarity**  
  Hell’s danger is not merely cruelty, but the seduction of perfect procedure without mercy.

## 2. Open Questions

- Who will implement the Hearth-Witness Mandate: Elder Gods, Ascended Mortal Gods, or a Celestial Synod?
- Can recently dead souls serve as auditors without delaying their rest or corrupting afterlife processing?
- Which mortal cultures embrace the Doctrine of the Open Palm, and which condemn it as divine propaganda?
- Are the Three Bells publicly taught, temple-restricted, or hidden in priestly law?
- Has the Black Hand already infiltrated departments related to intervention thresholds?
- How does Bold’s automated petition technology interact with divine Favor and the Weave?

## 3. Possible Contradictions to Check

- The council implies some divine offices can be censured or suspended. This should be reconciled with existing hierarchy of the Divine Bureaucracy.
- If Ascended Mortal Gods already oversee many offices, clarify why they have not solved the mortal-perspective problem.
- The Three Bells may overlap with existing divine emergency protocols, if any exist elsewhere in canon.
- The Doctrine of the Open Palm should not erase the older Doctrine of the Unheld Hand; it should be a reformist interpretation, not a universal replacement.
- The Petition of Abandoned Circumstance may affect established afterlife theology and soul-processing rules.

## 4. Mortal-Level Consequences

- **New religious movements** may form around the Open Palm, demanding more accountable gods.
- **Conservative temples** may denounce the reform as heresy, fearing increased petition volume and divine dependence.
- **Orden** may institutionalize divine audit procedures faster than anyone else, likely producing manuals, seals, and trade-compatible shrine regulations.
- **Bold** may attempt to automate or industrialize prayer-routing, risking artificial Favor loops or Weave stress.
- **Rural hearth cults** may gain prestige because their practical prayer distinctions prove theologically useful.
- **Death-priests, especially Katharim**, may become involved in evaluating Petitions of Abandoned Circumstance.
- **Anti-bureaucratic sects** may argue that the reform proves the gods were negligent all along.
- **The Black Hand** could exploit intervention thresholds by engineering crises that fall just below divine response.

## Voices from the Lesser Courts

**Deputy Archivist Meroval, Third Choir of Petition Intake:**  
If the Hearth-Witness Mandate passes, my department will require chairs sized for mortal bodies, grief-safe interview chambers, tea authorization, and at least four new categories of confidentiality seal. I have filed preliminary objections and a requisition for biscuits.

**An Infernal Compliance Devil, name withheld by contract:**  
The Divine side continues to confuse inefficiency with compassion. In Nyxar, all petitions are processed on time. Granted, they are processed into suffering, but punctuality has moral weight.

**A Katharim Death-Priest:**  
The Petition of Abandoned Circumstance is dangerous. The dead must not be taught that judgment is another form of bargaining. Yet if Heaven erred, Heaven must hear the accusation.

**A Beast-Folk Laugh-Priest:**  
We support the Open Palm, provided everyone remembers an open palm can clap. This is doctrinally important and also good at festivals.

**A Bold Conduit-Theologian:**
