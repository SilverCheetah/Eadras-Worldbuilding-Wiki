4. Post-Vault Gnomish Naming: Arathi-Babylonian Prestige Style
After Inki opens the First Vault, gnomish names change dramatically.

The gnomes begin imitating the recovered Arathi language, or at least what they believe to be Arathi language. Their names become longer, more formal, more ancient-sounding, and more tablet-like.

This produces a post-vault gnomish style with a Babylonian feel:

terminal -u, -um, -a, -atum
formal compound names,
sacred or technical loan-roots,
hyphenated constructions,
names that sound suitable for clay tablets, stone seals, and vault inventories,
cognomens no longer translated directly into Common.
A pre-vault name like:

Narik Locklistener

might become:

Nariqu-Bāb-Nāṣir

Meaning something like:

“Narik, Guardian of the Gate.”

12. Clean Canon Version
I would define the canon like this:

Grug names are the ancestral naming style of both gnomes and goblins: sharp, clever, technical, and chaotic, with names such as Grix, Vexor, Narkim, Tikzar, Rixul, and Phrikon. Grug cognomens are earned through invention or skill and include names like Sparkfinger, Gearwhisper, Lockbreaker, Overclocker, and Quickfix.

Goblin names are the rough sibling branch. They shorten and distort Grug names into sharper forms such as Grizz, Vexx, Nark, Tikk, Rix, and Frik. Their cognomens are chaotic and often half-honor, half-insult: Gearbiter, Fusegnaw, Wronglever, Boomhand, Locksnip.

Pre-vault gnomish names are the precise sibling branch. They refine Grug names into forms such as Grixel, Vexin, Narik, Rixil, and Phrikem, with craft-bench cognomens like Locklistener, Finefinger, Measuremark, Springhand, and Gearwhisper.

Post-vault gnomish names are transformed by Arathi influence after Inki opens the First Vault. They become longer, more formal, and Babylonian-feeling, using Arathi-style elements such as Bāb, Eš, Qir, Zēr, Nēr, Etem, Kudur, Mukin, Nāṣir, Iddin, and Eṭir. Thus a gnome once called Narik Locklistener might become formally known as Nariqu-Bāb-Nāṣir, “Narik, Guardian of the Gate.”

This gives gnomish naming a strong cultural arc:

Grugic spark → pre-vault craft → Arathi-vault grandeur.

The Arathi did not name every child after a vault component.
The gnomes do, because the vault changed everything.

12. Clean Canon Summary
You can define it like this:

Arathi naming conventions resemble Ancient Near Eastern naming traditions — especially Akkadian, Egyptian, and Hebrew. Their names are often compound, theophoric or arcane, and built around sacred forces such as light, order, crystal, memory, gates, conduits, and song. Names like Ra-iddin, Nabu-mukin, Bēl-Qiru, Ašar-kalib, Meri-Rā, and Kudur-nāṣir would be typical.

Post-vault gnomish names imitate recovered Arathi naming conventions. Older Grugic gnomish names such as Narik, Grixel, Vexin, and Tikzar are reshaped into formal Arathi-style names such as Nariqu-Bāb-Eṭir, Girikšēlu-Qir-Shakir, Vēšinu-Etem-Nāṣir, and Tikzāru-Nēr-Kalib. These names carry a Babylonian/Ancient Near Eastern feel, but remain distinctly gnomish because they focus heavily on vaults, locks, seeds, crystals, measures, and recovered relics.

In short:

Arathi names are ancient imperial names.
Post-vault gnomish names are workshop names dressed in the language of a dead empire.
