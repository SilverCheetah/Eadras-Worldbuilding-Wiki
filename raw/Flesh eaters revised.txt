Opening Vignette
The first thing fell from the sky on a day when the rain would not stop.

It had been coming down since before dawn—steady, soaking, turning the fields to dark mud and pressing the sky down so low it felt like you could reach up and touch it. You couldn’t see far. Sound carried strangely. The world felt smaller.

Marrin heard it more than he saw it.

A soft impact somewhere out in the barley.

He stood still for a moment, listening, rain tapping against his hood, waiting for a second sound that didn’t come. Then he walked out into the field, boots sinking, pushing wet stalks aside.

The hand lay palm-up between the rows, rainwater pooling in the lines.

Cleanly taken.

Marrin looked up.

There was nothing above him but cloud.

By evening, they knew whose it was.

Tomas Rell—gone since the night before.

No tracks. No sign of struggle.

Just gone.

Until he wasn’t.

“It’s not dragons,” someone said that night, too quickly.

“Dragons don’t do this.”

“They don’t eat people,” Harlan said. He’d worn the king’s colors once, marched with a dragon in the third campaign. “They hunt the hills. Herd beasts. They don’t come down into villages.”

No one argued with him.

They wanted him to be right.

They started counting after that.

At first it was one name spoken quietly at the hearth.

Then two.

Then three.

People who had been seen the day before. People who had gone to bed. People who had stepped outside for a moment and never come back.

No broken doors. No blood trails. No dragged footprints.

Just absence.

The second thing fell two days later.

The rain was heavier then, driving most people indoors—but not all.

There was a sound first—a heavy, wet impact in the center of the square that didn’t belong to anything they knew. A few people looked up instinctively, but the sky was a solid sheet of gray, the rain thick enough to blur anything beyond a few dozen feet. By the time their eyes adjusted, there was nothing to see above—only the falling water and the empty, featureless cloud.

Then they saw what had landed.

For a moment, no one moved. The rain ran over it, flattening cloth against skin, darkening everything until the shape of it resolved into something unmistakably human. Someone made a small sound, not quite a scream, and spoke a name that spread through the onlookers in a low, breaking ripple.

Lysa Fen.

Missing since dawn.

She had gone out searching for her brother.

What remained was enough to recognize her.

The rest was gone.

The torso had been torn open and emptied, not with any care or clean edge, but with the blunt certainty of something that fed where it chose. The ribs were bent outward, cracked and forced apart, leaving a hollow space that looked wrong even before the mind could name why. There was no pattern to the damage, no sense that anything had been taken for a reason—just absence, large and uneven, as though whatever had done it had eaten until it was satisfied and stopped.

One leg lay straight and intact, the other ended abruptly, too high to be anything but deliberate. One arm was missing entirely. The other remained only in part, reduced to something that suggested a limb without quite being one anymore.

Fragments had come down with her. They lay scattered a short distance from the impact, small and irregular, catching the rain in ways that made them look almost ordinary until you forced yourself to understand what you were seeing. Most people did not. They stepped back instead, eyes averted, unwilling to give those pieces enough attention to become real.

The face had been left untouched.

That was the worst of it.

Her eyes were open, fixed on nothing, rain collecting at the corners and spilling down her cheeks in thin, steady lines. Her mouth hung slightly open, as if the last breath had never quite finished leaving her.

There was more missing than present.

That was what made it clear.

This had not been a killing.

It had been a meal.

“They don’t eat people,” Harlan said again, quieter now.

No one answered him.

They did not find her brother.

They never did.

After that, no one went out alone.

Doors were barred. Windows shuttered. Fires kept low.

Still, people vanished.

A shepherd taken from within sight of the road.

Two boys sent to fetch water.

An old woman who had lived in the same house for forty years.

Each time, the same thing:

No sound.

No sign.

Just fewer people than there had been the day before.

They watched the sky after that.

Not because they expected to see anything—

but because they needed to try.

The bones came on a night of thunder.

Lightning broke over the ridge, turning the world white for a heartbeat.

In that instant—just long enough to register, not long enough to understand—something vast crossed the sky.

Gone before the darkness returned.

In the morning, the bones were there.

Stripped clean. Scattered, then gathered again into a rough circle just beyond the boundary stones.

Too many.

More than they could count against the missing.

A skull at the center.

Facing the village.

Someone began carving names into the inside of the meeting hall door.

They stopped when the wood began to splinter.

A month later, the tax collector came.

The rain was gone by then. The fields had begun to dry, the sky wide and clear again as if nothing had ever pressed it down.

He rode in at midday, ledger wrapped in oilcloth, two guards behind him.

Routine work.

Collect, tally, move on.

The fields were wrong.

Barley left standing too long. No harvest. No tending.

He kept riding.

The gates stood open.

Not broken.

Not forced.

Just open.

“Hello?” one of the guards called.

The sound carried too far.

No answer came back.

They rode into the square.

Stopped.

There were signs of people everywhere.

Doors shut. Some barred from the inside. Some hanging open.

Tools left where they had been dropped. A bucket overturned by the well. A cart half-loaded, one wheel sunk deep where it had been abandoned.

But no movement.

No voices.

No animals.

“Check the houses,” the collector said.

The guards dismounted.

They didn’t go far.

Beds unused. Tables set. Food long gone to nothing.

“No one here,” one of them said.

The collector didn’t answer.

He had seen the meeting hall door.

He pushed it open.

Names.

Carved from top to bottom.

Dozens of them.

Some deep and deliberate. Some hurried. Some overlapping where space had run out.

They had been counting.

Behind him, one of the guards shifted.

“Sir…”

The collector turned.

Something was caught on the roofline of the hall.

Not placed.

Not arranged.

Just… left.

A fragment.

He closed the ledger without opening it.

For a long moment, none of them spoke.

“Write it down,” one of the guards said finally.

The collector shook his head.

“Nothing to collect.”

They left before sunset.

They did not close the gates behind them.

By the time the report reached the next town, it had already begun to change.

Not in the facts.

Those stayed the same.

A village, gone.

No bodies.

No sign of battle.

No survivors.

Just a place that had emptied.

And above it—

clear sky.

Flesh-Eaters

Summary:
The Flesh-Eaters are a small, secretive cult of dragons who believe in absolute dragon supremacy. In their view, the only proper state of the world is one in which dragons exist as apex predators over all other races, and all non-draconic sapient life is prey. They reject all modern draconic philosophy and civilization, operating instead as hidden, mobile hunting cells.

Doctrine:
The Flesh-Eaters’ central belief is simple and absolute: everything that is not dragon is prey. They hold that dragons are the rightful apex of all life, that civilization and restraint are corruptions of true dragon nature, and that sapience does not grant moral status. They do not merely hunt sapient beings—they prefer them. Thinking creatures are believed to taste better, and fear and awareness are considered desirable qualities in prey. Killing sapients is not incidental—it is the point.

They reject treaties, coexistence, and any form of shared civilization. To them, modern dragon society is weakened and fundamentally wrong.

Cabal Terminology:
Flesh-Eaters categorize prey using blunt internal slang. Unicorns are “horn-horses.” Larger humanoid sapients, primarily humans and similarly sized races, are “snacks.” Small humanoid races—gnomes, goblins, halflings—are “not even snacks.” This classification is based almost entirely on size. “Snacks” are worth hunting and eating. “Not even snacks” are inefficient prey, often ignored unless encountered directly. This is not mercy. Larger races are food. Smaller races are beneath consideration.

Practices:
Flesh-Eaters hunt civilization rather than wilderness. They target villages, towns, and travelers, treating settlements as feeding grounds. Their prey is often taken suddenly and removed without trace. When remains are found, they show signs of partial consumption consistent with feeding rather than killing.

They cultivate fear deliberately, removing individuals one at a time, spacing attacks, and allowing uncertainty to spread. They do not simply consume bodies—they destabilize populations.

Territorial Behavior:
Flesh-Eaters mark territory through corpse-drops: skeletal remains, partially eaten bodies, and discarded fragments dropped into visible areas. This is a standard draconic territorial signal, used to communicate with other dragons. It indicates that an area is actively hunted and claimed. Normal dragons mark territory with wild prey. Flesh-Eaters use sapient remains. The method is normal; the target is not.

Organization:
Flesh-Eaters operate in small, independent, highly mobile cells. They avoid centralized authority and permanent settlements. They cooperate opportunistically for hunting and survival but lack stable hierarchy or long-term trust. Alliances form when useful and dissolve when no longer needed. Other Flesh-Eaters are not prey by default, but neither are they true allies.

They are highly secretive, limiting visible activity, rotating hunting grounds, and avoiding unnecessary exposure. Survival depends on balancing feeding with remaining undetected.

Counter-Hunt Behavior:
If confronted by a small hunting group of dragons, a Flesh-Eater cell may isolate an individual dragon, ambush it, and kill and consume it. If confronted by a large force, they avoid direct conflict, retreating into hidden lairs or fleeing to secondary locations hundreds of miles away, often during storms or poor visibility.

Discovery Conditions:
Flesh-Eater cells are rarely found through deliberate tracking. Most are discovered due to failure of secrecy discipline—visible attacks, sightings, or physical traces such as shed scales, blood, or remains near a lair. Discovery is usually the result of carelessness.

Reproduction and Recruitment:
Flesh-Eaters sustain their numbers by stealing dragon eggs, typically from civilized or reformed clans. Hatchlings are raised in isolation and indoctrinated from birth to view all non-dragons as prey. This produces complete ideological control.

Continuity and Survival:
Each Flesh-Eater cell creates a hidden doctrinal cache to ensure the cult survives its own destruction. These caches are dragon-sized chambers containing carved doctrine or tablets, symbolic plinths of dragon supremacy, and a psychic resonance crystal with an imprinted personality that acts as teacher and guide. A single dragon can discover a cache, learn the doctrine, and rebuild the cult.

Because each generation is reconstructed from fixed doctrine rather than inherited tradition, Flesh-Eater ideology shows very little drift across time. Independent cells separated by centuries behave in nearly identical ways. They do not evolve. They recur.

Rebirth Cycle:
A dragon discovers a cache, adopts the doctrine, steals an egg, and raises a new cohort. The cult is reborn. Their cultural phrase is “Always reborn.”

Susceptibility to Conversion:
Dragons most vulnerable to conversion tend to exhibit dissatisfaction with modern society, ennui, curiosity, or arrogance. Conversion is gradual but decisive once complete.

Status:
Flesh-Eaters number fewer than one hundred dragons at any given time. They are universally condemned and actively hunted by other dragons. Reports of their activity are escalated through clans to the Council of Ninety-Nine.

Draconic Response:
Confirmed Flesh-Eater activity triggers coordinated hunts. The minimum response is nine dragons. Near major settlements, forces of twenty-seven or more are deployed. Flesh-Eaters are treated as an unacceptable violation of dragon society.

Summary:
Flesh-Eaters are ideological predators. They represent the rejection of civilization, the assertion of primal dominance, and the belief that intelligence offers no protection. To them, the world is simple: dragons hunt, and everything else is hunted.
