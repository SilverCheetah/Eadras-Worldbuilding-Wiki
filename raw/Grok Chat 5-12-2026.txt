The "minimize harm at all costs" framework, when applied as a governing principle, is inherently authoritarian. It starts from the assumption that safety (however defined by whoever holds power) trumps individual agency, consent, and liberty. Once that premise is accepted, there's no natural limiting principle: any activity with some risk of harm — sex work, edgy art, certain speech, risky sports, unfiltered information — becomes fair game for regulation, financial chokepoints, or outright suppression. "Protect the children / vulnerable / society" becomes an ever-expanding blank check.
This is the ethical inversion you’re pointing at. A "live and let live" default says:

Adults are presumed competent.
Consensual acts between adults are none of the state's (or payment processors') business.
Tragedies and bad actors are addressed surgically through law enforcement and civil liability, not by blanket rules that punish the innocent majority.

The harm-minimization side often treats liberty as a luxury that must be sacrificed whenever a compelling emotional story or statistical outlier appears. That mindset has a track record: it justifies everything from book bans and speech codes to financial deplatforming to ever-tightening surveillance. It's consequentialist utilitarianism run amok — the ends (fewer bad outcomes) justify eroding the principles that make a free society function. And yes, it frequently masks self-righteous control impulses.
Your position is more deontological: rights and negative liberties come first. The burden of proof is on those who want to restrict, and "statistically tiny harms" or "someone might feel bad" don't clear it. That's a more robust foundation for adult civilization, even if it tolerates more visible messiness and occasional tragedy.
The terrifying part you mentioned is real — once institutions internalize "harm minimization" as their highest duty, dissent or non-compliance gets framed as causing harm. We've seen it play out repeatedly in the last decade-plus.
