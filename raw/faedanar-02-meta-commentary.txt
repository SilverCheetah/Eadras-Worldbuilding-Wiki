# HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT

This document preserves the structural implications of the Fae-da'Nar and frontier hospitality material. It explains why the laws, trade customs, and threshold city pattern work as worldbuilding.

---

# 1. SIMPLE LAWS FOR IMPOSSIBLE SOCIETY

[META] The Four Laws work because they are simple enough to govern beings with incompatible instincts. A detailed legal code would collapse under biological, magical, and cultural variation.

The laws are not moral completeness. They are survival thresholds.

This makes them believable: hidden mixed-species societies need rules that can be remembered under stress.

---

# 2. HUMAN FEAR AS ENVIRONMENTAL HAZARD

[META] Fae-da'Nar law treats human fear like fire: not evil in itself, but catastrophic if fed.

This is a strong frontier logic. Humans are not framed as villains. They are numerous, organized, retaliatory, and capable of turning fear into institutions. Woodkin survival depends on not giving that fear proof.

The First Law is therefore reputation management, diplomacy, and anti-extermination policy at once.

---

# 3. THE DRUIDS ARE THE LOAD-BEARING INSTITUTION

[META] The Fourth Law reveals that Druids are not decorative nature priests. They are the judicial infrastructure that allows hidden coexistence to function.

They are trusted because they are not sovereign. They judge without ruling. This is a rare and useful institutional form.

It also explains why Fey protect them, why they survived the Anti-Magic Crusade, and why violating Druid ground is not merely impolite but politically explosive.

---

# 4. FRONTIER HOSPITALITY IS THE HUMAN HALF OF THE COMPACT

[META] The human side matters. If only Woodkin have laws of restraint, coexistence feels one-sided. Frontier hospitality gives humans agency, wisdom, and responsibility.

"Treat the stranger as family" is strong because it is both moral and practical. It admits fear without surrendering to cruelty.

This makes frontier humans more than ignorant villagers. They are participants in a living border treaty.

---

# 5. PEACE-BRASS MAKES DIPLOMACY MATERIAL

[META] The brass/bronze tableware detail is excellent because it turns etiquette into economy.

Hospitality is not abstract. It requires imports, maintenance, levies, market rules, training children, and civic investment. A brass spoon becomes infrastructure.

This grounds magical diplomacy in mundane logistics, which makes it feel real.

---

# 6. IRON CONTEXT PREVENTS SIMPLISTIC TABOO

[META] The distinction "iron at the table is threat; iron in trade is consent" prevents Woodfolk iron-customs from becoming cartoon allergy.

Iron can be necessary, useful, dangerous, offensive, sacredly restricted, or tradeable depending on context. This gives the frontier economy complexity and allows mining towns and Woodfolk to need each other.

---

# 7. TRADE SNOWBALLING INTO CITY GROWTH IS A CIVILIZATIONAL ENGINE

[META] The threshold town growth pattern is one of the strongest extrapolations in the session.

It shows how a good compact can destroy itself through success. A settlement grows because it respects the Frontier. Growth brings outsiders who do not understand the respect. The city then pressures the forest with roads, pasture, timber, taxes, soldiers, and officials.

This is not simple villainy. It is the tragedy of scaling a relationship that was built face-to-face.

---

# 8. TRUST BEGINS WITH FACES; CITIES SPEND IT AS COIN

[META] This principle captures the shift from personal hospitality to institutional entitlement.

Woodfolk may trust Old Merra's inn, the Green Door, the third-day salt stall, and a particular Druid witness. A city later assumes it has inherited "Woodfolk trust" as a civic asset.

Institutions inherit reputation without inheriting virtue. This is a recurring Eadras problem.
