## Scene 1: The Long Quiet

The Imperial command tent is dim. The single oil-flame on the campaign table has burned down to its lowest setting, leaving the tent in red-gold half-light. Outside, the Eastern Front is sleeping — the watch-fires distant, the rotating sentries silent on their long arcs, the war for one quiet hour stilled.

Inside, on the Emperor's traveling-bed, Thoramis Firethorn lies as though asleep. His crown rests beside him on the small bedside cabinet, where he placed it himself an hour and a half ago before he and Kathira shared the last warmed cup of wine of his life.

Vorandethi Kathira Spellsinger of House Valtharion sits in the small carved chair beside the bed. Her long gray hair is unbound; she has been combing it slowly, with a polished bone-handled comb, for the past half-hour. The combing is a Velrathi-discipline practice — a small repetitive motion that holds the internal architecture intact when the architecture would otherwise crack.

She has not wept. She has not spoken. She has not raised the alarm.

She loves him. She has loved him for sixty of the eighty years of their engagement. She killed him with her own hand, in a cup she poured, with a poison she helped to design, in service of a bargain her people sealed.

The Long Quiet has begun. It will last six hours. At dawn she will rise, set down the comb, walk out of the tent, and tell the camp-marshal what she has done. The Throne will be empty. The gods will act. The war will turn.

For the next six hours, the daughter of Vorandar Narand sits with what she has done, holding the architecture, *holding the architecture*, holding the architecture.

The flame burns lower. The night holds.

A great span of years later, in Solarion, a partly-veiled Velrathi goddess in midnight-blue robes will sit in her office during the corresponding divine hour of every divine-week, and not move, and not speak, and *hold the architecture*. The Long Quiet has never ended. The Long Quiet is the goddess's daily working practice.

## Scene 2: The First Prayer

A small Velrathi household. Some years after the Apotheosis. Veyr'Keth, blood-red crystal halls, the geode's facets refracting the cold light into long red shadows on the floor.

A young Velrathi mother kneels beside the body of her husband. He has died of the strange Shadowdark-adjacent illness that took so many in the early decades of exile — slow, painful, requiring decisions about pain-control that no Velrathi mortal had been trained to make.

Six hours ago she gave him the final dose. She poured it into his medicine-cup with her own hand. He drank it without speaking. He died within the hour, in his sleep, with her beside him.

She has not wept. She has not yet called for the household-witness. She is holding the architecture.

She does not know that there is, some years now since the Apotheosis, a new Velrathi goddess. The Northern Court has been quietly adjusting its protocols, but the announcement-cascade has not yet reached her household — her mother's-line is from the lower nobility, two removes from the Court. She knows only that the Vorandethi-of-House-Valtharion-who-poisoned-the-Emperor has not been seen in the Veyr'Keth corridors since the season the strange new chamber appeared in the upper outer planes that the cosmologists are still arguing about.

She kneels. She has been combing her own hair, slowly, with the practiced Velrathi-discipline motion. Her hand stops. She finds herself thinking, with no preparation, with no liturgical training: *Vorandethi. You who did what I have just done. Bear me through the next six hours.*

In Solarion, on the divine main street, in the formal-Velrathi-Gothic office whose nameplate is barely a year old, Velveth's simulacrum in her chair at her desk lifts her head. The prayer arrives. She reads it. She signs it.

The young Velrathi mother in Veyr'Keth feels — though she cannot name what she feels — a small steadying warmth pass through her, the precise quality of a partly-veiled goddess's attention. The architecture holds. She finishes combing her hair. She rises. She calls for the household-witness. The Long Quiet has ended. She survives.

In Solarion, Velveth marks the prayer in her records as **the First Prayer**. The mother's name is written carefully. The mother will never know.

## Scene 3: The Corridor

It is a divine-morning in Solarion. The light from the gold-domed central district falls in long shafts across the divine main street, catching the dust-motes that rise from the corridor floor in the way they have risen for ten thousand years.

Velveth's simulacrum steps out of the Office of Velrathi Affairs. She is dressed in the dark midnight-blue robes she wears every divine-working-day, with the House Valtharion sigils at her collar. The Clarity Thorn tea-cup is in her left hand, finished, ready to be returned to the office sideboard before her morning routing of vow-prayers begins. She closes the door behind her with the precise Velrathi-care that she brings to every door-closing.

Three doors down, Imma Who-Did-Not-Stop-Laughing's simulacrum emerges from her own small pale-wood building. She is dressed in plain working robes of the kind Brella-line beast-folk wagon-Elders wear on cool mornings, with the small wagon-wheel sigil pinned at her collar.

The two goddesses look up at each other across the corridor at the same moment.

Imma nods first — the precise Velrathi-precision nod that she learned to give only after the Tome of Baldaran showed her the soul-life of Meleth Vaethari-of-the-Crimson-Spire on a long evening of careful curation. The nod is correctly executed: head dipping just past horizontal, returning at controlled speed, no smile, no embellishment, no extra angle.

Velveth returns the nod. Hers is shaped exactly the same way; it was Kathira's nod, long centuries ago, in Veyr'Keth corridors when she would pass Meleth Vaethari at formal-court intervals.

The two goddesses do not speak. They do not approach. Neither breaks stride for more than half a heartbeat.

Velveth turns and walks toward her tea-cabinet. Imma continues toward the small porch where the loaf-and-bowl wait on the railing.

The corridor is quiet again. The exchange has taken the time of one breath. *It will happen again tomorrow, and the day after that, every working day, for the remaining millennia of both their tenures.*

A junior celestial-spirit clerk passing with prayer-scrolls between the two offices notices, as the clerk has noticed for centuries now, that the two ladies of this stretch of corridor seem to share some quiet understanding. The clerk has never asked. The clerk knows better than to ask. The clerk continues down the corridor, prayer-scrolls in hand, toward the Bureau of Souls.

## Scene 4: The Treaty-Vial

A merchants' tea-house in modern Solis Bay (the Bold capital). Mid-afternoon, in the current age. A Tarran-descended human politician and a Gray Matriarchy trade-envoy — both representatives of peoples who would not exist in their current form if not for the Great Betrayal — sit across a small table.

Between them, a sealed clay treaty-vial of the kind that has become standard for the higher class of Bold commercial contracts: a Velrathi-archive design, replicated in modern Bold pottery, faithful to the form even though no Velrathi has touched a Prime-Material treaty-vial since the Banishment. The vial is the cultural memory of a people who no longer walk this world, preserved in pottery by the descendants of those who once bargained with them.

Inside the vial is the agreed-on contract for a complicated three-year trade arrangement involving Conduit-passage rights, mutual indemnification, and inheritance-of-debt clauses that will outlive both signatories.

The Tarran-descended human says, in the formal Bold mercantile-cadence: *"By Velveth, in whose office our peoples first learned to honor what we sealed at cost."*

The Gray Matriarchy trade-envoy, whose own people carry the collective shame of the Betrayal more directly than any other living lineage, replies: *"By Velveth, whose hand performed what duty required. May our bond be honored in full."*

The treaty-vial is sealed. The deal is done. The contract is now under Velveth's domain, and any breach by either party will be processed through her office's cross-pantheon channels.

In Solarion, in the Office of Velrathi Affairs, Velveth's simulacrum signs the routing-acknowledgment for the new contract without looking up from her cup of Clarity Thorn. The vial in her iconography catches one more small thread of cosmic-acknowledgment, like all the millions of others she has caught across the millennia.

The two mortals leave the tea-house separately, with the silent confidence of those whose bond is now divinely cross-signed.

Three streets away, a young Bold merchant — half-Tarran, half-something-else, in his first decade of independent commerce — overhears the closing phrases as the two emerge. He has never heard the full formal address spoken aloud before in public Bold commerce. He commits it to memory.

He will use it himself, three months later, in his first major contract. He is not Velrathi. He has never met a Velrathi. The Velrathi do not exist on this plane and have not for ten thousand years. But the goddess Velveth is *his*, too — the goddess of bargains-honored-in-full, available to any mortal of any people who needs the bearing she represents.

A great span of years after her mother-civilization's exile, the goddess who was once Vorandethi Kathira Spellsinger of House Valtharion is *the patron-deity of honest contracts across the entire Prime Material*, invoked daily by descendants of every people whose ancestors helped to elevate her. The bargain she enabled — *bargains are honored in full* — has become the structural premise of every serious commercial relationship her successor-peoples have built since.

Her simulacrum does not look up from her cup. She knows. She does not need to look.

Her own people are gone from this plane. The goddess remains.
