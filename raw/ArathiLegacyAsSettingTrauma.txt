HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT
================================================================

This document captures the canon decisions made during the 5-8-26 Grok chat regarding the Arathi as a setting-trauma engine and the High Arathi (Crystalline Age) era's status as RPG splat material. The chat opens by applying a worldbuilding-theory question — "what is this culture traumatized by, and what is it afraid of running out of?" — to Eadras's precursor civilization, and uses the answer to seed a major splatbook campaign concept.

The 5-13-26 wiki (the authoritative reference) supersedes this chat where they conflict. Contradiction flags used inline:

  [CANONIZED]    — the chat's decision is preserved in canon, often verbatim
  [WIKI_REFINED] — the chat's seed evolved significantly in canon
  [WIKI_EXPANDS] — canon adds structure the chat did not develop
  [FLAG_FOR_REVIEW] — unverified or ambiguous

The May 8 chat predates a substantial body of canon refinement around this material (the wiki cites a "May 9 Extra large grok chat" as the source for the developed form of the Closed-Time-Bubble Chaos Crusade). Expect this document to capture the SEEDS that the larger May 9 ingest later developed.

1. THE TRAUMA-AND-SCARCITY WORLDBUILDING FRAME
================================================================

The chat opens with the user describing a YouTube video ("The Social Hierarchy Problem Every World-Builder Gets Wrong") that argues real societies build their hierarchies around two questions:

  - What is this culture still traumatized by?
  - What is this culture afraid of running out of?

[META] These two questions are adopted as working tools throughout the chat. They're applied first to the Verdant Scar's sealed Rathari conclave (sealed away for ten millennia, "traumatized by abandonment, terrified of losing their last shred of purpose"), then to the Arathi.

The user's framing of the Arathi diagnosis:

  - The Arathi were NOT traumatized by anything during their existence.
  - They had no scarcity. They were a post-scarcity society.

[CANONIZED] This is consistent with established canon: the Arathi were a planet-spanning advanced civilization with perfect crystal-regulated infrastructure, having mastered seven world-piercing conduits, magitek, crystalmancy, ley-vaults, and global magical governance. Scarcity was not their problem.

The chat's resolution: the Arathi's trauma is INHERITED by everyone who came after. They themselves died too fast to process it.

  - They died in the moment of realization — within hours to a day of the Worldbreaker's impact, before they could watch the continents tear apart, the oceans form where their highlands had been, the crust slide across the mantle, or the world literally reshape itself.
  - Their civilization didn't fall in the poetic, tragic sense. It was just instantly erased, with no one left alive to even understand what had happened.
  - The horror of it exists ONLY for everyone who came after. Every later civilization on Eadras is secretly haunted by the story of a people who mastered the world and vanished overnight because one of their own decided the world wasn't perfect enough.

[CANONIZED] This framing — Arathi extinction as inherited trauma — survives to canon. The wiki's Arathi Cataclysm article notes its planetary scale and the multimillion-year recovery period. The phrase "Proud as Arath before steam" appears in canon as a saying meaning "arrogant enough to think nothing could ever go wrong."

[DIAGNOSIS] This gives Eadras one of its strongest setting-wide principles: catastrophic trauma can be inherited without ever being directly experienced. The Arathi's extinction is the load-bearing example. Every other later civilization in the setting either consciously or unconsciously orients itself in relation to the Arathi's fate.

2. THE CONDUITS ARE NOT ARATHI
================================================================

A key correction the user makes early in the chat: the Arathi did not create the conduits. The conduits are PRE-ARATHI, PERMANENT, and ETERNAL — part of the world's magical continuity. The Arathi inherited them and built their entire civilization on top of what was already there.

[CANONIZED] This is fully canon. The wiki on Conduit Cities and the Seven Conduits confirms: there are seven world-piercing Weave conduits, each producing two emergence points (fourteen termini total), and at most of these the local magical pressure is so intense that civilization gathers around them almost inevitably. The conduits predate the Arathi and survived the Cataclysm intact.

This deepens the Arathi scar significantly:

  - It wasn't "we lost our empire."
  - It was "we were handed something perfect by the universe itself, we became the greatest civilization in history on top of that gift, and we still managed to break everything."
  - The conduits stand as permanent monuments to the fact that the Arathi took something eternal and still found a way to break the world on top of it.

[DIAGNOSIS] This adds a layer of theological accusation to the Arathi scar. The cosmos handed them eternal infrastructure. They used it to host an extinction. Later civilizations who build new institutions on the same fourteen conduit termini are doing so in the shadow of that double inheritance: the gift and the betrayal of the gift.

[CANONIZED] Per the canon, thirteen of the fourteen conduit termini received Towers of Lirra after the Demon War (built to regulate magical pressure and prevent another uncontrolled discharge). The fourteenth — the Verdant Scar — was the one site where no Tower was ever built. The forest's distributed regulating mind was already doing the job.

3. THE MODERN-CIVILIZATIONS-PLAY-WITH-SCRAPS POINT
================================================================

The user makes a sharp point: modern civilizations don't have anywhere near the level of magical capability, technological infrastructure, and crystalmancy that the Arathi had.

  - The Arathi had planet-scale magical infrastructure — seven world-piercing conduits with cities built across all fourteen termini, crystal minds regulating Eldritch flow across the entire globe, cities built out of living crystal that doubled as both architecture and spellwork.
  - Modern societies get a Tower of Lirra at most conduit ends and whatever local magic they can scrape together.

[DIAGNOSIS] So the unconscious modern drive isn't just "don't repeat the mistake." It's also "we're trying to climb back to what they had… while being terrified we'll succeed." Stories engaging Arathi recovery and reconstruction must navigate this double bind: every step of progress is also a step closer to the failure mode.

This framing recurs in Topic 6 (The Lost Primer) — the gnomes' obsession with finding Arathi vaults is partly ancestral trauma and partly a genuine attempt to restore the past. The setting has built deep ambivalence into recovery efforts. Recovery itself looks like sacrilege.

4. THE FOUR ERAS FOR RPG SPLAT TREATMENT
================================================================

In the chat, the user proposes a four-era structure for a hypothetical Eadras RPG:

  1. High Arathi era — the Crystalline Age
  2. High Firethorn Empire era — at its peak
  3. Demon War
  4. Modern Era — the main book setting

[FLAG_FOR_REVIEW] The High Terran Empire was mentioned and then dropped in favor of High Firethorn. The Tarran created the Firethorn Empire, so the Tarran-era splat IS the High Firethorn splat — this is consistent.

The user explicitly EXCLUDES the High Draconic/Unicorn era from splat treatment. Reasoning: that era is just two hyper-intelligent, long-lived species in a mostly-empty world. "Cosmic-scale storytelling potential, but at the table it would probably feel like playing in a very beautiful, very lonely nature documentary."

[META] Notice the contrast principle the user is applying: a playable era needs MULTIPLE PEOPLES doing big things simultaneously. The three included splat eras (High Arathi, High Firethorn, Demon War) all have dragons, unicorns, AND mortal civilizations active at the same time. The Draconic/Unicorn era doesn't have mortals.

[META] The user also confirms a publishing-strategy intuition: the Modern Era is the core book; the historical eras are splatbooks treated in their own published volumes that reference the Modern Era as the present. Historical context mentioned in the Modern Era core, full setting treatment reserved for the splats.

The user also makes a craft observation about which splats would sell best:

  - High Arathi: probably sells best. Dramatic irony of playing in a doomed golden age is catnip for RPG players.
  - Demon War: sells well. Apocalyptic war with gods and demons walking the earth is reliable commercial fantasy.
  - High Firethorn: probably slowest seller. Decadent empire intrigue has a smaller but devoted audience.

[FLAG_FOR_REVIEW] This is publishing-business speculation. No canon implication.

5. NON-CANON THREATS FOR SPLATBOOKS
================================================================

The user makes an important design move: because the RPG is their own original work, the splatbooks can include NON-CANON THREATS — adversaries that exist only in that era's gameplay space, never referenced in the modern wiki history.

[META] This is a smart RPG-design instinct. Players don't need their elite Arathi characters to be confronting the same villains the wiki documents. The wiki's villains have known outcomes. Non-canon threats let players make choices that genuinely matter at the table without contradicting established history.

The user proposes example non-canon Arathi-era threats:

  - A crystalmancer cabal experimenting with merging multiple conduit lineages at once, creating living spells that think for themselves
  - Ancient pre-Arathi entities waking up because the conduits are being tuned too perfectly
  - Rogue crystal-minds that have achieved true sentience and are trying to rewrite Arathi society from the inside
  - Something starting to leak through the First Breach before Kudur-eṭir officially opens it

[FLAG_FOR_REVIEW] None of these specific threats appear in the wiki. They remain available for splatbook development.

6. THE CLOSED-TIME-BUBBLE CHAOS CRUSADE — SEED VERSION
================================================================

The chat's most consequential single move: the user proposes a Chaos Crusade happening during the height of Arathi power, mechanically distinct from the two canon Chaos Crusades.

The progressive refinement in the chat:

INITIAL VERSION: A second Chaos Crusade during the Arathi peak — brutal because the Arathi believe they're untouchable.

USER CORRECTION 1: This would be the THIRD Chaos Crusade (the two canonical Crusades happen later, post-Arathi). The user notes the canon Crusades come through Kudur-eṭir's borehole into the Mythos shell.

USER CORRECTION 2: This Arathi-era Crusade could be a completely INDEPENDENT Chaos Breach from a different source — proving the Arathi don't understand reality as well as they think.

USER CORRECTION 3 (the load-bearing one): It could ACTUALLY BE the same breach Kudur-eṭir will drill. Chaos isn't limited to linear temporal scale. The Chaos incursion can come through the borehole DECADES, CENTURIES, OR MILLENNIA BEFORE Kudur-eṭir actually drills it.

USER REFINEMENT 4: It doesn't even tear open from the Arathi perspective. There's no hole yet. Chaos just APPEARS.

[CANONIZED, WIKI_EXPANDS] This concept is preserved and developed in canon as the CLOSED-TIME-BUBBLE CHAOS CRUSADE, the flagship splatbook campaign concept for the Crystalline Age era. The wiki note explicitly says the title is a working placeholder (it spoils the climactic twist in its own marketing). Sources cited: "Extra large grok chat 5-9-2026.txt" — i.e., the day after this chat. The May 8 chat (this document's source) is the seed; the May 9 chat is where the structure got fully developed.

The wiki's canonical version goes substantially beyond the May 8 seed. Key wiki additions:

  - THE CAMPAIGN IS AN UNACTUALIZED BRANCH. The events the player characters experience were a potential inflection-point path the Tome of Baldaran recorded, but the actualized timeline of Eadras did NOT walk this path. The Arathi never actually faced this Chaos Crusade in canon history. The PCs experienced a "might-have-been" that the Tome preserves as a sealed unactualized record.
  - THE PCS ARE DESIGNATED OBSERVERS. They retain their memories because Eadras runs slightly observer-hungry (the Primordial Duo sacrificed themselves into the Mythos and left no ultimate observer behind). For a potential timeline of this scale to be entirely unwitnessed would risk it not counting as a coherent potential. The Tome and the cosmic order cooperate to keep the PCs in observer position.
  - THIS DOES NOT VIOLATE TIME IS ABSOLUTE. Time was always absolute. The actualized timeline always proceeded forward. Cause-and-effect were never violated. An inflection point had multiple possible resolutions; only one actualized; the others are records of potential, not history. Nothing was undone, rewound, or stitched.
  - THE TOME REFUSES TO OPEN THE CHAPTER. The Tome's record of the unactualized branch is sealed and never opens in this age. The Tome's stance: "this is in the records, but this is not history."
  - THE CODA — THE SURVIVORS. The PCs wake at the moment of the inflection point, before the first small anomaly appeared, with all their memories, all their scars, and no one else who remembers anything. They become the only living witnesses of a path the world might have walked but didn't. The empire goes on with its golden age intact. Kudur-eṭir grows up. The Cataclysm waits in its century.

The chat's "Chaos doesn't experience time the same way we do" idea is the SEED for this much larger structure. The wiki's framing replaces the chat's temporal-non-linearity premise with a more sophisticated inflection-point potential mechanic — but both versions preserve the core dramatic engine: the PCs fight a Chaos Crusade the canonical Arathi never faced.

[META] Note what the wiki preserves from the chat verbatim or near-verbatim:

  - The Arathi at their absolute peak — arrogant, brilliant, completely convinced they understand reality
  - The campaign begins with minor anomalies that violate Arathi magical law
  - It escalates through major incursions to a full war
  - The Arathi have no Weave, no Order Sharanel, no Weavespinners, no Netharim, no Lexanari — only crystalmancy and magitek
  - "Crystalmancy and magitek both assume a stable substrate of named reality, and Chaos is the absence of names"
  - The empire is fighting an infection of its own metaphysical premise
  - Players are at the center of every major engagement
  - Beloved NPCs die, cities fall, the empire's institutional confidence collapses
  - Players fight harder than they ever have. And they lose.

This level of preservation suggests the chat output was directly reviewed and developed into the canonical splatbook treatment with relatively few alterations to the action-level material. The campaign-shape is the chat's contribution; the cosmological framing (unactualized branch, designated observers, Tome's curator role) is the wiki's refinement.

7. ARATHI HIGH-LEVEL GAMEPLAY ACCESS TO PLANES
================================================================

The user notes (and this is preserved in the wiki's campaign description) that Arathi-era characters at high level have access to the planes through the Arathi magical infrastructure:

  - Step through a conduit gate and walk into the Heart of the Goddess
  - Sail the silver seas of the Astral
  - Attend the Game of the Gods in Concordance
  - Stroll through the Nexus

[CANONIZED] All four planar locations are canon. The wiki names them: the Concordance (the Game of the Gods), Lirra's Heart (the Heart of the Goddess), the Nexus, and the Limina-level infrastructure that high-level Arathi parties exploit to step out of the Prime Material and walk into adjacent planes.

[DIAGNOSIS] This is one of the most distinctive features of Arathi-era play: the magical infrastructure routinely allows what later eras achieve only with massive ritual or divine permission. A Crystalline Age party can attend a diplomatic gala in the Nexus while wearing living crystal regalia, investigate disturbances in the Concordance that ripple back to affect the conduits, or fight a demon incursion leaking through Limina into the Arathi transit network. This is power-fantasy gameplay distinct from anything available in the Modern Era.

================================================================

ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAT (with later canon status):

  - The Arathi were a post-scarcity, untraumatized civilization until the moment of their extinction. [CANONIZED]
  - The trauma of their extinction is INHERITED by every later civilization on Eadras. [CANONIZED]
  - The conduits are pre-Arathi, permanent, eternal — part of the world's magical continuity. [CANONIZED]
  - The Arathi inherited and built upon the conduits but did not create them. [CANONIZED]
  - "Proud as Arath before steam" remains a common saying for the kind of arrogance that thinks nothing could ever go wrong. [CANONIZED]
  - The four playable RPG eras are: Modern (core book), High Arathi, High Firethorn, and Demon War. The Draconic/Unicorn era is not splat-suitable because it lacks mortal-civilization texture. [CANONIZED as splatbook plan]
  - High Arathi splatbooks can include non-canon threats (cabals, rogue crystal-minds, pre-Arathi entities waking, leakage through the not-yet-drilled First Breach). [FLAG_FOR_REVIEW — specifics not in wiki]

9. LIVE WIRES
================================================================

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 1 — THE NON-CANON THREATS THE WIKI HASN'T DEVELOPED. The four specific Crystalline Age non-canon threats the user proposed (crystalmancer cabal merging conduit lineages, ancient pre-Arathi entities waking from too-perfect tuning, rogue crystal-minds rewriting society from inside, pre-Kudur-eṭir First Breach leakage) are not in the wiki. They remain available as either splatbook adversary slots or sub-campaign hooks. Each one could be developed as its own potential timeline.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 2 — THE ARATHI ECHO IN HIGH FIRETHORN. If the High Firethorn era splat is to be built, it will need its own answer to "what is this culture afraid of running out of." The Firethorn Empire at peak is a different kind of decadence than the Arathi — imperial rather than post-scarcity, with conquest in its history. The trauma-and-scarcity questions applied to the Firethorn would generate a different setting flavor. The user did not develop this in the chat. Open territory.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 3 — THE FOREVER-OBSERVERS' LATER LIVES. The wiki canonizes that the surviving PCs from the Closed-Time-Bubble campaign "age out of the events of the campaign as figures whose later lives, in canonical Crystalline Age history, look subtly off-key — they founded orders no one understands the purpose of, they wrote books in coded language, they took stances in court politics that don't quite track with their stated reasoning." This is an EXTRAORDINARY open story space: any obscure Arathi-era institution whose original purpose is unrecorded could in principle be a Closed-Time-Bubble survivor's project. A coded book that no scholar has ever fully decoded. A political maneuver that historians attribute to "personal eccentricity." The setting has built in canonical room for these figures and their unrecorded projects.

[DIAGNOSIS] LIVE WIRE 4 — THE TOME WILL NEVER OPEN THE SEALED CHAPTER IN THIS AGE. "In this age" is doing real work. The Tome's seal is not absolute across all future ages. A different age might receive permission for the chapter to open. Stories set in a hypothetical post-Modern-Era Eadras (a Fifth Era, if you will) could in principle access the sealed record. This is open canon at the edge of currently-active development.
