# 1. CORE PREMISE

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The Doctrine of the Open Palm is a reformist correction to the Doctrine of the Unheld Hand.

It does not abolish the older doctrine. The Unheld Hand remains the foundation: gods must not dominate mortal agency or rule mortal society by constant direct command. The Open Palm clarifies that restraint must not become absence, abandonment, bureaucratic cowardice, or procedural excuse.

The council's final correction can be summarized:

"The Hand remains unheld. It shall not be absent."

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The oldest remembered primordial warning behind divine restraint is rendered as:

"Do not fill the space we opened."

This is not a legal code. It is a theological warning: the gods must not occupy the mortal space created by the Primordial Sacrifice.

# 2. THE DOCTRINE OF THE UNHELD HAND

[WIKI_REFINED] [IN-WORLD] The Unheld Hand emerged historically from wreckage, not theory. Earlier direct-rule theocracies and divine intervention wars showed that mortal institutions deform under constant divine command.

When gods directly rule, law becomes prophecy, craft becomes relic-making, magic becomes supplication, and mortal self-governance atrophies. The doctrine therefore remains necessary.

[WIKI_REFINED] [IN-WORLD] The doctrine later drifted. Mortal churches often teach it as if it descended complete from the Primordial Sacrifice, but the council clarifies that later history changed its use.

Important historical drifts:
- after early direct-rule collapses, restraint became prudence;
- after Arath, restraint became fear;
- after the Demon War, restraint became policy;
- after the Anti-Magic Crusade and Thousand-Year Dimming, restraint often became excuse.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The central danger is that a true theological restraint can become bureaucratic avoidance. A doctrine created to protect mortal dignity can be used to justify delayed mercy.

# 3. DIVINE BUREAUCRACY

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The Divine Bureaucracy is a real cosmic institution. It routes prayers, prevents contradictory miracles, regulates priestly authority, manages domain conflicts, processes petitions, and prevents ambitious divine or ascended powers from converting worship into domination.

It is not purely corrupt. It preserves ancient languages, dead nations, prayers in extinct forms, and names no mortal remembers. It also misfiles, delays, conceals, and abstracts suffering until harm becomes paperwork.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The Bureaucracy is both shield and disease.

As shield:
- it prevents divine whim;
- it stabilizes miracle routing;
- it restrains favoritism;
- it limits cultic war;
- it keeps divine action from becoming arbitrary rule.

As disease:
- it breeds procedural evasion;
- it multiplies departments;
- it makes clerks answerable to categories before consequences;
- it creates exploitable delays;
- it lets divine offices preserve records while concealing responsibility.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] A semi-comic Celestian site called the Shrine of Failed Simplifications preserves failed attempts to streamline divine administration. Junior clerks leave broken quills and quietly weep there. The shrine is comic, but it encodes a serious fact: reforms often create more departments than they remove.

# 4. PRAYER ROUTING AND MORTAL PRAYER TYPES

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Rural hearth traditions distinguish between four forms of prayer:
- asking;
- thanking;
- witnessing;
- releasing.

Only asking heavily burdens divine petition channels. This distinction becomes theologically important during Open Palm reforms because it allows priests to teach mortals how to pray without turning every fear into a divine demand.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Some war temples teach three prayers:
- before battle;
- after battle;
- never during panic unless command has failed.

Panic-prayer can spread through armies and destabilize command, morale, and divine channels.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Death-priests teach a related principle sometimes called the Last Economy: do not spend a dying breath begging for what cannot be returned; spend it naming what must be carried onward. Shani complicates this by affirming that sometimes the holiest final prayer is simply, "I am tired."

# 5. THE THREE BELLS OF INTERVENTION

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The Three Bells of Intervention are an old precedent from the Broken Conclaves, originally military-religious but now suitable for wider doctrinal use.

First Bell: mortals identify the crisis and act.

Second Bell: mortal institutions fail despite good-faith effort.

Third Bell: the crisis threatens continuity beyond the immediate victims: Weave stability, soul-cycle continuity, reality stability, species survival, or irreversible civilizational collapse.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] At the Third Bell, divine intervention becomes permitted.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Permitted does not mean guaranteed. This remains the wound in the doctrine. The gods cannot promise rescue every time a threshold is crossed, because such a promise would encourage mortal recklessness, dependency, and manufactured crises.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The Three Bells may become priestly discernment doctrine, emergency escalation protocol, and miracle triage standard during the First Rebrightening.

# 6. HEARTH-WITNESS MANDATE

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The Hearth-Witness Mandate is a proposed divine administrative reform approved in principle by Ioa during the council.

It requires major divine offices that process mortal petitions to maintain mortal or recently mortal auditors empowered to observe consequence, not merely procedure.

Potential Hearth-Witnesses include:
- widows;
- veterans;
- farmers;
- midwives;
- failed magistrates;
- repentant tax collectors;
- recently dead souls who willingly serve before final placement.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Hearth-Witnesses exist because Ascended Mortal Gods become divine too quickly to fully preserve mortal time-sense. A system comprehensible only to immortals inevitably forgets urgency.

[FLAG_FOR_REVIEW] [IN-WORLD] Recently dead Hearth-Witness service must be handled carefully. Shali objects to exploiting the dead as clerks; Shani suggests some dead souls may crave meaningful service before rest. This remains a doctrinal and afterlife-administrative tension.

# 7. PETITION OF ABANDONED CIRCUMSTANCE

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] In some afterway halls, souls may file a Petition of Abandoned Circumstance. These petitions accuse divine negligence, routing failure, or institutional delay of contributing materially to suffering or death.

Most petitions are denied, not because the pain is false, but because divine responsibility is difficult to assign.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The existence of these petitions creates theological danger. If every tragedy becomes litigation against heaven, divine administration collapses under accusation. If no tragedy can accuse heaven, divine restraint becomes immune from judgment.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Death's complaint is central: when intervention fails, mortals arrive before Shali and Shani asking why no hand came. There is no dignified answer when the truth is "routing error."

# 8. INFERNAL COMPARISON

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The Infernal Bureaucracy is not dangerous merely because it is cruel. It is dangerous because it is clear.

Hell processes. Hell has measurable outputs, uniform procedure, punctuality, and no obligation to preserve dignity beyond contract terms. This seduces tired divine clerks who wish mercy had cleaner categories.

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] The central maxim is:

"Mercy without discipline becomes indulgence. Discipline without mercy becomes Hell."

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Infernal clarity is a temptation to divine offices. A tired administrator may begin by wishing prayer had cleaner categories and end by wishing souls arrived already sorted.

# 9. MORTAL THEOLOGIES OF THE UNHELD HAND

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Mortal traditions around divine restraint include at least five major schools.

Orthodox Unheld: common in old temple cities. Teaches that divine restraint grants mortals dignity. Rites emphasize tools, keys, bread, and law-tablets instead of begging for intervention.

Empty Palm Lament: strong among famine-scarred villages and war widows. Teaches that the gods abandoned the world after the Demon War. Its hymns are accusations more than praise.

Stewardship Compact: favored in Orden and civic leagues. Holds that gods provide principles, mortals build institutions, and bureaucracy is sacred if audited.

Wild Refusal: found among Qoxli's deeper circles and some Beast-Folk communes. Holds that life cannot breathe under cosmic architecture. Seasonal rites avoid roofs, written prayers, and permanent altars in migration paths.

Bold Revision: argues that if the gods will not hold the hand, mortals may build machines to do it. This worries Lirra because automated petition engines tied to conduit grids and semi-living adjudicators may generate artificial Favor loops.

# 10. MORTAL CONSEQUENCES

[CANONIZED] [IN-WORLD] Open Palm reform does not enter history through one divine proclamation. It filters through dreams, marginalia, priestly disputes, bureaucratic reforms, synods, death-priest petitions, and Ascended Mortal God pressure.

Likely effects:
- conservative temples resist reform as miracle inflation;
- Open Palm reformists accuse conservatives of sanctifying neglect;
- Orden codifies audit-compatible theology;
- Bold tries to automate petition routing;
- rural hearth traditions gain prestige;
- Katharim become central to Abandoned Circumstance petitions;
- Black Hand cells look for new offices to corrupt.
