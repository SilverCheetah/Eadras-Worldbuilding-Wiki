## HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT

This is a seed-level ingest of an entirely new piece of canon. As of the wiki snapshot dated 2026-05-13, **no Dragon Empire article exists** — the Empire is mentioned nowhere in the public wiki, and this document is the first written record of it.

The Empire emerged from a single short Grok session (5-12) in which it was developed alongside two adjacent ideas (the desert empire's tchoukou cheese and goblin tunnel cheese), then crystallized at the end of that session into a deliberate philosophical contrast: the Dragon Empire as the authoritarian-meritocratic pole of a three-culture ethical triangle. ## 1. CORE PREMISE

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The **Dragon Empire** is a polity in the **far east** of Eadras — distant from the western Azure Basin / Orden / Tariq al-Honor zone where most modern Eadran cultural canon has been concentrated. It is governed by a **small coalition of literal dragons** who are treated as **semi-divine** by the populace, and it operates as a **bureaucratic meritocracy** in which every adult citizen carries a publicly visible record of their tested skills and their ranks within those skills.

Three premises are load-bearing:

1. **Dragons rule directly.** The Empire is not a kingdom whose royal family claims dragon-descent or dragon-favor; the actual ruling class is composed of actual dragons. Mortals serve, administer, and obey. Dragons set the direction.
2. **The dragons are semi-divine in the eyes of the populace.** Whether the dragons are *literally* divine — Ascended Mortal Dragon Gods, dragon-descended near-gods, or simply very old, very powerful dragons whom mortals revere as gods — is partially open canon (see `[FLAG_FOR_REVIEW]` below). The lived religious experience of citizens, however, is that they live under the immediate, visible presence of beings whom they are entitled to worship as gods.
3. **The Empire is run by tested merit, displayed visibly.** Every adult — including the dragons themselves — is graded on a comprehensive lattice of skills. The Empire provides free public education in all of them. Citizens are expected to study, test, and advance. Their current standings are public knowledge, worn on the body, embroidered on the uniform, and readable at a glance by anyone who passes them in the street.

The combination — dragon theocracy + bureaucratic exam-state — is unusual. It is what makes the Empire distinct from every other polity on Eadras and gives it its specific authoritarian-elegant character.

## 2. ORIGIN — THE MERCENARY COUP

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]` `[FLAG_FOR_REVIEW]`

The Empire's founding is described in the source chat in broad strokes only: a group of **dragon mercenaries**, dissatisfied with the leadership they served under, eventually concluded that they themselves should be in charge — and acted on it. The chat frames this as "classic soldier logic — *our bosses suck, guess I'll be the boss now*" — i.e., the founding act was less a planned conquest than a gradual recognition by a band of dragon-mercs that they could simply seize what they had been hired to defend.

This origin gives the Empire two of its distinctive features:

- **No imperial family.** There is no dynastic dragon line, no "House of the First Dragon Emperor." The ruling coalition is structurally a confraternity of equals — closer to a senate or war council than a dynasty — and individual dragons come and go over time. (See §3.)
- **A meritocratic ethos at the top.** Dragons who took power because they were the most capable of those present have built an institutional culture that asks the same question of everyone else: *are you capable, and can you prove it?* The exam-state below the dragons is the bureaucratic expression of the same logic that put the dragons there in the first place.

`[FLAG_FOR_REVIEW]` — The chat does not specify *when* the coup happened, *whom* the dragons had been working for, or *whether the coup occurred in a previous geopolitical era* (e.g., as a fall of some prior far-eastern human or beast-folk kingdom). The dating and the identity of the displaced power are open canon and should be settled before the Empire is integrated with the broader Eadran timeline.

## 3. STRUCTURE OF THE RULING COALITION

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The ruling body of the Empire is a **coalition of dragons** rather than a fixed council with named seats. The chat's exact phrasing — *"a coalition of dragons that actually come in and out and go out"* — establishes the key structural features:

- **No permanent seats.** Dragons join the ruling coalition and leave it across the centuries. The coalition's membership at any given moment is the dragons who are currently *active* — engaged in the work, present at the relevant councils, and acknowledged by the others as participants.
- **No imperial throne.** There is no single Dragon Emperor / Empress. The coalition rules collectively, and decisions emerge from coordination among its members rather than from the will of one apex figure.
- **Membership is by recognition, not heredity.** A young dragon does not inherit a coalition seat by being born to a coalition member; a dragon enters the coalition by demonstrating the capacity to participate in it — by being skilled enough, accomplished enough, and present enough to merit recognition by the existing members.

Because dragons are extraordinarily long-lived, the coalition tends to be small, very experienced, and very high-skill. Most dragons in the coalition have spent centuries to millennia building their skill records to the high ranks before they took a coalition role at all. Junior dragons in the Empire exist — and are themselves graded on skill, like everyone else — but they do not typically sit at the coalition table.

The coalition's relationship to territory is also non-traditional. The chat does not settle whether each dragon controls a personal demesne within the Empire, whether territorial authority rotates, or whether all imperial territory is administered through the bureaucracy with the coalition acting as a collective head. (See LIVE WIRES.) What the chat *does* establish is that the bureaucracy runs day-to-day governance, and the coalition supplies the strategic direction and the ultimate authority above the bureaucracy.

## 4. AESTHETIC AND CIVILIZATIONAL TONE

`[CANONIZED]` `[META]`

The Empire's aesthetic is explicitly a fusion of two real-world Asian imperial traditions:

- **Tang Dynasty** — for the elegance, the cosmopolitan court culture, the sophistication of the literary and scholarly traditions, the high arts, the imperial bureaucracy's exam-state DNA, and the urbane confidence of a self-consciously civilized empire.
- **Khmer Empire (Angkor)** — for the monumental architecture, the massive stone temples carved with imperial dragon iconography, the terraced rice-paddy hinterland, the tropical lushness, the religious-imperial fusion of god-king theology with state ritual, and the impression of a civilization where the dragons themselves are carved into every facade.

The dragons in the source-chat's framing are *"fire-breathing bureaucrats"* — a phrase that captures the Empire's tonal signature exactly. The dragons are not wild apex predators ruling from mountain caves; they are *officials*, working in stone palaces and ritual temples, presiding over an administered population, surrounded by paperwork and protocol.

This places the Dragon Empire at a deliberate aesthetic distance from the other major Eadran civilizations canonized so far: the Velrathi-Gothic, the Tarran-Roman/Ottoman, the Amunorian-Egyptian/Renaissance/Ottoman/Islamic-Golden-Age, the Firethorn imperial inheritance. The far east of Eadras was not previously a fleshed-out cultural zone in the wiki; the Dragon Empire establishes that the far east has its own deep civilizational tradition with its own distinctive idiom, drawing on Asian imperial models the rest of the setting has not.

The Empire is not described as Buddhist or Confucian *per se* — the source chat does not invoke either tradition explicitly. The point of contact is **the exam-state institutional logic** (Imperial Chinese in inspiration), the **monumental Khmer temple aesthetic**, and the **god-king religious vibe** (dragons-as-semi-divine slotting into the niche real-world Khmer god-kings occupied).

## 5. THE SKILL RANK SYSTEM

This is the institutional core of the Empire and the section with the most concrete canon. Sections 5.1 through 5.5 walk through the system as a whole.

### 5.1 Universal coverage of skill

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The Empire's skill lattice covers **all areas of life**. The chat's framing: *"basically, all areas of life are pretty much covered. All areas of skill."* This is not a system of three or five testable bureaucratic competencies in the Confucian-classics mode; it is a comprehensive grading system that touches every productive, creative, military, scholarly, and craft domain a citizen might enter. Cooking has skill ranks. Smithing has skill ranks. Calligraphy has skill ranks. Swordsmanship, archery, animal husbandry, accountancy, midwifery, sailing, poetry, healing, weaving, sculpture, every craft and every art and every administrative function — each is a graded discipline with its own pictogram and its own ranking ladder.

A citizen is expected, across their adult life, to **earn ranks in some of these skills** — to choose disciplines, study them, test in them, and accumulate visible records of competence. The number of skills any given citizen carries is variable; the Empire does not require everyone to master every skill, but it does expect every adult to be a serious practitioner of something, and to bear the public marker of their standing in whatever they practice.

### 5.2 Free public education

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The Empire provides **free public education** in all the skills it grades. This is a deliberate institutional commitment: the state does not gatekeep entry to disciplines behind family wealth, guild membership, or hereditary access. Any citizen who wishes to study a skill may do so. Tutors, training halls, and apprenticeship streams exist in every settlement of sufficient size, funded out of the imperial treasury, run by ranked instructors who are themselves graded under the system they teach.

The implication is structural rather than utopian. Free universal access to skill-instruction is part of what makes the meritocracy *function* as a meritocracy: a citizen who fails to advance cannot blame closed access to training, only their own application. (Whether the Empire's actual practice lives up to this ideal — whether wealthier citizens nonetheless get better tutors, whether rural districts are underserved, whether certain skills are de facto restricted to certain castes despite the official policy — is open canon and likely a rich source of internal contradiction worth working with.)

The free-education commitment is also a piece of the Empire's *legitimacy*. The dragons can credibly say to a discontented citizen: *the system was open to you; the tools were free; the records show what you did with them.* Whether or not the citizen accepts that argument is a separate question.

### 5.3 Citizen self-improvement as civic duty

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The expectation that **citizens will educate themselves and earn higher ranks** is framed in the source chat as a civic duty. A citizen who does not engage with the system at all — who refuses to study, who carries no skill markers, who declines to be ranked — is not a citizen in good standing. They are something between a vagrant and a category error.

This is not just an economic or productive expectation. It is a moral and political one. The Empire's coherent self-image rests on every adult being a known quantity — a person whose competences can be read from their plaque or their uniform, whose place in the social fabric is defined by what they can do. A citizen without ranks is, in the Empire's worldview, *not yet a citizen* — they are someone who has refused to enter the social contract the Empire requires.

The chat does not specify the consequences of refusal. (See LIVE WIRES.) The likely answer, given the rest of the system's logic, is that ungraded adults are marginalized but not actively punished — they exist on the edges of imperial society, doing the work that does not require certification, denied the social and economic doors that ranks open.

### 5.4 The pictogram-and-hashmark notation

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The skill rank system is recorded in a **pictogram-and-hashmark notation** that is the visual signature of the Empire.

The notation has two components:

- **A pictogram for each skill.** Every graded discipline has a simple, distinctive pictogram — a hammer-and-anvil for smithing, a brush for calligraphy, a fish for fishing, a sword for swordsmanship, and so on. The pictograms are designed to be **legible across language barriers**: a beast-folk visitor, a human merchant, a goblin trader, or a foreign envoy who has never learned to read Imperial script can still tell at a glance what skills another person carries. The pictogram is the universal lingua franca of competence.
- **Hashmarks beside the pictogram for the rank in that skill.** A citizen with a low rank in a discipline carries the pictogram with one or a few hashmarks; a master of that discipline carries the pictogram with many hashmarks. The hashmark count is the rank.

The exact number of ranks per discipline is open canon (see LIVE WIRES). What is locked is that the notation is **visual, additive, and rank-explicit** — anyone literate in the system can read a person's standing in a discipline by counting marks.

`[CANONIZED]` — The notation works **across species and languages**. This is institutionally important. The Empire is a multi-species polity (humans, beast-folk, dragons, presumably others) and a trade-engaged power. The pictogram-and-hashmark system means that a master swordsman is recognizable as a master swordsman whether the observer reads Imperial script or not, whether the observer is human or otherwise, whether the observer is from inside the Empire or from a foreign land. The notation projects competence across linguistic and cultural lines in the way mathematical notation does in our world.

### 5.5 Dragons are graded too

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The dragons themselves are graded on skill, like everyone else. **Even the rulers wear the marks.**

In practice, most dragons rank extremely high in many disciplines — they live for centuries to millennia and accumulate exam-passed competences across vast spans of time. A senior dragon of the ruling coalition is likely a master-or-near-master of dozens of skills, with the pictogram-and-hashmark record to prove it. The dragons' authority is partially grounded in the very visible fact that their skill records are vastly more accomplished than any mortal's.

But the grading principle applies to them in the same way it applies to everyone else. A junior dragon — too young to have earned high ranks across many disciplines — is **not** automatically senior to a master-ranked mortal in the disciplines where the mortal outranks them. A century-old dragon who has earned only mid-ranks in three or four skills carries those mid-ranks publicly, and a master-ranked mortal smith or calligrapher in those same skills holds higher institutional standing in *that discipline* than the dragon does.

The cultural effect is that the Empire's meritocratic logic is not a hypocrisy — it is genuinely enforced at the top. Dragons are revered as semi-divine; they also wear their rank records. Both things are true simultaneously, and the citizens see them both at once.

How dragons display their ranks is partially open canon. The source chat raised the question and left it unresolved: do dragons wear scaled-up plaques on their bodies? Are their ranks magically projected, perhaps as glowing pictograms in the air around them? Are they tattooed onto scale? Etched into a personal ceremonial item? See LIVE WIRES.

## 6. PHYSICAL DISPLAY OF RANK

`[CANONIZED]` `[IN-WORLD]`

The pictogram-and-hashmark records are not abstract — they are physically worn and instantly readable in public.

The Empire distinguishes between **civilian display** and **uniformed display**:

### 6.1 Civilians — the plaque on a cord

Ordinary citizens wear their skill records on a **plaque hung from a cord around the neck**. The plaque is the citizen's portable license — the document that records their tested skills and their current ranks in each. When a citizen advances a rank, the plaque is updated, either by issuing a new plaque or by adding marks to the existing one.

The chat is explicit that **tattooing is rejected** as a method of display. The source's reasoning: ranks change. A plaque can be re-inscribed or replaced when the citizen advances; a tattoo cannot. (Whether tattooing has any role in the Empire — as a punitive mark, as a status mark above and beyond skill ranks, as a ceremonial mark for certain rites — is not addressed and is open canon.)

The plaque material and styling almost certainly **varies by social class** (and possibly by the ranks recorded). The source chat raised this possibility and left it open. A common citizen's plaque may be plain wood or bronze; a wealthier or higher-ranked citizen's plaque may be jade, lacquered wood with inlay, gold-leafed, or more elaborate. The specific gradations are open canon.

### 6.2 Military and bureaucrats — embroidered on the uniform

For **military personnel and official bureaucrats**, the skill record is **embroidered directly onto the uniform** — like rank tabs.

This is a more permanent and more institutionally legible form of the same notation. A soldier or bureaucrat's standing in their relevant disciplines is sewn into the cloth they wear on duty, in the formal pictogram-and-hashmark style, where it can be read at a glance by superiors, subordinates, peers, and the public alike.

The embroidered form has practical advantages: it cannot be lost or removed (whereas a plaque might be), it identifies the wearer's institutional role and competences simultaneously, and it ties the citizen's individual skill record directly to their public functioning in office or in arms. A military officer's uniform reads as both "this is an officer of the Empire" and "this is a Master Swordsman with high ranks in tactics, leadership, and three other relevant disciplines" — the institutional and the personal merged into a single readable garment.

It is implicit that *replacing* an embroidered uniform on rank advancement is a recognized administrative ritual — possibly with ceremonial weight when major thresholds are crossed.

### 6.3 What this means for everyday encounters

The cultural consequence is that in the Empire, **strangers know each other's competences before they speak**. A traveler entering a market sees at a glance who the master smith is, who the high-ranked physician is, who the master cook is, who the senior bureaucrat is, who the master swordsman is. Negotiations, hires, deference, and challenges all proceed from a baseline of public information about ranks.

It also means that **failure is visible**. A citizen who carries low ranks in everything, or carries no plaque at all, is read in public the same way. The Empire's social fabric is unusually transparent in this regard — and unusually unforgiving of citizens who have not engaged with the grading system.

## 7. RELATIONSHIP TO OTHER EADRAN CIVILIZATIONS

`[CANONIZED]` `[META]`

The Dragon Empire's distinctiveness as a polity is sharpened by deliberate philosophical contrast with two adjacent cultures developed in the same source-chat session. The companion document `cheese-cultures-and-the-three-civilizational-ethics.md` handles the contrast in full; this section summarizes its implications for the Empire as such.

The contrast is three-pointed:

- **The Dragon Empire** is the **authoritarian-meritocratic** pole — a hyper-administered society organized around graded skill, public records, and the visible authority of semi-divine rulers. Its tendency is toward ever-expanding regulation justified as "harm minimization" and "purity" — the meritocratic-virtuous logic at full power.
- **The desert empire** (the tchoukou-making culture) is the **practical-pragmatic middle ground** — a harsh-environment trading civilization where survival concerns outweigh ideological purity, where cooperation is necessary but bureaucratic perfection is impossible, and where outsiders' attempts to impose moral frameworks are received with skepticism.
- **The goblin tunnel cultures** are the **live-and-let-live anarchic** pole — chaotic, free, productive in their own way (the brie-blue tunnel cheese is exhibit A), unconcerned with central authority, comfortable with messiness and risk, occasionally tragic in the costs of that freedom.

The Empire sees the other two as existential threats. From inside the Empire, **goblin chaos is an offense against the natural order of graded competence and visible standing**, and **desert pragmatism is an unprincipled refusal to accept the proper hierarchy of harm-minimizing institutions**. The Empire's natural impulse is to extend its meritocratic edicts and "purity ranks" outward — to insist that all civilized peoples should be measurable, graded, and properly administered, and to view those who resist as either barbarians or rebels.

The other two cultures see the Empire as **arrogant busybodies who would strangle vitality rather than tolerate imperfection**.

This three-way ideological tension is one of the strongest seeds for narrative and conflict in the Empire's regional context: trade routes that cross all three cultures and require constant negotiation of incompatible legal regimes; smuggling that runs un-certified goods through imperial customs; ideological espionage in both directions; and the perpetual question of whether the Empire's expansionist meritocratic logic will eventually push it into open conflict with its neighbors.

The Empire's official theological voice — the dragons' own preferred mythic register — is the *"grim satisfaction"* tone documented in the companion mythic-retellings doc, where the dragons read every catastrophe in history as the natural consequence of mortals overreaching their rightful station. This is consistent with the Empire's politics: hierarchy is good, deference is good, mortals who try to grasp beyond their grade get what they deserve.

## 8. WHAT IS NOT YET CANON

The source chat is short and develops the Empire in broad strokes only. A great deal is **deliberately unspecified** and remains open canon. The major open items are listed below; many are also enumerated as LIVE WIRES at the end of this document.

- **Geography.** "Far east of the world" is the only spatial framing. The Empire's actual continent, neighbors, ports, and frontier are not specified. Its placement on Eadras's continental geography (against the canonical post-Cataclysm continents) is open canon.
- **Dating.** When the dragon mercenary coup happened is unsettled. Whether the Empire predates the Demon War, the Firethorn Empire, or the Amunorian Empire is unspecified, and the answer matters for the Empire's geopolitical relationship to the wider Eadran timeline.
- **Population composition.** The Empire is implicitly multi-species (humans, beast-folk, dragons), but the exact mix of mortal races, the place of beast-folk under draconic rule, the role of other intelligent species (goblins? lizardfolk? snakefolk?), and the demographic balance are not specified.
- **Religion.** What the Empire's official religious cult looks like, how the dragons-as-semi-divine framework relates to the existing Eadran pantheon (Elder Gods, Ascended Mortal Gods), and whether the dragons claim a relationship to any of the canonical dragon gods (the Nine Embers, Cindirith, Tiamat, Bahamut equivalents) is open canon.
- **Magic.** How magic works inside the Empire — whether it follows the canonical Lirran Weave, whether it is taught in the same skill-rank system as smithing or calligraphy, whether dragons monopolize certain magical disciplines — is unspecified.
- **Foreign relations.** Whether the Empire trades with the Orden, the Azure Basin, the goblin tunnels, the desert empire, or any other canonical Eadran polity — and what those relations look like — is unspecified.

Each of these is a distinct story-prompt and a distinct piece of integration work, and they should be addressed in future sessions as the Empire is brought into closer contact with the rest of canon.

## 9. AUTHOR CRAFT NOTES

`[META]`

A few observations on the Empire as a piece of authorial craft that are worth preserving from the source-chat conversation:

**The Empire was developed as a deliberate philosophical foil.** It crystallized into its full shape at the end of the source chat, when the author connected several thematic threads — real-world frustration with "harm minimization at all costs" authoritarianism, the contrasting cheese-aging environments of the desert nomads vs the goblin tunnels, the meritocratic exam-state model of Imperial China — and recognized that the Dragon Empire could carry the authoritarian-meritocratic pole of an ideological triangle. The Empire is *narratively interesting because it is internally coherent* — it makes sense from inside; it is not a strawman of authoritarianism but a working civilization whose internal logic produces both genuine achievement and genuine oppression. That ambiguity is the point.

**Tang + Khmer is the right fusion for an Eadran far east.** The far east of Eadras has been thinly drawn in earlier wiki work, and the choice of Tang+Khmer rather than Tang+Heian, Khmer+Srivijaya, or any other combination is specific. The Tang side supplies the urbane scholarly empire vibe (the exam-state inheritance, the cosmopolitan court, the formal aesthetic of the bureaucracy). The Khmer side supplies the god-king theology, the temple-mountain monumental architecture, the tropical agrarian hinterland, and the dragon-iconography-everywhere visual texture. Both are imperial idioms with strong religious-state fusion, both produced civilizations that mortals saw as semi-divine in their height, and both leave room for the dragons themselves to slot in as the apex of the religious-political pyramid.

**Pictogram-and-hashmark is genre-native.** The notation is the kind of detail that makes a setting feel lived-in: it is *visual*, it is *cross-linguistic*, it is *evidence-of-competence-on-the-body*, and it pays off in every encounter a character has in the Empire. It also handles the meta-problem of how RPG characters or novel protagonists would actually know a stranger's competences in a multi-species, multi-language polity. It is a much stronger choice than a hidden registry or a magical aura-reading system, because it is *physical* and *legible* to ordinary observation.

**The "even dragons are graded" rule is what saves the system from being a cosmetic meritocracy.** A meritocracy where the rulers are exempt from grading is just an aristocracy with extra steps. By insisting that the dragons themselves wear their pictogram-and-hashmark records, the Empire makes the grading principle culturally credible from top to bottom. Whether this credibility survives close inspection (whether dragon ranks are honestly earned and audited, or whether they are inflated by deference) is a beautiful internal tension worth exploiting.

## 10. CROSS-REFERENCES AND INTEGRATION HOOKS

The Dragon Empire intersects several existing canon zones, even though no wiki article currently links to it. The hooks worth noting:

- **Dragons-as-rulers** connects to the canon of dragon gods, Ascended Mortal Dragon Gods (per the wiki, dragons have produced more Ascended Mortal Gods than any other lineage), and the Nine Embers / Cindirith / draconic divinity tradition. The Empire's dragon coalition almost certainly includes very old dragons whose institutional standing intersects this canon, and *might* include Ascended Mortal Dragon Gods themselves — though whether the coalition is composed of mortal dragons or includes one or more Ascended members is open canon.
- **Free public education + meritocratic exam state** can be contrasted with the Amunorian eunuch bureaucracy (which is a closed credentialing system where commoner children are selected and made into a separate class). The two systems share an institutional anti-aristocracy impulse but solve it in opposite ways — Amunorian by creating a sealed caste of administrators loyal to the bureaucracy itself, Dragon Empire by opening the credential to everyone and grading them publicly.
- **Pictogram-and-hashmark notation** can be contrasted with the Amunorian **hieroglyph-rune hybrid script** (which is a magical infrastructure script on imperial buildings, not a personal-rank system). Both are pictographic-feeling visual systems with magical-feeling significance, but they serve very different functions.
- **Tang + Khmer aesthetic** introduces a new architectural idiom to Eadras alongside the canonical Velrathi-Gothic, Tarran-Roman/Ottoman, Amunorian-Renaissance/Ottoman/Egyptian/Islamic, and Elfen-song-shaped traditions. The far-eastern far-Asian register is genuinely new.
- **Authoritarian-meritocratic political archetype** complements the existing political archetypes in Eadran canon: the Amunorian divine-monarchy + merchant-prince + eunuch bureaucracy, the Orden trade compact, the Bold Conduit Republic, the Azure Basin polities (Venethar lagoon republic, Serene Principalities, etc.), the post-Banishment Gray Matriarchy. The Empire adds a *far-eastern* political archetype the setting did not previously have.

## LIVE WIRES

Open story prompts and unresolved questions surfaced by this material:

- **What did the dragon mercenaries displace?** Whose army were they grumpy with, and what kingdom or polity fell when they decided to take charge? The original power may still exist as a memory, a diaspora, or a foreign-exile faction with a grudge.
- **When did this happen?** Pinning the Empire's founding on the Eadran timeline determines its relationship to the Firethorn Empire, the Amunorian Empire, the Demon War, and the Sorcerer Crusade. A pre-Firethorn founding makes the Empire one of the oldest continuous states on Eadras; a post-Demon-War founding makes it a young rival to the Amunorians.
- **What does the dragon coalition's table look like?** How many dragons sit at it on a typical day? How are decisions made when they disagree? Is there a tradition of formal debate, ritual challenge, or quiet consensus?
- **What do dragons wear, if anything, to display their ranks?** Plaques scaled to body size? Magically projected pictograms? Inscribed scale-etchings? Ceremonial mantles? This is one of the more visually-loaded open questions and is worth settling because it determines what an imperial audience with a coalition dragon actually looks like.
- **How are exams administered?** Who tests citizens? Are the test-givers themselves graded in the testing discipline? What happens when an exam is failed — can it be retaken? Is there a fee? A waiting period? A public record of attempts?
- **What is the full rank ladder?** How many ranks per discipline? Are the lower ranks easier and the higher ones harder (the usual shape)? Is rank-5 calligraphy roughly comparable to rank-5 swordsmanship in terms of public standing, or are some disciplines weighted higher? Is there a cap?
- **What disciplines exist at the meta level?** Is there a discipline of *teaching*, with its own ranks, that the public educators hold? A discipline of *bureaucratic administration* held by the bureaucrats? A discipline of *imperial protocol* held by court attendants?
- **Are there forbidden or restricted disciplines?** The framing of the Empire as authoritarian-meritocratic implies the existence of disciplines the Empire grades downward, marks as suspect, or actively forbids. What gets a citizen interrogated for studying it?
- **What happens to citizens who refuse to be graded?** The marginalization is implicit; the specific mechanisms (legal disabilities, ineligibility for service, social exclusion, taxation differential) are open.
- **What does the Empire's military look like?** A meritocratic exam-state with embroidered uniforms suggests an extremely orderly, ranked, and impressive standing army. Does it field dragon shock troops? Beast-folk auxiliaries? A navy of war-junks for trade-route enforcement? What are its frontiers and what does it fight on them?
- **How do the goblin tunnels read in imperial doctrine?** The companion three-cultures doc establishes the contrast; the Empire's specific legal framing of goblin chaos as a category — barbarism, contagion, a "purity emergency" — is open and is a strong prompt for any cross-cultural conflict story.
- **How does the Empire deal with the desert empire?** The middle pole of the three-cultures triangle is the most likely to have stable trade relations with the Dragon Empire (both are pragmatic enough to negotiate).
